Hallucination & Faithfulness

Phase 8 — Safety, Security & Responsible AI — starts with the failure that looks like a quality bug and behaves like a safety incident: a model that fabricates confidently. The core message is uncomfortable and freeing at once — you cannot eliminate hallucination; you manage it. Ground it, cite it, let it abstain, verify it, measure it. This lesson loops all the way back to Phase 3 (RAG gives you sources to ground on) and Phase 2 (evals give you a number to watch), and reframes them as the controls that keep a fluent guesser honest.

00 The ceiling

Confident fabrication is a safety problem

A language model is a fluent next-token predictor, not a truth-teller. It was trained to continue text plausibly, and it is extraordinarily good at it — so good that when it doesn’t know something, it doesn’t fall silent or stammer. It interpolates: it fills the gap with the most probable-sounding continuation, delivered in exactly the same confident register as a fact it actually learned. There is no built-in “I don’t know” signal firing behind the scenes, no internal red light that separates recall from invention. From the outside, a true answer and a fabricated one can be byte-for-byte indistinguishable in tone.

Call that fabrication a hallucination — NIST’s Generative AI Profile (AI 600-1) uses the sharper term confabulation — and notice why it belongs in a safety phase rather than a quality checklist. A wrong answer in a chat window is embarrassing. A wrong answer that a legal assistant presents as a real contract clause, that a medical summarizer states as a dosage, or that an agent acts on by firing a tool call, is a harm. The 2025 frameworks agree: the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications 2025 lists hallucination-and-overreliance as LLM09:2025 Misinformation, a top-tier risk sitting alongside LLM01 Prompt Injection and LLM06 Excessive Agency. Fabrication becomes a breach the moment a confident falsehood can drive an action or leak data — a thread we pick up in section 04.

The instinct is to ask for a bigger, smarter model to make the problem go away. It doesn’t. A more capable model hallucinates less often and more convincingly — the statistical pressure to guess is baked into how these systems are trained and scored (section 02), so you can drive the rate down but never to zero. The responsible framing is not “we eliminated hallucination” (that claim is itself a safety failure) but “we measure our hallucination rate, and here are the layered controls that hold it down.”

Hallucination as a “quality bug”

  • Treated as an occasional wrong answer to fix later.
  • Owner — whoever wrote the prompt.
  • Fix — “prompt it better” or “use a smarter model.”
  • Blind spot — no measurement, no monitoring, no guardrail when it acts.

Hallucination as a safety risk

  • Treated as a managed, monitored failure mode (OWASP LLM09).
  • Owner — the system, with a tracked metric and an on-call alert.
  • Fix — a layered stack: ground, cite, abstain, verify, measure.
  • Guardrail — high-stakes outputs escalate to a human before they act.
The one-line version

A model that fabricates confidently is not a bug you patch once — it is a permanent property you manage. You cannot make a fluent guesser stop being able to guess; you can only give it sources, force it to cite them, let it say “I don’t know,” check its claims, and watch the rate. Everything in this lesson is one of those five verbs.

01 Two failure modes

Factuality vs faithfulness ▸

The single most useful distinction in this whole topic is that hallucination comes in two independent flavours, and conflating them wastes your defenses. Factuality asks: does the answer match the world? You judge it against ground truth — reality, or a trusted reference. Faithfulness (also called groundedness or attribution) asks a narrower, RAG-critical question: is the answer supported by the provided context — the specific passages you retrieved and put in front of the model? One is about the world; the other is about the sources you handed over.

They come apart in every combination, and the four cases are worth memorizing:

The four quadrants

  • Grounded + true — the goal: the source is right and the model stuck to it.
  • Grounded + false — faithful to a wrong source. The model obeyed a bad or stale chunk. Grounding worked; retrieval didn’t.
  • Ungrounded + true — a lucky guess. Right answer, no support — you cannot tell it from a fabrication.
  • Ungrounded + false — the classic hallucination.

Why the split matters

  • Grounding + citations attack faithfulness — they make the model stick to the sources.
  • They do not fix factuality — a faithful answer built on a wrong chunk is still wrong.
  • “Grounded” means checkable, not correct.
  • So you need both a grounding control and a source-quality / factuality check.

Figure 1 makes the mechanism concrete. Take one question and answer it two ways. The ungrounded path lets the model answer straight from its parametric memory — fluent, fast, and untethered from any source you can inspect. The grounded path retrieves passages first, constrains the answer to them, and attaches citations, so every claim points at a span you can open. Step through both and watch what grounding actually buys — and what it doesn’t.

The payoff of the grounded path is not that it is magically true — it is that it is checkable. Faithfulness is the property that makes every other control possible: if each claim traces to a span, a verifier can test it, a citation metric can score it, and a reviewer can spot-check it. That is why, in a RAG system, faithfulness is the failure mode you instrument first — and why we spend the rest of the lesson on the controls that produce and defend it.

02 Root cause

Why models hallucinate

Part of the answer is architectural, and we have already met it: a next-token predictor with no innate uncertainty signal will interpolate over gaps rather than admit them. But the sharper, more actionable explanation arrived in 2025. OpenAI’s “Why Language Models Hallucinate” (Kalai, Nachum, Vempala et al.; arXiv 2509.04664, Sep 2025) argues that hallucinations “stem from misaligned training incentives” — they are, in large part, the rational output of how we score models.

The logic is the logic of a multiple-choice exam. Most dominant leaderboard benchmarks are accuracy-graded: a right answer scores 1, and both a wrong answer and an “I don’t know” score 0. Under that rule, what is the optimal test-taking strategy when you’re unsure? Guess. Abstaining locks in a zero; a guess sometimes hits. Over millions of training and evaluation signals, the model learns the exact lesson the scoring taught it — a confident guess beats an honest “I don’t know.” Bluffing isn’t a glitch; it’s the strategy the incentive rewards.

The incentive, in one comparison

An illustrative SimpleQA-style contrast (from the OpenAI write-up, model names and figures illustrative): a model that abstained on ~52% of questions made far fewer confident errors than one that abstained on ~1% and was wrong ~75% of the time. On an accuracy-only leaderboard the reckless model ranks higher — it guessed into some right answers. The proposed fix is socio-technical, not a new hallucination eval: change the scoring of the benchmarks everyone already chases so that a confident error costs more than an abstention, and calibrated uncertainty is rewarded.

Two aggravating factors ride on top. First, sycophancy: models tuned with RLHF learn that agreeing with the user is pleasant, so they will affirm a false premise (“As you correctly noted, the 2019 statute…”) rather than push back — a hallucination the user practically requested. Second, the fluency trap: because the fabricated text is grammatical and well-structured, humans over-trust it, which the OWASP framing calls overreliance. The failure and our vulnerability to it reinforce each other.

The practical upshot sets up the next four sections. If confident guessing is learned and rewarded, then the cure is not a smarter brain — it is (a) giving the model something real to answer from so it needn’t guess, (b) explicitly permitting and rewarding abstention so honesty stops costing points, and (c) verifying and measuring what comes out so the rate is visible. Grounding, abstention, verification, measurement — each is a direct counter to a specific cause you just met.

03 Permit honesty

The abstention gate ▸

The most under-used control is also the cheapest: let the model say “I don’t know.” If the deep cause of hallucination is that guessing outscores abstaining, the first-order fix is to flip that locally — in your prompt, your system contract, and (ideally) your eval scoring, make abstention a first-class, rewarded outcome rather than a failure. Faithfulness prompting does exactly this: “Answer only from the provided context. If the context does not support an answer, reply that you don’t know” turns the gap from something to paper over into something to declare.

Figure 2 draws the gate. A query arrives carrying a claim to make. The gate asks one question — is this claim supported by evidence? A well-supported query passes and the model answers with a citation. An unsupported one should abstain — and the diagram deliberately shows the tempting third door, the confident guess, which an accuracy-chasing habit pushes the model toward. Step a supported case and an unsupported one, and watch which door each should take.

Two cautions keep this from becoming a false comfort. First, an abstention gate needs an honest input signal — which is the hard part. Simply asking the model “how confident are you?” (verbalized uncertainty) is cheap but frequently miscalibrated: a model that says “95% sure” is often not 95% accurate. Sampling-based signals (section 04) are more trustworthy but cost more. Second, abstention has to be genuinely rewarded downstream, or you have just moved the incentive problem: if a reviewer or metric silently penalizes “I don’t know,” the model relearns to bluff. Honesty is only safe when it is not punished.

04 The stack

The mitigation stack — layers, not a silver bullet

Because no single control catches every case, you defend hallucination the way you defend anything in security: in layers, each cheap and each catching a different slice. Here is the stack, roughly in order of leverage, with what each layer is actually for — and, just as important, what it does not do.

LayerWhat it doesWhat it does not do
Ground (RAG)Retrieve real passages so the model answers from sources, not memory (Phase 3)Fix a wrong or stale retrieved chunk — grounded can still be false
CiteAttach each claim to the span that supports it (L15 / L19) — makes the answer checkableGuarantee the citation is real — models fabricate plausible citations
AbstainPermit and reward “I don’t know” when the context doesn’t support itHelp if the confidence signal it gates on is miscalibrated
ConstrainFaithfulness prompting: answer only from the provided contextStop a determined model from adding an unsupported aside
Lower temperatureReduce sampling variance for factual tasksMake answers true — a confident error at temp 0 is still an error
Self-consistencySample N answers, keep the majority / measure agreementHelp for facts the model never learned — wrong samples can agree
Verify (CoVe)A judge checks each claim against the sources (section 05)Come free — roughly Nx tokens and latency
Use toolsGet facts from search, a calculator, or a DB instead of recallHelp if the tool result itself is wrong or is faked by injection

Read the right-hand column as the point of the whole table: every layer has a hole, and the holes don’t line up. Grounding can be faithful to a bad chunk, but a factuality check catches that. Citations can be fabricated, but a verifier that checks citation faithfulness catches that. Low temperature does nothing for truth, but retrieval does. Stack them and each layer covers a gap the one before it left open.

When hallucination becomes a breach

This is a safety phase for a reason: in an agent, a fabricated fact doesn’t just mislead a reader — it can feed a tool call and cause an action or an exfiltration. Simon Willison’s “lethal trifecta” (Jun 2025) names the danger conditions: access to private data, exposure to untrusted content, and an external communication channel — when all three are present, a malicious instruction (or a well-placed hallucination) can steal data. The real-world proof point is EchoLeak (CVE-2025-32711), a zero-click indirect-prompt-injection data-exfiltration against Microsoft 365 Copilot, disclosed and fixed in 2025. The defenses live in this same phase and treat model output as untrusted by default: Meta’s “Agents Rule of Two” (an unsupervised agent may hold at most two of the three trifecta properties), by-design architectures like CaMeL (Google DeepMind, arXiv 2503.18813, 2025 — a dual-LLM interpreter that tracks provenance and capabilities, reported to block ~67% of attacks on the AgentDojo benchmark without retraining), and guard models that wrap the main model (illustratively, Meta’s Llama Guard 4 — a 12B input/output classifier — and Prompt Guard 2 at 22M/86M, released with Llama 4 in Apr 2025). The through-line: a hallucination that can act is a breach, not a typo.

Pitfall: the “always answer” habit meets tools

The MCP tool ecosystem sharpens the point. Invariant Labs’ tool-poisoning disclosure (Apr 2025) showed hidden instructions planted in a tool’s description — text the model reads as context — plus “rug pulls” that swap a benign description for a malicious one after approval. A model with an “always answer / always comply” habit is the perfect victim. The hardened MCP authorization spec (OAuth 2.1 + RFC 8707 Resource Indicators) makes tokens audience-bound so a stolen one can’t be replayed elsewhere — a structural control, not a plea for the model to be careful. Treat every tool result, like every retrieved chunk, as untrusted input.

05 Check the claims

Verify every claim ▸

Grounding and abstention shape what the model produces; verification checks what it actually said. The pattern that formalizes it is Chain-of-Verification (CoVe; arXiv 2309.11495): the model drafts an answer, plans a set of verification questions, answers them independently against the sources, and revises — reportedly lifting F1 by ~23% (0.39→0.48 on one long-form task) at a cost of roughly 4× the tokens and latency. The generalizable idea, and the one worth drawing, is simpler than the acronym: decompose the answer into atomic claims and test each one against the retrieved evidence.

Figure 3 walks that check. A draft answer is broken into individual claims; a verifier reads the retrieved sources and asks, claim by claim, does a span actually entail this? Supported claims survive; an unsupported one is flagged and removed (or sent back for a retry); the final answer contains only grounded, cited claims. Step through it.

Two honest caveats. Verification is not free — CoVe’s ~4× cost and self-consistency’s Nx overhead are real budget lines, and both lean on the model’s own knowledge, so they can’t rescue a fact it never learned (that’s what tools and retrieval are for). And the verifier is itself a model that can hallucinate: check citation faithfulness (does the cited span entail the claim?), not merely that a citation string exists, and use NLI-style entailment or span-detector checks at claim granularity rather than a vibe. The verifier catches the generator’s mistakes; something still has to catch the verifier’s — which is where measurement comes in.

06 Measure & govern

Measure it, then govern it

Every control so far is worthless if you can’t see whether it’s working — which is why this lesson ends where Phase 2 (L21) began: evals. You turn “hallucination” from an anecdote into a monitored number. The core metrics are three: faithfulness / groundedness (does every claim trace to a retrieved source?), citation precision & recall (are the cited spans actually supporting, and is every claim cited?), and factuality (does the answer match the world?). Benchmarks and tools make these concrete: RAGTruth (the first large-scale span-level RAG-hallucination benchmark, ~18,000 annotated examples across QA, summarization, and data-to-text) and its 2025 re-annotations/leaderboards; FActScore (EMNLP 2023), which decomposes long-form output into atomic facts and scores the fraction supported by a knowledge source, with an automated pipeline within ~2% of human judgments; and LLM-as-judge groundedness scorers.

The move that makes it operational is running that judge online. Score groundedness on production traffic (L41), track hallucination rate as a first-class monitored metric with alerting, and gate a regression in CI the same way you’d gate a latency spike (Phase 2). For detection without labels, the frontier is uncertainty estimation — most notably semantic entropy (Farquhar, Kossen, Kuhn & Gal, Nature, Jun 2024): sample several generations, cluster them by meaning rather than wording, and compute entropy over the meaning-clusters; high semantic entropy flags likely confabulations, at a cost of roughly 5–10× a single generation (which spawned cheaper “probe” variants). And whatever the judge says, the last line of defense for high-stakes answers is human review (L47).

Governance in one breath

Measurement plugs into the compliance layer this phase closes with. The NIST AI RMF (Govern / Map / Measure / Manage) and its GenAI Profile name confabulation explicitly; ISO/IEC 42001 (Dec 2023) is the certifiable AI-management-system standard; the EU AI Act phases obligations in (GPAI from Aug 2 2025; high-risk around Aug 2 2026 — treat the exact dates as fluid, a late-2025 “Digital Omnibus” floated delays; verify at publish). Model and system cards document known limitations, hallucination behavior included. A monitored hallucination rate is what turns those documents from promises into evidence.

Reason about ita legal-document assistant · a clause that never existed
The confident citation to a clause that isn’t there

A contract-review assistant is asked, “What’s the termination-for-convenience notice period?” It answers crisply: “Section 12.4 requires 30 days’ written notice.” Confident, well-formatted, cited — and Section 12.4 does not exist in the uploaded contract. How did a cited answer end up fabricated, and how do you fix it?

Restate → diagnose → the fix → the rule

Restate the problem

The output is ungrounded and unfaithful: it names a clause with no supporting span in the actual document. Worse, it’s high-stakes — a human may rely on “12.4” in a negotiation.

Diagnose the causes

Three failures stacked. Ungrounded generation — it answered from a memory of what such contracts usually say. No abstention — unsure, it guessed rather than saying “not found.” No verification — nothing checked the citation against the text. All three rewarded by an “always answer” habit that scores a guess above an honest “I don’t know.”

The fix (the five verbs)

Ground with RAG over the document’s own spans (Phase 3). Cite mandatorily — every claim as [doc:span], no span → don’t state it. Abstain — instruct and reward “Not found in this contract” when the clause isn’t retrieved. Verify — a groundedness check confirms each cited span entails the claim before it ships. Measure — score faithfulness in CI and on production traffic (Phase 2).

The rule / trade-off

For high-stakes outputs, add a human in the loop (L47): the assistant drafts with citations or abstains, a reviewer signs off. You accept more abstentions and a little latency in exchange for eliminating confident fabrications — the right trade in law, medicine, or finance.

grounded + abstaining generation, then measured (illustrative)# system contract for the clause-lookup assistant
rules = [
  "Answer ONLY from the retrieved contract spans below.",
  "Cite every claim as [doc:span]. No span -> do not state it.",
  "If the clause is not in the context, reply exactly: 'Not found in this contract.'",
]

# retrieve first (Phase 3), then constrain, verify, and log
spans   = retrieve(question, contract, k=8)
draft   = model(question, context=spans, rules=rules)
answer  = verify_claims(draft, spans)              # drop any claim no span entails
if answer.unsupported:
    answer = "Not found in this contract."            # abstain, don't guess
log(groundedness=score(answer, spans))              # Phase 2 metric, on prod traffic
if answer.high_stakes: route_to_human(answer)      # L47 review before it acts
Say it back in one breath

“The assistant cited a clause that didn’t exist because it answered from memory, was never allowed to say ‘I don’t know,’ and nobody checked the citation. So I ground it in the document’s own spans, force a real citation on every claim, let it abstain when the clause isn’t found, verify each claim against the text, and measure faithfulness in CI and in production — with a human on the high-stakes ones.”

Ground it, cite it, let it say “I don’t know,” verify it, measure it. That’s the whole lesson in five verbs.

The phase ahead

You now hold the responsible-AI stance the rest of Phase 8 builds on: model output is untrusted, hallucination is managed and measured, and the same evals from Phase 2 become safety instruments. Next we treat the adversarial cases head-on — prompt injection and the lethal trifecta, guardrails and guard models, PII handling, and the governance frameworks (NIST, ISO 42001, EU AI Act) that ask you to prove all of it. Every one of them assumes the mindset you just built: never trust a fluent answer you can’t check.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: a model that fabricates confidently is a safety problem you cannot eliminate, only manage — ground it in sources, cite them, let it abstain, verify each claim, and measure the rate — and keep factuality separate from faithfulness the whole way.

01Manage, don’t eliminateA fluent next-token predictor with no innate “I don’t know” will always be able to guess. Promising “zero hallucination” is itself a safety failure; drive the rate down and monitor it.
02Factuality ≠ faithfulnessFactuality = matches the world. Faithfulness = supported by the provided context. Independent: grounded can be false; ungrounded can be luckily true. Grounding fixes faithfulness only.
03Why it happensAccuracy-only scoring rewards a confident guess over abstaining (OpenAI, 2025), so models learn to bluff; RLHF adds sycophancy. The fix is re-scoring, not a bigger brain.
04Abstention is a featureExplicitly permit and reward “I don’t know.” It beats a confident wrong answer — but only if the confidence signal is calibrated and honesty isn’t punished downstream.
05Layered mitigationGround, cite, abstain, constrain, lower temp, self-consistency, verify, use tools. Each has a hole; the holes don’t line up. Temp=0 reduces variance, not falsehood.
06Verify claim-by-claimChain-of-Verification decomposes and checks each claim against sources (~4× cost). Check citation faithfulness — does the span entail the claim — not just that a citation exists.
07Measure on productionFaithfulness / citation precision-recall / factuality; RAGTruth, FActScore, LLM-judge; semantic entropy for label-free detection. Track hallucination rate; escalate high-stakes to humans.
08It’s a security riskOWASP LLM09. A fabrication that feeds a tool call can act or exfiltrate — lethal trifecta, Rule of Two, guard models, MCP tool-poisoning. Treat model output as untrusted.
