Chunking Strategies

Before a single vector is indexed, you have already made the decision that caps your retrieval quality: where to cut the document. Chunk too coarse and one vector averages a dozen topics into mush; chunk too fine and you sever a fact from the heading that gives it meaning. This lesson climbs the ladder of strategies — fixed-size, structure-aware, semantic, small-to-big, contextual retrieval, late chunking — all of them attempts to satisfy one invariant: a chunk must be independently meaningful AND independently retrievable.

00 The two failure modes

Embed it all, or shred it small

You have a twenty-page API reference and a retriever that fetches the top-k passages for a query. The most naive move is to embed the whole document as one vector. It fails immediately, and the reason is arithmetic: an embedding is an average of the meaning in the text it covers. Average "authentication," "rate limits," and "webhooks" into one point and you land in a region that is near none of them. A query about rate limits gets back a vector that is equally lukewarm about everything — low precision, the right passage never surfaces.

So you swing the other way and shred the document into tiny pieces — one sentence each, maybe. Now a single fact, "The limit is 1,000 requests per minute," gets severed from the heading "Enterprise tier" three lines above it. The chunk holding the number no longer knows which tier it describes; its embedding reads like generic rate-limit prose, indistinguishable from the Free and Pro sentences. Low recall — the model answers with the wrong tier, or "not found." That is the bottleneck in one breath: too coarse blurs topics; too fine severs context.

Every technique in this lesson lives between those two cliffs. The trick is not to pick a magic number but to understand the principle that both failures violate — and then to choose the cheapest strategy that honours it for your documents and your queries. That principle is next.

01 The invariant

Chunk to match retrieval granularity

The organizing insight is small and it does all the work: chunk to match retrieval granularity. A chunk is the unit your retriever returns, so it has to be sized like an answer. Make that precise with a two-part invariant every good chunk must satisfy:

  • Independently meaningful — a human who reads only that chunk, with no surrounding context, understands what it says. The Enterprise number without the word "Enterprise" fails this half.
  • Independently retrievable — its embedding sits near the queries it should answer (and the chunk carries the keywords a lexical search like BM25 would match). A perfectly self-contained chunk whose vector lands in a generic region fails this half.

The two halves can fail separately, which is why naming both matters. A chunk can be meaningful but not retrievable: imagine a paragraph that resolves its own pronouns and reads cleanly, yet never uses the user's vocabulary — it makes sense to a human but its vector drifts away from the query. And a chunk can be retrievable but not meaningful: a fragment stuffed with the query's keywords that nonetheless omits the actual answer — it ranks first and tells you nothing. You need both at once.

Measure size in tokens, not characters

Chunk length is a budget against the embedding model's context window, so count it in tokens (what the model actually sees), not characters or words. And let the query type set the budget: factoid lookups ("what's the Enterprise rate limit?") want small, precise chunks (~256–512 tokens); analytical or narrative questions want larger ones (~1024+ tokens) so the context survives; code RAG wants ~1000–1500 tokens so a function (typically 300–800 tokens) is never cut mid-logic. There is no universal size — there is a size that matches your retrieval granularity.

Hold that invariant in mind as we climb. Each rung on the ladder is just a cheaper or more reliable way to satisfy "meaningful AND retrievable" — starting with the dumbest possible cut.

02 The universal baseline

Fixed-size & the sliding window ▸

The baseline ignores the document's meaning entirely: pick a window of N tokens, slide it across the text, and emit a chunk each time. To stop a fact from being guillotined at a seam, you let consecutive windows overlap — repeat a slice so a boundary sentence survives into both neighbours. This is cheap, deterministic, and dumb — the universal first pass. The practitioner default in 2025–26 is roughly 512 tokens with 10–20% overlap (common range 256–1024), then tune against an eval set. And do not underrate it: a 2026 seven-strategy benchmark on academic papers placed recursive-512 first (~69% accuracy), beating semantic chunking (~54%). The simple thing is often the strong thing.

Overlap is a hedge, not a default

Overlap is insurance against a sentence getting fragmented at a boundary — but the premium is real (every repeated slice is indexed and embedded twice). A January-2026 systematic analysis (SPLADE retrieval on Natural Questions) found overlap delivered no measurable retrieval benefit and only raised indexing cost. Keep 10–20% as a reasonable hedge, but treat it as a knob to validate, not a number to assume. The cleaner fix for boundary loss is to stop cutting blindly — which is the next rung.

03 Cut where it hurts

A fact severed at the boundary ▸

The fixed-size window's flaw is that it cuts at token offsets, which have nothing to do with where meaning ends. Structure-aware (recursive) splitting fixes this by cutting where the author already cut — on the document's own seams. Step through a fact getting severed, missed, and then repaired two different ways:

Recursive splitting works down a separator hierarchy — try to split on paragraphs (\n\n), then lines (\n), then words (' '), then characters — taking the largest natural unit that fits the size budget. LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter is the workhorse default. Structure-specific variants go further: MarkdownHeaderTextSplitter splits on #/##/### and carries each heading as metadata, so "Enterprise" travels with the number even after the cut; RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.from_language() respects function and code-block boundaries for code and markdown. You cut where the author cut, so meaning stays intact — and for most documents in 2025–26 this is simply enough.

Why this beats overlap

Overlap and structure-awareness both repair the severed-fact problem, but they are not equals. Overlap pays for the heading in every chunk, blindly, whether or not a fact needed it. Structure-aware splitting pays nothing extra and only keeps together what the author meant to keep together. The diagram's two green hits look identical from the query's side — but one cost you duplicated tokens across the whole corpus and the other cost you a smarter splitter you wrote once.

04 The ladder & trade-offs

Climbing the ladder of strategies

Above structure-aware splitting the rungs get cleverer — and each buys a specific thing the rung below could not. Climb in order, and stop as soon as the invariant is satisfied for your corpus.

Semantic chunking places boundaries where the embedding similarity between consecutive sentences dips — the intuition being that a topic shift is a natural seam. It is appealing, but recent benchmarks rarely justify its cost: you must embed every sentence to find the dips, and the payoff is small. Chroma-style evaluations show only a ~2–3 point recall edge over recursive splitting (e.g. ~0.919 vs ~0.88–0.90 at 400 tokens), and Renyi Qu et al. (2025) found the gains inconsistent. Reach for it only on dense, boundary-ambiguous prose where recursive splitting visibly struggles.

Parent-document / small-to-big retrieval attacks the precision-vs-context tension head-on by decoupling what's findable from what's enough. Index small, precise child chunks (~100–500 tokens) so matching is sharp; but when a child matches, return its larger parent section (~500–2000 tokens, or the whole section) to the LLM so the model gets enough context to actually answer. It is built into LangChain's ParentDocumentRetriever and LlamaIndex, reports broad-context accuracy gains often cited around 15–30%, and is now described as the most widely adopted production pattern for the trade-off. Retrieve small to find; feed big to understand.

At the top sits agentic / LLM-based chunking: an LLM reads the document and decides the split points from its semantics and structure, sometimes mixing strategies within one document. Highest coherence, highest cost — one LLM call per document — so it is reserved for high-stakes, high-value corpora. The pendulum is swinging back

If the ladder looks like "always climb higher," read the 2026 benchmarks again. Multiple of them show recursive-512 beating semantic chunking on accuracy and cost, and at least one finds overlap adds little. The prevailing attitude is "measure, don't assume": start at the bottom of the ladder, and only climb when your eval set (Lesson 21) tells you the simpler rung leaves recall on the table. Cleverness you cannot measure is just cost.

05 Context at the boundary

Contextual retrieval & late chunking ▸

The recurring enemy is the same: context lost at the cut. Two 2024–25 techniques attack it directly, from opposite ends of the cost curve. Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval (Sept 2024) spends an LLM call per chunk to write the missing context back in; Jina's Late Chunking (EMNLP 2024) spends one forward pass per document and no LLM at all. Step through the contextual version — the same fact climbing from rank 27 to rank 1:

Contextual Retrieval, mechanically. Before embedding (and before BM25 indexing), an LLM — Claude 3 Haiku in Anthropic's setup, with prompt caching — writes a 50–100 token blurb situating each chunk in its source document, roughly "give a short, succinct context to situate this chunk within the overall document for the purposes of improving search retrieval." That blurb is prepended to the chunk, then the combined text is embedded and lexically indexed. Anthropic's measured top-20 numbers: contextual embeddings alone cut retrieval failure ~35% (5.7% → 3.7%); adding contextual BM25 cuts it ~49% (→ 2.9%); adding a reranker (Lesson 18) cuts it ~67% (→ 1.9%). Prompt caching makes the one-time context generation cost about $1.02 per million document tokens — cheap enough to be a production default, not just a paper. Their setup assumed ~800-token chunks in ~8,000-token documents.

Late Chunking, mechanically. Reverse the order of operations. Run the whole document (up to ~8,192 tokens) through a long-context embedding transformer to get token-level embeddings — every token now carries document-wide context — then mean-pool the tokens of each chunk into that chunk's vector. The pronoun-laden chunk that began "It supports up to 1,000…" inherits the document's subject without any LLM call. It is training-free and adds zero LLM calls, but it requires a long-context embedding model (e.g. jina-embeddings-v2/v3). Its BEIR gains grow with document length and are near-zero on short text (NFCorpus nDCG@10 ~23.5 → ~30.0; SciFact ~64.2 → ~66.1).

Worked exampleone fact · three chunkers
Walking the Enterprise rate limit from "missed" to "rank 1"

A 20-page API reference. Buried in the Enterprise tier section is the sentence "The limit is 1,000 requests per minute." The user asks: "What's the Enterprise rate limit?" The underlying text never changes. We only change how it is packaged — and watch the same fact's retrieval rank move.

Think it through — same fact, three packagings

1 · Fixed 512, no structure

The cut falls between the "Enterprise tier" heading and the number. The chunk holding "1,000 requests per minute" contains no word like Enterprise or even tier, so its embedding reads as generic rate-limit text — nearly identical to the Free and Pro limit sentences. The query lands near all three; the right chunk comes back around rank 20, or not in top-k. The invariant is violated: the chunk is not independently meaningful.

2 · Recursive by heading

Split on the ## Enterprise tier boundary and carry the heading as a prefix/metadata. Now the chunk literally contains "Enterprise"; its embedding separates cleanly from Free/Pro, and the query lands near it — rank jumps into the top 3. Cheap, deterministic, no model calls. For most docs, you stop here.

3 · Contextual (Anthropic-style)

An LLM prepends a ~60-token blurb before embedding. The chunk is now unambiguous even out of context, and contextual BM25 also catches the exact token "Enterprise" — rank 1, robustly, even against near-duplicate tier sentences.

  "Give a short, succinct context to situate this"
  "chunk within the overall document for retrieval.",

record = context + "\n\n" + chunk      # prepend, THEN index
embed(record);  bm25_index(record)     # ~$1 / 1M doc tok, cached
Say it back in one breath

“The text never changed — only the packaging. Fixed-512 severed the number from its 'Enterprise' heading, so it embedded like Free and Pro and came back around rank 20. Recursive-by-heading kept the heading with the number and jumped it into the top 3 for free. Contextual prepended an LLM blurb before embedding and BM25, pinning it to rank 1. Same chunker inputs, very different retrieval — because a chunk must be independently meaningful AND independently retrievable.”

Cost ladder: recursive is free; contextual adds an LLM call per chunk (~$1/M tokens with caching); late chunking would recover much of the same boundary-context by embedding the whole page first — no per-chunk LLM call at all.

06 Tune it, don't guess it

Chunking is the hyperparameter you tune

The professional move is to stop treating chunk size, overlap, and strategy as things you choose and start treating them as hyperparameters you tune against retrieval metrics — recall@k, nDCG@10, retrieval-failure rate — on a held-out eval set. This is the direct hand-off to Lesson 21: chunking is precisely what evaluation measures, and the only honest way to pick between recursive-512, semantic, and contextual is to score all three on your corpus and read the numbers.

Two cautions keep you from over-fitting to folklore. First, bigger is not automatically better: a July-2025 study reported a "context cliff" near ~2,500 tokens where answer quality can start to degrade as chunks grow, and found sentence-level chunking matching semantic chunking up to ~5,000 tokens at a fraction of the cost. Second, no single strategy is optimal across a heterogeneous corpus — which is exactly what is driving 2025–26 "adaptive chunking" and agentic approaches that pick (or mix) a strategy per document, and what is pushing structure-, layout-, and multimodal-aware chunking for PDFs, tables, and figures rather than treating everything as flat prose.

Where this feeds

Chunking sets the ceiling for everything downstream. These chunks flow into the retrieval pipeline of Lesson 15; the reranking of Lesson 18 can only reorder what your chunks made findable; and Lesson 21 evaluation is what tells you whether any of it worked. The punchline of the whole lesson: you cannot rerank or eval your way out of chunks that violate the invariant. Get the cut right first — meaningful AND retrievable — and every later stage has something good to work with.
