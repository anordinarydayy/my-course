Model Routing & Cost Engineering

This opens Phase 6 · Inference Optimization — Latency & Cost, the phase about making a working system cheap and fast enough to actually ship. It is a ★Core lesson because it is the single largest cost lever there is — and it is not a faster model. Query difficulty is a distribution: the overwhelming majority of requests are easy, and a thin tail is genuinely hard. Send the easy ~95% to a small, cheap model and reserve the flagship for the hard ~5%, and your blended cost falls sharply at roughly equal quality. The whole lesson is how to do that reliably — the routers, the cascade, the gateway that fronts them, and the FinOps loop that keeps the invoice honest.

00 The brute-force bill

The all-flagship bill

The first version of every AI product wires one model into everything. You pick the strongest model you can afford — because it makes the demo look good and it "just works" — and every request, from "what are your hours?" to "reconcile these three ledgers," goes to the same expensive endpoint. It ships. Then the bill arrives, and it scales linearly with traffic, and finance starts asking questions. The instinct is to wait for a cheaper, faster flagship. That is the wrong lever.

Look at what you are actually paying for. Pull a week of production logs and cluster them by what the request really demanded. You will find a long, fat head of trivial work — FAQ lookups, format conversions, short rewrites, yes/no classifications, boilerplate extraction — and a short, thin tail of genuinely hard work: multi-step reasoning, cross-document synthesis, subtle code debugging. A frontier model answers the trivial head perfectly, and so does a model that costs ten to twenty times less per token. On that head, you are buying a sledgehammer to crack peanuts, once per peanut, all day long.

So the cost problem is not "the model is too expensive." It is "we call the expensive model on requests that never needed it." Once you frame it that way, the fix is obvious in outline: match the model to the request. The rest of this lesson is the machinery that makes that matching cheap, safe, and measurable — because the naive ways to do it (route by hope, or by a hard-coded rule someone wrote once) break the instant real traffic gets weird.

The one-line version

The biggest cost lever is not a faster model — it is not calling the expensive model when you don't have to. Difficulty is a distribution; most requests are easy. Route the easy majority to a cheap model and the hard tail to a flagship, and the blended cost per request collapses while measured quality barely moves. Everything else in this lesson — routers, cascades, gateways, caching — is a way to exploit that one fact without shipping worse answers.

01 The insight

Difficulty is a distribution ▸

Here is the whole idea as one moving picture. Requests arrive with a wide spread of difficulty; a router reads each one, scores how hard it is, and sends it to the tier that can handle it for the least money. Step through one easy request and one hard request and watch the blended cost — the number on the invoice — settle far below the all-flagship baseline, even though the flagship still does all the hard work:

The math is worth doing on a napkin. Suppose the cheap tier is ~$0.30/Mtok and the flagship is ~$5/Mtok — a ~16× gap that is typical of a small tier versus a frontier model in mid-2026. If 95% of traffic clears on the cheap tier, the blended input price is roughly 0.95 × $0.30 + 0.05 × $5 ≈ $0.54/Mtok — nearly a ten-fold cut versus sending everything to the flagship, and the flagship still handled every hard query. This is not a hypothetical: RouteLLM (UC Berkeley / Anyscale / Canva, ICLR 2025) trained routers that hit ~95% of GPT-4-level quality while sending only 14–26% of queries to the strong model, and vendor write-ups through 2026 report blended savings in the 45–85% range depending on the task. Exact percentages are model-pair-specific and drift, but the shape — a fat easy head, a thin hard tail, a big blended saving — is robust and reproduced everywhere.

Why this is durable, not a hack

Cheap-tier prices are drifting up (Gemini 3.5 Flash launched at roughly 3× its predecessor; "the era of cheap AI is ending"). That makes routing more valuable over time, not less: as the absolute cost of every tier rises, the leverage of not touching the expensive one on 95% of traffic grows. Routing is a structural property of your workload, not a temporary arbitrage on a briefly-cheap model.

02 The router zoo

Four ways to route

"Route the easy ones to the cheap model" is a slogan until you have to decide, per request and in milliseconds, which bucket it falls in. There are four router archetypes, and they sit on a gradient from near-zero cost and near-zero smarts to full-model flexibility at full-model price. Pick the cheapest one that is smart enough for your traffic.

Router archetypeHow it decidesDecision costBrittle / fails when
HeuristicLength / keyword / regex rules you write by hand~0Intent is subtle; easy to game; rules rot as traffic shifts
Classifier / learnedA small model or embedding scores difficulty (RouteLLM's default: matrix-factorization / BERT-style)~100 ms, cheapTraining data drifts from live traffic; needs retraining
CascadeTry cheap first; escalate on low confidence or a failed verifierCheap, but sequentialEvery rejected stage adds a full round-trip of latency
LLM-as-routerA model reads the query and names the target tierA whole extra inferenceOverkill for easy routing; adds a round-trip and its own cost

The heuristic is where everyone starts and where everyone gets burned: "route anything under 200 characters to the cheap model" works until a user pastes a 40-character question that hides a research project, or a 2,000-character prompt that is really just a long greeting. It is free, and it is only as good as your ability to predict phrasing — which is exactly the thing language models exist because we can't do. The learned classifier is the workhorse: a small model or an embedding model predicts a difficulty score, you route on a threshold, and you retrain as traffic drifts. It is what RouteLLM ships by default, and it is cheap enough to sit in front of every request. The cascade inverts the question — instead of predicting difficulty up front, it lets the cheap model try and only escalates on doubt (Section 03). And the LLM-as-router is the most flexible and the most expensive: a full model call whose only job is to pick where the real call goes. Reserve it for genuinely hard routing decisions where a cheap classifier can't tell the tiers apart.

Pure router (predict, then forward)

  • Pays the routing decision once, then forwards a single model call.
  • Latency is one decision + one inference — flat and predictable.
  • Risk: a wrong "easy" verdict ships a bad answer with no second chance.
  • Best when a classifier can score difficulty accurately up front.

Cascade (try, then escalate)

  • Pays flagship price only on the fraction that escalates.
  • Every rejected stage adds a full round-trip on the critical path.
  • Self-correcting: a shaky cheap answer gets a second, stronger attempt.
  • Best when confidence is measurable and latency budget is generous.
The latency objection is usually a misframing

People reject routers with "but the router adds latency." Do the arithmetic: a ~100 ms ML classifier is about 12.5% of a typical ~800 ms p50 model call — noise next to the tier it saves you from calling. The router is almost never the bottleneck; the model choice is the cost that matters. The one real exception is the LLM-as-router, which adds a full extra inference round-trip — that one you spend deliberately, not by default.

03 Try cheap first

The cascade: escalate on doubt ▸

A cascade refuses to guess difficulty up front. Instead the cheap model answers, and a gate — a confidence score or a verifier — decides whether that answer is good enough to ship or needs a stronger model. You pay flagship price only on the fraction that escalates. Step through one request that passes the gate and one that fails it, and watch the per-query cost split:

The gate is the whole design. Cheap gates read the model's own token-level logprobs — low average confidence is a decent proxy for "I'm guessing." Sturdier gates use self-consistency (sample a few times; disagreement means escalate), a small learned confidence estimator, or a separate verifier / LLM-judge that grades the draft against the question. Whatever you pick, the escalation threshold is a dial you calibrate against measured quality, and 2025–2026 work formalizes exactly this as a decision-theoretic problem (papers like "Is Escalation Worth It?" and "Cost-Saving LLM Cascades with Early Abstention"). The saving is real: your flagship spend tracks the escalation rate, not your traffic. The cost you trade for it is latency — every rejected stage adds a full round-trip on the critical path, and inside a multi-step agent loop that calls the model many times per task, a 2–3 stage cascade can turn a few-second response into tens of seconds.

Two ways to get the threshold wrong

Set the escalation threshold too high and you over-route to the flagship — the savings evaporate. Set it too low and you ship the cheap model's weak answers. It is not a set-and-forget constant; it needs ongoing experimentation against measured quality. And default ambiguous queries toward escalation: an unnecessary flagship call is far cheaper than a confidently-wrong cheap answer that triggers a re-run, a support ticket, or a lost user. Cheap on the mean, conservative on the tail.

04 One place to change it

The gateway: one control plane ▸

Routing, caching, retries, rate limits, and budgets are cross-cutting concerns. Scatter them across every call site and you get a thousand slightly-different copies of the same logic, none observable, none enforceable. An LLM gateway (LiteLLM self-hosted, OpenRouter-style managed) collapses all of it into one OpenAI-compatible endpoint that every request passes through. Step through three requests — one served from cache, one routed to the cheap tier, one that fails over to a backup provider — and watch the single control plane absorb each case:

The gateway is where the abstract levers become one config file. Caching serves near-duplicate requests for free. Failover retries a 429 or 5xx on a backup provider so the caller never sees the error. Spend caps — hard per-user / per-team / per-org budgets, enforced on virtual keys — stop a runaway agent loop from spending your month in an afternoon. And every call emits latency, cost, and error telemetry, so you can actually see which model served what. The main 2026 decision is self-hosted versus managed, and it is a real trade-off:

LiteLLM (self-hosted)

  • Zero token markup — you pay providers directly.
  • Hard per-user / per-team / per-org budget caps and virtual keys.
  • Six routing modes: weighted, latency-based, rate-limit-aware, least-busy, lowest-cost, custom Python — plus fallback lists.
  • Cost: you run and operate the infrastructure.
  • At ~$50k/mo spend, the 0% markup saves ~$2,750/mo versus a 5.5% fee.

OpenRouter (managed)

  • No infra to run; one endpoint, many providers.
  • Charges a ~5.5% credit fee per token.
  • As of these sources: no virtual keys or hard budget caps.
  • Auto Router (powered by NotDiamond) picks a model per prompt and deprioritizes providers that errored in the last ~30s.
  • Best when ops time is scarcer than the markup.

Either way, the win is the same: one place to change routing, not a thousand. Portkey, Helicone, Bifrost, and Braintrust round out a market that matured through 2026, and providers now ship routing as a built-in product too — GPT-5's real-time router (launched Aug 2025, refined in GPT-5.1) auto-selects between a fast model and a "thinking" model per request. Which is a warning as much as a feature: GPT-5's launch router drew a user backlash over opaque downgrades. Whatever routes your traffic, log which model actually served each request — hidden routing you can't inspect is a debugging and trust problem waiting to happen.

05 Optimize the invoice

Cost per successful task

Routing gets you most of the way; the rest is cost engineering — a FinOps loop, not a one-time cut. And its first move is to change the unit you optimize. The number that matters is not cost per token. It is cost per successful task: total spend ÷ delivered answers, after retries, tool calls, context bloat, and reasoning overhead. A model that is 30% cheaper per token but triggers 20% more follow-up requests, human reviews, or re-runs can be a net loss on the only line item that ships.

The trap is concrete. Take a benchmark from the sources: one model at 100% success cost $0.000105 per success, while a much cheaper-per-token model at 83% success cost $0.000005 per success. Which is "cheaper"? It depends entirely on the cost of a failure — a wrong answer in a code-review bot is a shrug; a wrong answer in a billing agent is a chargeback. The State of FinOps 2026 reframes the whole practice around "cost per unit of value" and treats LLM cost as a design constraint that engineers own and check pre-release, like latency and reliability — not a bill that finance discovers later.

With the right metric in hand, the levers stack. None is magic alone; you measure blended cost against quality and turn each one up until quality starts to move:

LeverWhat it doesIllustrative effect (mid-2026)

Discounts compound. Sonnet-4.6 batch + cache-read ≈ $3 × 0.5 × 0.1 ≈ $0.15/Mtok effective input — roughly 95% off list when the work is offline and the prefix is reused.

Two levers carry sharp edges. Prompt caching only pays off with genuine prefix reuse — Anthropic's write premium means a frequently-changing prompt can cost more cached than not, so watch your cache-miss rate and structure prompts so the stable prefix is cacheable. And a semantic cache can return a stored answer for a subtly different query — wrong entity, a negation, a changed constraint — so tune the similarity threshold conservatively and exclude high-stakes or personalized queries entirely. Underneath all of this, the reason a "cheap tier" exists at all is a serving stack that makes small models fast: vLLM-style paged attention plus continuous batching (~2–4× throughput at equal latency), speculative decoding (a small draft model proposes, the target verifies), and FP8 / FP4 quantization — which cuts memory and boosts throughput but can quietly degrade domain-specific accuracy, so treat a precision change as a change-managed event with hold-out evals in CI, never a free win.

The FinOps loop, in one sentence

Measure cost per successful task and quality together, continuously; pull the cheapest lever that doesn't move quality; re-measure; repeat. Optimizing any single lever in isolation — or the per-token sticker price — is how a "cheaper" model quietly becomes more expensive on the number that ships.

06 Put it together

Build it, end to end

Time to make it real. You have a bill dominated by flagship calls, and a week of logs showing that most of your traffic is trivial FAQs. Below is the build: a cascade (cheap model plus a confidence gate that escalates only the ~5% it can't handle), fronted by a gateway that gives you caching, failover, and a hard spend cap for free, and instrumented on $ per successful task. It is the honest version of a "cost optimization" — a small amount of glue that changes the shape of the invoice.

Build walkthroughcascade + gateway · billed per success
A flagship bill that shouldn't be flagship-shaped

Your support assistant sends every message to the flagship. The logs say ~95% are FAQs — "how do I reset my password," "what are your hours" — that a cheap model nails, and ~5% are genuine escalations that need the strong model. You are paying ~$5/Mtok for peanut-cracking. The fix is not a faster flagship; it is a cascade behind a gateway, measured on the only number that ships.

The whole pattern, in one small file

route.py — a cascade behind a gateway, billed per successful task# one control plane: routing + cache + failover + a HARD spend cap
gw = Gateway(
    models=["cheap-tier", "flagship"],       # ~$0.30 vs ~$5 /Mtok (illustrative)
    fallbacks={"cheap-tier": ["cheap-backup"]},  # auto-failover on 429 / 5xx
    cache=True,                            # exact + semantic; a hit costs $0
    max_budget=500)                        # hard monthly cap, per virtual key

def answer(q):                              # THE CASCADE
    draft = gw.completion("cheap-tier", q,
                          logprobs=True,
                          max_tokens=512)          # cap the pricier output side
    if confidence(draft) >= 0.85:            # the tunable escalation threshold
        return draft                           # ~95% stop here — cheap
    return gw.completion("flagship", q)       # escalate the hard ~5%

# the metric that matters: total_spend / successful_tasks  (NOT per token)
Restate & the naive miss

The naive system routes everything to the flagship because it "just works." But most of the traffic is trivial — the flagship is being paid frontier prices to answer FAQs. A hand-written length rule would misroute the moment a short question hides a hard one, so instead we let the cheap model try and gate on its own confidence.

The move — cascade behind a gateway

Every query goes to cheap-tier first; if the logprob-based confidence clears 0.85 we ship it, else we escalate to flagship. The Gateway wrapper adds caching (near-duplicate FAQs return for $0), failover (a 429 retries on a backup, invisibly), and a hard max_budget so a runaway loop can't blow the month. One config, not a thousand call sites.

The math — a ~10× blended cut

Blend the tiers: 0.95 × $0.30 + 0.05 × $5 ≈ $0.54/Mtok versus $5 all-flagship — a ~9–10× cut, and the flagship still handled every hard query (the escalated 5% pay both stages, which nudges it up slightly, not off the shape). Because the gate escalates on doubt, measured quality barely moves — and you watch that, not assume it.

Say it back in one breath

“Difficulty is a distribution, so I don't send everything to the flagship. The cheap model answers first; a confidence gate escalates only the ~5% it isn't sure about. A gateway in front adds caching, failover, and a hard spend cap in one config. Blend 95% cheap and 5% flagship and the bill drops ~10× at roughly equal quality — and I measure total_spend / successful_tasks, not per token, so a ‘cheaper’ model that fails more can't fool me.”

The single metric to watch: cost per successful task, plotted against measured quality, over time. If quality holds and that number falls, the routing is working; if quality dips, loosen the threshold before you chase the next lever.

That is the entire pattern: match the model to the request, let a gate catch the cases you got wrong, and centralize the plumbing so the levers are one config away. Everything else in Phase 6 — caching mechanics, the Batch API, the serving stack under the cheap tier — is a way to push the same two numbers, cost per successful task and latency, further down without giving up quality. The learned routers are only getting better (RouteLLM opened the door; RL-trained, cost-aware orchestrators like xRouter and the cascade decision-theory papers walked through it), which means the lever you build today gets sharper on its own.
