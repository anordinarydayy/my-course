Embeddings & the Vector Space

An embedding turns a piece of text into a list of numbers — a point in space — positioned so that things which mean the same land near each other. That one trick, "meaning becomes geometry," is the engine under semantic search, RAG, recommendations, and an agent's memory. Get it and half of AI engineering opens up.

Original study notes. An intuition-first guide to embeddings for engineers. Model names, dimensions, and prices are current as of mid-2026 and move fast — treat them as illustrative and check the live docs / the MTEB leaderboard before committing. 00 The problem

Matching meaning, not words

A user searches your help center for "I can't get into my account." The perfect article is titled "Recover a forgotten login and reset your password." Classic keyword search finds nothing — the two strings share almost no words. Yet to a human they obviously mean the same thing. Closing that gap, between matching characters and matching meaning, is what embeddings are for.

The idea is to stop comparing text as strings and start comparing it as positions in space. If we can place every piece of text at a point such that similar meanings sit close together, then "find related text" becomes "find nearby points" — a geometry problem a computer is very good at. The thing that assigns those points is an embedding model, and the point it assigns is an embedding (a vector).

The one-line idea

An embedding turns meaning into math you can compare. Text in, a fixed-length list of numbers out, arranged so that distance ≈ dissimilarity. Everything in this lesson — search, RAG, clustering, memory — is some variation of "embed it, then measure which points are close."

01 The transformation

Text becomes a vector ▸

Start with the mechanics. You hand a piece of text to an embedding model — and note, this is a different kind of model from the chat LLMs of the last lessons: an encoder that reads the whole input and emits a single fixed-length vector, rather than a decoder that generates tokens. Out comes a list of a few hundred to a few thousand numbers. Step through it:

Two things to lock in. First, the output is a fixed length regardless of input length — a tweet and a paragraph both become, say, a 1,536-number vector. The model has compressed the meaning into that fixed budget of numbers. Second, those numbers aren't human-readable — no single one means "is about passwords." Meaning is spread across all of them, and only emerges when you compare two vectors. A lone embedding is useless; embeddings only do work in relation to each other.

02 The intuition

Meaning as geometry

Why does "a list of numbers" capture meaning? Because the embedding model was trained so that position encodes meaning: texts about similar things are pushed close together, and unrelated texts are pushed apart. The result is a space where regions correspond to topics — a neighborhood of "billing" text over here, a neighborhood of "login problems" over there. Plot a few items (projected down to 2D for our eyes) and the clustering is the whole point:

There's a famous party trick from the early word-embedding era (word2vec) that makes the geometry vivid: king − man + woman ≈ queen. The direction from "man" to "woman" is roughly the same as the direction from "king" to "queen" — so relationships become vector arithmetic. It's a wonderful intuition pump, with one honest caveat: it only lands cleanly because the search excludes the input words, and it's a property of old per-word embeddings.

Word vectors vs. modern embeddings

Today's embedding models are contextual and work on whole sentences or documents, not isolated words. The same word gets a different vector depending on its sentence ("river bank" vs "savings bank"), and an entire paragraph collapses to one vector that captures its overall meaning. So keep the king/queen picture for intuition, but know that in practice you embed chunks of real text and compare those — which is exactly what powers retrieval.

03 The measurement

Measuring closeness: cosine similarity ▸

If meaning is position, similarity is distance — but which distance? The overwhelming default for text is cosine similarity, which measures the angle between two vectors and ignores their length. A small angle means the vectors point the same way — similar meaning. It ranges from +1 (same direction, highly similar) through 0 (perpendicular, unrelated) to −1 (opposite). Watch it judge a query against two documents:

You'll also meet two cousins. Dot product is cosine's faster relative — it factors in length as well as angle, useful when magnitude carries signal (like popularity). Euclidean (straight-line) distance measures actual gap. The practical fact that ties them together: if your vectors are normalized to unit length — many models output them that way — then cosine and dot product give the identical ranking, and dot product is cheaper because it skips the division. So in practice you'll often see "cosine" and "dot product" used interchangeably for normalized embeddings.

Why angle beats raw distance for text

Cosine ignores vector length and looks only at direction. That's the right call for text because length often tracks incidental things (a longer document, a more emphatic phrasing) rather than topic. Two passages about password resets should count as similar whether one is a sentence and the other a page — and cosine says they are, as long as they point the same way.

04 Putting it together

Semantic search, end to end ▸

Now the payoff — the pattern you'll build over and over, and the core of RAG. Semantic search: embed everything once, then at query time embed the question and return the nearest neighbors. Walk the whole thing on our help-center query:

Look at what happened: the winning document — "Steps to recover a forgotten login and change your password" — shares not one keyword with "reset my account password," yet it ranks first, because their meanings point the same way. The unrelated office-hours note scores near zero. That is the superpower keyword search never had. In a RAG system you take the top one or two of these and paste them into the model's prompt as context — which is the whole of Phase 3, built on exactly this step.

05 The practicalities

Choosing & using an embedding model

You don't train embedding models; you pick one and call it. A representative slice of the 2025–2026 field (prices per 1M tokens, illustrative — verify before use):

Four facts will save you real bugs:

  • More dimensions ≠ strictly better. Higher-dimensional vectors capture more nuance but cost more storage, RAM, and search time. Many modern models support Matryoshka embeddings — you can truncate to the first N dimensions and re-normalize, trading a little accuracy for big savings. (OpenAI's -3-large cut to 256 dims still beats the old 1,536-dim ada-002.)
  • Use the same model to index and to query. Embeddings from two different models live in incompatible spaces; mixing them gives near-random results. Switching models means re-embedding your entire corpus.
  • Mind query-vs-document asymmetry. Many models want a prefix or instruction on queries (search_query:) different from documents (search_document:). Getting this wrong silently degrades retrieval — follow the model's docs exactly.
  • Compare on your data, not the leaderboard. The MTEB leaderboard is a great shortlist, but the top model overall may not be best for your corpus or language. Run a small retrieval eval on real queries — which is, again, the evaluation discipline from Phase 2.
06 The reach

What embeddings unlock

Semantic search is the headline, but the same "embed, then compare" move powers a surprising amount of the field. Once you can turn meaning into comparable vectors, you can:

Find & group

  • Semantic search & RAG — retrieve the most relevant context for a prompt.
  • Clustering — group similar tickets, reviews, or documents with no labels.
  • Deduplication — spot near-duplicate content by tiny distance.
  • Recommendations — "items near what you liked."

Decide & remember

  • Classification — label by nearest known category.
  • Anomaly detection — flag points that sit far from every cluster.
  • Agent memory — store past interactions as vectors and recall the relevant ones.
  • Cross-modal — image↔text in one space (CLIP) for "search photos by description."

One scaling note that points ahead. Comparing a query against every stored vector is O(n) — fine for thousands, hopeless for hundreds of millions. Production systems use Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) indexes — names like HNSW and IVF — that find the near-neighbors without scanning everything, trading a sliver of recall for enormous speed. That machinery, and the vector databases built around it, is the subject of a later lesson; for now, just know that "find nearby vectors fast at scale" is a solved, well-understood problem you'll reach for, not build.

The mental hook to carry forward

Whenever a task is really "find the things most similar in meaning to this" — relevant docs, similar tickets, the right category, what to remember — your first thought should be embeddings + nearest-neighbor search. It's one of the highest-leverage patterns in the whole field, and it sits one rung above prompting on the adaptation ladder: you're giving the model your data without touching its weights.
