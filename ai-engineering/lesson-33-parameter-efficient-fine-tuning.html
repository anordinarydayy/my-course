Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning

This is where Phase 5 — Fine-tuning & Model Adaptation — stops being aspirational. Full fine-tuning works, but it asks you to update every weight, hold a cluster of GPUs just for optimizer state, and ship a fresh multi-gigabyte copy of the model for each task. That is unaffordable for one task and unmanageable for fifty. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) is the escape hatch: freeze the huge pretrained model, bolt on a tiny trainable piece — a few megabytes — and train less than one percent of the parameters. LoRA is the idea; QLoRA squeezes it onto a single consumer GPU; and one base plus a rack of adapters lets you serve hundreds of task-specific models at roughly the price of one. This lesson is that whole arc: the bottleneck → the low-rank trick → the serving economics → the rules that decide when it is enough.

00 The bottleneck

Full fine-tuning's true cost

You have a strong open-weights model and a few thousand examples of the behaviour you want. The obvious move is full fine-tuning: unfreeze all the weights and let gradient descent nudge every one of them toward your data. It works — and it is also the most expensive thing you can do, for a reason that surprises people the first time they hit an out-of-memory error on a model that "fits."

The trap is that the memory bill is not the size of the model. It is several times the size of the model. Train with Adam — the default — and every trainable parameter drags along two extra fp32 moment estimates (the running mean m and variance v), plus an fp32 gradient, plus the weight itself. Add it up and you are paying on the order of 12–16 bytes per parameter, not the 2 bytes a bf16 weight occupies. A 7B model whose weights are ~14 GB suddenly wants ~100 GB of live state to train — past any single accelerator, so you are renting a multi-GPU node and wiring up sharding before you have learned anything.

Then comes the second bill, the one that shows up in storage and ops rather than at train time. Every full fine-tune produces a brand-new, full-size checkpoint. Ten tasks means ten complete copies of a tens-of-gigabytes model to version, download, cold-load, and keep warm in memory. The GPU you saved for is now thrashing weights in and out just to answer requests for different tenants. Full fine-tuning does not scale in the one dimension that matters commercially: number of tasks.

The naive dodges are worse than they look. You can freeze most layers and fine-tune only the top few — but you are guessing which layers hold the behaviour, and you usually leave capacity on the table. You can distil into a smaller model — but that is a separate, expensive project. Both are trying to route around the same missing primitive: a way to specialize an enormous frozen model by training something tiny beside it.

The reframe that unlocks everything

Adapting a model does not require rewriting the model. The knowledge is already in the frozen weights; a task usually needs only a small, low-dimensional correction on top. So freeze W entirely — no gradients, no optimizer moments, no new checkpoint — and train a small add-on that carries the delta. Train <1% of the parameters, ship a few-MB adapter instead of a full model, and both bills collapse at once. That add-on is LoRA, and the rest of this lesson is what it looks like and how far it now reaches.

01 The low-rank trick

LoRA: freeze W, learn a detour ▸

LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation, from Hu et al. in 2021 and still the reference formulation — starts from one observation: the update a task needs, ΔW, tends to be low-rank even when W is enormous. So instead of learning the full d×d matrix of changes, LoRA factors it through a narrow bottleneck. Freeze the pretrained weight W. Beside it, add a detour that squeezes the input down to a tiny rank r and expands it back: ΔW = B·A, where A is r×d and B is d×r, with r ≪ d. Step through where the signal goes:

The arithmetic is the whole point. A full update to a d×d matrix is d² parameters; the low-rank detour is only r·(d+d). At d=4096 and r=16 that is a ~128× reduction on that one matrix — and across a whole model LoRA typically trains just 0.1–1% of the parameters. The original paper reported roughly 10,000× fewer trainable parameters and ~3× less GPU memory versus full fine-tuning on GPT-3. Two knobs shape the result: α is a scaling factor applied as α/r so you can raise rank without rescaling the whole update, and the initialization (A random, B zero) means the adapter starts as a no-op — at step zero the model is exactly the base.

And because the adapter is a separate factor, you get a choice at inference time that full fine-tuning never offered. Merge B·A back into W and you have a single fused weight with zero added latency — indistinguishable from a normally fine-tuned model. Or keep it separate, a few-megabyte file you hot-swap onto the frozen base per request. That second option is a superpower we cash in during Section 04.

02 The knobs that matter

Rank, alpha, and target modules

LoRA has few knobs, but they are not equally important, and getting the priority wrong is the most common reason a run underperforms. In rough order of leverage: which matrices you adapt (target modules), then the learning rate, then rank r, then α.

Target modules — adapt the MLP, not just attention

The single highest-leverage decision is where you attach adapters, and the field's default was wrong. Hugging Face PEFT historically defaulted to adapting only the attention query and value projections. But Thinking Machines' "LoRA Without Regret" (Sept 2025) showed that attention-only LoRA produces stunted learning curves and underperforms full fine-tuning — and, crucially, that raising the rank does not rescue it. LoRA only matches full FT when you adapt all the weight matrices, and especially the MLP / feed-forward projections (the up, gate, and down projections), which in a modern transformer hold most of the parameters. Standard practice today: adapt the attention q/k/v/o and the MLP up/gate/down. Skipping the MLP is the mistake to unlearn.

Learning rate, rank, and alpha

LoRA's optimal learning rate is roughly 10× higher than full fine-tuning's — a finding that held across supervised and RL post-training in the 2025 work. Reusing a full-FT learning rate silently under-trains the adapter, and it is a genuinely easy trap because everything else looks configured correctly. Rank is the capacity dial: common starting points are r = 8 / 16 / 32, and the honest answer is to start small and raise it only if evaluation demands more. Alpha is usually set to r or 2r and applied as α/r; at very high rank that classic scaling gets unstable, which is exactly what rsLoRA (Section 05) fixes by scaling with α/√r instead.

Attention-only LoRA (the old default)

  • Adapts q_proj, v_proj only.
  • Leaves the parameter-dense MLP untouched.
  • "Stunted" learning curves; a gap to full FT that more rank cannot close.
  • Looks fine on a tiny demo, disappoints on real tasks.

All-matrices LoRA (2025 practice)

  • Adapts attention q/k/v/o and MLP up/gate/down.
  • Tracks full fine-tuning across supervised + RL tasks.
  • Learning rate ~10× the full-FT value; modest ranks suffice.
  • The extra adapters are still tiny — you pay almost nothing for them.
The rank-sizing pitfall, both ways

Too low a rank underfits on genuinely new domains: the Databricks/MIT study "LoRA Learns Less and Forgets Less" (TMLR 2024) found full fine-tuning's effective update rank is 10–100× higher than a small LoRA, which is why low-rank adapters lag on hard continued-pretraining like code or math. Too high a rank creeps back toward full-FT cost and can overfit a small dataset. And do not assume LoRA prevents catastrophic forgetting — freezing the base reduces it relative to full FT (that same paper's "forgets less"), but does not eliminate it, and you cannot patch it away by early stopping or by tweaking the trainable-parameter count.

03 Shrink the frozen base

QLoRA: a 4-bit frozen base ▸

LoRA slashes the trainable state, but the frozen base still has to sit in memory for the forward pass — and a 13B base in bf16 already crowds a 24 GB card before you have loaded a single batch. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) removes that last wall with a simple bargain: since the base never trains, store it in 4-bit. Watch how the forward and backward passes treat the two halves differently:

Three innovations make 4-bit safe. NF4 (4-bit NormalFloat) is a datatype built for the fact that pretrained weights are roughly zero-centred and normally distributed — it puts its precious few quantization levels where the weights actually live, making it information-theoretically well-matched rather than a naive linear grid. Double Quantization then quantizes the quantization constants themselves, shaving about 0.37 bits per parameter of overhead. And Paged Optimizers use NVIDIA unified memory so the transient spikes from gradient checkpointing page out to CPU RAM instead of triggering an OOM at the worst moment. The 4-bit base is never trained — it is dequantized on the fly during each forward and backward pass and immediately discarded; gradients accumulate only in the bf16 adapter.

The payoff is the headline result of the field: QLoRA reproduces 16-bit LoRA quality at a tiny accuracy cost, while dropping the memory floor far enough to fine-tune a ~65B model on a single 48 GB GPU, or a 7B–13B on a 24 GB consumer card — exact fit depending on sequence length, batch size, and optimizer settings. Fine-tuning left the cluster and moved onto one machine.

The merge gotcha unique to QLoRA

Because the base is quantized, how you merge matters. Fusing a QLoRA adapter straight back into the 4-bit base re-quantizes the sum and injects quantization error into weights that were previously fine. Merge into a dequantized / fp16 copy of the base instead — or, better for multi-tenant serving, do not merge at all and keep the adapter separate. Which sets up exactly the next section.

04 The killer economics

One base, many adapters ▸

Here is where PEFT stops being a training convenience and becomes a business model. A LoRA adapter is a few megabytes and it snaps onto an unmodified base. So instead of loading N fine-tuned models into N GPUs' worth of memory, you load one shared base and keep a rack of tiny adapters beside it; a router picks the right adapter per request. The catch is throughput. If you naively swap the adapter weights in and out for every request, you serialize the batch and destroy the very efficiency you were after. The fix is specialized batched kernels that let a single GPU batch run different adapters for different rows at once: Punica's SGMV (Segmented Gather Matrix-Vector) and S-LoRA's unified paging with custom CUDA. With those, S-LoRA reported serving on the order of 2,000 adapters concurrently on one shared base at up to ~4× the throughput of naive baselines. This is now productized: vLLM ships native multi-LoRA, Predibase's LoRAX (LoRA eXchange) is built around it, and Fireworks lets you run up to ~100 adapters on one dedicated deployment at no extra cost. "A thousand fine-tuned models at the cost of one" is a literal description of the serving stack.

Merge or swap — you cannot have both

Section 01 said you can merge B·A into W for zero-latency inference. Multi-tenancy is the reason you often won't. A merged adapter is fused into the base and can no longer be hot-swapped; serving many tenants requires the unmerged adapter plus batched kernels. Merge when you ship one specialized model and want it fast; keep separate when the whole point is to serve many. Choosing "merge" here quietly forecloses the economics of this entire section.

05 The 2025 verdict

When LoRA matches full fine-tuning

For a few years the received wisdom was "LoRA is the cheap approximation; full fine-tuning is what you do when quality really matters." 2025 largely dismantled that framing — with an important caveat that tells you exactly when the old worry still applies.

Thinking Machines' "LoRA Without Regret" identified a low-regret regime: when the number of trainable parameters comfortably exceeds the amount of information the task actually has to add, correctly-configured LoRA tracks full fine-tuning — on both supervised and reinforcement-learning post-training. "Correctly configured" carries real weight: it must be on all layers (the MLP lesson from Section 02), it needs that ~10× learning rate, and it is less tolerant of very large batch sizes than full FT. It is also cheaper per step, not just per checkpoint — roughly two-thirds of the FLOPs of a full-FT pass. Hugging Face TRL shipped an official reproduction guide, so this is repeatable rather than a one-off claim.

That reconciles neatly with the earlier Databricks/MIT result. "LoRA Learns Less and Forgets Less" found that a low-rank LoRA underperforms full FT on hard new domains — continued pretraining on code or math, where the update's effective rank is genuinely large — while forgetting the base model's other skills less. Put the two together and you get a decision rule: for post-training a capability the base already latently has (instruction-following, style, tool-use, a domain it has seen), configured LoRA matches full FT; for injecting a large body of genuinely new knowledge, reach for higher rank or full FT. Same family, different regimes.

DoRA

  • Weight-Decomposed LoRA (NVIDIA): split each weight into magnitude + direction, adapt only the direction.
  • Better quality, no inference overhead; QDoRA is its 4-bit form.

rsLoRA

  • Rank-stabilized: scale the adapter by α/√r instead of α/r.
  • Fixes the instability that classic scaling hits at high rank — the recommended fix when you push rank up.

LoRA+

  • Give matrix B a higher learning rate than A.
  • Faster convergence for free. One flag, like the rest — alongside PiSSA, VeRA, OFT, (IA)³.

All of these are one-flag options in Hugging Face PEFT, so trying them costs almost nothing. And the ecosystem has voted: nearly every managed fine-tuning API — OpenAI, Together, Fireworks, Google Vertex, AWS Bedrock — defaults to LoRA/QLoRA under the hood rather than full FT. Together and Fireworks even hand back the actual adapter file, which you can load into vLLM or Ollama on your own cluster; OpenAI additionally exposes DPO and RL fine-tuning on top of supervised. Meanwhile 2025–2026 research keeps extending the family — OPLoRA and orthogonal-projection methods to curb forgetting, full-rank variants like RandLoRA, adapter-compression work to pack even more tenants per GPU. The default is LoRA; the frontier is choosing the right member of its family.

06 Put it together

Build: QLoRA on one GPU

Time to make it concrete. You have 5,000 domain examples and one 24 GB GPU. The temptation is to full-fine-tune a 13B and be done — but the optimizer-state arithmetic from Section 00 kills that before the first step. Here is the QLoRA configuration that actually fits, and the reasoning behind every knob.

Build walkthrough13B · 5k examples · one 24GB GPU
"Fine-tune a 13B on the card I actually have"

You want a 13B model to adopt a specific domain's format and vocabulary from 5k examples, with one 24 GB consumer GPU. Full fine-tuning needs ~12–16 bytes/param of live state — the optimizer moments alone blow the budget on this card. So you don't. You freeze a 4-bit base and train a <1% adapter on top.

The config that fits — QLoRA in Hugging Face PEFT

qlora_13b_config.py — 4-bit frozen base + a bf16 LoRA adapterfrom transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, BitsAndBytesConfig, TrainingArguments
from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model, prepare_model_for_kbit_training
import torch

# 1) load the 13B base in 4-bit NF4 — this is the FROZEN, quantized W
bnb = BitsAndBytesConfig(
    load_in_4bit=True,
    bnb_4bit_quant_type="nf4",               # NormalFloat, matched to weights
    bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True,          # quantize the quant constants too
    bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=torch.bfloat16,   # dequantize on the fly in bf16
)
base = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("org/model-13b", quantization_config=bnb)

# 2) the adapter — only ~0.2% of params actually train
lora = LoraConfig(
    r=16, lora_alpha=32, lora_dropout=0.05,   # rank 16, alpha = 2r
    target_modules=["q_proj","k_proj","v_proj","o_proj",   # attention
                    "gate_proj","up_proj","down_proj"],   # MLP — do NOT skip
    bias="none", task_type="CAUSAL_LM",
)
model = get_peft_model(prepare_model_for_kbit_training(base), lora)
model.print_trainable_parameters()             # trainable ≈ 0.2% of 13B

# 3) paged AdamW survives gradient-checkpointing memory spikes
args = TrainingArguments(optim="paged_adamw_8bit",
                         learning_rate=2e-4,        # ~10x a full-FT LR
                         gradient_checkpointing=True)
Restate & why full FT is out

The base is 13B. Full FT would need weights + fp32 grads + two Adam moments — ~12–16 bytes per parameter, hundreds of GB of live state, nowhere near a 24 GB card. QLoRA freezes the base in 4-bit and trains a tiny adapter, so the only optimizer state is for the ~0.2% that moves.

The moves — 4-bit base, adapt everything, tune the LR

load_in_4bit + nf4 + double-quant shrink the frozen base; the compute dtype dequantizes to bf16 only during the pass. r=16, alpha=32 gives modest capacity, and target_modules covers attention and the MLP gate/up/down — the Section 02 rule. The 2e-4 learning rate is the ~10× LoRA value; reusing a full-FT LR here would silently under-train.

The trade-off dial — rank, and don't merge yet

Rank is the one knob to sweep. Too low and the new format is underfit; too high and you drift back toward full-FT cost and memory for little gain — so start at 16 and raise only if eval warrants (bump to rsLoRA's α/√r if you go high). And if you'll serve several domain variants, don't merge: keep the few-MB adapter swappable so one base can back them all.

Say it back in one breath

“A 13B won't full-fine-tune on 24 GB because optimizer state is ~12–16 bytes per parameter, so I freeze the base in 4-bit NF4 with double quantization, add a rank-16 LoRA on the attention and MLP projections — about 0.2% of params — train it in bf16 with a paged AdamW at a ~10× learning rate, and keep the adapter unmerged so I can hot-swap variants on one shared base.”

Every knob traces to a bill: 4-bit shrinks the frozen base, the <1% adapter shrinks the optimizer state, the MLP targets buy the quality, and staying unmerged buys the serving economics.

That configuration is the whole lesson compressed into one file: freeze the giant, train something tiny beside it, quantize what you froze, and decide at the end whether to fuse or to swap. Swap the 13B for a 65B and a 48 GB GPU and the same recipe holds; swap the single adapter for a hundred and you have the multi-tenant stack of Section 04. The next lessons in Phase 5 build on this — what data to fine-tune on, how to evaluate the result, and when adaptation beats a better prompt in the first place — but the mechanism you'll be adapting is this one.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: you don't have to rewrite a giant model to specialize it — freeze W, train a tiny low-rank adapter beside it (y = Wx + (α/r)·BAx), quantize the frozen base to 4-bit so it fits one GPU, and share that base across a rack of swappable adapters so a hundred tenants cost about one.
