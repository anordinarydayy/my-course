Sampling & Decoding

The model hands you a probability distribution; decoding is how you turn it into an actual token. That step happens outside the model, it's entirely yours to control, and it decides whether your output is rock-solid and repeatable or wild and creative. Here are the dials, what each one does, and which to reach for.

00 Where decoding lives

The choice the model leaves to you

Recall the one-sentence model from Lesson 01: a foundation model maps a sequence of tokens to a probability distribution over the next token. That's where the model's job ends. It does not pick a word — it ranks the whole vocabulary. The step that turns that ranking into one concrete token is called decoding, and it runs in the serving layer, not the neural network. Because it's a separate step, it's yours: a handful of parameters on every API call quietly determine how the model behaves.

This is the most under-appreciated lever a new AI engineer has. People reach for a bigger model or a longer prompt when the real problem is a decoding setting. Output too random and inconsistent? That's a sampling setting. JSON that's occasionally malformed? A decoding setting. Bland, repetitive prose? Also decoding. None of those need a different model — they need a different dial.

The mechanism, in one line

Every decoding strategy is just a way of reshaping or trimming the distribution before you draw from it. Temperature reshapes it; top-k, top-p, and min-p trim its tail; constrained decoding masks out illegal tokens entirely. Then a single token is drawn. Understand "reshape, trim, draw" and every knob below is a variation on it.

01 The master dial

Temperature: the master dial ▸

The single most important knob is temperature. Mechanically, it divides the model's logits by a number T before the softmax turns them into probabilities — softmax(logits / T). You don't need the math; you need the effect. Temperature controls how peaked or flat the distribution is, without changing the ranking. Drag it through the values:

Two ranges to memorize. Toward zero, the distribution sharpens until only the top token has any real mass — focused, confident, repeatable, and at exactly T = 0 it becomes greedy decoding (always take the rank-1 token). Above one, the gaps between tokens compress, so unlikely words get a genuine shot — varied, surprising, and eventually incoherent as the long tail of nonsense gets in. The model's "native" distribution is T = 1.

The oven-dial analogy

Temperature is the dial on an oven. Low and the writing is rigid and predictable — the same safe word every time. High and it gets loose and inventive — until you crank it too far and the text "melts" into gibberish. Crucially, the dial never changes which word is most likely; it only changes how much the runners-up matter. For anything where there's a right answer — extraction, classification, code — you want this dial near zero.

02 Cutting the tail

Trimming the tail: top-k, top-p, min-p ▸

Temperature reshapes the whole distribution, but the real danger is the long tail — the thousands of low-probability tokens that are individually tiny but collectively can be sampled into nonsense, especially at higher temperature. Three truncation methods cut that tail off before you draw. They differ in how they decide where to cut:

  • Top-k keeps a fixed count — the k most likely tokens, nothing else. Simple, but rigid: k=5 is too many when the model is certain and too few when it's genuinely torn.
  • Top-p (nucleus sampling) keeps the smallest set whose probabilities add up to p (typically 0.9–0.95). It's adaptive: the set shrinks to one or two tokens when the model is confident and widens when it's unsure. This is the most common default.
  • Min-p (a 2024 method, ICLR 2025) keeps every token at least p× as likely as the top token (p around 0.05–0.1) — a relative bar. Its trick: the threshold rises automatically when the model is confident, so you can run a high temperature for creativity without the tail leaking nonsense in.
The reusable shape

Top-k is a fixed headcount; top-p is a budget; min-p is a quality bar relative to the best option. All three then renormalize what survives and let temperature draw from it. In practice you tune either temperature or top-p — not both at once (they interact confusingly), and reach for min-p specifically when you want high-temperature variety without losing the plot.

03 Anti-repetition

Repetition controls

Left alone, models — especially smaller or local ones — can fall into loops, repeating a word or phrase. Three penalties fight this by subtracting from the logit of tokens that have already appeared, before the softmax. They differ only in the bookkeeping:

  • Presence penalty — a one-time flat penalty the moment a token appears at all. Nudges the model toward new topics and vocabulary. (OpenAI range −2 to 2.)
  • Frequency penalty — a penalty that grows with how many times a token has appeared. Specifically suppresses high-frequency, verbatim repetition — the best tool against literal loops.
  • Repetition penalty — the open-source-stack variant (Hugging Face, llama.cpp): it divides the logit of seen tokens by a factor (~1.1–1.2; 1.0 = off).
Watch-out: don't penalize structure

These penalties are great for long prose, but turn them off for code and structured output. Source code and JSON must repeat tokens — braces, keywords, indentation, commas. A frequency penalty there will start avoiding the very characters the syntax requires, producing subtly broken output. Keep penalties small (≤ 0.5) for chat, and at zero whenever the format matters.

04 The other strategies

Greedy & beam search

Two non-sampling strategies round out the picture. Greedy decoding always takes the single highest-probability token — it's just temperature 0. It's deterministic up to the hardware (more on that in §6) and perfect when you want the model's single most-confident answer, but it's myopic: locally optimal choices can paint it into a corner, and for open-ended text it tends to repeat and go bland.

Beam search tries to fix greedy's myopia by keeping the top B partial sequences ("beams") alive at once, expanding all of them, and finally returning the highest-likelihood whole sequence. It sounds smarter — and for tasks with one roughly-correct target it is — but it's almost never used for chat. Here's the comparison:

Greedy

  • Take the top token every step.
  • Deterministic, fast, simple.
  • Myopic; repetitive on long open-ended text.
  • Good for: a single confident answer.

Sampling

  • Draw a token by (reshaped) probability.
  • Varied, human-like, controllable via the dials above.
  • Non-deterministic by design.
  • Good for: chat, writing, ideation.

Beam search

  • Keep B whole sequences, pick the most likely.
  • Maximizes total likelihood.
  • Bland & generic for open text; slow.
  • Good for: translation, summarization, ASR.

The deep reason beam search loses for open-ended generation is counter-intuitive: the most likely sequence is usually a boring one. Real, interesting human text is not the highest-probability continuation — it has surprise in it. Maximizing likelihood marches toward safe, repetitive, generic prose. That's why frontier chat APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini) don't even expose beam search; it lives on in translation and speech pipelines where there genuinely is one right-ish answer.

05 Guaranteeing valid output

Constrained decoding ▸

Now the technique that matters most for production: making the model emit output your code can trust. If you need JSON matching a schema, "ask nicely and hope" fails a fraction of the time — and a fraction of a million requests is a lot of broken parses. Constrained (structured) decoding makes invalid output impossible by construction. The mechanism is beautifully simple: at every step, a small state machine compiled from your schema computes which tokens are legal next, and the engine sets the logits of every illegal token to −∞ before sampling. The model literally cannot pick a token that breaks the grammar. Watch it build a JSON object:

You rarely implement the masking yourself — you turn it on. The surfaces, from highest-level to lowest:

  • Provider structured outputs. OpenAI's response_format: {type:"json_schema", strict:true} guarantees schema adherence; Google's Gemini takes a responseSchema; Anthropic's tool-use validates arguments against an input schema. Function/tool calling is constrained decoding under the hood.
  • JSON mode is a weaker cousin: it forces valid JSON syntax but not your specific schema (no guarantee the right keys are present). Prefer schema-strict modes.
  • Engine-level grammars for open-weight models: GBNF (llama.cpp), and libraries like Outlines, llguidance, and XGrammar (the default backend in vLLM and others), which compile a regex/grammar/JSON-schema into the token mask.
Two things to know before you ship it

First, the cost is a one-time schema compilation — the first request with a new schema is a little slower; identical schemas after that are nearly free per token (tens of microseconds). Second, don't constrain too early: forcing the model straight into a rigid object can hurt the quality of what's inside it. For anything requiring thought, let the model reason in free text first, then constrain only the final structured answer. Structure guarantees the shape, not the correctness — you still need evaluation for that.

06 A useful myth to bust

The determinism myth

A natural reflex once you understand temperature: "I'll set temperature = 0 and get the same output every time." It's the right instinct — and it doesn't quite work. Setting temperature to zero removes the sampling randomness (no more weighted dice), but it does not guarantee identical output on a shared API. Anthropic's own docs say it plainly: even at temperature 0, results are not fully deterministic.

We met the reason in Lesson 01: batch-invariance. A serving system batches your request with whatever other traffic is live, and the GPU's arithmetic order shifts with the batch size. Because floating-point addition isn't perfectly associative, a different batch can produce slightly different logits — enough, occasionally, to tip a close call between two near-equal tokens and send the rest of the generation down a different path. You don't control the batch size; it depends on everyone else. (A 2025 result from Thinking Machines Lab showed you can engineer batch-invariant kernels to get bit-identical output — at a real speed cost — but that isn't how production endpoints run today.)

What determinism actually buys you

So separate two ideas. A seed parameter plus temperature = 0 gets you best-effort reproducibility — invaluable for debugging, caching, and regression-testing your evals. What it does not get you is "correct" or even "stable to the bit." Design accordingly: greedy decoding makes output far more consistent, but never write a test that asserts an exact output string, and never build a feature that breaks if a word changes. Determinism is a debugging convenience, not a contract.

07 Putting it to work

Which dial, when

You'll spend more time choosing settings than you'd think, so here is the decision table to internalize. The single most useful habit: tune temperature or top-p, not both, and pick your settings from the task, not from a default someone copied.

TaskTemperatureTruncation / extras
Data extraction → JSON0 (greedy)constrained decoding / json_schema strict
Classification / labeling0constrain to the label set (or logit-bias); no free sampling
Code generation0–0.2structured/grammar decoding; no repetition penalty
Factual Q&A / RAG0–0.3top-p 0.9; low/no penalties
General chat~0.7top-p 0.9–0.95; mild frequency penalty if it loops
Brainstorming / ideation0.9–1.1top-p 0.95 or min-p 0.05–0.1; presence penalty for variety
Creative writing1.0–1.3min-p 0.05–0.1 (lets temperature run high safely)

The pattern is one axis: correctness vs variety. For correctness, push temperature down and constrain the output. For variety, push temperature up and use min-p so the tail stays sane. Here's that logic applied to a real bug.

Tune the dialsymptom → settings
An extraction endpoint that won't behave

Your service extracts a contact record from an email into JSON. It works most of the time — but the same email sometimes yields slightly different fields, and roughly 1 in 50 responses is malformed JSON that crashes the parser downstream.

Diagnose the dials

Symptom 1 — varies run to run

Same input, different fields. That's sampling randomness — the call is using a default temperature near 1, drawing different tokens each time (§1). For an extraction task there's a right answer; variety is pure downside.

Symptom 2 — occasionally invalid

1-in-50 malformed JSON is the tell of "ask nicely and hope." Nothing is stopping the model from emitting a stray token that breaks the syntax (§5).

Fix the temperature

Set temperature = 0 (greedy). Now you get the model's single most-confident extraction, and the same email gives the same answer (up to the batching caveat in §6 — close enough for this).

Fix the validity

Turn on structured output — json_schema with strict: true (or tool-calling, or a grammar for an open model). Malformed JSON becomes impossible; the 1-in-50 failures go to zero by construction, not by retry.

Don't over-correct

Resist adding a frequency penalty — it would start dodging the repeated braces and commas JSON needs (§3). And remember: the schema guarantees the shape, not that the extracted values are right — that's a job for evaluation (Phase 2).

Say it back in one breath

“It varies because we're sampling at a high default temperature, and it occasionally breaks because nothing constrains the output. So: temperature to zero for a single confident answer, and schema-strict structured output so invalid JSON can't be generated at all. No repetition penalty — it would fight the syntax. The shape is now guaranteed; correctness is an eval problem.”

The inverse case is just as common: a creative-writing feature that feels flat and repetitive needs the opposite move — raise temperature to ~1.1, add min-p ~0.07 so it stays coherent, and a small presence penalty for variety. Same two dials, turned the other way.

The wrinkle on the newest models

One forward-looking note: the latest frontier reasoning models are starting to remove the sampling knobs entirely. Some of the newest Claude and OpenAI reasoning models reject a non-default temperature / top_p / top_k (or require you not to set two at once) — they fix their internal sampling for reliability, and you steer behavior through prompting and an "effort" parameter instead of the dials. So the skills in this lesson are split: the mechanics (reshape, trim, constrain) are permanent and apply everywhere, including every open-weight model; the specific API knobs are receding on the very newest hosted models. Always check what the model you're calling actually exposes.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

Decoding is the step between the model's distribution and your text, and it's entirely yours: reshape it with temperature, trim its tail with top-p or min-p, mask it with a grammar, then draw — or just take the top token. The settings are small; their effect on behavior is enormous.

01Decoding is yoursThe model outputs a distribution; you choose how to turn it into a token. It's a serving-layer setting, not a model change.
02Temperature = peakednessLow sharpens (focused, repeatable, 0 = greedy); high flattens (varied, eventually nonsense). It never changes the ranking.
03Trim the tailTop-k = fixed count; top-p = cumulative budget (adaptive); min-p = relative-to-best bar (lets high temp stay sane).
04One dial at a timeTune temperature OR top-p, not both. Keep repetition penalties small, and off for code/JSON.
05Beam isn't for chatThe most-likely sequence is the blandest. Beam suits translation/ASR; chat uses sampling.
06Constrain for validityMask illegal tokens to −∞ so invalid JSON is impossible. Use schema-strict structured output; reason first, constrain last.
