00Concept · AI Engineering

The AI-Engineering Landscape

Before the techniques, the map. AI engineering is the craft of building products on top of foundation models you didn't train — and like any craft, it has a small number of moving parts and a default order to learn them. This first lesson gives you that map so every later lesson has a place to land.

00 Orientation

What this field actually is

Here's the one-sentence definition to anchor everything: AI engineering is building applications on top of pre-trained foundation models — you adapt a model someone else trained, you do not train one from scratch. That single word, adaptation, is the whole shift. The hard, expensive part — pre-training a large model on a slice of the internet with thousands of GPUs for months — has already been done by a handful of labs. Your job starts the moment that model is available behind an API or as a set of weights.

It helps to place the role against its neighbors, because the boundaries are where people get confused:

ML Research

  • Invents new architectures and training methods.
  • The people who gave us the transformer, new optimizers, RLHF.
  • Math- and experiment-heavy; measured in papers.

ML Engineering

  • Trains models: curates data, labels, builds features, runs training, deploys.
  • Model-first — build the model, then an app around it.
  • Needs a dataset and the compute to fit it.

AI Engineering

  • Adapts existing foundation models into products.
  • Product-first — ship the app today, tune the model later (if ever).
  • Starts with an API call, not a training run.

The cleanest way to feel the difference is an analogy. Classic ML engineering is building the engine, then building the car around it — you spend months on the model before there's anything to drive. AI engineering flips the order: you buy the engine off the shelf and build the car today, and you only crack the engine open to tune it if the off-the-shelf one genuinely isn't enough. That inversion is exactly why this work is now led by software engineers and not only by people with ML PhDs.

And that is the good news for you specifically. You arrive with the two things this job leans on hardest: you can build software, and you can reason about systems. Models are reached by making API calls, which removes the old barrier to entry — no specialized degree, no labeled dataset, no GPU cluster. Studies of real job postings bear this out: the most-requested skills are Python, SQL, Docker, Kubernetes, and a cloud platform — a systems and deployment profile far more than a deep-learning-theory one. You don't need to derive backpropagation to do this well. You need to understand the model well enough to predict its behavior, and to wire it into a reliable system. This course teaches exactly that layer, and nothing below it.

The mental reframe

Stop thinking of the model as a thing you build and start thinking of it as a component you compose — a remarkably capable, slightly unreliable component with a usage meter attached. Every skill in this course is a way to get more reliable behavior out of that component, or to spend less money doing it.

01 The field map

Nine areas, one map ▸

AI engineering looks sprawling from the outside, but the whole field fits into nine areas of competence. Everything you will ever read about — every blog post, framework, and conference talk — slots into one of these. Learn the map once and you stop feeling lost. The nine areas, and the order this course takes them in, are below; step through them in the diagram.

Two things about this map are worth saying out loud now, because they shape everything that follows. First, evaluation sits in the middle, marked in rust, not because it comes third chronologically but because it is the hardest problem and it touches every other area — we'll come back to why in a moment. Second, safety and security is drawn apart, in violet, because it isn't a stage you do once at the end; it's a concern that runs through all the others. A prompt has to be injection-resistant, a tool has to be least-privilege, a RAG system has to avoid leaking data. We gather it into one phase to study it systematically, but in practice it's everywhere.

Why a map beats a checklist

You will not learn these areas strictly in order in real life — a project might need RAG and evals before it ever needs fine-tuning, and most never fine-tune at all. The map's job is to let you locate any new idea instantly: "oh, that's a retrieval problem," "that's an inference-cost problem." Naming the area is half of solving the problem.

02 The default order

The adaptation ladder ▸

If the nine areas tell you what exists, the adaptation ladder tells you in what order to reach for things. When you need a model to do something it doesn't do well out of the box, you have a menu of ways to adapt it — and they are not equal in cost. The discipline is to start with the cheapest thing that could possibly work, and only climb when you hit a real wall.

The ladder, from cheap-and-fast to expensive-and-slow, is: prompting → retrieval (RAG) → agents → fine-tuning. Climb it in the diagram.

There's a clean way to keep the rungs straight — picture a professional chef you've hired. Prompting is giving a clear order: "medium-rare, no garlic." RAG is handing the chef your recipe binder and pantry, so they cook with your ingredients and your knowledge — without changing how they cook. Agents let the chef act on their own: taste, adjust, fetch a missing ingredient, decide the next step. Fine-tuning is sending the chef back to culinary school to permanently change how they cook. You'd try the first three long before you'd pay for the fourth.

An honest caveat: it's a default, not a law

Plenty of practitioners push back on calling this a strict "ladder," and they have a point. RAG and fine-tuning fix different problems: RAG closes a knowledge gap (the model doesn't know your data), while fine-tuning closes a behavior gap (the model won't follow your format or tone reliably, no matter the prompt). If your problem is "wrong facts," no amount of fine-tuning fixes it; if your problem is "won't output clean JSON," more retrieval won't help. So read the ladder as the default reaching order — cheapest first — not a sequence of mandatory upgrades. Diagnose the gap, then pick the rung that addresses it.

One more reason to favor the lower rungs: fine-tuned models go stale. Base models improve every few months, and a model you fine-tuned last quarter can be worse than next quarter's base model out of the box — so you've taken on a maintenance tax for a result that's now behind. Prompts and retrieval pipelines, by contrast, ride the improvements for free. This is why "fine-tuning is often a last resort" is such common advice.

03 The crux

Evaluation: the hard center

Of the nine areas, one deserves special billing, and it's the one beginners always underrate: evaluation — measuring whether your AI system is actually any good. In a normal codebase you write assert add(2, 2) == 4: the answer is one value, the test is binary, and it passes or fails the same way every time. Foundation models break all three of those assumptions at once.

  • The output is open-ended. There is no single right answer to "summarize this document" — there's a fuzzy region of acceptable answers. A summary can be fluent and confident and still wrong.
  • The output is non-deterministic. The same input can produce a different answer on the next run (you'll see exactly why in the next lesson). A test that passes once might fail next time.
  • Regressions are silent. You tweak a prompt, bump a model version, or change a retrieval setting — and quality quietly drops with no error thrown. Nothing crashes. You just ship something worse and find out from users.

That last point is the killer. In ordinary software, a bad change usually announces itself — a stack trace, a red build. In an AI system, a bad change can be completely invisible until it reaches production. So the only defense is to turn evaluation into a continuous, automated discipline — sometimes called eval-driven development or "eval-ops." You build a golden set of representative cases, you score every change against it, and you gate merges on the result, exactly like a test suite — except the assertions are graded rubrics, not equalities.

But how do you grade fluffy text at scale? Human review is the gold standard and has the highest value per case, but it's slow and expensive and doesn't scale to thousands of examples per commit. The dominant answer in 2025 is LLM-as-a-judge: you use a (often stronger) model, given a clear rubric, to score another model's output. It's cheap and scalable — and it has its own biases (it can favor longer answers, or answers in its own style), so the current frontier is about calibrating the judge itself, treating it as a measuring instrument with known error bars. We give evaluation its own phase later in the course; for now, just absorb the priority:

The one sentence to remember

If you can't measure it, you can't improve it — and with non-deterministic models, you can't even tell when you've made it worse. Evaluation is what lets you change anything with confidence. It's not the glamorous part, but it's the part that separates a demo from a product.

Notice, too, where evaluation sits in the source material that defines this field. In Chip Huyen's AI Engineering, evaluation gets two chapters, and they come before the chapters on prompting, RAG, and fine-tuning. That ordering is a deliberate signal: you set up the way you'll measure success before you start optimizing, not after.

04 The whole machine

The production stack ▸

A model behind an API is not a product. The gap between "I got a good answer in a notebook" and "ten thousand users get good answers reliably and affordably" is filled by a stack of components — and that stack has become standardized enough that you can draw it. Below is the path a single request takes through a mature AI application. Each layer exists to solve a specific failure of the naive "just call the model" approach.

You don't build all of this on day one — and you shouldn't. This is the same "start at the top, drill down only as needed" discipline as the adaptation ladder, applied to architecture. A weekend prototype is just the model. You add a layer the moment its absence becomes a problem: add RAG when the model needs your data; add guardrails when users send things you can't trust; add a gateway when you want to swap models or survive a provider outage; add a cache when the same questions recur and the bill hurts; add agent patterns when a task genuinely can't be done in one call. And wrapping all of it, from the very first real user, is monitoring — because of everything we just said about silent regressions, you cannot operate an AI system you can't see into.

This stack is also the through-line of the whole course. Each layer is a phase: context engineering (P3), safety (P8), gateways and ops (P7), agents (P4), and the monitoring rail (P7) that makes evaluation (P2) possible in production. The map, the ladder, and this stack are three views of the same territory.

05 The route ahead

How to use this course

This course walks the nine areas in a dependency-aware order — high-leverage, low-barrier things first. You're reading Phase 0 right now: enough understanding of the model itself to reason about its behavior instead of treating it as magic. Here's the road, and what each phase buys you:

  • Phase 0 — Foundations. How the model works, tokens and cost, sampling, embeddings, choosing a model. The vocabulary everything else assumes.
  • Phase 1 — Prompting & structured output. The first and highest-leverage rung of the ladder: getting reliable, parseable behavior out of a model with words alone.
  • Phase 2 — Evaluation. The hard center, set up early so every later phase has a way to tell whether a change helped.
  • Phase 3 — RAG & retrieval. Putting your own data into the model's context.
  • Phase 4 — Agents & tools. Letting the model act — and knowing when not to.
  • Phases 5–8 — Fine-tuning, inference optimization, architecture & ops, and safety. The deeper and the cross-cutting concerns, for when prompt-and-RAG hits its ceiling and when you take something to real production scale.

Two principles will serve you the whole way. The first: build more than you read. This field rewards hands-on iteration over theory — a prompt you actually tested beats ten you read about. The second, from Anthropic's guidance on agents and just as true everywhere else: start with the simplest thing that works, and add complexity only when a simpler setup genuinely can't express the task. Reach for a framework, an agent, or a fine-tune only after the plain version has failed you for a reason you can name.

The posture to carry forward

You already have the foundation this field stands on — you can build and ship software, and you can reason about distributed systems. AI engineering is a layer on top of that, not a replacement for it. Treat each new technique the way you'd treat a new library: understand what problem it solves, what it costs, and when not to use it. That instinct, more than any single tool, is what makes a good AI engineer.
