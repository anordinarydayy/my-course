The Model Landscape & Choosing a Model

There is no "best model" — there's a menu, and the skill is matching the model to the job. This lesson maps the landscape (open vs closed, the capability tiers, multimodal), teaches you to read a model card and distrust a leaderboard, and gives you a decision framework that ends, like everything in this field, at "run your own evals."

00 The framing

A menu, not a winner

Ask "which model is best?" and you've asked the wrong question. Best at what, under what budget, at what latency, for whose data? The frontier is a tight cluster — several models from different labs trade the lead every few weeks — and below them sits a deep menu of cheaper, faster, and open options, most of which are more than good enough for most tasks. The job isn't to find the single best model; it's to match a model to a job, and usually to use several across one application.

The instinct to "just use the most capable model for everything" is the single most expensive mistake in AI engineering. You can pay 30 to 100× more per token for capability that the overwhelming majority of your requests don't need. The mature pattern — which this whole lesson builds toward — is to treat models like a team you staff: route the easy work to something cheap and fast, and reserve the flagship for the genuinely hard slice.

The hiring analogy

Choosing a model is like staffing a team. You don't put your principal engineer on every routine ticket — you'd go broke, and they'd be bored. You route the password-reset to a junior and escalate the gnarly distributed-systems bug to the principal. Paying flagship token rates to classify support tickets is paying a principal's salary to reset passwords. The art is the routing.

01 The big divide

Open vs closed — and a word that misleads

The first axis of the landscape is who holds the weights. Closed (API-only) models — the GPT, Claude, and Gemini families — live behind a vendor's endpoint; you send tokens, you get tokens, the weights never leave their servers. Open-weight models — Llama, DeepSeek, Qwen, Mistral, Gemma — publish their trained parameters, so you can download and run them on your own hardware.

Here's the trap, and it matters legally: "open weight" is not "open source." Open-weight means you get the parameters — but the training data, the training code, and the recipe usually stay secret, so you can't actually reproduce the model. Genuinely open-source models (weights plus data plus training code, like AI2's OLMo) are rare. And the licenses differ enormously:

  • Permissive (Apache-2.0 / MIT) — e.g. Qwen, Mistral (recent), DeepSeek. Commercial use and fine-tuning with no royalties and minimal strings. The clean choice for a business.
  • Source-available, restricted — e.g. the Llama Community License: free to use commercially unless your product exceeds ~700M monthly active users, at which point you need Meta's permission, plus attribution requirements. That's a real legal exposure a large company has to weigh.

Closed / API

  • Top-end capability, newest first, zero ops.
  • Managed scaling; you pay per token, idle costs nothing.
  • Your prompts leave your network; pricing & deprecation are the vendor's call.
  • Best when capability and speed-to-ship matter most.

Open-weight / self-host

  • Full control, privacy (data stays in your VPC), no deprecation surprises.
  • Cheaper at high, steady volume; customizable / fine-tunable.
  • You own the ops, the GPUs, and the idle cost.
  • Best for privacy, scale economics, or regulatory needs.

The capability gap between the best open and best closed models has narrowed to a few points on most benchmarks, so the choice is increasingly about control, privacy, license, and cost-at-scale rather than raw quality. A rough rule of thumb: below roughly a few thousand dollars a month of API spend, hosted APIs win on total cost because idle GPUs are brutal; above that, self-hosting an open-weight model can pay off — if you have the ops appetite.

02 The capability ladder

Tiers & mixture-of-experts ▸

Within every provider, the lineup follows the same three-tier shape. Learn the shape once and you can read any vendor's menu at a glance. Step up the ladder:

One architectural fact explains a lot of the menu: most frontier models are now Mixture-of-Experts (MoE). Instead of every parameter firing for every token, a router activates only a few specialist sub-networks per token. So a model can carry huge total capacity while only using a slice of it on each token — DeepSeek-V3, for instance, has about 671B total parameters but activates only ~37B per token. The practical consequence for you: cost and latency scale with the active parameters, but the memory footprint scales with the total — which is why these models are cheap to run per token but still need big GPUs to host.

Don't reach for the top rung by default

The single most useful habit: start on the lowest tier that could plausibly work, and climb only when it visibly fails. Most engineers do the opposite — prototype on the flagship because it "just works," then ship it and watch the bill. A cheap model plus a good prompt clears a startling fraction of real tasks; you discover the genuinely hard ones by trying the cheap path first.

03 Beyond text

Multimodal models

Models increasingly handle more than text. A multimodal (or large multimodal) model takes some mix of text, images, audio, and video as input — and the most capable ones can produce non-text output too. Two distinctions matter when you're choosing:

  • Native vs adapted. A native multimodal model was trained jointly on all modalities from the start (it reasons over one shared representation); an adapted one is a text model with a vision encoder bolted on. Native tends to reason across modalities more coherently.
  • Input vs output modalities are separate. Many models accept images but only emit text. If you need generated images, audio, or video, that's a different capability — and often a different model. Always check the model card, not the marketing: blogs over-claim, and a card will tell you a model that "understands video" still only outputs text.
CapabilityTypical inputTypical output
Vision-language (most flagships)text + images / PDFstext only
Native "omni" (e.g. Gemini, Qwen-Omni)text + image + audio + videotext + image + speech
Image / video generationtext prompt (+ ref image)image / video

For an app builder, the takeaway is modest but practical: figure out exactly which modalities you need in and out, then filter the menu to models that genuinely support that pairing — and confirm it on the card before you design around it.

04 The spec sheet

Reading a model card ▸

A few of these hide real money. Output is priced several times higher than input, so output-heavy work dominates the bill (Lesson 02). 05 Trust, but verify

Benchmarks & their limits

Leaderboards are how the field keeps score, and they're useful for a first shortlist — but they're badly misused. Know what each kind measures, and trust none of them blindly:

BenchmarkMeasuresThe catch
LMArena (Chatbot Arena)blind human preference (Elo)rewards style/length; gameable
MMLU / MMLU-Probroad knowledge (multiple choice)saturated — everyone near the ceiling
GPQA / HLEhard expert & science questionsharder to game, but narrow
SWE-bench Verifiedresolving real GitHub issues (coding)the headline agentic-coding metric
τ-benchmulti-turn agentic tool useclosest to real agent reliability

Three failure modes to keep in mind. Contamination: benchmark questions leak into training data, so a model recalls rather than reasons. Saturation: when every frontier model scores 90%+, the benchmark stops discriminating. And "benchmaxxing": vendors tune to known suites, so the gains don't transfer to your workload. The top model on a public leaderboard rewards verbose, generically-pleasing answers — which may be exactly wrong for your terse, domain-specific, latency-sensitive task.

The only benchmark that matters is yours

Use leaderboards to pick a shortlist of three or four candidates, then settle it with your own evaluation set on your own data — 50–100 real examples from your task, scored for quality, cost, and latency. This is the same evaluation discipline that runs through the whole course (Phase 2), pointed at model selection. A benchmark tells you a model is capable in general; only your eval tells you it's right for this job.

06 The decision

Choosing & routing ▸

Pull it together into a method. Choosing a model means weighing a handful of axes — capability needed, cost, latency, context size, modality, privacy/residency, license, reliability — and the answer is rarely a single model. The dominant production pattern is routing: send each request to the cheapest model that can handle it, and escalate only the hard or high-stakes ones. Watch the traffic split:

Where you run the chosen model is the last decision. The options, roughly from least to most control: first-party APIs (lowest ops, newest first); cloud platforms (AWS Bedrock, Google Vertex, Azure Foundry — for compliance, data residency, and a unified API across many models); fast inference providers (Groq, Together, Fireworks, with OpenRouter as an aggregator) to serve open-weight models without owning GPUs; and self-hosting (vLLM in production, Ollama for local dev) when data must never leave your network. Here's the whole framework applied to a real feature.

Choose a modelrequirements → pick
A support-ticket auto-responder

Classify each incoming support ticket's intent and draft a reply from a knowledge-base article. Thousands a day. Tickets contain customer PII. Users wait in a chat window.

Work the axes

Capability

Classify + draft from a snippet is moderate, not frontier. Start on a cheap/fast tier (Haiku / Flash / a small open model), not a flagship.

Latency & context

User is waiting → want fast first-token. Need ~3–5 KB articles (~10K tokens) in context → any modern window covers it; no 1M-context premium needed.

Privacy / residency

PII changes everything. Either a cloud platform with a data-processing agreement and residency (Bedrock / Vertex), or self-host an open-weight model (Qwen / Llama via vLLM) so data never leaves your VPC.

License

If you self-host and you're a large product (>700M MAU), prefer Apache/MIT models (Qwen, DeepSeek) over the Llama license to avoid the MAU clause.

Route & escalate

Add a confidence gate: if the cheap model's draft scores low, or the ticket is flagged angry/legal, escalate to a flagship (Sonnet / a top GPT). ~80% stay cheap, ~20% escalate.

Validate

Ignore the leaderboard. Build a 50–100 ticket eval set from real data, run each candidate, measure resolution rate + cost + latency, and pick the cheapest model that clears the bar — flagship only on the escalation path.

Say it back in one breath

“It's a moderate task at high volume with PII and a waiting user, so I start cheap and fast, keep the data in-VPC or under a DPA, and pick a permissive-license model if I self-host. I route the routine ~80% to the cheap model and escalate only the hard or sensitive tickets to a flagship behind a confidence gate — and I decide with a real eval set, not a benchmark.”

The through-line of the whole lesson: don't default to the biggest model; treat models as a menu, route per request, and let your evaluation — not a leaderboard — make the final call. That hands off directly into the evaluation phase.
