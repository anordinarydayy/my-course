When to Fine-tune (and When Not To)

Phase 5 — Fine-tuning & Model Adaptation — opens on its most important and most misread decision, and this is its ★Core lesson: when should you fine-tune a model at all? The honest answer is rarely, and almost never first. Fine-tuning sits at the very top of a ladder of adaptation techniques — above prompting, few-shot, retrieval, and tools — and every rung costs more effort, more infrastructure, and slower iteration than the one beneath it. Climb only when the rung below is genuinely exhausted. The whole lesson turns on a single diagnosis: are you missing facts, or missing form?

00 The naive reach

“Let’s just fine-tune it”

A model isn’t good enough on your task. Its answers drift off-format, or it flubs a fact, or it won’t hold the tone you need. Someone on the team says the obvious grown-up thing: “let’s fine-tune it on our data.” It sounds like the serious move — the thing real ML teams do, the most sophisticated option on the menu. That is exactly why it is the most common and most expensive mistake in applied AI.

Be precise about what fine-tuning is. It means continuing to train the model’s weights on a dataset of your own examples so that its behaviour shifts toward them. That is a heavier commitment than it looks. You need labelled data in your production format, a training run and the compute for it, an eval harness to know whether it helped, a serving path for a now-custom model, and — the part everyone forgets — an owner for its entire lifecycle. Worse, the artefact is frozen and pinned: the instant your requirements shift or the base is upgraded, you may be doing it all again. A fine-tune is not a fix you ship and forget; it is a dependency you adopt.

None of that means fine-tuning is wrong — there are tasks where it is exactly right, and we will earn our way to them. It means fine-tuning should almost never be your first reach. Below it sits a ladder of cheaper, faster, more reversible adaptations, and the discipline of this lesson is to climb that ladder in order and stop the moment the problem is solved — for most problems, two or three rungs down, before the weights ever move.

The naive reach

  • “Fine-tune on everything.” Reach for the top rung first because it sounds the most capable.
  • Assumes every failure is a lack of intelligence the model can be trained into.
  • Pays weeks of effort, buys a frozen artefact, and often fixes the wrong thing.
  • Discovers the requirement changed — and the fine-tune is already stale.

The disciplined climb

  • Start at the bottom rung and climb only when it is genuinely exhausted.
  • First diagnoses the failure: missing facts, or missing form?
  • Solves most problems with prompting, few-shot, or retrieval — in hours, not weeks.
  • Treats fine-tuning as a last resort you reach for with a specific reason and a passing eval.
The one-line version

Fine-tuning is the top of the adaptation ladder and usually the last resort, not the first tool you reach for. Before you touch a single weight, ask two questions: have I exhausted the cheaper rungs? and is this failure about facts or about form? The rest of the lesson is those two questions, drawn out.

01 The ladder

The adaptation ladder ▸

By 2025–2026 this has hardened into consensus. Vendor docs and practitioner guides describe the same escalation order for adapting a model to your task: (1) prompt engineering → (2) few-shot / in-context examples → (3) RAG / retrieval → (4) tools & agentic loops → (5) fine-tuning — increasingly with distillation as a sixth rung on top. One widely-cited 2026 guide states the sequence bluntly as “Prompt → RAG → Fine-tune → Distill.” The framing is not arbitrary: each rung up costs strictly more effort, infrastructure, data, and iteration latency. Every source agrees fine-tuning “should usually be the final step,” and that teams skip to it anyway because it “sounds like the most sophisticated option.” Watch the climb:

Read the ladder as a cost gradient: the rung you want is the lowest one that clears the bar, because lower rungs iterate faster and reverse trivially.

RungWhat you actually changeIterate inRough cost
1 · PromptThe words of the instructionseconds~free
2 · Few-shotA handful of examples inside the promptminutesextra prompt tokens
3 · RAGFacts fetched fresh at query timehours–days~$70–1,000 / month
4 · Tools / agentsActions the model can takedaysinfra + orchestration
5 · Fine-tuneThe model’s own weightsweeks + ongoingtraining + higher serving
The rule of the ladder

Climb one rung at a time, and stop the moment the problem is solved. Do not jump to fine-tuning because it sounds impressive — jump to it only when the rung below has genuinely failed and you can say exactly why a cheaper rung cannot close the gap. If you cannot articulate that reason, you are not ready to fine-tune.

02 The diagnosis

Form, not facts ▸

Here is the one diagnosis that decides everything, and it is worth memorising as a reflex. When a model fails, the failure is one of two kinds. Either it does not KNOW something — a fact about your product, a figure from today, a clause in a document it was never trained on — which is a knowledge gap. Or it knows perfectly well but will not reliably PRODUCE the right shape — the format, the tone, the structure, the brevity, the refusal pattern — which is a behaviour or form gap. These two failures look similar from the outside (a bad answer came back) and demand opposite fixes.

The slogans capture it: “RAG teaches what to know; fine-tuning teaches how to think,” and more bluntly, “fine-tuning is for form, not facts.” A knowledge gap belongs in retrieval — fetch the fact at query time, because retrieval always serves the current version. A form gap is where fine-tuning shines — you teach not new information but a consistent way of responding. Route the failure to the wrong fix and you get the worst of both worlds — step through both cases:

Why not just fine-tune the facts in? Because fine-tuning bakes a snapshot of the world into the weights. The day the fact changes, the model keeps confidently emitting the old value, with no idea it is now wrong. A 2025 paper (“From Parameters to Prompts,” arXiv:2505.23410) even formalises a measurable factuality gap that fine-tuning introduces. This is the empirical backing for the rule: knowledge belongs in retrieval, not in the weights. Hold the whole decision as a two-by-two — knowledge or behaviour, stable or volatile?

The gapStable requirementVolatile / changes often
Knowledge (a fact)(rarely) continued pretrainingRAG / retrieval
Behaviour (a form)Fine-tune (LoRA / QLoRA)Prompt + few-shot
Fine-tuning teaches form, not facts

If you can fix the failure by handing the model the right information, it was a knowledge gap — use RAG. If the model already has the information and simply won’t render it the way you need, that is a form gap — and only then does fine-tuning enter the conversation. The single most expensive error in this lesson is fine-tuning to inject knowledge that changes.

03 Green light

When fine-tuning genuinely earns its place

Now the affirmative case — because fine-tuning is not a trap, it is a specialised tool, and there are jobs only it does well. Every one of them is a behaviour problem, not a knowledge one. A narrow, stable output format the model keeps breaking — a proprietary markup, a rigid report layout — where no amount of prompting quite makes it reliable. A consistent tone or persona you need to hold across thousands of replies. Latency and cost: distil a large model’s behaviour into a small, cheap one you can serve fast, which is the highest-ROI reason of all. High-volume classification or structured extraction, where a long few-shot prompt would dominate your token bill — fine-tuning folds those examples into the weights so the prompt shrinks to nothing. And a skill few-shot can’t fit: a behaviour so intricate that the examples needed to demonstrate it won’t fit in a context window.

The through-line: a stable behaviour, exercised at enough volume to amortise the cost of owning a fine-tune. Stability and volume separate a good fine-tune from a bad one.

Green light — fine-tune

  • A stable output format the model keeps breaking at volume.
  • A consistent persona / tone you must hold everywhere.
  • Distillation — a small cheap model imitating a big one’s behaviour for latency and cost.
  • High-volume classification / extraction where long prompts dominate cost.
  • A skill few-shot can’t fit into the context window.

Red light — use a lower rung

  • You want to add knowledge / facts → RAG.
  • A one-off or low-volume task that can’t amortise the cost.
  • You haven’t exhausted prompt + few-shot yet.
  • Requirements change weekly — the fine-tune goes stale.
  • You have no eval and no failing baseline to prove it helps.

The pull toward fine-tuning is strong, so gate it behind a precondition checklist — every box a genuine yes before you start. One well-known 2026 guide lists five: (1) an eval exists, and a strong prompt + RAG baseline provably fails it; (2) the failure is behaviour, not missing knowledge; (3) you have hundreds of high-quality examples matching your production format; (4) an owner is committed to the adapter’s lifecycle for twelve months or more; and (5) the ongoing operating cost is justified by the gain. Miss any one — especially the first — and you are about to spend weeks proving nothing.

This is where Phase 2 comes back

Notice condition (1): an eval the prompt + RAG baseline fails. That is not paperwork — it is the only way to know a fine-tune helped rather than silently regressing something else. Evals are your proof mechanism. It is telling that OpenAI’s Reinforcement Fine-Tuning automatically creates an Evals run for every job: grading is built into training because a fine-tune with no eval is a guess. Never fine-tune without a baseline you can beat and a metric that says you beat it.

04 Red light

When not to — and the staleness treadmill ▸

The red-light cases mirror the green ones. Don’t fine-tune to add knowledge — that is RAG’s job, and baking facts in makes them rot. Don’t fine-tune a one-off or low-volume task that can’t amortise the cost. Don’t fine-tune before prompt and few-shot are exhausted — skipping the ladder is the most common and most expensive mistake. And above all, don’t fine-tune when your requirements change weekly — because then you climb onto the staleness treadmill:

This is not a thought experiment — it is happening in the market. OpenAI is winding down its first-party fine-tuning platform on a phased schedule: from May 7, 2026 organisations that never fine-tuned can’t start new jobs; from July 2, 2026 those without recent fine-tuned inference lose creation access; and from January 6, 2027 no customer can create new fine-tuning jobs at all. Legacy fine-tuned models (the ft-gpt-4 era) are being retired as their base models are deprecated, and users are steered toward stronger bases (the GPT-5.x family). Read that as this lesson’s thesis written in a deprecation calendar: base models improve faster than most fine-tunes can be justified or retrained — and a fine-tune is pinned to one base’s lifecycle, so when that base retires, so does your fine-tune.

The pitfalls, in one place

Skipping the ladder — jumping to fine-tuning before prompting, few-shot, and RAG are exhausted. Fine-tuning for facts — a stale, confidently-wrong model and a measurable factuality gap. The retraining treadmill — requirements shift faster than you can retrain. Catastrophic forgetting — full fine-tuning quietly erodes general capability. No eval, no proof — you can’t tell if it helped or silently regressed. Cost and lock-in surprises — fine-tuned models are served at higher per-token rates and pinned to a base that will eventually die.

05 The modern how

If you must: do it the 2026 way

You have climbed the ladder honestly, diagnosed a genuine behaviour gap, and cleared the checklist. What does “fine-tuning” actually mean in 2026? Almost never full fine-tuning — in practice it means parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), specifically LoRA / QLoRA. Instead of updating all the weights, you train a tiny adapter — typically 0.1–1% of the parameters, at low rank (r commonly 8–64) — while the base stays frozen: memory drops roughly 10–20× while retaining around 90–95% of full-fine-tune quality on many tasks. QLoRA loads the base in 4-bit and trains the adapter in higher precision, putting a ~65B-parameter fine-tune within reach of a single 48GB GPU.

The frozen base is not just a cost trick — it is the main defence against the biggest failure mode. Full fine-tuning is prone to catastrophic forgetting: the model gets better at your narrow task while quietly getting worse at everything else (most pronounced in the ~1B–7B range, per arXiv:2406.04836). Because LoRA freezes the base and isolates your changes in a small adapter — parameter isolation — prior knowledge is preserved far better. The common stacks: HuggingFace PEFT / TRL, Unsloth (~2–5× faster, ~70% less VRAM), Axolotl, and Torchtune.

There are three method families, and picking the right one is part of the diagnosis. SFT imitates labelled input→output pairs; DPO learns from “A is better than B” preferences (tone, style); and RFT — which OpenAI made publicly available around May 2025 — optimises against a grader when correctness is checkable (code, math, a schema that validates). The table is your cheat sheet:

MethodOptimisesReach for it when
SFT — supervisedImitate labelled input→output pairsa stable format or skill with clean examples
DPO — preferencePrefer A over B (tone, style)you have preference pairs, not gold answers
RFT — reinforcementMaximise a grader / rewardcorrectness is checkable (code, math, schema)

Two more things separate a good fine-tune from a wasted one. First, data quality beats quantity, decisively. The LIMA result (“Less Is More for Alignment”) is the anchor: roughly 500–2,000 curated examples typically outperform tens of thousands of scraped ones. “Hundreds” of clean, production-format examples is the practical entry bar — and training recursively on synthetic data risks model collapse. Second, the highest-ROI move at the top of the ladder is often not adding capability but distillation: use a large teacher model’s outputs to fine-tune a small, cheap student for one narrow task — reports cite up to ~30× cheaper inference and ~4× faster. And the ecosystem has followed the open-weight models: managed LoRA of Llama, Mistral, and Qwen stays cheap (Together AI lists LoRA up to 16B around $0.48 / 1M training tokens) via Together, Fireworks, Vertex AI (Gemini tuning is LoRA under the hood), and Azure — even as first-party frontier fine-tuning contracts.

The modern default

If you fine-tune in 2026, you almost certainly mean a LoRA/QLoRA adapter on an open-weight model, trained on a few hundred curated examples, proven against an eval. That default preserves the base’s knowledge, costs little, and keeps you off the frontier-deprecation treadmill — and distillation into a small student is how you actually cut serving cost, not fine-tuning a bigger model.

06 The rule

The rule, and a case to reason through

Compress the whole lesson into a procedure you can run in your head. One: reproduce the failure and diagnose the gap — is the model missing a fact (knowledge) or missing a form (behaviour)? Two: if it’s knowledge, route to RAG and stop. Three: if it’s behaviour, climb the cheap rungs — prompt, then few-shot, then structured-output APIs — and stop the moment one works. Four: only if a stable, high-volume behaviour still fails a prompt + RAG baseline on a real eval do you reach for a small LoRA/SFT, owned and revalidated over its life. Let’s run it on a case.

Reason about itone team · two gaps
“Let’s fine-tune so the model knows our product docs”

A support team ships an assistant and hits two complaints. (a) It gets facts about the product wrong — quotes an old refund window, misses a feature shipped last month. (b) Its replies won’t hold the strict JSON support-ticket schema their system ingests; it keeps returning prose or dropping fields. Their plan: one big fine-tune on “our docs and our past tickets.” Right call?

Restate → the naive take → the insight → the rule

Restate the problem

Two different failures have been bundled into one fix. Complaint (a) is the model not knowing a current fact. Complaint (b) is the model knowing the content but not shaping it into the required schema. Same team, opposite gaps.

The naive take

“Fine-tune on everything — docs and tickets together.” This bakes the docs into the weights as a snapshot that goes stale the next release, and only maybe nudges the format. You pay weeks of effort and end up with a model that hallucinates old facts and still isn’t reliably valid JSON.

The insight

Split by gap. (a) is a KNOWLEDGE gap → put the docs in RAG and retrieve them live, so answers track the current version. (b) is a BEHAVIOUR gap → reach for the cheap rungs first: a few-shot example plus a structured-output / JSON-schema API usually fixes format outright. Only if it still breaks at production volume is a small SFT/LoRA on a few hundred schema-correct replies warranted.

The rule / trade-off

Route each gap to its own rung; never fine-tune to add facts. And prove any fine-tune against a Phase-2 eval that the prompt + RAG baseline provably fails — otherwise you can’t tell whether the fine-tune helped or quietly regressed something else.

the two gaps, routed differently# BEFORE — one fine-tune for everything (the anti-pattern)
train(base, data = product_docs + past_tickets)   # bakes facts in → goes stale

# AFTER — route each gap to its own rung
# gap (a) KNOWLEDGE / facts  →  retrieve, don't bake
answer  = model(prompt, context = rag.search(query))   # docs stay live

# gap (b) BEHAVIOUR / form  →  few-shot + schema FIRST
schema  = { "ticket_id": str, "priority": "P0|P1|P2", "summary": str }
reply   = model(prompt, response_format = schema, examples = few_shot)

# ...only if it STILL breaks at volume, a small adapter on the FORM
adapter = lora_sft(base, examples = 400 schema_correct_replies)
Say it back in one breath

“They wanted one fine-tune to teach the model our docs and our JSON format — but those are two different gaps. The wrong facts are a knowledge gap, so the docs belong in RAG where they stay current; baking them into weights would just memorise a snapshot that rots. The broken schema is a behaviour gap, so I’d try few-shot and a structured-output API first, and only drop to a small LoRA if it still fails at volume — measured against an eval the baseline can’t pass.”

Diagnose the gap before you pick the rung. Facts → retrieve. Form → climb, and fine-tune last.

The phase ahead

You now hold the decision that governs the whole phase: fine-tuning is the top rung, reached only for a stable, high-volume behaviour that a prompt + RAG baseline provably can’t match.
