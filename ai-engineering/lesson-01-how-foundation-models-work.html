01Concept · AI Engineering

How a Foundation Model Works

You don't need to derive backpropagation to build with a model. You do need a correct mental model of what it's doing — enough to predict its behavior, debug its mistakes, and stop being surprised by them. This lesson gives you that, at the level of intuition, and earns one sentence you'll use for the rest of the course.

Original study notes. An intuition-first explanation for engineers, not a maths course. Where it states recent results it draws on public sources — Attention Is All You Need, the DeepSeek-R1 report, Stanford's "lost in the middle," Thinking Machines Lab's 2025 work on inference non-determinism — but every explanation, diagram, and example here is our own. 00 The anchor

The one-sentence model

Here is the sentence. Tape it to your monitor: a foundation model is a function that takes a sequence of text and returns a probability distribution over what the next token should be — and generation is that function called in a loop. That's it. Everything that feels magical about a chatbot — the essays, the code, the apparent reasoning — is that one operation, repeated, very fast, by a model that learned to do it well by reading a large slice of the internet.

The reason the sentence matters is that it tells you what the model is not. It is not a database you query — there is no table of facts it looks things up in. It is not a reasoning engine executing logical steps — there is no separate solver inside. It is a next-token predictor whose output can look like a lookup or a proof because, having read enough text, the most plausible continuation usually is the correct one. Usually. When it isn't, you get a confident, fluent, completely wrong answer — and now you know exactly why.

The improv analogy

Think of the model as an improv actor who never breaks character. It isn't recalling a stored fact; it's continuously saying the most plausible next thing given everything said so far. Most of the time the plausible line is also true. But the actor will never stop the scene to say "I don't actually know that" — so when the plausible continuation happens to be false, you get a hallucination: same fluent delivery, no flag raised. Hold onto that picture; we'll return to it when we debug a real failure at the end.

The rest of this lesson unpacks the sentence one clause at a time: what a "token" and a "distribution" are (§1), how the model decides which earlier tokens matter — attention (§2), what the "loop" looks like (§3), why the same prompt can give different answers (§4), how a raw predictor becomes a polite assistant (§5), and what changed when models learned to "think before answering" (§6). Then we'll use all of it to debug a mistake (§7).

01 Input and output

Tokens in, a distribution out ▸

First, the units. A model does not see characters or words — it sees tokens, which are sub-word chunks: common words are usually one token, while rarer words, code, and punctuation split into several. (The next lesson is devoted entirely to tokens, because they turn out to be the unit of both your context window and your bill.) For now, just hold that text becomes a list of tokens on the way in.

Now the crucial part — the output. When you give the model a sequence, it does not hand back a word. It hands back a probability for every single token in its vocabulary — and that vocabulary is enormous, typically tens of thousands to over a hundred thousand tokens. Most get a probability near zero; a handful get most of the mass. Step through what comes back for a simple prompt:

This is the single most useful thing to internalize about the output side. The model's honest answer to "what comes next?" is always a distribution — a ranked set of options with weights. Turning that distribution into one actual token is a separate decision, made by a sampling step that sits outside the model, and it's where a lot of the model's apparent personality (and its randomness) comes from. We'll pull that step apart in §4.

02 The mechanism

Attention, intuitively

To predict the next token well, the model has to know which earlier tokens matter. In "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired," what does "it" refer to? A human resolves that instantly to "the cat." The mechanism that lets a model do the same is attention, and it is the core idea of the transformer — the architecture every modern foundation model is built on.

The intuition is small and worth getting exactly right: every token looks at every other token in the sequence and decides how relevant each one is, then builds its own meaning from a weighted blend of the relevant ones. When the model processes "it," attention lets that position reach back across the whole sentence and pull most strongly from "cat." That's how a pronoun gets resolved, how a verb finds its subject, and how a fact stated in paragraph one informs a sentence in paragraph ten.

Two refinements make this real, and you only need them at headline level. First, attention runs in multiple "heads" at once — think of several highlighters reading the same sentence, each marking a different kind of relationship: one tracks subject–verb agreement, another tracks which noun a pronoun points to, another tracks tone. Their findings get combined. Second, attention is what made transformers fast to train: unlike the older recurrent models that read one word at a time, attention lets the model consider the whole sequence in parallel, which is exactly what GPUs are good at — and that parallelism, more than any single clever idea, is why models could be scaled up to today's sizes.

Two honest caveats

Attention is the headline, but it isn't the whole model — between attention layers sit ordinary neural-network layers that do much of the "knowledge and computation" work; we're zooming in on attention because it's the part that makes the architecture special. And here's the consequence that matters for you: attention has to look at every token in the context. The more you stuff in, the more each token's attention is spread thin — which is the seed of a very practical problem we hit in §4 and again all through the RAG phase.

03 Generation

The autoregressive loop ▸

We have the input (tokens), the output (a distribution), and the mechanism (attention). Now watch them combine into generation. The model produces text one token at a time, and each new token is fed back in before the next is predicted. The fancy word for "conditions each new token on all the previous ones" is autoregressive. Step the loop:

A few consequences fall straight out of this picture, and each one will save you grief later:

  • Output is slow and serial. The model can't write the tenth word until it has written the ninth — each token waits for the one before it. That's why responses stream in, and why long outputs cost real wall-clock time. (Input, by contrast, is processed in parallel — which is why prompts feel instant and generation doesn't.)
  • The model literally re-reads everything, every token. At each step it attends over the entire sequence so far. A long conversation isn't free background context — it's re-processed on every single token. Hold that thought; it's the engine of the cost explosion you'll meet in the next lesson.
  • There's no plan. The model didn't decide to write "mat" before it started the sentence. It committed to each token in turn. Which is why getting a model to "think out loud" before answering genuinely helps — those intermediate tokens become context that make the final tokens more likely to be right. That's the whole idea behind reasoning models in §6.
04 Non-determinism

Why the same prompt gives different answers

Run the same prompt twice and you can get two different answers. New engineers find this maddening; you should find it expected, because you now know the output is a distribution. There are two separate sources of that variation, and confusing them is a classic mistake.

Source one: sampling (you control this)

Turning the distribution into a token requires choosing, and the default choice is to sample — roll a weighted die over the candidates rather than always taking the top one. The main knob is temperature. Low temperature sharpens the distribution toward the single most likely token (more focused, more repetitive); high temperature flattens it (more varied, more "creative," more prone to nonsense). Temperature near zero collapses to greedy decoding — always take the top token. Two companion knobs, top-k (only consider the k likeliest tokens) and top-p / nucleus (consider the smallest set whose probabilities add up to p), trim the tail so the die never lands on something absurd.

the choice that lives outside the model

# the model returns logits; a sampler turns them into one token
probs = softmax(logits / temperature)   # ↓temp = sharper, ↑temp = flatter
probs = top_p(probs, p=0.9)             # drop the unlikely tail
next_token = sample(probs)              # a weighted die roll → different runs differ
# temperature = 0  →  next_token = argmax(probs)  (greedy, “deterministic”)

Source two: the hardware (you mostly don't)

Here's the part that trips up even experienced engineers, and the honest 2025 answer to it. You might think "fine, set temperature to 0 and I'll get the same answer every time." You won't — not reliably, not on a shared API. For years the hand-wave explanation was "floating-point rounding plus concurrency." Work from Thinking Machines Lab in 2025 pinned down the real culprit: batch-invariance. A serving system batches your request together with whoever else is calling right now, and the GPU's internal arithmetic order changes with the batch size. Because floating-point addition isn't perfectly associative — (a + b) + c can differ from a + (b + c) in the last bit — a different batch size yields slightly different numbers, which can tip a close call between two near-equal tokens and send the whole continuation down a different path. You don't control the batch size; it depends on other people's traffic.

The result that makes it concrete

In that study, one open model run 1,000 times at temperature 0 produced 80 distinct completions — the first divergence appearing around the 100th token. Only after the team rewrote key GPU operations to be batch-invariant did all 1,000 runs come out bit-for-bit identical — at a real speed cost. The takeaway for you: "temperature 0" means "stop adding randomness," not "guaranteed identical output." Tellingly, the newest frontier models have started removing the temperature knob from their APIs altogether — a quiet admission that you should design for variation, not bank on determinism.

So the engineering posture is: never assume the model is a pure function. Don't write a test that asserts an exact output string. Don't build a feature that breaks if the wording shifts. And — connecting forward — this is precisely why evaluation (Phase 2) has to grade a range of acceptable answers instead of checking for one. The non-determinism you just learned about is the reason the hardest problem in the field exists.

05 The two trainings

From base model to assistant

One more thing the one-sentence model leaves out: a raw next-token predictor is not the helpful assistant you talk to. Getting from one to the other takes two very different stages of training, and knowing which stage produced a behavior is a debugging superpower.

Pretraining is the expensive part you'll never do: next-token prediction over a vast corpus — trillions of tokens — until the model has absorbed grammar, facts, code, and reasoning patterns as a side effect of getting good at prediction. The result is a base model: deeply knowledgeable, and almost unusable as a chatbot. Ask it a question and it might continue with three more questions, because on the internet a question is often followed by more questions. It has the knowledge but not the behavior.

Post-training supplies the behavior, with a fraction of the data but a lot of care:

  • Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) — show the model thousands of curated (instruction, good response) pairs so it learns to act like an assistant: answer the question, follow the format, adopt the helpful tone.
  • Preference tuning — then nudge it toward responses people actually prefer. The classic recipe, RLHF, has humans rank outputs, trains a reward model on those rankings, and optimizes the model against it. The modern workhorse, DPO (Direct Preference Optimization), skips the separate reward model and optimizes directly on "this answer was preferred over that one" — simpler and cheaper, which is why SFT then DPO is now the common path. Anthropic's Constitutional AI variant has the model critique its own answers against a written set of principles, replacing much of the human labeling.
The debugging superpower

When a model misbehaves, ask which training stage owns this? A weird refusal, a rigid output format, a sycophantic "Great question!", an oddly corporate tone → that's post-training, and you fix it with prompting or model choice. A missing or outdated fact, "it just doesn't know about our product" → that's a pretraining limitation (data coverage and cutoff), and no amount of tone-tuning helps — you fix it with RAG. Base and instruct checkpoints are the same brain with different manners; naming the stage tells you which rung of the adaptation ladder to reach for.

06 The 2025 shift

Reasoning models & test-time compute ▸

For most of the LLM era, the way to get a smarter answer was to train a bigger model on more data — scale at training time. The defining shift of 2024–2025 was a second knob: scale at inference time. Let the model generate a long internal chain of thought before committing to an answer, and accuracy climbs with the number of "thinking" tokens you let it spend. These are reasoning models — OpenAI's o-series, DeepSeek-R1, Google's Gemini "thinking" models, and Claude's extended/adaptive thinking. Watch one work:

Two things make this more than a parlor trick. First, the "thinking" is not a new mechanism — it is the same autoregressive loop from §3, turned inward. The model generates intermediate tokens that aren't the final answer but make the final answer more likely to be right, exactly as predicted by "there's no plan, so thinking out loud helps." Second — and this is the genuinely new part — the step-by-step thinking is a trained behavior, not a prompt you write. Earlier, you got reasoning by asking for it ("let's think step by step"). Reasoning models learned to think, through reinforcement learning, and do it on their own.

The open-source story here is DeepSeek-R1 (early 2025), notable because it's transparent about how. Its R1-Zero variant was trained with reinforcement learning from scratch — no supervised examples of reasoning first — and the model developed self-verification and long chains of thought on its own, a moment the report describes almost with surprise. You don't need the algorithm names; you need the shift in posture:

What this changes for you, the engineer

Reasoning is now a knob with a price. Vendors expose it as a "thinking budget" or an "effort" level — Claude's adaptive thinking lets the model decide how hard to think; Gemini lets you cap the thinking tokens. More thinking buys accuracy on genuinely hard, multi-step problems (maths, code, planning) and is wasted money and latency on easy ones. And a billing trap to file away now: you pay for the thinking tokens even though the model usually only shows you a summary of them. Knowing when a task deserves the budget — and when it doesn't — is a real part of the job.

07 Putting it to work

What this means for you

You don't learn the mechanism for its own sake — you learn it so that model behavior stops being mysterious and starts being predictable. Here's the most common failure in production, walked through the lens you just built.

Reason about the behaviorsymptom → cause → fix
“The model cited a paper that doesn't exist”

Your assistant answered a research question and confidently referenced “Smith et al., 2021, in Nature” — complete with a plausible title and page numbers. The paper does not exist. Nobody typed it; the model produced it.

Trace it through the mechanism

Symptom

A fabricated but perfectly formatted citation — a hallucination. Fluent, specific, and false.

Mechanism

The model is predicting the most plausible next tokens (§0–1). After “according to,” a citation-shaped string is highly probable. A real citation and a fake-but-realistic one look almost identical to a next-token predictor — both are exactly what tends to follow.

Why it happens

There is no database being consulted (§0). The exact author/year/journal isn't reliably stored as a retrievable fact; it's reconstructed from statistical pattern. When the pattern is strong but the specific fact is weak, you get the improv actor filling the gap in character — never flagging the uncertainty.

What does NOT fix it

Lowering temperature makes it more confident in the same fabrication, not more truthful (§4). Fine-tuning on tone (§5) is the wrong stage entirely — this is a knowledge problem, not a behavior problem.

The fix

Give it the facts instead of asking it to invent them: retrieval (RAG) — fetch real sources and have the model cite only those; constrain it to answer “not found” when retrieval is empty; and verify any citation programmatically. This is the adaptation ladder in action: the gap is knowledge, so you climb to the rung that supplies knowledge.

Say it back in one breath

“It hallucinated a citation because the model predicts plausible text, and a realistic-looking reference is plausible text — it isn't looking anything up. So I won't fix it with temperature or tone; I'll fix the knowledge gap with retrieval, ground every citation in a fetched source, and verify before display.”

The same trace explains most production surprises: wrong facts → pretraining/knowledge → RAG. Weird format or refusals → post-training/behavior → prompting. Varies run-to-run → sampling + hardware → design for a range, don't assert one string.

Beyond debugging, two structural facts from this lesson will shape decisions all course long. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) is how most frontier models are now built: instead of every parameter firing for every token, a router activates only a few specialist "expert" sub-networks per token — so a model can have huge total capacity (DeepSeek-V3 carries 671B parameters) while only using a small slice (37B) on any given token, keeping it affordable to run. And models are increasingly multimodal: images become patches, audio becomes audio tokens, all projected into the same representation the transformer already processes — so “everything becomes tokens” extends from text to pictures and sound. You don't tune either of these as an app builder, but they explain the capabilities and the prices you'll be choosing between in the model-landscape lesson.

The posture to keep

Treat the model as a capable, slightly unreliable component whose failures have mechanical explanations. When it surprises you, don't shrug — trace it: is this a knowledge gap, a behavior artifact, or just sampling variance? The trace points straight at the fix, and at which rung of the adaptation ladder to climb. That diagnostic habit is worth more than any single fact about transformers.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

One sentence carried the whole lesson: a foundation model maps a token sequence to a probability distribution over the next token, and generation is that map run in a loop. Everything else — attention, sampling, non-determinism, post-training, reasoning — is detail on that sentence.

01Next-token predictorNot a database, not a reasoner. It returns a distribution over the next token, then loops. Plausible ≠ true.
02Output is a distributionThe model ranks every token in a ~100k vocabulary. A separate sampler picks one — that's where randomness enters.
03Attention = relevanceEach token looks at every other and blends in the relevant ones. More context → attention spread thinner.
04Autoregressive loopOne token at a time, each fed back in. Serial and slow; the whole history is re-read every token.
