Three Kinds of Eval

Last lesson left you with a stack of checks but not the checks themselves. There are exactly three ways to decide whether a model's output is good: code can decide, a person can decide, or another model can decide. Each is fast where the others are slow, trustworthy where the others are cheap, and blind where the others see. This lesson is a tour of all three — what each is for, and the bias each one smuggles in.

00 Three deciders

Who gets to decide it's good?

"Is this answer acceptable?" is a judgment, and a judgment needs a judge. There are only three candidates for the job, and the whole field of evaluation is built from them:

Code decides

  • Deterministic / programmatic.
  • Exact match, regex, contains, JSON-schema validation, numeric tolerance, "run the unit tests."
  • Instant, free, perfectly reproducible.
  • But it can only check verifiable things — structure and facts, never quality or tone.

A person decides

  • Human evaluation.
  • An expert or rater scores the output, or picks the better of two.
  • The gold standard — the ground truth everything else is calibrated against.
  • But slow, costly, and hard to scale; people disagree and tire.

A model decides

  • LLM-as-a-judge.
  • A strong model scores the output against a written rubric.
  • Cheap enough to run on thousands of cases; the pragmatic middle.
  • But biased and fallible — you must validate it before you trust it.

None is "the right one." They sit at different points on the same trade-off — cost, scale, and trust — and a real eval suite uses all three: code for everything it can verify, a judge for quality at volume, and humans for the ground truth that anchors the other two. The skill is knowing which question goes to which judge.

01 Same output, three verdicts

Three judges, one answer ▸

Take a single output — a support reply your model just wrote — and hand it to each of the three judges in turn. Watch what each can and can't tell you:

The division of labor falls out naturally. Code answers the parts with a right answer — there is an apology, there is a timeframe — in microseconds, for nothing. It simply has no opinion on whether the tone lands. A human has exactly that opinion and it's the one you trust most, but you can't afford to ask them about all ten thousand replies a day. An LLM judge approximates the human's opinion at the code's scale — the whole reason it's exploded in use — at the price of a bias you have to manage. Three judges, three questions, three costs.

02 Do you have the answer key?

Reference-based vs reference-free

Cutting across those three judges is a second distinction that decides what you can even ask: do you hold a correct answer to compare against?

Reference-based

  • You have a gold answer — an answer key.
  • Score by comparing output to it: exact match, F1, similarity, or a "reference-guided" judge.
  • Natural for classification, extraction, QA, translation.
  • Strong signal — but you must own the correct answers, which is expensive to produce.

Reference-free

  • No gold answer — you score intrinsic quality instead.
  • Measures like faithfulness (are the claims supported by the source?), coherence, relevance, safety.
  • Essential for RAG and agents, where enumerating every right answer is impossible.
  • Cheaper to set up — but the metric itself is a judgment, so it inherits judgment's biases.

The clearest example of reference-free is faithfulness in retrieval-augmented generation: you can't pre-write the one correct paragraph, but you can ask "is every claim in this answer entailed by the retrieved context?" — a quality you score without ever holding a reference answer. Frameworks like RAGAS built a whole reference-free toolkit on exactly that move, which is why RAG and agent evaluation lean heavily reference-free. The rule of thumb: if you can cheaply write down the right answer, go reference-based; if you can't, score the qualities a right answer would have.

03 Inside the model judge

How a judge actually scores ▸

"LLM-as-a-judge" isn't one technique — it's three scoring modes, and picking the right one is most of the battle. Step through them on the same pair of candidate answers:

The practical lesson from the research is blunt: relative is easier than absolute. A model asked "rate this 1–10" gives you a wobbly, drifting number — its sense of what "7" means shifts from call to call. The same model asked "which of these two is better, A or B?" is far more reliable, because comparison is an easier cognitive task than calibrated scoring. So strong eval suites lean on pairwise comparisons (the engine behind public model-ranking arenas), reach for reference-guided grading when a gold answer exists and correctness is sharp (math, code, reasoning), and use bare pointwise scoring sparingly — useful, but the least trustworthy of the three on its own.

Why a judge can work at all

It feels circular — using a model to grade a model. It works because judging is easier than generating. Recognizing that an answer is irrelevant, contradicts its source, or skips the question is a much lower bar than producing the perfect answer in the first place — the same reason you can spot a wrong proof faster than you can write a right one. Strong judges (especially with a clear rubric and chain-of-thought, as in G-Eval) reach high agreement with humans on general tasks. That agreement is the thing you measure and earn, though — never assume it.

04 The fine print

Every judge is biased

No judge is neutral. Each family carries failure modes you have to design around — and naming them is what separates an eval you can trust from a number that lies to you:

  • Deterministic checks are brittle. They mistake surface form for substance — "Paris." fails an exact-match for "Paris" — and n-gram overlap metrics like BLEU/ROUGE correlate only weakly with human judgment on anything generative. They're perfect at structure and useless at quality.
  • Humans are inconsistent. The gold standard still drifts: raters disagree (low inter-annotator agreement), tire over a long session, read an ambiguous rubric two ways, and bring cultural and personal taste. You manage it with multiple raters, agreement metrics, and crisp instructions — but it never fully goes away.
  • LLM judges have signature biases. Four recur often enough to have names:
    • Position bias — in a pairwise test, the judge favors whichever answer it saw first (or last), regardless of content. Swapping order can flip the verdict.
    • Verbosity / length bias — it rewards longer, more fluent answers even when they add nothing.
    • Self-preference — a model tends to rate its own outputs (or those of its family) higher.
    • Sycophancy — if you hint at the answer you want, it tends to agree with you.

The honest summary: a judge is not an oracle, it's a noisy measuring instrument. That's not a reason to avoid it — every instrument is noisy — it's a reason to characterize and correct the noise before you rely on the readings. The next section shows the most common correction in action.

05 Bias, then mitigation

Position bias, defused ▸

Position bias is the easiest judge bias to see and to fix, so make it concrete. You ask a judge to pick the better of two answers, A and B — and then you do one more thing most people skip:

Worked examplebuild & validate a judge
Earning the right to trust a judge

You want an LLM judge to score whether support replies are "helpful and correct," so you can run it on thousands of replies a day instead of reading them by hand. Before you trust a single one of its scores, you validate it the way you'd validate any model.

The alignment loop

v1 — "rate 1–10, helpful?"62%vague rubric, absolute scale, single order

Step 1 — get human labels first

A domain expert hand-labels ~100 real replies as good/bad. This is your answer key for the judge itself — you can't measure a judge's accuracy without ground truth to score it against.

Step 2 — measure the judge vs the labels

v1, the obvious "rate it 1–10" prompt, agrees with the humans only 62% of the time — barely better than a coin flip on a balanced set. If you'd trusted it blind, your eval would be mostly noise.

Step 3 — iterate the judge like a model

Tighten the rubric and add labeled examples (→ 74%). Switch from absolute scoring to pairwise with order-swapping and penalize verbosity (→ 88%). Each change is measured against the same human labels.

Step 4 — only now, scale it

At 88% agreement the judge is a useful instrument, and you let it grade the daily flood — reserving humans for spot-checks and the close calls. Use a different, strong model than the one under test, so self-preference can't inflate the scores.

Say it back in one breath

“A judge is a model, so I validate it like one: I get human labels, measure how often the judge agrees, and iterate the rubric and scoring mode until agreement is high enough to trust. The naive 'rate it 1–10' judge agreed only 62% of the time; pairwise comparison with order-swapping and a real rubric got me to 88% — and I used a different model as the judge so it couldn't favor its own work.”

The discipline that makes a judge trustworthy is the same discipline as the rest of the phase: don't assume quality, measure it — and that includes measuring your measuring instrument.

06 The mindset

Validate the judge before you trust it

The one habit to carry out of this lesson: treat an LLM judge as a model you have to validate, not an oracle you get to assume. The standard workflow is now well-worn — define the criteria, collect a set of human-labeled examples, draft the judge prompt, measure how well the judge agrees with the humans, iterate until the agreement is high enough, and only then let it run at scale. A judge you haven't checked against human labels is generating numbers, not measurements, and numbers that feel rigorous while being wrong are worse than no numbers at all.

And mind the bill. Judge calls multiply fast: every example, times every prompt variant you're comparing, times every run, times the order-swaps and repeated samples you add for reliability. A thorough eval can quietly cost more than the feature it's evaluating. The usual economies — use a cheaper model for the cheap checks, reserve a strong judge for the close and high-stakes calls, sample a subset rather than grading everything, cache what you can — keep evaluation affordable enough that you'll actually run it on every change, which is the only way it helps.

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: there are three judges — code, humans, and models — each trading cost against scale against trust; a real suite uses all three, and the model judge in particular must be validated against human labels before you believe a word it says.
