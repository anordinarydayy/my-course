Embeddings & Vector Databases

Lesson 04 turned text into geometry: an embedding model drops every chunk into a 384-to-3,072-dimensional space where meaning becomes distance, and retrieval becomes “find the nearest vectors to my query.” This lesson is about doing that fast, over millions of vectors — the approximate-nearest-neighbour indexes that turn a linear scan into a handful of hops, the quantization that beats the memory wall, and the one decision that shapes your whole stack: stand up a dedicated vector database, or is Postgres already enough?

00 The bottleneck

The O(N·d) wall

Start from where Lesson 04 left off. You have an embedding model that maps text into a vector — say 1,536 floats — and a corpus of those vectors. A query arrives, you embed it, and now you need the k nearest vectors by cosine or dot product. The obvious implementation writes itself: loop over every stored vector, compute the distance, keep the top k. It is correct, it is exact, and it is exactly what falls over first.

The cost of that loop is O(N·d) per query — you touch all N vectors and do d multiply-adds for each. Put numbers on it: 10M chunks at 1,536 dims is about 15 billion multiply-adds for a single query, and it grows linearly with the corpus. Double the documents, double the work. At a few hundred queries per second under a sub-100ms p95 budget, exact brute force collapses — you simply cannot finish 15 billion operations per query, hundreds of times a second, in time.

There is a second wall, quieter but just as hard. Those same 10M float32 vectors at 1,536 dims are ~61 GB of raw data (10M × 1,536 × 4 bytes). Keeping that in RAM on every replica is expensive; spilling it to disk makes the scan slower still. So the problem has two distinct faces — a compute wall (too many operations) and a memory wall (too many bytes) — and the rest of this lesson is two different families of tricks, one for each. The insight that unlocks both is the same: stop insisting on the exact answer.

The one-line version

Exact nearest-neighbour is a linear scan: O(N·d) compute and O(N·d) bytes. Approximate nearest neighbour (ANN) gives up a sliver of recall to escape both — a few graph hops instead of N comparisons, and compressed vectors instead of raw float32. Everything below is how that bargain is struck and tuned.

01 The bargain

Brute force vs a few hops ▸

The fix is to accept being almost right. An ANN index won’t guarantee the true top-k; it returns a set that is the true top-k the vast majority of the time. We measure exactly how often with recall@k — the fraction of the real top-k neighbours the index actually returns. Production systems deliberately run at 0.90–0.99 recall, never 1.0, because the last fraction of a percent costs far more than it is worth. That greyed-out dot on the right is the whole bargain in one pixel: ANN found 9 of the 10 true neighbours and missed one, and in exchange it did eight hops instead of ten million comparisons. For retrieval feeding a language model, that trade is almost always correct — the model is robust to one slightly-off passage, and you would never pay 1000× the latency to recover it. The next two sections are the two dominant ways to take those few hops.

02 The two families

HNSW & IVF: skipping the scan ▸

Two index families dominate production. The first, and the one most defaults reach for, is HNSW — Hierarchical Navigable Small World. It builds a multi-layer proximity graph (think skip-lists crossed with small-world networks): the top layer is sparse with a few long-range links, and each layer down is denser with shorter links, until layer 0 holds every vector. A search enters at the top, greedily hops toward the query until no neighbour is closer, drops a level, and repeats — turning a linear scan into a logarithmic walk. Step through the descent:

The other family is IVF (inverted file). Instead of a graph, it clusters the vectors around a set of centroids into posting lists; at query time it finds the few centroids nearest the query and scans only those nprobe lists, ignoring the rest of the corpus. It is the same bet — look at a small fraction of the data — reached a different way. The two have real, different trade-offs:

HNSW — graph

  • Recall/latency: excellent for real-time, high-recall workloads; slows only sublinearly as you raise ef_search.
  • Build: heavier — M (links per node) and ef_construction set at build time.
  • Memory: the graph edges cost extra RAM on top of the vectors.
  • Knob: ef_search at query time, no rebuild.

IVF — clusters

  • Recall/latency: scales roughly linearly with nprobe — simpler, more predictable.
  • Build: cheap — just train centroids and assign vectors.
  • Memory: lighter, and pairs naturally with PQ (IVF-PQ).
  • Filtering: often handles metadata-filtered search more gracefully; knob is nprobe.

The short rule: reach for HNSW when you want the best recall-for-latency on live traffic and can afford the build and the RAM; reach for IVF / IVF-PQ when build cost, memory, or heavy filtering matter more. Both are still vector-shaped, though — and 61 GB of them won’t fit cheaply in RAM. That is the memory wall, and it’s next.

03 Beating the memory wall

Quantization & the memory wall

HNSW and IVF cut the compute; neither shrinks the bytes. A graph index over 61 GB of float32 still needs ~61 GB (plus edges) resident to be fast. The fix is quantization: store each vector in fewer bits, accept a coarser distance estimate, and pay a tiny accuracy tax. There’s a ladder of how aggressive you go:

The tiers, concretely: scalar (float32→int8) is 4× smaller for a barely-perceptible recall hit. Product quantization (PQ) splits each vector into sub-vectors and codes each against a small codebook for ~8–64×. Binary (1 bit per dim) is 32× smaller and up to ~40× faster — but naive 1-bit usually craters recall below ~70%, so it is only viable with a rescoring pass: the quantized vectors produce a fast candidate shortlist, then you pull the full-precision vectors for just those few candidates and reorder. The 2024–2025 breakthrough is RaBitQ (Gao & Long), which keeps binary’s 32× compression while preserving high recall via an unbiased distance estimator — Milvus 2.6’s IVF_RABITQ reports ~95% recall at roughly a quarter of the original memory. Qdrant 1.15 went further with asymmetric quantization (binary-stored vectors queried at higher precision) and 1.5-bit / 2-bit modes — a finer memory/recall dial than plain binary.

The pattern that recurs everywhere

Store small in RAM, keep full vectors on disk, rescore the top candidates. Almost every modern engine implements some version of this two-stage shape — it is how you get binary’s memory savings and float32’s recall. Hold this thought for the worked example: it is also exactly what pgvectorscale bolts onto Postgres.

Two more memory tricks close the loop. DiskANN (Microsoft Research’s Vamana graph) keeps the graph on SSD with bounded RAM, indexing ~1B vectors on a single 64 GB box at 95%+ recall and ~5ms latency; SPANN, a memory–disk hybrid inverted index, is reported ~2× faster than DiskANN to reach 90% recall at equal memory. And from the model side, Matryoshka embeddings (MRL) pack coarse-to-fine information so a vector can be truncated to fewer dimensions with graceful degradation — OpenAI’s text-embedding-3 at 256 dims (~8% of 3,072) still beats the old ada-002’s full 1,536 on MTEB, and truncating to ~50% typically costs only 1–4 points of quality. Truncation is the cheapest memory lever of all: a smaller vector before the index even sees it.

04 The query-time knob

The recall–latency dial ▸

Here is the single most useful mental model in this whole lesson. Both index families expose one runtime knob — ef_search for HNSW, nprobe for IVF — that decides how hard the index looks on each query. Turn it up: more candidates examined, higher recall, more latency and CPU. Turn it down: the reverse. It is tuned at query time, with no index rebuild. Drag the dial and watch the two meters move:

The shape is the lesson: recall rises steeply at first — every extra candidate catches neighbours you were missing — then flattens into diminishing returns near 0.99, while latency keeps climbing roughly linearly (steeper for IVF’s nprobe, gentler for HNSW’s ef_search). So you don’t chase 1.0; you find the highest recall that still fits your p95 budget and stop. Practical IVF starting point: nprobe ~4–8, raised until recall is acceptable without blowing p99. Because it’s a query-time dial, you can even set it per request — crank it for a high-stakes query, ease off for a cheap one. This is the recall–latency–memory triangle made adjustable, and it’s the lever you reach for first when a system is too slow or too lossy.

05 Index vs database

What a vector database adds

So far everything has been the algorithm — HNSW, IVF, quantization. A flat ANN library like FAISS gives you exactly that and nothing else: an index in memory, a search() call, and a pile of problems you now own. Where does the vector goes? How do you update one when a document changes? How do you filter to one tenant? What happens when the box restarts? A vector database is the algorithm plus the answers to all of those:

  • Metadata filtering — combine the vector search with predicates: WHERE tenant = X AND date > Y alongside “nearest to this vector.”
  • Hybrid search — fuse dense (semantic) results with sparse keyword/BM25 results, usually via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF). This is the standard fix for vector search’s blind spot on exact IDs, codes, and rare keywords.
  • Persistence & replication — durable storage, restart-safe, replicas for HA and read throughput.
  • Real-time CRUD — upsert and delete single vectors without rebuilding the whole index.
  • Horizontal scaling & multi-tenancy — shard across nodes, isolate tenants, scale out past one machine.

Two of these are deceptively hard, and worth dwelling on. Hybrid search is now table stakes, not a differentiator — Weaviate, Qdrant, and Pinecone all support dense+sparse fusion natively, because pure vectors reliably whiff on “find ticket ABC-1234” or a rare product code. And combining filtering with ANN fights the index itself: pre-filtering (mask out non-matching vectors, then search) can sever the graph’s connectivity so badly that the walk can’t reach enough matches — the dreaded overfiltering, where a tight filter returns too few results. Post-filtering (search, then drop non-matches) wastes work and can return nothing if the top-k all fail the predicate.

Overfiltering, and the fix

The classic failure: a graph index plus a selective filter returns 3 results when you asked for 10, because the ANN walk kept landing on vectors the filter rejected and ran out of road. pgvector 0.8 (late 2024/2025) tackled this with iterative index scans (relaxed_order / strict_order) that keep pulling more candidates until the filter is satisfied — reported up to ~9× faster on filtered queries on Aurora. Qdrant and Weaviate are likewise singled out for strong filtered-search performance. If filtering matters to you (and in multi-tenant RAG it always does), test it explicitly — it’s where naive setups break.

One frontier worth a flag: multi-vector / late-interaction retrieval (ColBERT-style token-level embeddings) trades more storage for higher-quality matching, and engines are starting to ship native multi-vector indexes for it. It’s a sign of where retrieval quality is heading — but for most systems today, dense ANN plus hybrid plus good filtering is the workhorse. Which leaves the question that decides your architecture: which store?

06 The decision

Postgres, dedicated, or serverless

The naive instinct in 2026 is to reach for a dedicated vector database by reflex. Often that’s the wrong move — it adds a second system to operate, sync, and keep consistent with your source-of-truth data. The right question is scale and what you already run. Three roads:

The 2025–2026 shape worth internalising: the object-storage-first / serverless tier is the cost story of the moment. Turbopuffer, LanceDB, and Pinecone serverless decouple storage from compute — cold vectors park on S3 at ~2¢/GB instead of ~60¢/GB of RAM, and you pay for query compute only on access (Cursor, Notion, and Linear are real adopters). Meanwhile pgvector momentum is real: with v0.8 iterative scans fixing filtered search and pgvectorscale’s StreamingDiskANN pushing into the tens of millions, “just use Postgres” has become a defensible default — and the bar for standing up a separate vector DB has risen accordingly. Let’s make the decision concrete.

Worked examplechoose a store · two scenarios
Same question, two answers: which store?

Two teams ask you the same thing — “which vector store?” Scenario A: 10M chunks, rich metadata filters, <100ms p95, hundreds of QPS. Scenario B: 200k chunks already living in a Postgres table, modest traffic. The answer is opposite in each — and the deciding factor is scale and what they already run.

Read the numbers before reaching for a tool

Scenario A — the bottleneck bites

10M × 1,536 dims is ~15B multiply-adds per query; exact search is dead on arrival under a 100ms p95 at hundreds of QPS. This needs a real ANN engine. Pick an HNSW-based dedicated store that does filtered + hybrid search well — Qdrant, Weaviate, Milvus, or Pinecone serverless.

Scenario A — tame both walls

Beat the memory wall with quantization: keep scalar/binary (or RaBitQ) vectors in RAM, full-precision on disk, and rescore the top candidates — roughly 4×–32× less RAM. Beat latency with the dial: set ef_search so recall@10 lands ~0.95–0.98 inside the p95 budget. Push filters into the index (don’t post-filter and overfilter), and add hybrid (dense + BM25 via RRF) so exact IDs aren’t missed. Payoff: ~6–10 hops instead of an O(N) scan, single-digit-to-low-teens millisecond p99 at ~0.97 recall.

Scenario B — don’t build a second system

200k vectors is tiny — the bottleneck never bites. Standing up a dedicated store here buys you a sync pipeline and an ops burden for nothing. Add the pgvector extension, store the embedding beside the row it describes, build an HNSW index, and you’re done. Filters are just SQL WHERE clauses in the same query.

Scenario B — the payoff is consistency

Vectors and relational data stay transactionally consistent: update a document and its embedding in one transaction, no eventual-consistency gap, no second source of truth. You only graduate to pgvectorscale (StreamingDiskANN + binary quant) or a dedicated store if you cross tens of millions of vectors or hit a latency wall.

Scenario B — vectors live next to the rows they describeCREATE EXTENSION vector;
ALTER TABLE chunks ADD COLUMN embedding vector(1536);
CREATE INDEX ON chunks USING hnsw (embedding vector_cosine_ops);

-- one query does filter + similarity, transactionally
SELECT id, body
FROM   chunks
WHERE  tenant_id = 42 AND created_at > now() - interval '90 days'
ORDER BY embedding <=> :query_vec   -- cosine distance, HNSW-accelerated
LIMIT  10;
Say it back in one breath

“The store is decided by scale and what I already run. Ten million chunks under a 100ms budget needs a dedicated HNSW engine with quantization and the ef_search dial tuned to ~0.97 recall, plus filtering and hybrid pushed into the index. Two hundred thousand chunks already in Postgres needs nothing new — pgvector keeps the vectors transactional with the rows, and one SQL query does filter plus similarity. The O(N·d) bottleneck only bites once N is large; below that, ‘Postgres is enough’ is the right call.”

Match the tool to the scale. Reach for a dedicated store when the numbers force it — not by reflex.

The phase ahead

You now have the index layer: from an embedding (Lesson 04) to a searchable ANN store, the recall–latency–memory dial, and a defensible choice of engine. Next, retrieval quality moves up a level — getting the right text into the index (chunking) and the right results out of it (reranking and fusion), then evaluating the whole pipeline with exactly the gate machinery of Lesson 14. A fast index that retrieves the wrong passages is still wrong; the next lessons close that gap.

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: exact nearest-neighbour is an O(N·d) scan that hits a compute wall and a memory wall; ANN indexes (HNSW, IVF) trade a sliver of recall for a few hops, quantization trades precision for bytes, one query-time dial trades recall for latency — and the store you pick is decided by scale and what you already run.
