Evaluating Retrieval Quality

Your RAG bot just failed the end-to-end answer gate from Lesson 13. That single score tells you the patient is sick — not what's wrong. Did retrieval fail to surface the right chunk, or did the model get the right chunk and botch the answer? You can't improve retrieval you can't see, and you can't see it through a final-answer number. This lesson resolves that by decomposing the eval into the two stages a RAG pipeline actually has, measuring each independently, and turning the verdict into a CI gate — closing Phase 3 by looping the whole retrieval stack back into the Evaluation phase.

00 The opaque score

One bad number, two suspects

You did everything Phase 2 asked. You chunked the corpus, embedded it, wired a vector index, fetched top-k, and stuffed the context into the prompt. Then you ran the whole-system eval from Lesson 13 against your golden set, and the verdict came back ugly: human raters score roughly 40% of answers wrong. The gate is red. And now you're stuck, because that number is opaque — it condemns the pipeline without naming the culprit.

Here's why a single end-to-end score can't fix you. A RAG pipeline has two distinct stages, and the failure could live in either. The retriever might never have surfaced the chunk that holds the answer — in which case the model was doomed before it generated a token. Or the retriever did its job, handed over the perfect chunk, and the generator ignored it, fabricated around it, or answered a different question. Same red score, opposite root causes, opposite fixes. Pour weeks into prompt engineering when the real fault was retrieval and you'll move the number not at all.

The insight that breaks the deadlock is almost embarrassingly simple once named: stop scoring only the final answer. Score the two stages separately. Measure whether retrieval put the right evidence on the table, and measure whether generation used that evidence honestly. Two numbers instead of one, and suddenly the fault has an address.

The one-line version

An end-to-end answer score collapses two failure modes into one number. Decompose the eval into a retrieval stage (did we fetch the right chunk?) and a generation stage (did we use it faithfully?), measure each on its own, and the aggregate's mystery becomes a diagnosis. Everything else in this lesson is the metrics, the diagram, and the decision tree that make the decomposition operational.

01 Split the pipeline

Decompose: retrieval vs generation

The two stages are different kinds of problem, so they want different toolkits. Retrieval is an information-retrieval (IR) problem: out of a corpus, did the top-k results contain the documents that matter? That's a question decades of search research already answers with rank metrics. Generation is a grounding problem: given some context, did the model produce claims that actually follow from it? That's a natural-language inference question, judged today by an LLM. Measuring them with each other's tools is the classic category error — you can't catch a missing chunk with a faithfulness score, and you can't catch a hallucination with recall@k.

Retrieval stage

  • Job: surface the chunks that contain the answer in the top-k.
  • Failure mode: the answer-bearing chunk is absent or buried — the model never sees it.
  • Measured with: IR metrics — recall@k, precision@k, hit-rate, MRR, nDCG.
  • Golden data: each query mapped to its relevant chunk IDs.
  • Fix lever: chunking, k, embeddings, hybrid search, reranking.

Generation stage

  • Job: answer the question using only the retrieved context.
  • Failure mode: ignores the context, fabricates, or answers the wrong question.
  • Measured with: the RAG triad — faithfulness, answer relevance, context relevance.
  • Golden data: often none — an LLM judges reference-free.
  • Fix lever: grounding prompt, output format, model choice.

The principle that falls out is an invariant you'll lean on for the rest of the lesson: every RAG failure is, at bottom, either a retrieval failure or a generation failure, and the two are independently measurable. Hold that and the rest is just naming the metrics. We start with the IR side, because it has the cleaner math — and a slider that makes its central trade-off impossible to forget.

02 IR on a golden set

Retrieval metrics & the k trade-off ▸

Retrieval metrics have one hard prerequisite: a golden set that maps each query to its relevant chunk IDs. This is the same machinery you built in Lesson 12 — except instead of labeling the correct answer, you label which chunks contain it. Without those ground-truth relevance labels you literally cannot compute recall@k; there is no truth to compare the top-k against. Reuse the golden-set discipline, change the label, and retrieval becomes measurable.

The two workhorse metrics are mirror images:

  • recall@k = (relevant chunks in the top k) ÷ (total relevant chunks). The "did we even fetch it?" metric. If recall is low, the right evidence never reached the model.
  • precision@k = (relevant chunks in the top k) ÷ k. The noise metric. If precision is low, the model has to find signal in a pile of distractors.

Three more round out the IR toolkit. hit-rate@k is the binary version — was there at least one relevant chunk in the top k (FAQ bots often want ~0.9). MRR (mean reciprocal rank) averages 1÷rank of the first relevant chunk: rank 1 scores 1.0, rank 3 scores 0.33 — it rewards putting truth at the top. nDCG@k is rank-discounted (logarithmic) and supports graded relevance, so a perfectly-ranked list scores 1.0 and good chunks buried deep are penalized. All range 0–1.

Now the trade-off that governs everything. Slide k upward and recall climbs while precision falls — you fetch more truth and more noise. Watch it move, then watch how reranking dissolves the dilemma:

The recipe the slider argues for is now industry-standard: fetch wide for recall, then rerank for precision. Cast a recall-oriented net of ~50 candidates with a hybrid retriever (BM25 keyword search fused with vector similarity via reciprocal-rank fusion, so an exact-term hit and a semantic hit both surface), then run a cross-encoder reranker to reorder and cut down to the top ~5. Reranking commonly buys a 20–40% faithfulness lift downstream, because the generator finally sees a clean, relevant context window. Target bands people cite in mid-2026: recall@k around 0.8+ for critical queries, MRR > 0.7, nDCG@10 > 0.8, hit-rate@10 ~0.9 for FAQ bots. Hit those and the retrieval stage is no longer your suspect — which sends us to the other half of the pipeline.

03 Judging generation

The RAG triad ▸

Retrieval metrics need labeled chunks. Generation metrics often need nothing — and that's what makes them runnable in CI on every query, not just the labeled few. The dominant framing is the RAG triad (from TruLens, with Snowflake's groundedness judge), three reference-free scores computed by an LLM-as-judge over the (question, context, answer) triple:

  • Context relevance — is the retrieved context on-topic for the question? Catches a retriever that pulled junk. (Question ↔ Context.)
  • Faithfulness / groundedness — is every claim in the answer supported by that context? A hallucination check. (Context ↔ Answer.)
  • Answer relevance — does the answer actually address the question asked? Catches an evasive or off-topic reply. (Question ↔ Answer.)

The subtle, load-bearing point is what faithfulness is not. Faithfulness measures groundedness, not correctness. Modern implementations (RAGAS, DeepEval, Phoenix) decompose the answer into atomic claims and check each one for entailment against the context, NLI-style — claim-level scoring has replaced the old holistic "is this grounded?" prompt. So an answer can score a perfect 1.0 on faithfulness while being flat wrong: it faithfully reported what the retrieved chunk said, and the chunk was the wrong chunk. Faithful to what? is the question that keeps you honest. Watch each corner of the triad break in turn, and notice how the fault localizes:

These judges are reliable enough to gate CI, which is the whole reason they matter. RAGAS faithfulness correlates above 0.8 (Pearson) with human ratings; Snowflake's groundedness judge reports F1 around 0.81 and Cohen's κ ~0.54 (moderate-to-substantial agreement) on grounded-fact benchmarks. The 2025–2026 tool shelf is crowded and converging: RAGAS (metric-rich offline evals), TruLens (origin of the triad, tracing + feedback functions), DeepEval (pytest-style, the strongest CI/CD story), Arize Phoenix (tracing/observability), and ARES (fine-tunes a lightweight judge from a few hundred human labels plus prediction-powered inference, claiming robustness under domain shift). MLflow now ships RAGAS, DeepEval, and Phoenix scorers natively — a sign RAG eval has moved from bespoke scripts into mainstream MLOps.

A 2026 caution: smarter is not always more faithful

Don't assume a bigger model fixes groundedness. On Vectara's grounded-summarization hallucination leaderboard (HHEM), the best models sit near ~0.7–1.5% hallucination (illustratively, a Gemini-2.0-Flash-class model ~0.7%, a GPT-4o-class model ~1.5%) — yet several 2025-era reasoning models score worse, exceeding 10% on long-document grounded tasks, apparently reasoning their way past the context. The faithfulness number is the check precisely because "use the smarter model" is an unreliable fix. And citation accuracy is its own frontier: without explicit attribution training, cited sources actually support the claim only ~65–70% of the time, which is why 2025 research now separates citation correctness (the source supports the claim) from citation faithfulness (the model genuinely relied on it, rather than post-rationalizing).

04 Two metric families

Retrieval vs generation metrics

Put the two families side by side. The column that decides how often a metric can run in CI is "reference-free?" — reference-free judges run on every production query; metrics that need a labeled reference run only against your golden set. That asymmetry is exactly why generation metrics became the always-on layer and retrieval metrics the offline gate.

A practitioner rule of thumb to keep the faithfulness target honest: faithfulness below ~0.85 implies roughly 1 in 7 responses carries an unsupported claim — a hallucination shipped to a user. For anything customer-facing, that's the line you defend.

Now the punchline of the whole lesson, the diagnostic 2×2. Cross retrieval recall against faithfulness and every cell prescribes a different fix — from a single end-to-end score you could never read this:

The two off-diagonal cells are the lesson in miniature: low recall + high faithfulness and high recall + low faithfulness can produce the identical bad end-to-end score, yet they demand opposite fixes. The decomposed numbers, not the aggregate, are what tell them apart. The decision tree in the next section walks that fork in motion.

05 The fork in the road

The diagnostic decision tree ▸

Here is the decomposition as a procedure you can run on any failing answer. Two yes/no questions, asked in order, route the same red symptom to opposite cures. Step through it, then make it concrete with a worked case:

Worked exampleHR policy bot · decomposed eval
The bot that was faithful to the wrong page

An internal HR-policy RAG bot is failing the end-to-end answer gate from Lesson 13 — humans rate ~40% of answers wrong, and the aggregate gives no clue why. The team extends the golden set they built in Lesson 12 so each of 50 evaluation queries now maps to the chunk IDs that actually contain the answer. Two suspects, one fork to walk.

Decompose, then read the two numbers together

decomposed eval report — HR policy bot# Stage 1 — retrieval, scored against golden chunk IDs
recall@5            = 0.45   # answer chunk misses top-5 over half the time
MRR                 = 0.31   # first relevant hit ranks ~3rd on average

# Stage 2 — generation, RAGAS / TruLens (reference-free judge)
faithfulness        = 0.93   # answers ARE grounded in what was retrieved
answer_relevance    = 0.88   # and they DO address the question
Read the signals together

The model is answering faithfully and relevantly — but from whatever chunks it was handed, which less than half the time are the right ones. High faithfulness over low recall means the bot is faithful to the wrong context.

Verdict: retrieval, not generation

Walk the tree: was the right chunk retrieved? recall@5 = 0.45 → NO → RETRIEVAL problem. Pouring effort into prompt engineering or a bigger model would have moved the end-to-end score not at all; the diagnosis says fix the fetch.

Fix the fetch, watch the score follow

Switch fixed-size chunking to section-aware semantic chunking; add a recall-oriented hybrid (BM25 + vector) fetch of ~50 candidates; cross-encoder rerank down to top 5. recall@5 rises to 0.86 and the end-to-end answer score climbs with it.

Now flip the verdict

Imagine instead recall@5 = 0.90 but faithfulness = 0.55. Same bad end-to-end score — opposite diagnosis: the right chunk WAS retrieved and the model ignored or fabricated around it → GENERATION problem → tighten the grounding prompt ("answer only from the context, cite chunk IDs, say I-don't-know if absent"), raise precision so noise doesn't distract, or change the model.

Say it back in one breath

“The aggregate said 'broken' but not why. So I decomposed: retrieval recall@5 was 0.45 while faithfulness was 0.93 — the bot was answering honestly from the wrong chunks, so it's a retrieval problem, not generation. I fixed chunking and added hybrid + reranking, recall jumped to 0.86, and the answer score followed. Flip the numbers — high recall, low faithfulness — and the very same bad score becomes a generation problem instead. Only the decomposed metrics told me which knob to turn.”

And both numbers now live as CI gates (Lesson 14) over the golden set, so the regression can't silently creep back.

06 Loop back to the gates

Closing Phase 3: retrieval as a gate

Decomposition gives you numbers; the Evaluation phase gives you the discipline to defend them. This is where Phase 3 loops back into Phase 2's gates from Lessons 10–14. Every chunking change, every k, every embedding or reranker swap now runs against the golden set as a required CI gate — exactly the "no eval, no merge" machinery of Lesson 14, just with retrieval metrics as the assertion. recall@k and faithfulness become numbers you can regression-test, and a clever-looking chunking refactor that quietly drops recall is blocked before it ships, the same way Lesson 14 blocked a worse prompt.

In production this settles into the three-tier eval architecture that's becoming standard for 2026 RAG: (1) offline RAGAS/DeepEval on a golden set that blocks deploys; (2) a canary LLM-as-judge pass over ~1k production queries before full rollout; and (3) continuous monitoring on 1–5% of sampled live traffic. It's the gate → canary → harvest loop of Lesson 14, now wrapped around the retriever as well as the model — and because the generation judges are reference-free, tiers 2 and 3 can run on real traffic with no labels at all.

The invariant to carry forward

You can't improve retrieval you don't measure, and a final-answer score doesn't measure it. Decompose into retrieval (IR metrics on a golden chunk-labeled set) and generation (the RAG triad), localize the fault with the decision tree, then gate both. That's the through-line of this entire course: define good (L10), measure it (L11–L13), enforce it (L14), and now point that same instrument at every piece of the retrieval stack.

That closes Phase 3. You can now build a retriever and prove which part of it works, with metrics that survive contact with non-determinism and live as gates that never sleep. Next, Phase 4 — Agents hands the model tools, memory, and multi-step plans — and every new degree of freedom is a new place to mis-step, which means a new thing to evaluate. The instrument you just finished building is exactly what you'll read to keep agents honest.

Summary & self-test
