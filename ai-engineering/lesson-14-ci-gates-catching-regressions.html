CI Gates & Catching Regressions

You have a golden set, a scorecard, and the discipline to run them. The last step is to stop relying on discipline. This lesson wires evaluation into CI — so a prompt edit or a model swap that makes the product worse is blocked before it ships, automatically, the same way a failing unit test blocks a merge. It's how everything in this phase becomes a gate that runs itself.

00 The gap in green

Green CI, a worse product

Picture a pull request that edits one line of a system prompt. It runs through your CI: the linter passes, the type-checker passes, every unit test passes, the build is green, the deploy succeeds. By every signal your pipeline knows how to emit, this change is safe. And the product is now measurably worse at its actual job — the new wording quietly dropped a constraint, and answers degraded on a third of real cases.

This is the gap. A traditional CI pipeline verifies that the code runs; it has no opinion on whether the AI works. A free-text prompt has an invisible blast radius — its effects are statistical, delayed, and spread across inputs no assertion checks — so a regression sails straight through a wall of green checkmarks. The fix is the obvious one once you name the gap: add a check that actually measures product quality, and make it required, exactly like the unit tests. Run the golden set on every change, and refuse to merge anything that regresses.

The one-line version

"No eval, no merge." The eval gate is to AI quality what the unit-test gate is to code correctness: a required status check that turns "I think this is fine" into "the numbers say this is fine, on cases we trust." Everything else in this lesson is making that gate reliable despite a model's non-determinism.

01 The required check

The eval gate on a PR ▸

Make it concrete. A teammate opens a PR that tweaks the prompt. Watch the difference between a pipeline that only knows "did it crash?" and one with an eval gate that knows "did it get worse?":

The gate's output isn't a bare pass/fail, either — a good one posts back to the PR which cases regressed, with the before/after outputs side by side. That turns code review of an AI change from a gut-feel guess ("the new prompt reads nicer to me") into reading a measured delta ("this drops 6 points, here are the four cases it broke"). The reviewer is now looking at evidence. And because it's a required check, no amount of "it's a tiny change, ship it" can route around the measurement.

02 The flicker problem

Flaky evals & non-determinism

There's a catch that breaks the naive version of this gate, and it's the same property that started the whole phase: models are non-deterministic. Run the exact same eval twice — same prompt, same cases, even at temperature zero — and the scores won't be identical. So a gate built on strict equality, or on a single run's pass/fail, flickers: it goes red on a change that's actually fine, purely by chance. And a flaky gate is worse than no gate, because the team learns to ignore red — and then ignores the red that was real.

The fixes all push in one direction: turn a noisy point measurement into a stable statistical one.

  • Threshold, don't equate. Assert a floor ("faithfulness ≥ 0.85"), never byte-equality with a fixed string. You're checking a level, not pinning an output.
  • Pass-rate over many cases. Score the fraction of the golden set that passes, not each case in isolation. One case's coin-flip averages out across a hundred.
  • Sample and aggregate. For high-variance cases, run each a few times (a handful of trials) and use the aggregate, so one unlucky generation doesn't decide the verdict.
  • Pin what you can. Set temperature low or zero for eval runs, and prefer cheap deterministic checks over an LLM-judge in the gate wherever a deterministic check is possible — less noise, less cost.
  • Gate on the confidence bound. Fail only when the lower end of the score's confidence interval crosses the threshold, so normal wobble doesn't trip it but a true drop does.

The mantra from people who've shipped this: you can't assert your way out of non-determinism. You manage it statistically. Which leaves one decision that matters more than any other — where to put the threshold.

03 The dial that decides everything

Setting the threshold ▸

The threshold is the single dial that separates a gate people trust from one they route around. Set it wrong in either direction and the gate is useless. Watch the same five wobbling eval runs under three threshold choices:

The rule that falls out: set the threshold at baseline minus a tolerance margin sized to your measured run-to-run noise — tight enough to catch a real regression, loose enough to absorb the wobble. It's a product and risk decision, not a purely technical one: a medical assistant runs a narrow margin and accepts the occasional flaky re-run; a casual tool runs a wider one to keep the pipeline smooth. Start conservative, watch how often it fires, and tune. The threshold is where you encode "how much worse is unacceptable," and that's a judgment only you can make.

04 Layers of gate

What gates on what

"The eval gate" is really several gates with different jobs. A mature pipeline stacks them:

  • Regression gate — don't drop below the current baseline (minus tolerance). This is the workhorse: it catches a change that makes things worse than today, which is most of what you fear.
  • Absolute-threshold gate — must clear a fixed bar to ship at all (e.g. "safety pass-rate ≥ 99%, no exceptions"). For dimensions where "better than yesterday" isn't good enough and only an absolute floor will do.
  • Per-category gate — no single category or user segment is allowed to collapse, even if the aggregate rises. This is the direct fix for the masked regression of Lesson 12, where overall accuracy held at 92% while refunds quietly fell to 61%. Gate each category, not just the mean.
  • Promote-the-baseline (the ratchet) — when a change genuinely improves the score, the new score becomes the bar that future PRs must clear. Quality only moves one direction; every gain is locked in and can't silently erode later.
The ratchet is the whole point

A regression gate alone keeps you from getting worse. The promote-the-baseline ratchet is what makes you reliably get better: each improvement raises the floor, so progress accumulates instead of sloshing back and forth. Combined with "every failure becomes a case" from Lesson 12, you get a system that monotonically climbs — new cases raise coverage, new baselines raise the bar, and nothing you fixed is allowed to break again.

05 Beyond the merge

Gate → canary → harvest ▸

The pre-merge gate is necessary but not sufficient — offline cases can't anticipate everything real traffic throws at you. So the gate is the first stage of a longer pipeline that keeps measuring after the merge and feeds what it learns back to the start. Step through the full loop:

Worked examplesilent model swap · scheduled gate
The regression that fired at 3 a.m.

You didn't open a PR. You didn't touch a line. Your provider auto-migrated the model behind a stable version label overnight — the exact silent swap from Lesson 10. Nothing in your repo changed, so a PR-triggered gate never ran. What catches it?

A gate that runs on a schedule, not just on PRs

eval-gate.yml — runs on every PR AND nightlyon:
  pull_request:        # catch changes you make
  schedule:
    - cron: "0 3 * * *"  # catch changes THEY make

# gate: pass-rate must hold within tolerance of baseline
assert: overall_pass_rate >= baseline - 0.03
assert: faithfulness        >= 0.85   # absolute floor
assert: every_category      >= baseline_cat - 0.05
The trap is already set

Because the same eval gate runs on a nightly schedule, not only on pull requests, it re-scores production behavior even when your code is frozen. The golden set, the thresholds, the per-category checks — all of it runs against whatever model the API is serving right now.

3 a.m. — the swap lands

The provider routes your stable label to a successor model. The nightly run scores it: overall pass-rate falls from 91% to 83%, and faithfulness dips below the 0.85 floor. Two assertions fail.

The gate pages you, the swap is contained

The scheduled run goes red and alerts on-call — with the regressed cases attached. You pin the explicit old model version (or fast-follow with a prompt fix), and the degraded behavior is rolled back before the morning's users feel it. The "felt different" complaint never gets filed.

Say it back in one breath

“A model swap I didn't make and couldn't see was caught by a gate that runs on a schedule, not just on my PRs — because the regression came from the provider, not my repo. Overall pass-rate and faithfulness crossed their thresholds, the nightly run went red and paged me, and I pinned the old version before users noticed. The gate doesn't care whether the change was mine.”

Pre-merge gates catch your regressions; scheduled/online gates catch theirs. The silent swap from Lesson 10 is only silent if nothing is listening — so something always is.

06 The mindset

Evals are the new CI

The frame that closes the phase: treat evaluation the way you treat testing — as infrastructure that runs itself, gates every change, and is never optional. "Evals are the new unit tests" was the slogan of Lesson 10; this lesson is its payoff. The golden set is your test suite, the threshold is your assertion, the PR gate is your required check, and the scheduled run is your watch against changes you didn't make. None of it depends on a person remembering to look.

Two cautions keep the gate alive in practice. First, keep it fast and cheap, or people will route around it: run a small, cheap, mostly-deterministic smoke suite on every commit, and the full judge-heavy suite nightly or pre-release — a gate that takes an hour and a fortune is a gate that gets disabled. Second, remember that CI evals and runtime guardrails are different jobs: the gate measures quality offline before shipping; guardrails block bad outputs online as they happen. You want both, and neither replaces the other. Get this right and quality stops being something you hope for between releases — it becomes a property your pipeline enforces on every change, yours and the model provider's alike.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: wire the golden set into CI as a required, statistically-robust gate — run it on every change and on a schedule, threshold it at baseline-minus-tolerance, gate per category, ratchet the baseline up — so no regression, yours or the provider's, can ship silently.
