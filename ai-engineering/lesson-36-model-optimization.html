Model Optimization

Phase 6 — Inference Optimization: Latency & Cost — opens with the bluntest lever of all. The cheapest tokens don’t come from a clever cache or a faster kernel; they come from a smaller model. “Model optimization” means shrinking the model itself — changing its weights or its architecture — as opposed to the serving tricks that make a fixed model run faster (that’s the next lesson). Three families do the shrinking: quantization (fewer bits per weight), distillation (train a smaller student), and pruning (remove weights outright). Each trades a little quality for a lot of cost and latency — and, crucially, they stack.

00 The lever

The cheapest token comes from a smaller model

You have a model that passed your evals in Phase 2. It behaves. But at scale two numbers hurt: the latency per response and the bill per million tokens. The naive reflexes are to reach for a smaller off-the-shelf model — and fall off a quality cliff you already ruled out — or to throw more GPUs at the problem and pay for it. The better question is narrower: keep the exact behavior you validated, and shrink the thing that produces it.

There are only two places to cut. You can shrink the model — make the weights fewer, or smaller, or coarser — which is this lesson. Or you can optimize how you serve a fixed model — batching, caching, speculative decoding, routing — which is the next lesson. They are genuinely different levers, and keeping them separate keeps you honest: model optimization changes the artifact on disk; serving optimization changes the machinery around it. They compose beautifully, but if you blur them you’ll double-count savings or blame the wrong thing for a quality change.

Model optimization has exactly three families, each attacking a different axis. Quantization stores (and sometimes computes) each weight in fewer bits — same shape, coarser numbers. Distillation trains a smaller student network to reproduce a bigger teacher’s behavior — fewer parameters, learned from scratch. Pruning deletes weights — whole heads, layers, or channels — leaving a physically smaller network. Different axes (bits, parameters, structure) means they don’t fight; you can do all three to the same model and multiply the wins.

Shrink the model — this lesson

  • Quantization — fewer bits per weight (FP16 → INT8 → INT4, FP8/FP4).
  • Distillation — a small student trained to mimic a big teacher.
  • Pruning — remove weights: heads, layers, channels.
  • Changes the weights / architecture themselves; the artifact gets smaller.

Optimize serving — next lesson (L37)

  • PagedAttention / vLLM, continuous batching.
  • Speculative decoding (EAGLE-3, Medusa).
  • Prompt caching (~90% off cached input) and routers (RouteLLM).
  • The model is unchanged; only the machinery around it changes.
The one-line version

The cheapest tokens come from a smaller model — and there are three ways to make one: quantize it, distil it, or prune it. Everything in this lesson is a way of trading a point or two of quality for a large, durable cut in memory, latency, and cost. Serving tricks are real and important, but they belong to a different lever.

01 Fewer bits

Quantization: fewer bits per weight ▸

The first and cheapest family is quantization: store each weight in fewer bits. A weight that was an FP16 number (2 bytes) becomes INT8 (1 byte), or INT4 (half a byte), or on the newest accelerators an FP8 or FP4 float. Nothing about the network’s shape changes — the same layers, the same parameter count — only the precision of the numbers. It sounds like it should only save memory. It does more, and understanding why is the whole point.

Autoregressive decode — generating one token at a time — is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. To produce a single token the accelerator has to stream every weight (and the whole KV cache) out of HBM and through the tensor cores; at batch size one the math finishes long before the bytes arrive. So the bottleneck is bytes moved per token. Halve the bits and you roughly halve the bytes streamed each step — which cuts decode latency roughly in proportion and frees the VRAM. That is the load-bearing idea: weight quantization is a speed win at batch one because it’s a bandwidth win, and a compute win at large batch because low-precision tensor cores (FP8/FP4) do the math faster too. The numbers are illustrative but the ratios are the mechanism: FP16 → INT4 is 2 bytes → 0.5 byte, about 4× less VRAM and ~4× fewer bytes per token in bandwidth-bound decode; INT8 sits at 2×. Prefill (processing the prompt) is compute-bound instead, so it benefits mainly from the low-precision math that FP8 and FP4 tensor cores provide, not from the bandwidth cut. Two knobs, one family — and both point the same way: fewer bits, less to move, less to store.

Why it speeds up, in one sentence

At batch one, decode spends its time waiting for weights to arrive from memory, so cutting the bytes per weight cuts the wait almost linearly — quantization is a latency win, not just a memory win. At large batch, the same low precision also lets the tensor cores compute faster. Either way, fewer bits is fewer nanoseconds.

02 How to quantize

PTQ, QAT, and the march to FP4

There are two ways to quantize, and they differ by whether you retrain. Post-training quantization (PTQ) takes a finished model and re-encodes it: calibrate on a few hundred samples, compute per-channel scales, done in minutes with no gradient updates. The two dominant INT4 weight-only methods are GPTQ (Hessian-based, it compensates the rounding error of each weight against the others) and AWQ (activation-aware — it notices that ~1% of weight channels are “salient” and scales them so quantization doesn’t crush them). As of 2026, AWQ is the de-facto default INT4 format for production serving, with optimized kernels (Marlin, Marlin-AWQ) shipping across vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM; GPTQ is a close second, slightly behind on average. Most teams never quantize anything themselves — they pull a pre-quantized checkpoint off Hugging Face.

Quantization-aware training (QAT) is the heavier option: simulate the low-precision rounding during training or fine-tuning so the model learns weights that survive it. It costs a training run but recovers the most accuracy at aggressive bit-widths (INT4, FP4) and on small models — exactly where PTQ starts to struggle. A second axis cuts across both: weight-only quantization (e.g. W4A16 — 4-bit weights, FP16 activations) only shrinks the weights, while weight+activation quantization (W8A8) also quantizes the activations so it can feed genuine low-precision tensor cores. Weight-only is easier and the mainstay of local INT4; W8A8 is how FP8 and FP4 turn into compute wins.

PTQ — post-training

  • No retraining. Calibrate on a small set; apply in minutes.
  • GPTQ (error compensation), AWQ (protect salient channels).
  • Astonishingly good on large models; the everyday default.
  • Frays on small models at INT4 — that’s QAT’s cue.

QAT — quantization-aware

  • Retrains with the rounding simulated in the loop.
  • Recovers the most accuracy at INT4 / FP4 and on small models.
  • Costs a training run — reach for it when PTQ isn’t enough.
  • For NVFP4, a well-tuned GPTQ is often already competitive.

The precision floor keeps dropping. FP8 (E4M3 for weights and activations) as W8A8 is widely reported near-lossless across model scales and is a safe production default on H100/H200/Ada — illustratively ~1.4–1.8× the throughput of BF16 on an H100, since the card quotes roughly double the tensor-core peak at FP8. FP4 is the 2025–2026 frontier on Blackwell. Both FP4 formats are OCP e2m1 4-bit floats (1 sign, 2 exponent, 1 mantissa) sharing a per-block scale — “microscaling.” They differ in the fine print:

Pitfall: outliers and the layers you must not crush

Transformers have a handful of activation channels with ~1000× the range of the rest; a naive per-tensor scheme lets those outliers dominate the error. That’s the whole reason AWQ (protect salient channels), SmoothQuant-style scaling, and finer blocks (NVFP4’s block-16) exist — and why quantizing activations is harder than weights. Keep the sensitive parts higher-precision on purpose: embeddings, the LM head, the first/last layers, and (in an MoE) the router are outlier-prone. And don’t assume a recipe that’s lossless on a 70B transfers to a 3B — small models degrade sharply at INT4, which is exactly where QAT earns its keep.

03 A smaller student

Distillation: a small student mimics a big teacher ▸

Quantization keeps the same network and coarsens its numbers. Distillation does something more ambitious: it trains a genuinely smaller network — the student — to reproduce the behavior of a big teacher. Instead of learning only from hard labels (“the next token is cat”), the student learns from the teacher’s soft output — the full probability distribution over every token — which carries far more information than a single winner. Done well, the student keeps most of the teacher’s quality at a fraction of the parameters. This is how the small, cheap, fast model tiers you buy are actually minted, and it’s the “fine-tune for latency” idea from Phase 5 made concrete.

Classic knowledge distillation is off-policy: run the teacher over a fixed dataset, minimize the forward-KL between student and teacher distributions. It imitates well but suffers exposure bias — the student trains on the teacher’s text yet at inference must continue its own, and the mismatch compounds. The 2024–2025 upgrade is on-policy distillation: let the student generate its own rollouts and have the teacher grade them. Training on the student’s real output distribution closes that gap and raises its ceiling. It is now the dominant paradigm for minting capable small tiers.

Off-policy KD

  • Match the teacher’s soft logits on a static dataset.
  • Forward-KL; simple and cheap to run.
  • Risks exposure bias (train / inference mismatch).

On-policy distillation

  • Teacher grades the student’s own rollouts.
  • Trains on the distribution the student actually produces.
  • Cuts exposure bias, raises the ceiling — the 2025 default.
Caveat: mind the capacity gap

Bigger teacher is not always better. 2025 research shows that too large a teacher–student capacity gap can degrade — even reverse — the benefit: the student can’t represent what the giant is trying to teach. Match the teacher to the student’s capacity, and prefer on-policy signals so the student learns from where it actually is. Distillation costs more than PTQ (it’s a real training run), but the win is bigger and durable — you ship a permanently smaller network, not a re-encoding of a large one.

04 Remove weights

Pruning & sparsity: delete weights, keep the behavior ▸

The third family is the most literal: pruning removes weights. The naive form, unstructured pruning, zeroes out individual weights wherever they look unimportant. It can reach dramatic theoretical sparsity — but a zero in a dense matrix still gets multiplied on ordinary hardware, so it buys no wall-clock speedup unless you have sparse kernels or hardware support (NVIDIA’s 2:4 semi-structured sparsity is the practical middle ground). Great for a research plot, often useless for latency.

Structured pruning removes whole units — entire attention heads, whole layers (depth), or MLP/embedding channels (width). The result isn’t a sparse matrix full of holes; it’s a genuinely smaller dense network that speeds up on any GPU, no special kernels required. Watch it happen:

The canonical 2024–2025 recipe is NVIDIA’s Minitron: prune (in width and/or depth), then re-train the survivor with distillation from the original as teacher. Compressing Llama-3.1 8B down to 4B this way reused illustratively ~40× fewer training tokens than training a 4B from scratch, and adding the distillation step recovered a large chunk of accuracy (~16% MMLU in that work). A useful rule of thumb falls out of it: width pruning tends to preserve accuracy better; depth pruning cuts latency harder at the same parameter count — because removing layers removes sequential work, while removing channels removes parallel work.

Unstructured pruning

  • Zero out individual weights.
  • High theoretical sparsity, but zeros still get multiplied.
  • Needs sparse kernels / 2:4 support to become real speed.

Structured pruning

  • Remove whole heads / layers / channels.
  • Yields a smaller dense net — fast on any hardware.
  • Pair with re-training / distillation (Minitron) to recover.
Pitfall: memory saved is not latency saved

The classic mistake is to prune unstructured, report the sparsity, and expect the model to run faster. It won’t — not on a dense GPU without sparse kernels. If you need latency on ordinary hardware, prune structured so you end up with a smaller dense model. Reserve unstructured sparsity for cases where you actually have the kernels (or the 2:4 hardware path) to cash it in.

05 Stack them

The three families compose

Here is the payoff of the “different axes” framing. Quantization changes bits, distillation changes parameters, pruning changes architecture — three orthogonal levers, so they stack instead of fighting. The standard production pipeline reads left to right: prune and/or distil down to a smaller dense model, then quantize that model, and add a quantized KV cache on top. Minitron already fuses the first two (prune + distil); real deployments then AWQ-INT4 or NVFP4 the resulting student and turn on an FP8 KV cache. The savings multiply.

The framing to carry away: distillation is the expensive-but-durable win — a training run that yields a permanently smaller network — while quantization is the cheap, immediate, composable win you apply last. A shipped example makes it concrete: OpenAI’s gpt-oss (August 2025, Apache-2.0) was released natively quantized in MXFP4 on its MoE expert weights — the first flagship open-weights model designed around 4-bit — which is what lets the 120B variant (116.8B total / 5.1B active) fit on a single 80 GB GPU and the 20B run in ~16 GB. Quantization has graduated from a post-hoc trick to a first-class release format.

And the quantized KV cache is now mainstream, not exotic: vLLM exposes --kv-cache-dtype fp8 (E4M3), giving roughly 2× KV compression and 2–3× the batch headroom on Hopper/Ada, with FlashAttention-3 able to run attention itself in FP8. It composes with everything above — but it’s also a place to be careful (next section). Serving-side levers — speculative decoding, routers, prompt caching — then stack on the outside without touching the model at all.

The composition order

Think of it as coarse-to-fine. Distil (or structured-prune-then-distil) to fix the parameter count — the durable, expensive move. Quantize the result to fix the bits — the cheap, immediate move. Then hand it to the serving stack (L37) for batching, caching, and speculation. Each stage assumes the previous artifact and multiplies its savings.

06 The rule

Pick the lever: quantize first, distil when you must

All three families work, but they cost wildly different amounts to apply, which dictates the order you reach for them. When latency is fine and only the bill hurts, the first move is almost always PTQ to INT4 weight-only: no retraining, minutes to apply, roughly quarters the memory and bandwidth (so it also speeds decode), for a point or two of quality — verified on your golden eval. Only if that isn’t enough do you spend a training run on distillation. Reason it through:

Which lever do you pull first?

You serve a 70B model (illustratively $1.19 per 1M tokens on H100, BF16). Latency is acceptable, but the monthly bill is not. You have one week. Do you distil a smaller model, prune it, or quantize it — and in what order?

Restate → the naive take → the insight → the rule

Restate the problem

The behavior is validated and latency is fine; the single failing metric is cost per token. You want the largest cost cut for the least engineering, without falling below your eval bar.

The naive take

“Train a distilled 8B student — a smaller model is obviously cheaper.” True, and it’s the biggest durable win — but it’s a training run: data, compute, a fresh eval, days of work. Spending that before trying anything cheaper is over-engineering the first move.

The insight

Decode is bandwidth-bound, so INT4 weight-only PTQ (AWQ) quarters the bytes per token — ~4× less VRAM and faster decode — for a point or two of quality, with no retraining and minutes to apply. Pull a pre-quantized checkpoint, measure on the golden eval (Phase 2) at your real context length, ship. It’s the cheap, immediate, reversible move.

The rule / trade-off

Quantize first because it’s minutes and composable; distil only if INT4 still isn’t cheap enough. And the two stack: distil to a smaller dense student, then quantize that too. Quantization is the cheap win applied last in the pipeline but reached for first in time; distillation is the heavy, durable win you spend real training on.

llm = LLM(
    model="org/Llama-70B-AWQ-INT4",
    quantization="awq_marlin",    # optimized 4-bit kernels
    kv_cache_dtype="fp8_e4m3",   # quantized KV cache: ~2x compression
)

score = golden_eval(llm)         # short tasks look fine; check long-context too
assert score >= baseline - 0.02   # a point or two is the whole budget

# 3) still too expensive? the heavy, durable win — distil a smaller student,
#    then quantize IT too. the families compose.
student = distill(teacher=llm, target_params=8e9, on_policy=True)
Say it back in one breath

“My behavior was fine and my latency was fine — only the bill hurt. So I didn’t start with a training run. I pulled an AWQ-INT4 checkpoint: no retraining, minutes to apply, roughly a quarter of the VRAM and bytes-per-token, and because decode is bandwidth-bound it got faster too. I measured it on my golden eval at the real context length, and a point or two of quality was within budget. Only if that hadn’t been enough would I have distilled a smaller student — and then quantized that student as well, because the two stack.”

Quantize first (cheap, minutes, reversible); distil when you must (heavy, durable); they compose.

Pitfall: judging quantization on the wrong test

The trap is to bless a 4-bit build on short-task perplexity or MMLU. It can look fine there and still crater on the workload you actually run: reported drops up to ~59% on long-context tasks beyond 64K tokens, and up to ~32% on multi-step MATH, because quantization error accumulates along the generated sequence. INT8 stays gentle (~0.8%); INT4 is where it bites. Evaluate at your real context length and reasoning depth, keep sensitive layers higher-precision, and treat --kv-cache-dtype fp8 as something to test, not assume — it has shown needle-in-a-haystack regressions in some configs.
