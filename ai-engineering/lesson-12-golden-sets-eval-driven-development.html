Golden Sets & Eval-Driven Development

You have the three judges. Now you need the cases they judge — and a way of working that puts them at the center. A golden set is a curated set of real inputs with known-good behavior; eval-driven development is building against it the way you build against tests. The discipline is the one that made software tractable, pointed at AI: every real failure becomes a permanent case, and no regression gets to ship twice.

00 The artifact

The answer key you grade against

An eval method without cases to run is a thermometer with nothing to measure. The cases are the golden set: a curated, trusted collection of input → expected-behavior examples that represents what your system actually faces. It's the answer key the judges from the last lesson grade against, and the regression suite you run before every deploy. Everything in this lesson is about building that artifact well and putting it at the center of how you work.

Two things make a golden set "golden," and neither is size. The first is that it is curated and trusted — each case is checked by someone who knows the right answer, so a passing score actually means something. The second is that it is representative — it mirrors the real distribution of inputs (weighted toward what's common) and deliberately includes the edge cases and known failure modes that aggregate metrics paper over. A thousand cases scraped at random are worth less than fifty cases you understand.

Start absurdly small

The biggest mistake is waiting until you can build the "real" eval set, so you build none. Twenty hand-picked cases beat zero by a mile — even five that cover your main use case let you measure your next change. Start with a handful you can write this afternoon, then let production grow it. A golden set is something you accrete, not something you commission.

01 Before the metrics

Look at your data ▸

The instinct is to grab a list of generic metrics — relevance, coherence, helpfulness — and start scoring. The practitioners who've shipped real AI products say the opposite: look at your data first, let the failures tell you what to measure. The payoff of doing it in this order is that your metrics end up shaped like your actual failures instead of a generic checklist that misses them. A team that adopts "coherence" and "helpfulness" off the shelf is measuring what some library author guessed mattered; a team that reads fifty traces and discovers its real problems are "invents refund amounts" and "ignores the second question in two-part messages" builds evals that catch those — the ones costing it customers. Generic metrics feel productive and measure the wrong thing. Looking at your data is slower to start and far faster to a product that works.

02 The ratchet

Every real failure becomes a case

Here is the single habit that compounds into a moat. When a user hits a bad output, you don't just fix it — you capture it. Grab the exact input, write down the correct behavior, and add it to the golden set. From that moment, that failure is a permanent test: any future change that brings it back turns the eval red before it can reach a user. The bug can happen once. It can't happen twice silently.

This is exactly the regression test discipline that software engineering already runs on, transplanted into AI. The reflex is identical — "found a bug? write the test that catches it, then fix it" — with one adaptation: because outputs are open-ended and non-deterministic, the check at the end is usually a graded one (a judge, a similarity threshold, a pass-rate) rather than a strict assert ==. The mechanism that makes the ratchet turn is the same; only the comparison softened.

Why this is the moat

Anyone can copy your prompt. Nobody can copy the hundreds of hard-won cases your real users generated — the weird inputs, the edge categories, the failures you only discovered in production. That accumulated golden set is your understanding of the problem, written down and made executable. Two years of "every failure becomes a case" is a competitive advantage a well-funded competitor can't simply buy.

You stop trading one fixed bug for one new bug, because you can finally see the trade.

04 Keeping the set honest

Golden-set hygiene

A golden set is a dataset, and datasets rot in well-understood ways. A few habits, borrowed straight from machine-learning practice, keep yours trustworthy:

  • Split dev from test. If you iterate against the same cases over and over, you'll overfit to your own eval set — tuning the prompt to ace those exact examples while real performance stagnates. Keep a held-out test set you touch rarely, for an honest final read, separate from the dev set you iterate on daily.
  • Watch for leakage and contamination. Cases lifted from public benchmarks may already sit in a model's training data, inflating scores on things it effectively memorized. Prefer cases authored from your own production data, and refresh with examples from after the model's training cutoff.
  • Balance the classes. An aggregate score is an average, and averages hide minorities. If 80% of your cases are easy "billing" tickets, a catastrophic failure on the 5% "refund" cases barely moves the headline number — exactly the blind spot a per-category view exists to close. Keep per-category counts balanced and always read the per-category breakdown, not just the total.
  • Version the set, and check your labels. Treat the golden set as a versioned artifact that changes deliberately, in reviewable diffs. And remember the labels themselves can be wrong — spot-check inter-annotator agreement so you're not grading against mistakes.
The size-vs-signal rule

When in doubt, smaller and cleaner beats bigger and noisier. A hundred cases you've each read and trust give a clearer signal than ten thousand auto-generated ones riddled with mislabels — because a noisy eval set lies to you with the confidence of a big number. Grow the set, but never faster than you can keep it honest.

05 The loop earns its keep

The loop catches a swap ▸

Time to watch the whole practice pay off. You run a support-ticket classifier, you've built a balanced golden set, and a tempting model swap arrives — subtler than the one in Lesson 10, because this time the headline accuracy barely flinches. The swap the loop wouldn't let through

Your ticket classifier routes to billing, bug, or refund. A newer model promises lower cost and latency. You want it — but you run it through the eval-driven loop before it touches production.

From a trace to a caught regression

  "input": "You charged me twice after the app crashed mid-payment.",
  "expected": "refund",        // not "bug" — the money is the issue
  "source":   "prod-incident #4127", // every failure becomes a case
  "category": "refund / mentions-bug"
}
The set already holds the trap

Months ago, the old model once mis-routed a "double charge after a crash" ticket to bug. You captured that exact input, labeled it refund, and added it — case #4127. It has passed on every run since. It's a tripwire waiting.

Run the loop on the swap

Change one line — the model name — and run the golden set. Overall accuracy slips just one point, 92% → 91% — the headline is weighted by live traffic, where refunds are a sliver. The aggregate dashboard would wave that dip straight through; the swap "looks" free.

Read the per-category breakdown

But the breakdown shows refund collapsing from 91% to 61%: the new model reads "crash → double charge" as a bug, mis-routing money complaints. Because refunds are a small share of traffic, that 30-point crater barely dents the weighted average — the headline hides what the per-category view makes obvious. Case #4127 is red.

Keep or revert — with eyes open

The gate fails on the refund category. You either revert the swap, or add a refund-disambiguation line to the prompt and re-run until #4127 and its category are green again. Either way, the regression never ships — and your fix is itself now permanently guarded.

Say it back in one breath

“A real mis-routing became a permanent golden case. When I tried a cheaper model, overall accuracy barely moved — 92% to 91% — because refunds are a sliver of traffic. But the per-category breakdown caught refund collapsing from 91% to 61%, because a balanced set and a category view don't let a traffic-weighted average bury a minority. The loop made me keep-or-revert on evidence, so the regression died in CI instead of in production.”

Three ideas converging: every failure becomes a case, balance the classes and read the breakdown, and run the loop on every change. That's eval-driven development doing its whole job at once.

06 The mindset

Evals are version-controlled assets

The frame that ties this phase to the rest of your engineering: your golden set and its checks live in version control, beside your code, and change through reviewed pull requests. They are not a notebook someone ran once or a spreadsheet in a drive folder — they are first-class assets, exactly like the prompts from Lesson 08. A change to the eval set is a diff a teammate reviews. "No eval, no merge" becomes a branch rule, and the golden set becomes the shared, executable definition of what "working" means for your product.

You do not need heavy machinery to start. The simplest viable setup is a golden_set.jsonl in the repo and a script that runs it in CI — that alone puts you ahead of most teams. When you outgrow it, the ecosystem is mature: Braintrust, LangSmith, Promptfoo, OpenAI Evals, DeepEval and others give you dataset versioning, trace capture, judge tooling, and CI integration. But the tool is the easy part. The hard, durable part is the habit: look at your data, turn every failure into a case, run the loop on every change, and keep the set honest. Adopt that, and quality stops being a vibe and starts being something you can see, defend, and compound.

Where this leaves you

You can now answer the question that haunts every AI feature: did this change make the product better? — with a number, on cases you trust, before it ships. The phase isn't done: Lesson 13 scales this from one output to the whole system, and Lesson 14 wires it into CI so nothing worse can ship. But the golden set you just built is the instrument they all lean on — and so does every later phase: retrieval in RAG, planning in agents, weight changes in fine-tuning, cheaper inference, each a change you'll want to prove helped. You built the measurement first. Now you get to optimize against it.
