Evaluating the Whole System

Everything so far scored one thing: is the model's output good? But your users don't use a model — they use a system: retrieval, prompts, tools, and orchestration wrapped around it. A flawless model can sit inside a broken product. This lesson zooms out from the model to the system, and from one accuracy number to a scorecard — task success, faithfulness, safety, latency, and cost, all first-class.

Original study notes. The fourth lesson of the Evaluation phase. 00 The scope error

The model passed; the product failed

Here is a true and uncomfortable sentence: your model can be excellent and your product still bad. A user never calls a bare model. They call a system — their question gets embedded, a retriever pulls documents, a prompt template stitches everything together, the model generates, tools fire, an orchestrator loops, and an answer comes back. The model is one component in that chain. Score only the model and you've measured one link while shipping the whole rope.

This is the most common scope error in AI engineering. A team benchmarks the model, sees 95% on a reasoning test, and concludes the product is 95% good. Then real users get wrong answers — because the retriever surfaced the wrong document, or the prompt dropped a constraint, or the orchestrator took a bad branch, or the answer was technically right but arrived eight seconds too late. None of that is a model failure, and none of it shows up in a model eval. The unit of evaluation has to be the unit the user experiences: the whole system, end to end.

The reframe

"Is the output good?" was the right question for one model call. "Did the user accomplish their goal, safely, fast enough, and cheaply enough?" is the right question for a product. This lesson is that widening — from a single quality score to a multi-dimensional scorecard scored on the whole pipeline.

01 Three scopes

Model, component, system ▸

There are three scopes you can evaluate at, and the difference between them is the difference between "it broke" and "here's where it broke." Step through a RAG pipeline answering a question and watch each scope see something different:

The takeaway is a division of labor between two scopes. End-to-end (system) eval is your source of truth for whether the product works — it's what the user feels, so it's what you gate on. Component eval is your debugger: when the end-to-end score drops, per-component scores tell you which link broke, so you're not bisecting a black box by hand. Model benchmarks have their place — choosing a model — but they are the narrowest scope, and a great score there guarantees nothing about the product wrapped around it.

02 The metric that matters

Task success is the top line

If the system is the unit, then the headline metric is not "quality" in the abstract — it's task success: did the user actually accomplish what they came to do? Did the refund get issued, the bug get correctly triaged, the question get answered well enough that they stopped asking? Everything else is a proxy for that, and proxies lie. A response can score high on fluency and relevance and still leave the user's goal unmet. Outcome beats proxy, always.

You measure task success from two directions. Offline, on your golden set, you check whether the system produced the goal-satisfying outcome for each case — clean and repeatable, but a lower bound, because a recorded "correct path" misses other valid ways to succeed. Online, in production, you read the signals real users leave: explicit ones (thumbs, a satisfaction survey) and — more honestly — implicit ones:

  • Containment / resolution rate — the goal was met without escalating to a human. The cleanest proxy for "it worked."
  • Retries & regenerations — the user re-asked or hit "try again." A quiet vote that the first answer failed.
  • Escalation rate and its accuracy — how often it hands off, and whether it hands off at the right time (escalating a hard case is success; escalating an easy one is failure).
  • Abandonment & follow-up — the user gave up, or had to ask a clarifying follow-up the system should have pre-empted.

Offline success tells you whether a change is safe to ship; online success tells you whether it actually helped. You need both, and they close a loop — production signals become tomorrow's golden cases (Lesson 12), which raise the floor your offline gate enforces.

03 One row, many columns

The eval scorecard ▸

"Is it good?" doesn't have one answer because a product is good along several axes at once — and they trade off. So you stop reporting a single number and start reporting a scorecard. Watch a tempting "cheaper, faster model" change move every dimension at once:

Operational metrics earn their place on this card precisely because a correct answer that's too slow or too expensive is a product failure. Treat latency and cost as quality, not as afterthoughts on a separate dashboard. And report them honestly: latency as p95 (the tail your unhappiest users feel), not a flattering average; cost as cost per resolved task, not per token, so spend is tied to outcomes rather than verbosity. The scorecard's whole job is to make a tradeoff visible so a human can make it on purpose — instead of a single average quietly hiding it.

04 Grounded, not just fluent

Faithfulness & the RAG triad

One scorecard dimension deserves its own section, because it's where AI systems fail most expensively: faithfulness — is every claim in the answer actually supported by the source the system retrieved? A fluent, confident, well-toned answer that invented its facts is the hallucination that gets a product sued. For any system that grounds answers in retrieved data (the RAG pattern, Phase 3), three reference-free metrics — the RAGAS triad — pull the failure apart into its three possible causes:

Context relevance

  • Did the retriever pull the right, on-topic chunks?
  • Scores the retrieval step.
  • Garbage in → the model never had a chance.

Answer relevance

  • Does the answer actually address the question asked?
  • Scores whether the model stayed on the user's goal.
  • Catches fluent non-answers.

Faithfulness

  • Is every claim entailed by the retrieved context?
  • The inverse of hallucination rate.
  • Catches confident invention.

The power of these is that they're reference-free — they need only the question, the retrieved context, and the answer, all of which you already have in production logs. That means you can score faithfulness on live traffic, not just on a hand-labeled golden set, which is why faithfulness monitoring is a staple of production RAG. (There's a fourth, context recall — did we retrieve everything needed? — but that one needs a reference answer, so it lives on the golden set.) Notice how cleanly they map to the scopes from section 01: context relevance scores the retriever component, answer relevance and faithfulness score the model's use of it, and together they explain a low end-to-end score.

05 Agents change the question

Judge the journey, not just the destination ▸

When your system is an agent — it plans, calls tools, and loops over multiple steps (Phase 5) — a new failure appears: the agent can reach the right answer the wrong way, and a right answer by luck will be a wrong answer next time. So you evaluate the trajectory, not only the final output. Step through one:

Worked examplerefund agent · trajectory eval
The right answer by the wrong road

A support agent answers "What's the status of my refund?" with "Your $40 refund is processing, arriving in 3–5 days." The user is happy. The final-answer eval passes. Should you trust it?

What only the trajectory reveals

expected vs actual trajectory# expected: look it up before stating it
get_order(id) -> get_refund_status(order) -> reply()

# actual: skipped the lookup, asserted from memory
get_order(id) -> [no get_refund_status call] -> reply()
# "processing, 3-5 days" was a plausible guess — correct by luck
Final-answer eval: pass

The reply was fluent, on-topic, and — this time — happened to be true. An output-only eval has nothing to object to. The product looks fine.

Trajectory eval: fail

Comparing the tool-call path to the expected one, a deterministic check sees that get_refund_status() was never called. The agent didn't look up the status — it guessed a plausible one. The answer was right by coincidence.

Why the path matters more than this one answer

A guess that's right today is wrong tomorrow when the real status is "denied" or "delayed." Trajectory eval catches the latent failure now, before it becomes a confidently-wrong answer in production. Tool-call correctness is a deterministic check; the reasoning quality between steps is where an LLM-judge helps.

Say it back in one breath

“The final answer passed, but the agent reached it by skipping the get_refund_status tool and guessing — right by luck this time, wrong the moment reality differs. Evaluating the trajectory, not just the destination, caught a latent failure an output-only eval is blind to. For agents, the journey is part of the output.”

Rule of thumb: deterministic checks for the exact things (which tools, what arguments, what order), an LLM-judge for the fuzzy things (was each step's reasoning sound). Score the path.

06 The mindset

Weight by what the product needs

The scorecard raises an obvious question: if a change helps faithfulness but hurts latency, do you ship it? There is no universal answer — and that's the point. Resist collapsing the scorecard into one composite number, because a single average hides exactly the tradeoff you need to see. Keep the dimensions separate, and decide their weights from what your product actually requires. A legal or medical assistant weights faithfulness and safety so heavily that no latency win could justify a faithfulness drop. A casual brainstorming toy weights responsiveness and cost, and tolerates the occasional loose claim. Same dimensions, different product, opposite decision — and only a per-dimension scorecard lets you make it deliberately.

So the discipline that closes this lesson is: measure every dimension that matters to your users, keep them visible side by side, and weight them on purpose. Quality, task success, faithfulness, safety, latency, cost — each is first-class, each can be the one that sinks a release, and the engineer's job is to know which ones this product cannot compromise. Evaluation isn't a single gate you pass; it's a scorecard you read like a balance sheet.

Where this phase goes next

You can now define what "good" means for a whole system, across every axis that matters.
