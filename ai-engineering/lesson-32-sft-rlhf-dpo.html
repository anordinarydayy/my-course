SFT, RLHF & DPO

Phase 5 — Fine-tuning & Model Adaptation — opens with the stage that turns a raw pretrained model into an assistant. Pretraining buys capability; post-training (alignment) buys behaviour — following instructions, adopting a helpful, harmless persona, and matching human preferences. This lesson walks the modern toolkit end to end: SFT teaches the format, RLHF learns from human rankings through a reward model, and DPO proves you can often throw that reward model away and optimise preferences directly.

00 The naive fix

Pretraining buys capability; alignment buys behaviour

When pretraining finishes, you have a base model: a next-token predictor that has read a large slice of the web and learned, astonishingly well, what token tends to follow what. Ask it a question and it may not answer — it may cheerfully continue your text, list ten more questions, or drift into a Reddit thread, because “what comes next in this document” is all it was ever trained to do. It has no notion that you want a helpful reply, no consistent persona, and no instinct to refuse a harmful request. The knowledge is in there; the behaviour is not.

The naive instinct is to reach for scale: train a bigger base model on more tokens and surely it will start behaving. It won’t — not reliably. Scale sharpens capability (it knows more, reasons better), but instruction-following, tone, and values are a different axis entirely. A larger base model is a more capable autocomplete, not a more obedient assistant. Bridging that gap is a distinct, separate stage that happens after pretraining, and the field has a name for it: post-training, or alignment.

Post-training is not one trick but a small pipeline, and the canonical modern shape is remarkably stable across labs: SFT → preference optimisation / RL. First you show the model how to answer at all (supervised fine-tuning). Then you refine which answer it prefers, using human or AI comparisons (RLHF, DPO, or their descendants). Everything in this lesson is a way of doing that second step — and all of them stand on the first.

Base model (post-pretraining)

  • Knowledgeable — has absorbed web-scale facts and patterns.
  • Continues, doesn’t answer — completes text rather than replying.
  • No persona — no consistent helpful/harmless stance, no refusals.
  • Bigger ≠ better-behaved — scale adds capability, not obedience.

Aligned model (post-training)

  • Follows instructions — treats the prompt as a task to satisfy.
  • Formatted — answers in the shape you asked for.
  • Has a persona — helpful, honest, and declines the unsafe.
  • Preference-shaped — tuned toward answers humans actually prefer.
The one-line version

Pretraining makes a model that knows; post-training makes a model that helps. The modern recipe is SFT then preference tuning — supervised imitation to fix the format, then RLHF or DPO to fix the preferences. The rest of this lesson is those two moves, taken apart.

01 The foundation

SFT: imitate the ideal answer ▸

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT), also called instruction tuning, is the simplest and most important of the three. You assemble a dataset of (prompt, ideal completion) pairs — a question and the exact answer a good human would give — and train the model to reproduce the completion. That’s it: it is ordinary supervised learning, with the same cross-entropy (next-token) loss used in pretraining. The only twist is where the loss applies.

In practice the loss is masked to the completion tokens only: the model is graded on the answer it should have produced, not on re-predicting the prompt you already handed it. Get that mask wrong and you waste capacity teaching the model to echo questions. Step through one turn of the loop — draw a pair, predict, score, update — and watch what SFT actually optimises.

What SFT teaches is format and instruction-following — how to answer — far more than new facts. The base model already holds the knowledge; SFT reshapes how it is expressed: answer the question, use the requested structure, adopt the assistant voice. This is why a few thousand high-quality examples can transform a model’s usefulness, and why data quality dominates data quantity here.

SFT is also the cheapest and most stable of the three methods, which is exactly why it is the shared starting point: both RLHF and DPO begin from an SFT model, never a raw base model. The pitfalls are the mirror of its strengths — overfitting to surface format, catastrophic forgetting of base-model skills if you push too hard, acute sensitivity to data quality, and the ever-present bug of forgetting to mask the prompt so the loss lands on completion tokens only.

The invariant

SFT is imitation: show the model one gold answer per prompt and train it to reproduce that answer. It is powerful precisely because it is simple — but it can only teach the model to copy the single answer you provided. When there is no single right answer, and you only know which of two answers is better, you need the next tool.

02 Learn from preference

RLHF: rank, reward, and rein in ▸

SFT hits a ceiling on open-ended tasks. What is the single “ideal” answer to “write me a poem about the sea”, or the one correct tone for a refund apology? There isn’t one. But humans can easily say which of two drafts they prefer. RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — is built to learn from exactly that signal, and it is the recipe (InstructGPT, Ouyang et al., 2022) that produced the first genuinely helpful ChatGPT- and Claude-class assistants.

It runs in three stages. (1) Start from the SFT model. (2) Sample several outputs per prompt, have humans rank them, and train a separate reward model — a Bradley-Terry pairwise objective — to predict which output a human would prefer. (3) Optimise the policy with PPO to maximise that reward, plus a per-token KL penalty that leashes the policy to a frozen copy of the SFT model. Step through the whole pipeline:

The two ideas that make RLHF work are worth naming. The reward model turns a handful of discrete human rankings into a smooth, differentiable score for any completion — a learned stand-in for the human judge that PPO can optimise against millions of times. And the KL leash is the safety cable: it penalises the policy for drifting from the frozen SFT reference, so it can climb the reward without wandering into gibberish that happens to score well. Take the leash off and the policy will exploit every flaw in the reward model it can find.

Why the leash matters

The reward model is an imperfect proxy for human preference. Left unconstrained, the policy will chase the proxy off a cliff — producing text the reward model loves and humans hate. The KL penalty keeps the policy in the neighbourhood of a model we already trust (the SFT reference). It is the single most important stabiliser in the whole pipeline — and, as we’ll see next, still not enough on its own.

03 The cost

Three models, one tightrope

RLHF is powerful, but it is heavy, and the weight is concrete. During PPO you can have up to four models resident at once: the policy being trained, the frozen reference for the KL term, the reward model scoring outputs, and a value / critic network estimating future reward. Four models in memory, sampling on-policy every step, with a famously twitchy RL algorithm — that is why RLHF is expensive to run and notoriously hard to stabilise, with many hyperparameters that all interact.

The deeper problem is reward hacking (a.k.a. reward over-optimisation), and it is just Goodhart’s law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The policy optimises the reward model, not true human preference, so as training continues, real quality rises — and then falls — while the reward score keeps climbing. The model has learned to game an imperfect proxy. This is the core RLHF failure mode, and the main reason the KL leash exists.

But here is the sharp part: the KL penalty is necessary yet not sufficient. KL treats every deviation from the reference the same way, so a policy can adopt a low-probability spurious feature — a subtly sycophantic tone, say — at negligible KL cost while still scoring high on the reward model. Severe over-optimisation can happen even under a strict KL budget. You have a tightrope, and the safety cable only catches some of the falls.

Read that table as a to-do list you would rather not have. It is the practical reason a wave of open teams, staring at four resident models and an unstable RL loop, asked a simple question: do we actually need the reward model and the RL at all? In 2023 the answer arrived — and it was “often, no.”

04 The insight

DPO: your model is secretly a reward model ▸

Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., arXiv:2305.18290, May 2023; NeurIPS 2023) is one of those results that feels like a magic trick until you see the algebra. Its subtitle says it plainly: “Your Language Model Is Secretly a Reward Model.” The authors prove that the RLHF objective — maximise reward subject to a KL constraint — has a closed-form optimum. Rearrange that solution and the reward turns out to be a simple function of the log-probability ratio between the optimal policy and the reference. In other words, the policy implicitly encodes its own reward; you never needed a separate model to represent it.

That lets you collapse the whole RLHF machine. Instead of training a reward model and running PPO, you optimise the policy directly on (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples with a single binary-classification-style loss over log-probability ratios — policy versus a frozen reference — scaled by a temperature β (commonly around 0.1) that plays the role of the KL constraint. No sampling during training, no reward model, no RL loop. Step through it and watch what DPO deletes:

The payoff is fewer moving parts, and it compounds. Two models instead of four (policy plus frozen reference). One stable supervised-style loss instead of an RL loop. No reward model to train, validate, or serve. The result is stable, cheap, and reproducible — which is why DPO became the workhorse of open post-training, powering Zephyr, Llama-3-Instruct-era recipes, and Tülu. The β term still keeps the policy near the reference, so you keep the KL-style leash without the KL-style machinery.

One honest caveat before you fall in love with it. DPO is offline / off-policy by default: it learns from a fixed preference dataset, which may not reflect what your current model actually generates. And it has a known failure mode — likelihood displacement — where, during training, the probability of the chosen response can actually decrease, sometimes shifting mass toward unintended or even opposite-meaning responses (“unintentional unalignment”).

Pitfall: likelihood displacement

DPO only ever optimises the gap between chosen and rejected log-ratios — nothing pins the chosen probability up in absolute terms, so it can drift down as long as the rejected drops faster. The 2024–2025 fixes all add a stabiliser: DPO+NLL (a plain negative-log-likelihood term on the chosen response), DPO-Positive (DPOP), and DPO-Shift. If your chosen-response probabilities are sliding during training, this — not your learning rate — is usually why.

05 The landscape

The family, and where RL still wins

DPO spawned a family, each member tweaking the loss for a different situation. IPO (Identity Preference Optimization) adds a regulariser to fight DPO’s tendency to overfit the preference set. KTO (Kahneman-Tversky Optimization) is the useful oddball: it works on unpaired binary good/bad labels — no chosen-versus-rejected pairs required — using a prospect-theory-style utility, which matters when your data is “thumbs up / thumbs down,” not ranked pairs. ORPO (Odds-Ratio Preference Optimization) is reference-model-free and folds SFT and preference tuning into a single step, dropping the separate SFT stage entirely.

The tempting question is “which variant is best?” — and the honest answer is none reliably. Controlled studies keep finding the variants do not consistently beat vanilla DPO despite extra hyperparameter tuning. One illustrative run on GSM8K: DPO 58.91% vs IPO 56.40%, KTO 54.59%, ORPO 55.42%. Do not read that as a universal ranking — read it as: pick a variant for its data fit (KTO when you only have unpaired labels; ORPO when you want to skip a separate SFT stage), not for a promised accuracy bump. DPO remains the sensible default.

The 2025–2026 twist: online, verifiable, and AI feedback

Three shifts define the current frontier. First, DPO is increasingly run on-policy / iteratively: sample fresh responses from the current model, label them (often with an AI judge), run DPO, and repeat — closing the gap with online RL. Tülu 3 (AllenAI, arXiv:2411.15124, Nov 2024) explicitly mixes off- and on-policy preference data in its open, end-to-end recipe: SFT → DPO → RLVR.

Second, and the breakout of 2025: RLVR — Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards. It keeps the RL loop but replaces the learned reward model with a programmatic verifier: an exact-answer match, a unit-test / code execution, a math checker. Because the reward is ground truth, it largely sidesteps reward hacking — but it only works where correctness is checkable (math, code, formatted output). RLVR is how reasoning models are trained: it powers OpenAI o1/o3-style models, DeepSeek-R1 (arXiv:2501.12948, January 2025), and Qwen’s reasoning line. The RL algorithm of choice is now GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization, from DeepSeek), which drops PPO’s value/critic network and instead computes each response’s advantage relative to a group of sampled rollouts for the same prompt — cutting memory and cost. DeepSeek-R1-Zero even showed reasoning can emerge from pure RL on a base model with no SFT cold-start at all.

Third, RLAIF — RL from AI Feedback, including Anthropic’s Constitutional AI (arXiv:2212.08073, Dec 2022) — replaces expensive human preference labels with an AI “judge” guided by a written set of principles (a constitution). It matches RLHF on tasks like summarisation and dialogue while scaling far more cheaply, shifting human effort from labelling individual examples to writing the rules. The trade-off: if the judge has hidden biases, they get baked in and amplified across model generations — an echo-chamber / model-collapse risk.

DPO (offline pref.)

  • Reward: implicit, from a fixed (chosen, rejected) set.
  • Cost: cheapest — 2 models, one loss, no sampling.
  • Best for: chat, style, helpfulness on a small team.

RLHF / PPO (online)

  • Reward: a learned reward model over human rankings.
  • Cost: heavy — up to 4 models, unstable RL.
  • Best for: peak alignment with online preference collection.

RLVR / GRPO (verifiable)

  • Reward: a programmatic verifier — ground truth.
  • Cost: RL loop, but no reward-model hacking.
  • Best for: reasoning — math, code, checkable output.
The live debate

There is an active 2025–2026 argument that online, on-policy RL beats offline DPO at the frontier — some work blames DPO’s weakness on “representation misspecification.” The practical read: DPO help chat benchmarks but gives marginal or even negative gains on hard mathematical reasoning, which is precisely why RLVR/GRPO, not DPO, trains the reasoning models. The field has split by task type, not by a single winner.

06 The rule

Choosing your post-training method

Strip away the acronyms and the choice comes down to three questions: what does your data look like, what is your reward signal, and how much infra can you run? If you have preference pairs and want to align chat, style, and helpfulness with a small team, DPO is the default. If you can collect preferences online at scale and are chasing peak alignment, RLHF/PPO still earns its cost. If correctness is checkable — math, code, structured output — reach for RLVR with GRPO. And if human labels are the bottleneck, RLAIF lets an AI judge stand in. In every case, an SFT pass comes first (except ORPO, which folds it in).

The alignment run that didn’t need an RL cluster

You’ve fine-tuned an SFT model for a support assistant and collected roughly 8,000 human preference pairs (each a prompt with a chosen and a rejected reply). You have two engineers and a modest GPU budget — no RL infra team. Do you build the classic RLHF stack, or reach for DPO?

Restate → the naive take → the insight → the rule

Restate the problem

You have paired preference data and want the model to prefer the chosen replies. The constraint is people and infra, not raw model quality — two engineers can’t babysit an unstable four-model PPO loop.

The naive take

“Preferences means RLHF — that’s how ChatGPT was built.” So you plan a reward model plus PPO. But that’s a reward model to train, validate, and serve, a value network, a frozen reference, and a twitchy RL loop — up to four models resident and a reward you’ll spend weeks keeping from being hacked.

The insight

The RLHF objective has a closed form: the reward is implicit in the policy’s log-ratio to the reference. With paired data you can skip the reward model and the RL entirely and optimise the preference directly — DPO. Two models, one stable loss, offline, reproducible.

The rule / trade-off

With ~8k pairs and a small team, use DPO. Keep a β term (≈ 0.1) so the policy stays near the SFT model. RLHF still wins when you can collect preferences online and chase peak alignment; RLVR wins for reasoning, where a verifier gives a real, unhackable reward. None of those is your situation — so don’t pay for them.

two stacks for the same goal# RLHF stack — a reward model + an RL loop, up to 4 models resident
policy = sft_model
reward = train(reward_model, human_rankings)     # a whole extra model to fit & serve
policy = ppo(policy, reward, ref=sft_model)        # unstable · policy · ref · reward · critic

# DPO stack — one model, one loss, no reward model
policy = sft_model
policy = dpo(policy, pref_pairs, ref=sft_model, beta=0.1)
        # pref_pairs = (prompt, chosen, rejected) · beta keeps the KL-style leash
Say it back in one breath

“I have 8k paired preferences and a two-person team, and my first instinct was RLHF because that’s how the famous assistants were built. But RLHF means a reward model plus PPO — up to four models resident and a reward I’d spend weeks keeping from being hacked. The RLHF objective has a closed form, so with paired data I can skip the reward model entirely and run DPO: two models, one stable loss, offline. I keep a β leash to the SFT model. I’d only go back to RLHF for online preference collection, or to RLVR if my task were checkable, like math or code.”

Match the method to your data and infra. Paired preferences + small team → DPO. Verifiable answers → RLVR. Online preference at scale → RLHF.

The productised menu

Hosted fine-tuning now mirrors this toolkit. OpenAI’s API exposes SFT, DPO (preference fine-tuning), and RFT (Reinforcement Fine-Tuning) — the productised, hosted form of RLVR, with user-defined programmable graders for reasoning models. A tooling gotcha: RFT is billed by training-loop wall-clock time (illustratively ~$100 per hour on o4-mini, mid-2026), not by tokens like SFT and DPO — so a poorly designed grader or long rollouts get expensive fast. Other clouds (Vertex AI, Bedrock, Azure) mostly offer SFT/LoRA today. Confirm live pricing before you commit.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: post-training turns a knowledgeable base model into a helpful assistant, and it has a toolkit — SFT imitates ideal answers, RLHF learns from ranked preferences through a reward model and a KL-leashed PPO loop, and DPO proves that loop has a closed form so you can optimise preferences directly, no reward model needed. Pick the tool by your data, your reward signal, and your infra.
