02Concept · AI Engineering

Tokenization & Token Economics

A token is the atom of everything that touches money and memory in AI engineering. It's the unit your context window is measured in, the unit your bill is measured in, and the reason a feature that's free at a hundred users can be ruinous at a million. Understand tokens and you can reason about cost before you ever write code.

Original study notes. An engineer's guide to tokens and what they cost. Pricing and context-window figures are current as of mid-2026 and drawn from providers' published pages — they change constantly, so treat the numbers as illustrative and check the live pricing page before you quote one. 00 Why it matters

The unit that runs the bill

Most engineers meet tokens as a nuisance — an error that says "context length exceeded" — and never look closer. That's a mistake, because the token is doing two jobs at once, and both decide whether your product is viable:

  • Tokens are the unit of context. A model's context window — everything it can "see" at once: your system prompt, the conversation, retrieved documents, its own output so far — is measured in tokens. Run out and the model literally cannot consider the rest.
  • Tokens are the unit of billing. Every major API prices per million tokens, and charges separately for the tokens you send (input) and the tokens it generates (output). Your monthly invoice is, almost entirely, a token count multiplied by a rate.

Because the same unit governs what fits and what you pay, a surprising amount of AI engineering is really token accounting. "Will this fit in the window?" and "what will this cost at scale?" are the same kind of question, asked of the same number. By the end of this lesson you'll be able to glance at a feature — "a support bot, a thousand chats a day" — and estimate its monthly bill in your head, then know the two levers that cut it.

The reframe

Stop thinking in words and characters; start thinking in tokens. They don't line up — and the places where they don't (long words, code, other languages) are exactly where costs hide. The first half of this lesson is what a token is; the second half is what it costs.

01 The atom

What a token is ▸

A token is not a word and not a character — it's a subword chunk, drawn from a fixed vocabulary the model learned (typically 50,000 to 200,000+ entries). Common words are usually a single token. Rare words, names, code, and anything unusual get chopped into several. The rough conversion everyone keeps in their head, for English: about 4 characters per token, or ~100 tokens per 75 words. But "rough" is the operative word — watch where it breaks:

The pattern to take away: token count tracks how "surprising" the text is to the tokenizer. Ordinary English prose — what these models read most of in training — is dense, near one token per short word. Rare words, source code (with its punctuation and indentation), random IDs, and non-Latin scripts are out of distribution, so they shatter into many small pieces. A tokenizer is, quite literally, a learned text compressor: familiar text compresses well, unfamiliar text doesn't.

02 The mechanism

BPE in one idea

How does a tokenizer decide where to split? The dominant method is Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), and its core idea is almost embarrassingly simple. Start with individual characters. Then repeatedly find the most frequent adjacent pair and merge it into a single new token — thousands of times, until you hit your target vocabulary size. Frequent sequences like th, ing, and ␣def get promoted to their own tokens; rare combinations never do and stay fragmented.

That's the whole trick — it was a 1990s data-compression algorithm (Gage, 1994) that Sennrich and colleagues adapted for language models in 2016, and it still powers most tokenizers today. A few variants are worth recognizing by name, because they explain quirks you'll hit:

  • Byte-level BPE (OpenAI's tiktoken, GPT-2 onward) merges over raw UTF-8 bytes instead of characters. The payoff: any string is representable — emoji, rare scripts, garbage input — with no "unknown token." The cost: anything not in the merge table falls back to one-token-per-byte, which is why exotic input gets expensive.
  • WordPiece (BERT-family) merges by likelihood gain rather than raw frequency and marks word continuations with ##.
  • SentencePiece (Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama) treats text as a raw stream including spaces (it represents a space with a ▁ marker), so it needs no language-specific pre-splitting — which matters for languages like Chinese and Japanese that don't put spaces between words.
Why this explains the code tax

BPE learns its merges from training text that's mostly prose, so it builds efficient tokens for English words and few for deep indentation or unusual punctuation. That's why code historically tokenized badly — in the GPT-2 era, every single space of indentation was its own token. Newer tokenizers (GPT-4's cl100k_base and later) added dedicated tokens for runs of whitespace, making code markedly cheaper. Same text, newer tokenizer, fewer tokens: a reminder that "how many tokens is this?" has no answer until you name the tokenizer.

03 A gotcha

Every model brings its own tokenizer

Here's a trap that catches teams in production: each model family has a different tokenizer, so the same text is a different number of tokens depending on which model you ask. Count your prompt with OpenAI's tiktoken and you'll get one number; that number is wrong for Claude or Gemini, sometimes by 15–20% or more. Anthropic is explicit that tiktoken under-counts Claude tokens and recommends its own count_tokens endpoint; Google ships a free countTokens API for Gemini. The rule is simple: count with the tokenizer of the model you'll actually call.

the only reliable way to know a token count

# WRONG for non-OpenAI models — tiktoken is OpenAI's tokenizer
n = len(tiktoken.encoding_for_model("gpt-4o").encode(text))   # off for Claude/Gemini

# RIGHT — ask the target model's own counter
n = client.messages.count_tokens(model="claude-...", messages=[...])   # Anthropic
n = client.models.count_tokens(model="gemini-...", contents=text)        # Google

And it shifts within a vendor, too: when Anthropic moved to a newer tokenizer in the Opus 4.7 generation, the same text counted as meaningfully more tokens than on older Claude models — so a cost estimate made for one model version can be wrong for the next. "Token count" is never an absolute property of your text; it's a property of (text, tokenizer).

The multilingual tax

The starkest consequence of English-trained tokenizers is what researchers call tokenizer unfairness: the same sentence costs far more tokens in some languages than in English. A widely-cited 2023 study (Petrov et al.) measured differences up to ~15× across languages; non-Latin scripts are hit hardest — Burmese around 10× English, with Hindi, Thai, Armenian, and others several times over. The mechanism is the byte-level fallback from §2: a character outside the Latin range is several UTF-8 bytes, and if that byte sequence was rare in training, it never merged into an efficient token — so one visible character can become two, three, or four tokens.

Why this is a real engineering and fairness problem

Because tokens are the billing unit and the context unit, a user writing in Burmese or Hindi pays several times more for the same request and gets a smaller effective context window for the same content — a documented equity issue, not a rounding error. Newer, larger-vocabulary tokenizers (GPT-4o's o200k_base, Gemini's ~256K vocabulary) have narrowed the gap — one Tamil sentence dropped from 68 tokens to 21 — but they have not closed it. If you serve a multilingual audience, measure token costs in the languages your users actually write, not just in English.

04 The memory budget

Context windows in 2026

The context window is the token budget for a single request — the most the model can attend to at once. Windows have grown enormously: 1 million tokens is now the high end across the major families, with the occasional larger tier. Here's the rough state of play (and remember from the last lesson: a big window doesn't mean a reliable one — quality erodes as you fill it).

Model family (2026)Context windowNote

Three caveats keep the big numbers honest, and all three are token facts:

  • Long context is re-paid every turn. Filling a 1M window isn't a one-time cost — in a conversation, that context is re-sent (and re-billed as input) on each turn. We make this concrete in §6.
  • Quality degrades before the window fills. The "lost in the middle" effect from the last lesson means a 1M window is not 1M tokens of reliable attention. Curate; don't dump.
  • Some providers charge a long-context premium. Gemini 2.5 Pro, for instance, roughly doubles its input price above a 200K-token prompt. Big prompts can cost more per token, not just more in total.
05 The bill

The price of a token ▸

Now the money. Two facts shape every cost calculation. First, you pay separately for input and output tokens. Second — and this surprises people — output tokens cost several times more than input tokens, typically 3–5×. The reason is exactly the prefill-versus-decode split from the last lesson: input is read in one parallel pass (cheap per token), while output is generated one token at a time, each requiring a full pass through the model (slow, memory-bound, expensive). Output length is usually the cost lever that matters most — "be concise" isn't just style, it's budget. 06 The trap and the lever

Caching & the agent blow-up ▸

Here's the cost trap that ambushes every team building chatbots and agents. The API is stateless — it remembers nothing between calls. So to continue a conversation, you must resend the entire history every single turn: the system prompt, every prior message, every tool call and result. Turn 10 re-sends everything from turns 1–9 as fresh input. Watch what that does to the bill:

That quadratic growth is the cost-engineering insight of agent and chat systems: your bill is dominated by re-sent history, not by the new tokens each turn. A 50-turn agent run can pay for its early context dozens of times over. Two mechanisms tame it, and they're easy to confuse:

KV cache (within one request)

  • During generation, the model caches the attention keys/values of tokens it has already processed, so each new output token doesn't recompute the whole sequence.
  • It's what makes decode tractable at all — but it lives inside a single request and its memory grows with length (another reason long context is costly to serve).

Prompt caching (across requests)

  • You mark a stable prefix — system prompt, tools, fixed context — and the provider reuses its computed state across calls.
  • Cache reads cost ~0.1× of input (a 90% discount) on Anthropic and Google; writes carry a small one-time premium. This is the direct antidote to the N² blow-up.

Prompt caching is close to free money for any workload with a repeated prefix — the same system prompt across every chat, the same retrieved document across many questions. The discipline is to put the stable content first and the volatile content last, because a cache is a prefix match: change one byte early and everything after it is recomputed at full price. (Batch APIs add another ~50% off for work that can tolerate delay.) We'll go deep on caching strategy in the inference-optimization phase; for now, know that it exists and that it's the second great lever on cost.

07 Put a number on it

A worked cost estimate

Time to use everything. The skill that separates an AI engineer from someone who "tried the API once" is being able to estimate a feature's monthly bill on the back of an envelope — before committing to a model. Let's price a realistic feature end to end.

Worked estimatetokens → dollars / month
A customer-support assistant

A support bot handles 1,000 chats a day. Each chat is one round: a request carrying 3,000 input tokens (system prompt + a retrieved help article + the user's question) and a 500-token answer. What does a month cost, and how do we cut it?

Work the numbers

Monthly volume

1,000 chats/day × 30 = 30,000 requests/month. Input: 30,000 × 3,000 = 90M tokens. Output: 30,000 × 500 = 15M tokens.

Cost on a workhorse

At $3 in / $15 out per 1M: input 90 × $3 = $270, output 15 × $15 = $225. Total ≈ $495 / month. (Note the 500 output tokens cost almost as much as the 3,000 input tokens — the output rate dominates.)

Lever 1 · model choice

Move the routine traffic to a cheap model ($0.30 in / $2.50 out): input 90 × $0.30 = $27, output 15 × $2.50 = $37.50. Total ≈ $64.50 / month — roughly 8× cheaper for the same volume.

Lever 2 · caching

Most of those 3,000 input tokens are a stable prefix (system prompt + the same help articles). Say 2,500 of 3,000 are cacheable, billed at 0.1×. On the $3 model: cached input 75M × $0.30 = $22.50, uncached 15M × $3 = $45, output unchanged $225. Total ≈ $292.50 — the input bill alone fell from $270 to $67.50.

The estimate

So the same feature ranges from ~$65 to ~$495 a month depending on two decisions you control. Time = O(output tokens); cost = O(input + 5×output tokens), times the model's rate, minus what you cache.

Say it back in one breath

“Thirty thousand requests a month, ninety million input tokens and fifteen million output. On a mid-tier model that's about five hundred dollars; the output rate, not the token count, dominates. I'd route the routine cases to a cheap model and cache the stable prompt prefix at a tenth of the price — together that's roughly an eight-to-fifteen-fold cut, no quality lost on the easy traffic.”

Dry-run the edges: a chatbot (multi-turn) re-sends history → multiply input by the turn count and lean hard on caching (§6); a non-English audience → inflate the token estimate several-fold (§3); a reasoning model → add the hidden thinking tokens to output (Lesson 01, §6). Always sanity-check against the provider's own pricing page — rates move monthly.

This is why "token economics" is a real skill and not a footnote. The exact prices will be stale by the time you read this — that's fine. What doesn't go stale is the shape of the calculation: volume × tokens-per-call × rate, split input from output, then subtract what you can cache and downshift to a cheaper model. Run that estimate before you build, and you'll never be surprised by an invoice.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole lesson is one unit doing two jobs: a token is the atom of both the context window and the bill, so reasoning about tokens is reasoning about what fits and what it costs. Get fluent in tokens and cost stops being a surprise and becomes a design input.

Self-test before you move on

Cover the notes and answer aloud:
(1) Why isn't “how many tokens is this string?” a well-formed question until you add one more piece of information — and what is it?
(2) Give the ~4-chars rule, then name three kinds of text where it badly under-counts and say why.
(3) In one sentence, how does BPE build its vocabulary? Why does byte-level BPE never produce an “unknown” token?
(4) Why does the same sentence cost more tokens in Burmese than in English — and what two concrete harms does that cause the Burmese-speaking user?
(5) Why do output tokens cost several times more than input tokens?
(6) A chatbot's cost is climbing far faster than its traffic. What's the mechanism, and what's the single most effective lever to flatten it?
