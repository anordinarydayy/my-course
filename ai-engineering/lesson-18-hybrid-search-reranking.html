Hybrid Search & Re-ranking

A single retriever can only see one half of "relevance." Dense vector search understands meaning but goes blind on exact codes and IDs; keyword search nails exact tokens but goes blind on paraphrase. This lesson refuses the choice: run both, fuse their rankings with RRF, then re-rank the shortlist with a cross-encoder. It is the default production retrieval pattern of 2026 — recall won in stage one, precision won in stage two.

00 The blind spot in one index

Semantic ≠ lexical

You stood up a vector index in Lesson 16 over the chunks you cut in Lesson 17, and for a week it felt like magic: paraphrases, synonyms, vague intent — the embedding model "understood" all of it. Then a user typed SKU-44819 and the system confidently returned five semantically adjacent products, none of which contained that string. The dense retriever wasn't broken. It was doing exactly what it does: finding neighbours in meaning-space. The trouble is that an exact identifier has no neighbours in meaning-space — it is an arbitrary token, and the nearest embeddings are simply other products in the same aisle.

This is the bottleneck of single-retriever RAG. Dense embedding search and keyword (BM25) search each fail on a different, predictable class of query, and they fail in opposite directions:

Dense vector search misses…

  • exact identifiers: SKUs, order numbers, error codes like ERR_CONN_RST
  • API symbols, version strings (v2.5.1), acronyms
  • rare / out-of-vocabulary terms with no semantic cluster
  • cold-start corpora with nothing yet to embed against

BM25 keyword search misses…

  • paraphrases: "random disconnects" vs "wireless instability"
  • synonyms the document never spelled out
  • long natural-language questions whose words aren't in the doc
  • intent that lives between the words, not in them

The naive instinct is to pick the better one for your corpus and move on. That's the mistake. Relevance has two faces — semantic and lexical — and most real queries mix them in the same sentence: a model number and a vague complaint, an error code and a how-do-I question. Choose one retriever and you have permanently blinded yourself to half of every query. The fix is not a better single retriever; it's to stop choosing.

The one-line version

Semantic is not lexical. Dense search knows what you mean; BM25 knows what you said. Run both, merge the rankings, and re-order the survivors — that's the whole lesson, and the rest is making each step robust.

01 Fuse, don't choose

Reciprocal Rank Fusion ▸

So run both retrievers in parallel and merge their two ranked lists. The obvious merge — add the scores — fails immediately: a BM25 score is an unbounded term-frequency quantity, a cosine similarity lives in roughly [-1, 1], and there is no honest constant that puts them on the same axis. Hand-tune a weight and it's brittle, corpus-specific, and silently wrong the moment your data shifts.

Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) sidesteps the whole problem by throwing the raw scores away and using only rank position:

reciprocal rank fusionscore(d) = sum over retrievers i of  1 / (k + rank_i(d))     # default k = 60

A document that lands respectably in both lists accumulates two contributions and beats a document that tops only one — consensus wins. Watch a single shared document, ranked #1 in neither list, climb to the top of the fused ranking:

That one-line trick is why RRF is the default hybrid-merge in Elasticsearch, OpenSearch, Qdrant, Weaviate, Azure AI Search, and MongoDB Atlas. The constant k=60 comes from Cormack, Clarke & Buettcher (2009) and has held up for some fifteen years: a small k lets the very top ranks dominate, a large k flattens and dilutes the fusion. Equal-weight score blending (alpha ≈ 0.5) is a respectable alternative baseline, and Elasticsearch now ships weighted RRF so you can bias toward the lexical or semantic leg per corpus. But fusion only casts a wide net — it still ranks crudely. To turn recall into precision, you need a second stage.

02 Three retrieval shapes

Dense vs sparse vs hybrid

Before the second stage, fix the vocabulary. There are three retrieval shapes you'll wire together, and a fourth that is quietly eating the word "sparse":

ApproachMatches onWins atFails atRecall@10 (approx)
Dense / vectorsemantic similarity (cosine)paraphrase, synonyms, intentexact codes, IDs, rare terms, cold-startgood, incomplete
Sparse / BM25exact lexical overlapSKUs, error codes, names, acronyms; debuggableparaphrase, synonyms, word ordergood, incomplete
Hybrid + RRFboth, fused by rankbroad lexical and semantic coverageneeds two indexes + a reranker to orderhighest, ~91% reported
Learned sparse / SPLADElearned term weights + expansionexact match with semantic weightingheavier index than plain BM25state-of-the-art on MS MARCO

That last row matters more every quarter. SPLADE is learned sparse retrieval: a neural model reads a chunk and emits a sparse vector of term weights over the whole vocabulary — including terms the document never literally contained (learned expansion) — and stores it in an ordinary inverted index. You get BM25-style exact matching plus learned, semantic-aware term weighting, and it reaches state-of-the-art on MS MARCO. In 2026, "sparse" increasingly means learned-sparse, not just term frequencies, and several vector databases now offer native sparse-vector support so you can serve SPLADE the same way you serve BM25.

Why hybrid, concretely

Hybrid (BM25 + dense) reaches roughly 91% recall@10 in one widely-cited measurement — clearly above either retriever alone, because the two failure modes barely overlap. You are not averaging two mediocre signals; you are covering each one's blind spot with the other's strength. The cost is real, though: two indexes to build and keep in sync, and a fused list that is still only crudely ordered. Coverage is solved; ordering is not.

03 Two stages, two jobs

Retrieve, then re-rank ▸

Hybrid fusion gets the right documents into the room but seats them badly — the best answer might sit at rank 18. The naive fix, "score every chunk with the most accurate model you have," is impossible: the accurate model is far too slow to run over a million chunks per query. So split retrieval into two stages with two different model shapes, each optimised for a different thing.

Stage one casts a wide, cheap net for recall: get every right answer somewhere in the top ~100–150. Stage two reorders that shortlist for precision: run a slow, accurate cross-encoder only on the survivors. Step through the funnel — and watch the invariant that governs the whole pattern:

The invariant — never collapse the stages

Recall is won in stage one, precision in stage two. If the right document isn't in the bi-encoder's net, no reranker on earth can recover it — the cross-encoder only ever sees the shortlist. That cuts both ways into your numbers: retrieve wide (top ~100–150, where coverage is cheap) and rerank a shortlist (~50–75; quality typically plateaus past ~100 candidates, so more just buys latency). Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval retrieved the top 150, then reranked down to the top 20 it handed the model. Wide then narrow — and never the reverse.

What makes stage one fast and stage two accurate isn't a tuning knob — it's two genuinely different model architectures. That contrast is the next section.

04 The architectural split

Bi-encoder vs cross-encoder ▸

The two stages run two model shapes that differ in exactly one decision: when the query and the document meet. A bi-encoder keeps them apart; a cross-encoder fuses them. Everything else — the speed, the accuracy, whether you can precompute — falls out of that one choice. Watch both pipelines side by side:

PropertyBi-encoder (stage 1)Cross-encoder / reranker (stage 2)
Query + doc combined?No — encoded separatelyYes — jointly, with cross-attention
Precompute doc vectors?Yes, at index timeNo — run per pair at query time
Query-time costANN lookup, ~O(1)O(n) over the shortlist
Speedfast, millions of docsslow, tens–hundreds of pairs
Accuracygood recall, crude rankinghigh-precision ordering
Job in pipelinecast a wide netreorder the shortlist
Example modelsall-MiniLM, OpenAI / Voyage / Cohere embeddingsCohere Rerank 3.5/4.0, Voyage rerank-2.5, bge-reranker-v2-m3, Jina v2, zerank

There's a celebrated middle ground worth naming: ColBERT and late interaction. Instead of one vector per document, it keeps one embedding per token and scores a query against a document with token-level MaxSim — "late" because the query and document tokens interact only at scoring time, not inside the encoder. The accuracy lands between bi- and cross-encoders, and serving engines like PLAID and WARP make it fast, while SPLATE adapts ColBERTv2 to generate candidates with sparse retrieval (CPU-friendly). The cost is blunt: storing a vector per token is far larger than one per chunk. ColPali even pushes late interaction onto PDF page images. For most teams, though, the workhorse remains bi-encoder retrieve → cross-encoder rerank.

05 Fix the query, not just the index

Lifting recall at the query side

Two great stages can't save a bad query. If the user's words barely overlap the document's words — a terse fragment, an ambiguous pronoun three turns into a conversation, vocabulary that simply differs from the corpus — recall is lost before retrieval even runs. So the cheapest place to win recall back is often the query itself. Three transformations, in rising order of ambition:

Rewrite

  • clean and disambiguate the raw query
  • resolve "it / that one" against the conversation
  • one in, one out — cheap, low-risk

Multi-query

  • LLM writes 3–4 phrasings of the question
  • retrieve each one separately
  • fuse all the result lists back with RRF

HyDE

  • LLM drafts a hypothetical answer document
  • embed that, not the question
  • search doc-to-doc — bridges the vocab gap

HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings) is the clever one and the dangerous one. The insight: a question and its answer are written in different registers, so a question embedding sits awkwardly in a space full of answer embeddings. Have the model hallucinate a plausible answer paragraph, embed that fake document, and search with it — now you're comparing document-to-document. The catch is right there in the word "hallucinate": HyDE can invent specifics, and 2025's HyDE-Rethink analysis separates genuinely useful hypothetical generation from knowledge leakage, where the model is just reciting memorised facts rather than helping retrieval. Gains are dataset-dependent — measure with the eval gates of Lesson 14 before you trust it. Multi-query is the safer cousin: it never asks the model to assert facts, only to rephrase, and then leans on RRF to fuse the evidence.

Where this plugs in

Every query transform feeds the same stage-one candidate pool from Section 03 — and multi-query literally reuses the RRF node from Section 01 to merge its parallel retrievals. You are not adding a new pipeline; you are widening the mouth of the funnel you already built. Now let's run one real, nasty query through the whole thing.

06 The whole stack on one query

The production stack

Time to make it concrete. One messy support query, two relevant documents, and the difference between each retriever alone and the full hybrid-plus-rerank stack.

Worked examplesupport KB · code + paraphrase
The query that needs both halves

A support assistant indexes a networking vendor's knowledge base. A user asks: "My RT-AX88U keeps dropping Wi-Fi and throwing ERR_CONN_RST 0x80072EE7 ever since the last update — how do I stop the random disconnects?" This query is deliberately mixed: exact tokens (the model RT-AX88U, the code ERR_CONN_RST 0x80072EE7) and a paraphrase ("random disconnects…since the last update") of an article actually titled "Wireless instability after firmware upgrade." Two docs are relevant: Doc-A, the exact error-code page; Doc-B, the firmware-instability article (which contains no error code at all).

Think it through — what does each leg do?

Dense only

The embedding understands "random disconnects since the last update" and ranks Doc-B near the top (~rank 2). But the error code has no semantic neighbours, so Doc-A languishes around rank 25 — the user's most precise clue is wasted.

BM25 only

The mirror image: it matches the literal RT-AX88U and ERR_CONN_RST tokens and ranks Doc-A at #1, but it shares almost no words with "wireless instability after firmware upgrade," so Doc-B falls to ~rank 30. Each retriever alone surfaces one correct doc and buries the other.

Hybrid fuse (recall)

Run both and fuse with RRF (k=60). Doc-A (dense ~25, BM25 ~1) and Doc-B (dense ~2, BM25 ~30) each pick up a contribution from both lists, so both land safely inside the top ~20 candidates. Recall is won — neither correct doc was dropped.

Cross-encoder rerank (precision)

The reranker reads the full query jointly with each candidate, recognises that Doc-A directly addresses the exact error code under the named model, and promotes it to #1, with Doc-B at #2 as supporting context. Precision is won.

the two-stage pipeline, in pseudo-config# STAGE 1 — wide net, recall
dense  = ann_search(embed(query), top_k=150)
sparse = bm25_search(query,        top_k=150)
cands  = rrf_fuse(dense, sparse, k=60)        # Doc-A & Doc-B both survive

# STAGE 2 — reorder shortlist, precision
ranked = rerank(query, cands[:75])             # cross-encoder, per-pair
context = ranked[:5]                          # Doc-A #1, Doc-B #2 → LLM
Say it back in one breath

“Vector-only missed the error code, BM25-only missed the paraphrase — each saw half the query. Fusing both with RRF guaranteed both correct docs survived stage one, and the cross-encoder, reading query and doc together, ordered the exact-code page first. Recall came from running both retrievers; precision came from the reranker. Neither stage could have done the other's job.”

This mirrors Anthropic's reported result: adding BM25 to embeddings and then reranking cut failed retrievals by 67%. The model now answers from the precise fix, not a plausible-but-wrong neighbour.

The 2026 reranker landscape

The reranker has become a small arms race, and the names are worth carrying. On the paid side, Cohere Rerank 3.5 (a cross-encoder, ~4k-token context, 100+ languages, roughly 100 ms) gave way to Rerank 4.0 with pro (quality) and fast (latency) tiers, a 32k-token context, and JSON / semi-structured document support. Voyage rerank-2.5 and rerank-2.5-lite also pushed context to 32k and added instruction-following — you steer ranking with a natural-language instruction like "prioritise the most recent official documentation," which is pitched squarely at agentic and conversational retrieval. Open-weight rerankers are closing the gap fast: bge-reranker-v2-m3 (multilingual), Jina Reranker v2 (multilingual, plus multimodal rerankers for images and PDFs), and ZeroEntropy's zerank-1/zerank-2 (open-weight, with vendor-reported NDCG@10 around 0.85 at ~60 ms and roughly $0.025 per million tokens — treat every such number as approximate and mid-2026).

Two structural shifts matter more than any single model. First, context windows jumped ~4x to 32k tokens, so a reranker can now judge a whole long chunk or document instead of a truncated snippet. Second, native hybrid + rerank is built into the platforms — Elasticsearch, OpenSearch, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Vespa, Azure AI Search, MongoDB Atlas — so your job is to wire stages together and choose candidate counts, not hand-roll fusion. And because reranking adds latency and cost, the 2026 default (backed by a wave of "are LLM rerankers worth it?" studies) is to rerank only a shortlist of ~50–100, keeping the overhead bounded.

The compounding payoff — and what's next

The stack compounds. Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval — contextualise each chunk before embedding and BM25, then fuse and rerank — reported cumulative gains: contextual embeddings alone cut retrieval failures 35%, adding contextual BM25 reached 49%, and adding reranking reached 67% (top-20 failure rate 5.7% → 1.9%), with prompt caching making the per-chunk contextualisation cheap. That is the shape of modern RAG retrieval. The reranked top-5 you just produced flows into answer generation in Lesson 19; the whole retriever is held in place by the eval gates of Lesson 14 and measured end-to-end by the retrieval evals of Lesson 21. Coverage, ordering, and the query — three levers, one funnel.

Summary & self-test
