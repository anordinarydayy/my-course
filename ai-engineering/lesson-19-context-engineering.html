19Concept · AI Engineering

Context Engineering

Context windows ballooned past a million tokens, and the tempting instinct is to pour everything in and let the model sort it out. It doesn't. A model attends worst to the middle of its context, grows less reliable the longer the input runs, and is actively misled by plausible-but-wrong neighbours — all while you pay for every token twice, in money and in diluted attention. This lesson is about the discipline that succeeded prompt engineering: deciding what earns a place in the window, in what order, and why a smaller, curated context beats a stuffed one.

Original study notes. A concept lesson on managing the context window. It draws on public sources — the 2025–2026 “context engineering” writing from Anthropic and others, the “lost in the middle” finding (Liu et al.), Chroma’s “context rot” study, and Chip Huyen’s AI Engineering for framing — but every explanation, diagram, and example here is our own. 00 The instinct that fails

Stuff it all in

You have a model with a 200K — or a million — token window, a retriever that can fetch fifty passages, and a conversation that has run for forty turns. The path of least resistance is obvious: concatenate everything. Dump all the retrieved documents, the full chat history, every tool result, and the user’s question into one giant prompt and let the model figure out what matters. It needs no curation code, throws nothing away, and feels safe — surely more information can only help.

It is the single most common mistake in production AI, and it loses in four distinct ways. The model reads the middle of a long context far worse than the edges, so a fact buried at position 11 of 20 may as well not be there. Accuracy rots as the input grows, sagging long before the window is technically “full.” Distractor passages — topically similar but wrong — don’t just add noise, they actively pull the answer off. And every junk token costs you twice over: once on the bill (token economics, Lesson 02), and once in attention, which is a finite resource a transformer spreads thinner the more tokens it must consider. We will take each failure in turn, then build the discipline that answers all four.

The one-line version

More context is not more signal. A bigger window is permission to be lazy, and the model pays for your laziness with worse answers at higher cost. The job is not to fill the window — it is to fill it with just the right tokens for the next step.

01 Position matters

Lost in the middle ▸

Start with the most counter-intuitive failure. Take a single fact the model needs — the “gold” chunk — and hide it among a stack of irrelevant passages. Hold everything constant and just slide the gold chunk from the front of the context, through the middle, to the end. The fact never changes; only its position does. Watch what happens to accuracy:

That U-shaped curve is the lost-in-the-middle effect, documented across models and tasks: accuracy is highest when the relevant fact sits at the very start (primacy) or the very end (recency), and can fall by thirty points or more when it sits in the middle. The bias holds even for models explicitly built for long context. The ghost line adds the second blow — as the surrounding context grows from a few thousand to a hundred thousand tokens, the whole curve sags. The immediate, free win is just to stop fighting these biases: put your most important material at an edge, not in the middle, and keep the window short enough that the curve hasn’t sagged. That instinct — place by position, not by “natural order” — is the seed of context engineering.

02 It rots, and distractors bite

Context rot & distractors

Lost-in-the-middle is about where a token sits. The deeper problem is that a long context degrades the model everywhere at once. Chroma’s mid-2025 “context rot” study ran eighteen frontier models — across the GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Qwen families — and found every one of them becomes monotonically less reliable as the input grows. Crucially, the decay starts long before the window is full: a model advertised at 200K tokens can wobble noticeably around 50K. The window size is a ceiling, not a promise; the model does not use those tokens uniformly.

The same work isolated the most actionable result of all. A prompt of a few hundred focused tokens scored near-perfectly, while the same answer diluted into a hundred-thousand-token haystack degraded sharply across every model family. Same information, just surrounded by more — and accuracy fell. That is the empirical heart of this lesson: dilution itself is a cost, independent of whether the extra tokens are wrong.

When the extra tokens are wrong-but-plausible, it gets worse. A distractor — a passage on the right topic with the wrong answer — doesn’t merely waste space; it pulls the model toward its content. One distractor measurably lowers accuracy versus a clean baseline, and four compound the damage. There is a genuinely surprising twist: models scored better on a randomly shuffled haystack than on a logically coherent one, because coherent surrounding text manufactures more believable decoys. “Natural document order” is not your friend. And the costs are not only in quality:

An irrelevant passage

  • Off-topic — easy for the model to ignore.
  • Wastes tokens and a little attention, but rarely changes the answer.
  • The mild tax of a stuffed window.

A distractor passage

  • On-topic, confidently wrong — looks like the answer.
  • Actively competes for the model’s attention and can win.
  • One can flip a correct answer to a wrong one.

It is worth naming the failure modes so you can spot them: poisoning (an error or hallucination enters the context and is then repeatedly referenced), distraction (an over-long history crowds out the model’s own trained knowledge), confusion (superfluous content — including too many tool definitions — lowers answer quality), and clash (contradictory passages derail the reasoning). All four are diseases of an unmanaged window. And underneath them sits the bill: attention is roughly quadratic in the number of tokens, so a context twice as long is paid for in both money and latency, and re-sending a growing prompt every turn dominates the cost of any long-running task. Junk tokens are the worst purchase in AI engineering — you pay full price to make the answer worse.

03 The successor discipline

Context engineering

If the window is a liability when stuffed, the fix is not a bigger window — it is curation. In June 2025 the practice got a name. Tobi Lütke called it “the art of providing all the context for the task to be plausibly solvable by the LLM”; Andrej Karpathy sharpened it to “the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step.” Anthropic frames it operationally as curating and maintaining the optimal set of tokens during inference, against a finite attention budget — because attention is a depleting resource, the goal is the smallest set of high-signal tokens that does the job. Prompt engineering — wording the instruction well — is now just one move inside the larger discipline. The reframing matters: with frontier models this good, most failures are no longer model failures, they are context failures. The quality gap is what you feed the model, not the magic words.

Concretely, context engineering is five repeatable moves:

  • Select — retrieve wide, then rerank down to a token budget. Fetch fifty candidates with hybrid search; let a cross-encoder reranker (the one from Lesson 18) push the few that actually answer the question to the top.
  • Order — place the highest-signal material at the edges, exploiting primacy and recency instead of fighting them. The gold chunk goes first or last, never the middle.
  • Compress — summarize or extract so each surviving chunk shrinks to its answer-bearing sentence. Prune low-value tokens rather than carrying whole paragraphs.
  • Deduplicate — drop near-duplicate chunks. Two overlapping passages are not twice the evidence; they are one signal at twice the dilution.
  • Structure — give the survivors clear sections and delimiters (XML or markdown) so the model can tell instruction from data from history at a glance.

One invariant ties them together: every token must earn its place. The question is never “could this be useful?” — almost anything could — but “does this token raise the odds of a correct next step more than it dilutes the rest?” Held against the stuff-it-all default, the contrast is stark on every axis that matters:

04 The five moves in action

Curating a real prompt ▸

Theory lands when you watch it fix a concrete failure. Here is a customer-support bot answering one question — “What is the refund window for digital subscriptions?” — first the naive way, then re-shaped by the five moves. Step through it and watch the context shrink while the answer flips from wrong to right and the meters fall:

Worked examplesupport RAG · refund window
The buried gold chunk

A support bot must answer “What is the refund window for digital subscriptions?” The naive pipeline runs top-20 vector retrieval and concatenates all twenty chunks (~14K tokens) in raw similarity order. The one chunk that actually says “14-day refund window for digital subscriptions” lands at position 11 — dead center. Around it sit distractors: a chunk about the 30-day window for physical goods, a chunk about cancellation, and two near-duplicate FAQ snippets. The bot answers “30 days” — confidently wrong, pulled straight from the physical-goods distractor — and burns ~14K input tokens doing it.

Think it through — the five moves

1 · Select

Keep the wide retrieval, but add a cross-encoder reranker. The true refund chunk is scored against the question, not just nearby in embedding space — it jumps to rank 1 and the distractors sink.

2 · Deduplicate

The two near-identical FAQ snippets collapse to one. They were never two pieces of evidence — just one signal, doubling the dilution.

3 · Compress

Each survivor is extractively summarized to its answer-bearing sentence. The gold chunk becomes the single line “14-day window for digital subscriptions.”

4 · Drop distractors

Cap to a ~1.5K token budget. The physical-goods and cancellation chunks fall outside the budget and never enter the window — the 30-day decoy is gone.

5 · Order

Place the gold chunk at the very front, and restate the query at the end — exploiting primacy and recency instead of fighting them.

Say it back in one breath

“The model wasn’t the problem and a bigger window wouldn’t have helped — the gold fact was buried in the middle next to a plausible 30-day distractor, so the bot answered ‘30 days’ on 14K tokens. Reranking floated the right chunk to the top, dedup and compression cut the fat, a token budget evicted the distractors, and ordering put the answer at the edge. Same model, ~1.5K tokens, correct answer, about 10× cheaper.”

The win came from making every remaining token earn its place — not from more context, and not from a better model.

This is also the answer to the “did long context kill RAG?” question from Phase 3: it didn’t — retrieval got reframed as the Select stage of context engineering. The default 2026 production pipeline is still hybrid retrieval, cross-encoder rerank, dedup, and a token-budget cap; long windows just removed the hard ceiling, they didn’t remove the discipline.

05 Spend the window on purpose

The token budget ▸

The cleanest way to make “every token earns its place” concrete is to treat the window as a fixed budget you allocate, the way you’d allocate a memory budget — and to set it deliberately below what the model technically allows. Even with a million-token window, you might cap a request at 8K, because you know the curve from Section 1 sags long before the ceiling. Four things compete for that budget: the system prompt and tools, the retrieved chunks, the conversation history, and the query. Stuff them and you overflow; engineer them and they fit:

Two production lessons fall out of the budget view. First, the budget is a policy, not an accident: you decide, per request, how many tokens each segment may claim, and you trim deterministically when the sum exceeds the cap — rerank-and-cap the retrieval, compact the history, merge duplicates — rather than hoping it fits. Second, on agents and at scale, the dominant cost is re-sending a growing prefix every turn, so the highest-leverage metric is the KV-cache hit rate. The Manus team’s hard-won advice: keep the prompt prefix stable (a timestamp at the top silently invalidates the cache every call), keep context append-only, and offload overflow to the file system as “unlimited context.” The frontier of 2026 pushes this further with just-in-time retrieval: instead of pre-loading documents, the agent holds lightweight identifiers and pulls data on demand through tools — keeping the working window lean by default and spending budget only when a step actually needs it.

06 When context never stops

When context never stops growing

A single prompt is the easy case — you curate once and send. An agent is the hard case, and it is where context engineering becomes survival rather than polish. Over fifty or a hundred turns, every tool call, every observation, every intermediate thought piles into the window. Left alone, the same support bot from Section 4 would rot exactly as predicted: the context grows past the point where attention holds, the model starts referencing its own earlier mistakes (poisoning), and the run quietly degrades into nonsense while the bill climbs. The five moves still apply, but now they must run continuously, as the context is maintained across time. Four techniques carry the load:

  • Compaction — when the window approaches a threshold, summarize the older turns into a compact block and continue from the summary. The transcript stops being a liability and becomes a sliver, exactly like the history segment shrinking in Figure 3.
  • Memory / note-taking — write durable facts to files outside the window (a NOTES.md, a scratchpad, a progress log) and read them back on demand, so long-lived state never has to sit in the prompt being re-paid for every turn.
  • Context editing — automatically clear stale tool results once they are no longer needed, leaving a short placeholder. The window keeps the conclusions and sheds the raw bulk that produced them.
  • Sub-agent isolation — hand a self-contained job to a sub-agent with its own clean window, and let it return only a condensed summary. The orchestrator’s context never sees the sub-agent’s scratch work — it stays a one-line result.

These are not hypotheticals. In one published 100-turn web-search evaluation, combining a memory tool with context editing cut token use by about 84% and improved task performance by roughly 39% — the same information, far fewer tokens, a better result. A multi-agent research system that gave each sub-agent its own window beat a strong single-agent baseline on its research benchmark. And these patterns are now shipping as platform features rather than hand-rolled hacks: native memory tools, automatic context-editing policies, and SDK-level compaction. The 2026 move is from manually curating prompts to declaring policies the platform enforces.

Where this goes next

Context engineering is the load-bearing skill for everything in Phase 4 — Agents. An agent is, at bottom, a loop that decides what to put in the next context window; do that badly and no model is good enough to save the run, do it well and a modest model can sustain a hundred-turn task. The lost-in-the-middle curve, the rot, the five moves, the budget, compaction and memory — carry all of it forward. When we build agents next, “what enters the window, in what order, and what gets evicted” will be the central design question, every single turn.

Summary & self-test

One idea runs through the lesson: a context window is a finite attention budget, not a bucket — so curate what enters it (select, order, compress, deduplicate, structure), keep the high-signal material at the edges, and over an agent’s lifetime keep it bounded with compaction, memory, and context editing. Every token must earn its place.
