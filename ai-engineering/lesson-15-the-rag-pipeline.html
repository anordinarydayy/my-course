The RAG Pipeline

A model's knowledge is frozen at its training cutoff — it has never seen your wiki, last night's incident report, or the docs for the version you actually run, and it can't cite a source even when it's right. Retrieval-augmented generation is the cheapest durable fix: fetch the relevant text at query time and paste it into the context window, so the model answers from grounded, citable, updatable evidence. This is the keystone of Phase 3 — the shape every later lesson refines.

00 The bottleneck

Frozen at the cutoff

Ask a base model a question only your organization can answer — "what's the on-call rotation for the billing service?", "what changed in the v4.2 release?", "what did the post-incident review conclude?" — and it has no way to know. Its weights were frozen at a training cutoff months or years ago; your private data was never in them, and yesterday's data couldn't have been. Worse, even when it is right, it can't point you to a source you can verify. The model's knowledge is a snapshot, and you need a live feed.

The tempting fix is fine-tuning — keep training the model on your documents until it "knows" them. It's the wrong tool for this job, and knowing why is half the lesson. Fine-tuning is slow and costly to run, and every data refresh means another training run. It teaches behavior — style, format, tone, the shape of a good answer — far better than it injects discrete facts; cram a fact into the weights and the model may still hallucinate a confident variant of it. And it produces no citations: a fine-tuned model can't tell you which document a claim came from, because the claim is now smeared across billions of parameters. So when you ask the un-grounded model a question it can't answer, it does the worst possible thing — it answers anyway, fluently and confidently. That is the failure RAG exists to prevent.

The shape of the fix

Don't bake the knowledge into the weights — fetch it at query time and paste it into the context window. The model then answers from text it can see, quote, and link. Fine-tuning changes how the model behaves; retrieval changes what it knows right now. They are complementary, not rivals — and for fresh, private, citable knowledge, retrieval is the one you reach for first.

01 The first rung

Fetch it, don't bake it

It helps to place RAG on a ladder of ways to adapt a general model to your problem, in rough order of effort and cost. Prompting comes first — instructions only, no new knowledge. RAG is the next rung: it adds external knowledge at query time without touching the weights. Above it sit agents (multi-step action and tool use) and, at the top, fine-tuning (actually changing the weights). The lesson of the ladder is to climb only as high as your problem requires — and for "give the model private, fresh, citable knowledge," RAG is the first rung past prompting, the default move before you reach for anything heavier.

So why not skip RAG and pour everything into the prompt now that context windows reach a million tokens, or just fine-tune and be done? Because each alternative pays a different cost, and RAG sits at a sweet spot for one specific job. Hold the three side by side:

RAG

  • Update: edit a database, instantly
  • Freshness: as fresh as the index
  • Citations: yes — links the source
  • Per-query cost: low — only the relevant tokens
  • Best for: private, fresh, citable facts

Fine-tune

  • Update: retrain the model
  • Freshness: stale between runs
  • Citations: none
  • Per-query cost: low at inference
  • Best for: behavior, tone, format

Long context

  • Update: swap the pasted text
  • Freshness: as fresh as the paste
  • Citations: possible, manual
  • Per-query cost: high — pay for everything
  • Best for: small corpora, global reasoning
When you don't need RAG at all

Anthropic's own guidance: if your entire knowledge base fits comfortably under roughly 200K tokens, skip the pipeline — just put it all in the prompt (with prompt caching so you're not re-paying every call). RAG earns its complexity only when the corpus is too big to fit, or too costly to send in full, on every query. Don't build a retrieval stack to solve a problem that a paste-and-cache would handle. We'll return to this routing choice in Lesson 19.

02 The two-clock shape

Two clocks: ingest & query ▸

The naive mental model of RAG is one big function: "question goes in, answer comes out." That hides the most important structural fact about the system — it is actually two pipelines running on two different clocks, meeting only at a shared store. Offline ingest runs when your data changes (minutes, occasionally): load → chunk → embed → index. Online query runs on every question, in milliseconds: embed → retrieve → re-rank → assemble → generate. Separating them is the standard production pattern, because the two have opposite priorities — ingest can be slow and thorough; query must be fast and cheap. Step through the whole machine:

Notice the symmetry that ties the two clocks together: the same embedding model must embed both your chunks (offline) and the query (online), or the query vector and the chunk vectors live in different spaces and nearest-neighbor search is meaningless. That single shared component is why you can't casually swap embedding models without re-indexing everything. We'll unpack the store itself in Lesson 16, but the shape is now fixed: fill it slowly and carefully; read it fast and cheaply.

03 The invariant

Grounded, or confidently wrong ▸

Here is the one rule that makes RAG both powerful and fragile: a well-built RAG model answers ONLY from retrieved context. That grounding is the whole game — it's where the trustworthiness and the citations come from. But it has a dark twin. If retrieval misses — if the chunk that holds the answer was never fetched — the model does not say "I don't know." It fills the gap from stale memory and emits a fluent, authoritative, wrong answer, with no source and full confidence. A retrieval miss doesn't yield an abstention; it yields a hallucination. Watch the same question run with good and bad retrieval — the only thing that changes is what came back:

The unsettling lesson is that the model looks equally confident in both runs. You cannot tell a grounded answer from a hallucinated one by reading the answer alone — only by checking whether the right context was retrieved (which is exactly why the citation matters, and why Lesson 21 measures retrieval separately). This is what people mean when they say the quality ceiling of a RAG system is set at retrieval, not generation. The best model in the world cannot answer from a chunk that was never fetched. You can't prompt your way out of a retrieval miss.

Worked exampledocs Q&A bot · FrameworkX v4.2
The flag that shipped after the cutoff

A developer asks your docs bot: "In FrameworkX v4.2, what's the new flag to enable streaming responses?" Crucially, v4.2 shipped after the base model's training cutoff. The model has never seen it. Watch the same question hit a bare model and a RAG pipeline.

Bare model vs. RAG — same question, two outcomes

online query — runs per question, in millisecondsq = "enable streaming responses in FrameworkX v4.2?"
# hybrid retrieval: dense vectors + BM25 keywords, fused
hits = store.hybrid_search(embed(q), bm25(q), k=50)
# cross-encoder re-reads each (q, chunk) pair, keeps the best
top  = rerank(q, hits)[:5]
# the model answers ONLY from `top` — and cites it
answer = generate(prompt(q, context=top))
Bare model — stale memory

With no retrieval, the model answers from what it "remembers": it recommends stream_mode=on, a v3.x option that was renamed and removed in v4.2. No source, full confidence, and wrong — and the developer has no way to tell.

RAG — offline, the docs were already ingested

The v4.2 release notes were loaded, chunked at ~500 tokens, embedded, and indexed long before the question arrived. The index already holds the answer-bearing passage.

RAG — online, retrieval finds it

The question is embedded; hybrid search fires — dense catches the paraphrase "enable streaming responses," BM25 catches the literal token "v4.2" and the flag name. A reranker promotes the exact release-note passage to the top, and it's pasted into the prompt.

RAG — grounded answer + citation

The model returns the correct v4.2 flag with a clickable citation. Three payoffs: (1) correct on data newer than the cutoff; (2) a source the developer can verify; (3) when v4.3 ships next month, you just re-index — no retraining.

Now break it — delete the chunk

Remove the v4.2 passage from the index to simulate a retrieval miss, and re-run. The model doesn't abstain — it confidently hallucinates a plausible-but-wrong flag. Same model, same prompt template; the only thing that changed was whether retrieval found the right chunk. That is the whole lesson in one toggle.

Say it back in one breath

“A version-specific question about data newer than the model's cutoff. The bare model answered fluently from stale memory and got it wrong with no source; the RAG pipeline retrieved the v4.2 release note, reranked it to the top, and answered correctly with a citation. Delete that one chunk and the same model hallucinates — because in RAG the answer lives in the retrieved context, not the weights.”

Grounding is the source of both the trust and the fragility. Retrieval is the part you must get right; everything downstream only stitches what retrieval handed it.

04 The stage walk

Where quality is won or lost

If the ceiling is set at retrieval, then quality is decided stage by stage, long before the model ever generates a token. Walk the pipeline as a sequence of decisions, each of which can silently degrade the final answer even if every other stage is perfect:

StageThe decision — and where quality is won or lost
ChunkSize, overlap, boundaries. Too big dilutes the signal (the answer drowns in surrounding text); too small severs context (a fact loses the sentence that gave it meaning). This is the stage where pipelines silently fail. A common 2025–2026 default: recursive fixed-size splitting at ~400–512 tokens with 10–20% overlap. Several recent benchmarks found plain recursive chunking competitive with fancier "semantic" chunking on a cost/quality basis. See Lesson 17.
EmbedWhich model maps text to vectors. It decides whether "how do I reset my password" lands near the right passage about credential recovery. The 2025–2026 field churns fast: OpenAI text-embedding-3-large has stagnated, while Cohere embed-v4, Voyage 3/4, Google Gemini Embedding, and open BGE-M3 / Qwen3-Embedding leapfrog it. Check the MTEB leaderboard — but test on your data, and note MTEB v2 scores aren't comparable to v1.
IndexThe data structure for fast nearest-neighbor search. Almost everyone uses HNSW graphs (approximate, fast); hybrid stores pair it with an inverted index for BM25. Decides recall and latency. Common stores: Pinecone, Weaviate, Milvus, Qdrant, and pgvector in Postgres. The subject of Lesson 16.
Retrievetop-k is the recall-vs-noise dial: too small and the answer-bearing chunk never gets pulled; too big and the prompt floods with distractors. Hybrid retrieval (dense + BM25, fused) beats either alone. The first place to look when answers go wrong.
Re-rankA cross-encoder re-reads each query–chunk pair jointly and re-scores the candidates, turning "plausible matches" into "precise ones" so the prompt's few slots aren't wasted. Costs latency, buys precision. Tools: Cohere Rerank, Voyage rerank-2.5, open mxbai-rerank-v2, BGE reranker. See Lesson 18.
GenerateThe model stitches the retrieved passages into a fluent, cited answer. It cannot invent a fact retrieval never fetched. Generation polishes; it does not rescue. This is why the quality ceiling sits upstream.
Chunking is the silent killer — and a known fix

Because chunking fails quietly (no error, just slightly-wrong answers), it's worth a targeted upgrade. Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval (Sept 2024) prepends a 50–100 token, LLM-generated blurb situating each chunk in its document before embedding. On their tests it cut the top-20 retrieval failure rate by 35% (5.7% → 3.7%); adding contextual BM25 cut it 49%, and adding a reranker cut it 67% (down to 1.9%). Prompt caching makes it cheap (~$1 per million document tokens). It's a now-standard answer to the chunking failure mode — a preview of Lesson 17.

05 Recall, then precision

Top-k, then re-rank ▸

The two retrieval stages do opposite jobs with opposite tools, and understanding why is the key to good retrieval. First-stage retrieval uses a bi-encoder: it embedded every chunk once, offline, into a vector, and at query time it embeds the question and finds nearest neighbors. It's cheap and massively scalable — but it scored the query and each chunk separately, so it's only approximately right. Re-ranking uses a cross-encoder: it feeds the query and a candidate chunk into the model together and reads them jointly, which is far more accurate but far too slow to run over millions of chunks. So you run them in sequence: cheap bi-encoder retrieval casts a wide net (recall), then the expensive cross-encoder re-reads only the survivors (precision). Watch it play out in the embedding space:

Two threads from the brief are worth naming. First, hybrid search: dense vectors are great for paraphrase and semantics but blur exact tokens, while sparse BM25 nails IDs, error codes, proper nouns, and literal jargon — so you run both and fuse the results. Because cosine scores and BM25 scores live on incompatible scales, you fuse by Reciprocal Rank Fusion (combine by position in each list, not raw score). Second, re-ranking pays off concretely: reported lifts are roughly +5 to +15 NDCG@10 (benchmark-dependent), which is why "hybrid + rerank" is the default good-enough production recipe. We'll build both out in Lesson 18.

06 The 2026 reality

Naive RAG is dead

The 2023-style pipeline — chunk, dump in a vector DB, take top-k, hope — is what practitioners now openly call dead. Not RAG itself: the concept keeps growing fast. What died is the naive form. The live form, the one this whole phase teaches, is hybrid retrieval, contextual chunks, reranking, and increasingly agentic, multi-step retrieval. Three shifts are worth carrying forward:

  • Agentic RAG. Retrieval becomes a loop, not a single step. An agent decides whether to retrieve, judges whether the returned context is sufficient, spots gaps, issues follow-up queries (multi-hop), and only then answers — often orchestrated with LangGraph. This is the headline architectural change from 2024's retrieve-then-generate, and it leans on everything you'll learn about agents later in the course.
  • The "context engineering" rebrand. Practitioners — including Douwe Kiela, a co-author of the original RAG paper — increasingly fold RAG, prompt construction, and tool/MCP context under the banner of context engineering; some analysts have framed 2026 as "the year of context." The term is shifting, but the mechanics are exactly the ones in this lesson. Lesson 19 takes the name head-on.
  • Long context doesn't kill RAG; it reshapes it. Million-token windows exist, but dumping a whole corpus per query is far slower and costlier, and accuracy degrades on multi-needle retrieval as the window fills. RAG pays only for the relevant tokens. The 2025–2026 pattern is routing: simple lookups go to RAG; global, multi-hop reasoning over a small corpus goes to long context. And remember the floor from Lesson 1's sibling idea — under ~200K tokens, just paste it.

One more nuance to retire the "RAG vs fine-tune" framing for good: the frontier is combining them. Techniques like Finetune-RAG (2025) fine-tune a model to resist hallucinating when retrieval is imperfect, reporting meaningful gains in factual accuracy. The slogan that survives: fine-tune for behavior, retrieve for knowledge.
