00Lesson · System Design Interview

The 4-Step Interview Framework

A system-design round grades how you drive the 45 minutes at least as much as the answer you land on. A repeatable framework stops you from sharding a database before you know the read/write ratio — and hands the interviewer the exact structure they are scoring you against.

About these notes: original study material on running a system-design interview — a widely-taught, standard topic. The framing, examples, numbers, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. One diagram is interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to walk the loop. This is the lesson 00 that every later design drill quietly reuses.
00 The problem

Why you need a framework

Sit in enough mock interviews and a pattern jumps out: the candidates who fail rarely fail from a knowledge gap. They know what Kafka is, they can explain a B-tree index, they have read about consistent hashing. They fail because they have no process. Forty-five minutes is short. Without a plan, you spend it the way water finds a drain — straight to the most concrete thing in sight, which is almost always a box-and-arrow diagram of a database — and you draw it before you have any idea what the system is supposed to do.

This lesson teaches a repeatable framework: a fixed sequence of moves you run on every design question, regardless of whether it is a ride-hailing dispatcher, a payments ledger, or a news feed. Think of it as the operating system on top of which all the later lessons run. Every "design X" drill in this course assumes you already have this OS booted — it tells you what to do at minute 3, minute 12, minute 35, so your scarce working memory can go to the actual design instead of to "wait, what do I do now?"

The bottleneck this fixes: premature commitment

The core failure mode is premature commitment — locking in a decision before you have the information to justify it. A candidate hears "design GrabPay's wallet" and immediately writes PostgreSQL on the board, sharded by user_id, with a Redis cache in front. It might even be a fine answer. But they chose it in the first ninety seconds, before establishing whether the workload is read-heavy or write-heavy, whether balances need strong consistency (they do — it is money), what the QPS is, or whether the interviewer even cares about the storage layer. Now every later decision is anchored to an arbitrary first guess, and when the interviewer pokes at it, there is no reasoning to fall back on, only a defensive "well, that's what I'd use."

The framework's whole job is to impose an order that forces information-gathering before commitment. You scope before you estimate, estimate before you sketch APIs, sketch APIs before you draw boxes, and draw boxes before you go deep on any one of them. Each phase produces the input the next phase needs. By the time you write PostgreSQL on the board, you can say why — and "why" is the thing being graded.

The mental model

Treat the interview as a funnel, not a sprint. You start wide and vague (what are we even building?) and narrow to one or two concrete, defended decisions. The interviewer is watching the narrowing: each step should visibly cut the problem down using information you just gathered. A design that arrives fully-formed in minute two looks impressive for ten seconds and hollow for the next forty.

01 The clock

The 45-minute time budget

Before the four steps, internalize the time budget, because the steps only work if you finish them. The single most common way strong candidates lose points is running out of clock — burning twenty-five minutes perfecting the requirements and never drawing a system, or disappearing so deep into one component that the interviewer never gets to ask their favorite question. Time management is a graded skill, not an afterthought.

Here is a budget that fits a typical 45-minute round. It expands the four headline steps into six concrete phases by inserting estimation and API + data model as explicit slices — they are part of scoping and high-level design respectively, but they are easy to skip under pressure, so it pays to give them their own slot on the clock.

Treat these numbers as soft guides, not a stopwatch you obey blindly. The real discipline is the ratios: roughly the first third on framing, the middle half on building and defending, and the last few minutes reserved — always — for the wrap-up. If you are at minute 20 and still arguing about whether to support group chat in your messaging design, you are over budget on scoping and need to say "let me lock the scope here and start designing." Calling that out loud is itself a point in your favor: it shows the interviewer you are managing the round.

Interview tip

Glance at the clock (or ask "how are we doing for time?") around the one-third and two-thirds marks. Interviewers read explicit time-checks as seniority: a junior candidate gets lost in the weeds, a senior one budgets. The line to land is "I'll keep ~10 minutes for the deep dive and wrap-up, so let me move to the high-level design now."

02 The loop

The 4-step loop

Collapse the six phases back into the four steps you will actually narrate, and you get the spine of the whole framework. These are the four moves, in order, and the rest of this lesson is one section per move:

  • Step 1 — Scope the requirements. Turn a vague prompt into a written, bounded problem: the functional features you will build, and the non-functional targets (scale, latency, availability, consistency) you must hit. Estimation and explicit scope-cutting live here.
  • Step 2 — High-level design. Propose the API contract, sketch the data model, then draw the major boxes — clients, load balancer, services, database, cache, queue — and walk one request end-to-end along the happy path.
  • Step 3 — Deep dive. Pick the most interesting component (or let the interviewer pick) and go deep: trade-offs, the bottleneck, the failure mode, and how it scales.
  • Step 4 — Wrap-up. Summarize, name the bottlenecks you would attack next, cover monitoring and edge cases, and answer "what breaks at 10×."

It is a loop rather than a straight line because the steps feed back: a deep dive often surfaces a requirement you missed, which sends you back to re-scope, which tweaks the design. That is normal and good — say so out loud. Below, walk the loop step by step.

Interview tip

Announce the loop at the start: "I'll scope the requirements, do a quick estimation, sketch the API and a high-level design, then go deep on whichever part you find most interesting, and finish with bottlenecks and trade-offs." Thirty seconds of signposting tells the interviewer you have done this before and lets them redirect you if they care most about, say, the data model. You are handing them the rubric and saying "grade me against this."

03 Step 1 · scope

Step 1 — Scope the requirements

The first move is to convert a deliberately vague one-liner ("design a ride-hailing service") into a bounded, written problem. Interviewers keep prompts vague on purpose — the vagueness is the test. They want to see whether you ask before you build. Requirements split cleanly into two families, and you must cover both.

Functional requirements: what it does

Functional requirements are the concrete features and use cases — the verbs of the system. For a Grab-style dispatcher, the functional core might be: a rider requests a ride from A to B; the system finds nearby available drivers; a driver accepts; both sides see the car's live location; payment settles at the end. The skill here is ruthless prioritization. You cannot design everything in 45 minutes, so pick the two or three features that are the system and say so. Driver ratings, promo codes, scheduled rides, and multi-stop trips are real, but they are not the heart — name them and explicitly defer them.

This is the move that separates a focused candidate from a flailing one: cutting scope out loud. Say "I'll treat ratings, promotions, and ride scheduling as out of scope for today and focus on matching a rider to a nearby driver and tracking the trip — is that the right thing to optimize for?" You have just shrunk an impossible problem to a tractable one, on the record, with the interviewer's buy-in. If they wanted promotions in scope, they will tell you now, when it is cheap, instead of at minute 35.

Non-functional requirements: how well it does it

Non-functional requirements (NFRs) are the qualities — the adverbs. They decide your architecture far more than the feature list does, and they are the thing weak candidates skip. The checklist worth memorizing:

  • Scale. How many users, and how many active at peak? "100M registered, 5M daily active, peaking at dinner rush" is a different machine than "10k internal users."
  • Throughput (QPS). Reads and writes per second, separately. This sizes everything downstream.
  • Read/write ratio. A timeline is read-heavy (you scroll far more than you post); a payments ledger is write-balanced and append-only. This one ratio decides whether you reach for caches and read replicas or for write-optimized stores and queues. Choosing a database before you know this is the canonical mistake.
  • Latency. A p99 target. "Search autocomplete must feel instant — sub-100ms" rules out designs a 2-second batch job would allow.
  • Availability. How many nines? Money movement and core ride matching want high availability; an analytics dashboard can tolerate a blip.
  • Consistency. Strong or eventual? A wallet balance must be strongly consistent — you cannot let a customer spend the same dollar twice. A "likes" counter can be eventually consistent.
  • Durability. Once written, can it ever be lost? A completed payment: never. A draft message: maybe acceptable.

Worked example: scoping SeaMoney's wallet

Suppose the prompt is "design the wallet for SeaMoney." Good clarifying questions, asked out loud: "Are we doing top-ups and peer-to-peer transfers, or also merchant payments and bill splitting? Roughly how many wallets — tens of millions? What's the peak transaction rate — payday and the 9.9 sale will spike it. Do we need the balance to be exactly correct at all times, or is eventual fine?" The answers might land at: functional — top-up, P2P transfer, merchant pay; scope cut — bill-splitting and currency exchange deferred; NFR — ~40M wallets, ~5k transactions/sec at peak, write-heavy on the ledger but read-heavy on balance checks, p99 under 300ms, very high availability, and strong consistency on balances because it is money. That last line alone — "strong consistency, it's money" — tells the interviewer you understand the domain and instantly justifies a transactional store later.

Write the answers somewhere visible — a corner of the whiteboard, the top of the doc. They are your contract for the rest of the interview, and you will point back at them every time you justify a decision.

Interview tip

If the interviewer waves off your scale question with "assume it's big," state your own assumption and move on: "OK, I'll assume ~50M DAU and design for that — tell me if you want a different order of magnitude." Never freeze waiting for permission. Driving past ambiguity with a stated assumption is exactly the senior behavior they are probing for.

04 Step 1 · estimation

Estimation (back-of-the-envelope)

With scope fixed, spend a few minutes on back-of-the-envelope estimation — rough math that turns "it's big" into numbers you can design against. The point is not precision; it is order of magnitude. The difference between 100 requests/sec and 100,000 requests/sec is the difference between a single box and a sharded fleet, and you cannot make a single sane storage decision until you know which one you are in.

How the math actually works

The mechanism is a short chain of multiplications, each starting from a number you assumed in scoping. Work top-down from users to load:

  • QPS from DAU. Take daily active users, multiply by actions per user per day, divide by 86,400 seconds. That gives average QPS. Then multiply by a peak factor (commonly 2–5×) because traffic is never flat — a food-delivery app spikes hard at lunch and dinner.
  • Storage from writes. Multiply write QPS by bytes per write by the retention window (seconds in a day × days). A 1KB row written 5,000 times a second is ~430GB/day — a number that immediately rules some designs in or out.
  • Bandwidth. QPS × payload size. Matters most for media-heavy systems (a video service, an image CDN).
  • Memory for cache. Apply the rough 80/20 rule — if 20% of items serve 80% of reads, you only need to cache that hot 20% to absorb most of the load.

Worked example: Shopee's 9.9 sale flash traffic

Estimate the order-write load for a Shopee mega-sale. Say 60M daily active users during the campaign, and on average a user places 0.5 orders that day (most browse, some buy). That is 30M orders/day. Average QPS is 30M / 86,400 ≈ 350 order-writes/sec. But a flash sale is the opposite of flat — apply a peak factor of, say, 10× for the first minutes of a lightning deal, and you are designing for ~3,500 writes/sec on the order path. If each order record is ~2KB, that peak is ~7MB/sec of write throughput, and a day of orders is 30M × 2KB ≈ 60GB/day just for order rows. Now you have concrete inputs: that write rate justifies a write-optimized path (a queue to absorb the spike, perhaps), and 60GB/day means you are talking about sharding and a retention policy within months, not years.

Notice every number traces back to a scoping assumption. State each assumption as you go — "I'll assume 60M DAU and a 10× flash-sale peak" — so the interviewer can correct an input rather than your arithmetic. Round aggressively; nobody wants six significant figures on a whiteboard. A useful memorized peg: 1 day ≈ 86,400 ≈ 100k seconds, so "X per day" divided by 100k is "X per second" to one significant figure.

Do the estimation

  • Sizes the whole architecture: one box vs a fleet
  • Justifies sharding, caching, and queues with numbers
  • Shows quantitative judgment — a senior signal
  • Catches impossible designs early ("that won't fit in RAM")

Watch-outs

  • Don't rathole — 3–5 minutes, not 15
  • Don't chase precision; order of magnitude is the goal
  • Skipping it leaves later choices unjustified
  • State assumptions so a wrong input is correctable
Under the hood: the numbers worth memorizing

Keep a few pegs ready so you are not deriving them live: a day is ~86,400 seconds (round to 100k). A short text row is ~hundreds of bytes; a rich record ~1–2KB; a photo ~hundreds of KB; a minute of video ~tens of MB. A single commodity server handles low-thousands of simple QPS before you reach for more. Latency pegs: memory access is nanoseconds, an SSD read is tens of microseconds, a same-region network round trip is sub-millisecond, and a cross-region round trip is tens to hundreds of milliseconds — which is why you cache locally and replicate by region.

Interview tip

Tie every estimate to a design consequence in the same breath: "350 writes/sec average but ~3,500 at the flash-sale peak — that spike is why I'd put a queue in front of order processing rather than writing synchronously." An estimate with no consequence is trivia; an estimate that changes the design is the point.

05 Step 2 · contract

API & data model

Now you bridge from "what" to "how." Before drawing a single box, pin down two artifacts that make the rest of the design concrete: the API contract and the data model. They are the joints the whole architecture hangs from, and sketching them first prevents a vague, hand-wavy design where boxes connect to other boxes for no stated reason.

The API contract

The API contract is the set of operations clients can call — the system's public surface. You do not need full OpenAPI; you need the few endpoints that realize your in-scope features, with their key parameters and what they return. Defining them forces clarity, because each endpoint implies a data flow you will have to support. For the ride dispatcher, the contract is small:

POST /v1/rides              # rider requests a ride
  body:  { rider_id, pickup: {lat,lng}, dropoff: {lat,lng} }
  returns: { ride_id, status: "MATCHING" }

POST /v1/rides/{id}/accept  # driver accepts an offered ride
  body:  { driver_id }
  returns: { status: "ACCEPTED", eta_seconds }

GET  /v1/rides/{id}/location   # rider polls / subscribes to car location
  returns: { lat, lng, updated_at }

Just writing this surfaces design questions for free: how does a driver learn a ride is on offer (push vs poll)? Is live location pulled by the client or pushed over a websocket? You can flag these as deep-dive candidates. The contract is also where you decide auth, idempotency (a retried POST /rides must not create two rides — an idempotency key earns a nod), and pagination for any list endpoint.

The data model

The data model is the shape of what you store. A few entities with their key fields and relationships is enough. The crucial move is to derive the model from the access patterns the API implies, not from a tidy textbook normalization. How you will read and write the data dictates the schema, the indexes, and later the shard key:

ride     ( ride_id PK, rider_id, driver_id, status,
           pickup_geo, dropoff_geo, fare, created_at )
driver   ( driver_id PK, status, current_geo, vehicle )
location ( driver_id PK, lat, lng, updated_at )   # hot, high-write

Spotting that location is written every few seconds per active driver but read on demand tells you it is a different beast from the ride record — high-write, ephemeral, a candidate for an in-memory or geospatial store rather than the same relational table as the durable ride history. That observation, falling straight out of the data model, is exactly the kind of insight a deep dive builds on.

Why this order matters

API and data model are the contract that the boxes in your high-level diagram must honor. Draw the boxes first and you tend to invent components to fill space; derive them from a concrete contract and every box exists because some endpoint or access pattern needs it. The model also fixes your nouns — when you later say "the matching service reads the driver-location store," everyone knows precisely what you mean.

Interview tip

Pick a shard key candidate as you sketch the model and justify it by access pattern: "I'd partition rides by ride_id for even spread, but driver-location by geo-cell so a 'nearby drivers' query hits one partition." Defending the shard key with the read pattern is one of the highest-signal things you can say — the rubric in section 10 weights it heavily.

06 Step 2 · high-level design

Step 2 — High-level design

Now draw. The high-level design (HLD) is the box-and-arrow picture of the major components and how a request flows through them. The goal is a diagram a fellow engineer could glance at and understand the system's shape — not every retry policy and config flag, just the skeleton: clients, the entry point, the services, the stores, and the async machinery.

The standard cast of boxes

Most designs assemble from a familiar kit, and naming each box's reason-for-being as you place it is the whole exercise:

  • Clients — the apps and browsers making requests.
  • Load balancer — spreads traffic across stateless service instances and is the thing that lets you scale horizontally and survive a dead node.
  • Application services — your business logic, ideally stateless so any instance can serve any request and you can add or remove them freely.
  • Database — the durable store of record; its type follows from the data model and consistency NFRs you already established.
  • Cache — absorbs read load for hot data, justified by the read/write ratio you estimated.
  • Message queue — decouples slow or spiky work (the flash-sale order spike from section 04) from the request path, so a burst fills a buffer instead of toppling the service.

Walk the happy path out loud

A diagram is inert until you narrate one request through it. Trace the single most important flow end-to-end: a rider opens the app → request hits the load balancer → routed to a matching service instance → which queries the driver-location store for nearby drivers → writes a new ride row → and pushes an offer to a chosen driver. Walking this proves the boxes actually connect to satisfy a real use case, and it is where you naturally point back at the contract and model from section 05. Keep it to the happy path; you will handle failure branches in the deep dive.

Interview tip

Resist over-drawing. Six or seven boxes is plenty for the HLD; if you find yourself adding a tenth, you are doing the deep dive early. The interviewer will steer you into depth in a moment — your HLD job is to establish a correct, complete-enough skeleton and a narrated happy path, then hand them the wheel: "that's the high-level shape — which part would you like me to go deeper on?"

07 Step 3 · go deep

Step 3 — The deep dive

This is where the interview is won or lost. The deep dive is a focused exploration of one component, and it is where you demonstrate the depth that separates levels. The HLD shows you can assemble a system; the deep dive shows you understand why each piece works, what it costs, and what happens when it breaks.

Choosing what to dive on

Often the interviewer picks ("let's talk about how matching scales"). If they leave it to you, choose the component that is most central and most interesting — usually the one tied to your tightest NFR. In the wallet design, dive on the ledger and how you keep balances strongly consistent under concurrent transactions. In the feed design, dive on fan-out: do you write to every follower's feed on post (fan-out on write) or assemble the feed at read time (fan-out on read), and how do you handle a celebrity with 20M followers? Picking the juicy component signals judgment before you have said anything technical.

The four lenses to apply

Whatever component you pick, run it through four lenses — this is the structure that makes a deep dive feel rigorous rather than rambling:

  • Trade-offs. Name the real decision and argue both sides. SQL vs NoSQL, strong vs eventual consistency, fan-out on write vs read — put it in an honest pros/cons frame and then commit, justified by an NFR. "I'll fan out on write because reads must be sub-100ms and reads vastly outnumber posts" is a defended choice.
  • The bottleneck. Find where load concentrates and relieve it: a hot partition, a single-writer database, a chatty N+1 query pattern. The bottleneck is the thing that breaks first as you scale, so naming it shows you think about limits.
  • The failure mode. Pick a component and answer "what happens when this node dies?" If a database primary fails, how does a replica get promoted and how long is the write outage? If the cache falls over, does the database get stampeded — and do you protect it with request coalescing? This question is asked in nearly every interview; have an answer ready before it is asked.
  • Scaling. How does this piece grow with traffic? Replication for read scale, sharding for write scale, and the shard-key justification from section 05. Then the killer question: what breaks at 10×?

Worked example: the GrabPay ledger under concurrency

Diving on the wallet ledger: the bottleneck is that a single wallet's balance is a contended row — two simultaneous debits could both read the same balance and both succeed, letting a user overspend (a race condition, and with money, a real incident). The trade-off is how you serialize writes to one wallet: a row lock / serializable transaction in PostgreSQL (simple, strongly consistent, but the lock caps throughput per wallet) versus an append-only event-sourced ledger where balance is a fold over immutable entries (scales writes, gives a perfect audit trail, but reads must aggregate or snapshot). The failure mode: if the primary dies mid-transfer, an in-flight transfer must not be half-applied — so transfers are wrapped in a transaction and made idempotent on a client-supplied key, and a failover promotes a synchronous replica so no committed entry is lost. At 10×, a single hot wallet (a popular merchant on the 12.12 sale) becomes the limit, and you would shard by wallet and reconsider whether that one account needs its own write path. Notice how each lens produced a concrete, defensible sentence.

Strong deep dive

  • Picks one component and commits to depth
  • States a trade-off, then chooses, citing an NFR
  • Names the bottleneck and failure mode unprompted
  • Answers "what breaks at 10×" with a specific limit

Weak deep dive

  • Stays at HLD altitude, re-describing the boxes
  • Lists options without ever choosing one
  • Ignores failure until the interviewer forces it
  • Adds features instead of going deeper on one
The follow-up to expect

After you commit, the interviewer almost always pushes the boundary: "OK, now it's 10× the traffic — what's the first thing that falls over?" The strong answer names a specific resource (a hot partition, the single primary's write ceiling, cache memory) and the next mitigation. Have a "what breaks next" answer queued for every choice you make — the deep dive is really a sequence of these.

08 Step 4 · land it

Step 4 — Wrap-up

Reserve the last five minutes — protect them, do not let the deep dive eat them. The wrap-up is a deliberate landing that demonstrates engineering maturity: it shows you know your design is not perfect, you understand its limits, and you think about operating it in production, not just drawing it.

What a strong wrap-up covers

  • A crisp recap. Three sentences: what you built, the key decisions, and why. "A stateless matching tier behind a load balancer, a geospatial store for driver locations, an append-only ride record, with matching as the scaling hotspot."
  • Known bottlenecks & next improvements. Volunteer the weaknesses before they are found. "The driver-location store is the hot path; if we outgrow a single region I'd partition by geo-cell and add regional replicas." Naming your own design's limits is a strength signal, not a weakness.
  • Monitoring & observability. A production system you cannot see is a liability. Name the few golden metrics you would alert on — p99 latency, error rate, queue depth, replication lag — and you sound like someone who has carried a pager.
  • Edge cases. The awkward inputs: a driver who accepts then immediately cancels, a duplicate payment from a client retry, a rider with no drivers nearby, a clock skew between nodes.
  • What breaks at 10×. One more time, at the whole-system level: where does this architecture stop scaling, and what is the next structural change?

The failure-mode close

If you have time for one more thing, it should be resilience. State how the system degrades rather than collapses: does it fail open or fail closed when a dependency is down? Can it shed load gracefully? A design that says "if the recommendation service is down, we serve a generic feed instead of erroring" is far more convincing than one that assumes every dependency is always healthy. The wrap-up is your chance to show you have thought about the 3am incident, not just the demo.

Why the wrap-up earns disproportionate points

Most candidates run out of time and end mid-sentence in the deep dive, leaving the interviewer with no sense of closure. A candidate who lands the plane — recaps, names their own weaknesses, and gestures at production concerns — leaves a final impression of someone who builds and operates systems. Recency matters: the last two minutes are what the interviewer remembers while writing the feedback.

Interview tip

If you are running short, compress the deep dive rather than skipping the wrap-up. Say "I'll note the remaining failure modes quickly so we have time to wrap." Volunteering your own design's three biggest weaknesses, unprompted, is one of the highest-signal moves in the entire interview — it is the opposite of the defensive candidate who insists their design is flawless.

09 Meta-skill

Communication & anti-patterns

The framework gives you what to do; communication is how you do it, and it is graded just as hard. A system-design interview is a simulation of a design discussion with a future colleague. The interviewer is asking, implicitly: would I want to whiteboard a real problem with this person? Get the moves right but communicate poorly and you can still fail.

The communication rules

  • Think out loud. Silence is the enemy — the interviewer cannot grade thoughts they cannot hear. Narrate your reasoning even when you are unsure: "I'm weighing a cache here, but let me first check whether reads dominate." Externalize the search, not just the answer.
  • Propose, then justify. Lead with the decision, immediately back it with the reason: "I'll use a message queue here — because the flash-sale spike would otherwise overwhelm synchronous order writes." Decision-then-because is the unit of a senior answer.
  • Surface alternatives. Show you saw the other road before taking yours: "I could fan out on read instead, which saves write amplification, but it makes the hot read path slower — and reads are our tight NFR, so I'll fan out on write." Considering and rejecting an option is more convincing than never mentioning it.
  • Drive, don't wait. Move the design forward yourself. Don't sit in silence hoping for a hint; propose the next step and invite redirection: "I'll move to the data model next unless you'd rather dig into matching." You hold the pen.
  • Manage the clock. Explicit time checks, as in section 01, signal control.

The anti-patterns to avoid

Most failures are one of a small set of named anti-patterns. Recognize them so you can catch yourself mid-interview:

  • Designing before scoping. The original sin — drawing a database before you know the read/write ratio. Cured by Step 1.
  • Ignoring non-functional requirements. Listing features but never asking about scale, latency, or consistency, then "designing blind" with no constraints to justify anything.
  • Gold-plating one corner. Polishing an exquisite caching layer while the core matching logic is still undefined. Get the whole skeleton standing before perfecting any one bone.
  • Silence. Long quiet stretches while you think privately. The interviewer reads it as being stuck, even when you are not.
  • Refusing to commit. Endlessly listing options — "we could use SQL, or NoSQL, or a graph DB..." — without ever choosing. Indecision reads as not understanding the trade-off well enough to weigh it. Always commit, with a reason; you can revise later.
Interview tip

If you genuinely do not know something, say so and reason from fundamentals rather than bluffing: "I haven't used that exact technology, but the property I need here is X, so I'd reach for something that gives me X." Interviewers strongly prefer honest first-principles reasoning to confident hand-waving — and they will often hand you the missing piece once you have shown how you think.

10 Self-grade

The self-grade rubric

To practice deliberately, you need to grade yourself the way the interviewer does. After every mock, score each dimension 1–5 and write one sentence on what cost you the points. The six dimensions map directly onto the framework — which is the point: the rubric and the process are the same thing seen from two sides.

  • Scoping (1–5). Did you separate functional from non-functional, ask sharp clarifying questions, and cut scope out loud with buy-in? A 5 produces a written, bounded contract; a 2 starts designing on a vague prompt.
  • Estimation sanity (1–5). Did you do order-of-magnitude math, tie it to a design consequence, and state your assumptions? A 5 lets a number change the design; a 2 skips the math entirely.
  • Clear HLD (1–5). Is there a clean, complete-enough box diagram with a narrated happy path? A 5 is legible and justified; a 2 is a tangle nobody could follow.
  • Justified data choices (1–5). Is the store type, schema, shard key, and consistency level each defended by an access pattern? This is the single most weighted dimension — "I picked X because the read pattern is Y" four times over.
  • Bottleneck & failure modes (1–5). Did you name where it breaks first, what happens when a node dies, and what breaks at 10× — unprompted? A 5 volunteers these; a 2 waits to be forced.
  • Communication & trade-offs (1–5). Did you think out loud, propose-then-justify, surface alternatives, commit, and manage the clock? A 5 feels like a great design discussion; a 2 is silence and indecision.
How to use it

Record a mock, then score it cold the next day. The dimension you score lowest is your next practice target — and because each dimension maps to a step, you know exactly which part of the framework to drill. Most people discover their weak spot is "justified data choices" or "failure modes," because those require the deep-dive muscle the later lessons in this course are built to develop.

Summary & self-test

The whole lesson compresses to one sentence: run every design question through the same loop — scope, then high-level design, then a deep dive, then wrap-up — so each decision is gathered before it is committed and defended by a requirement you established earlier. Every later lesson in this course is just this framework applied to a specific system. Boot the OS first; the designs run on top of it.

00Process beats knowledgeCandidates fail from no structure, not no facts. Impose order to avoid premature commitment.
01Budget the 45 minutes~⅓ framing, half building, last few minutes reserved for the wrap-up — always.
02Run the 4-step loopScope → HLD → deep dive → wrap-up, looping back when a gap appears.
03Scope = FR + NFRFeatures AND scale/latency/consistency. Cut scope out loud, get buy-in.
04Estimate to the orderQPS, storage, peaks — and tie every number to a design consequence.
05Contract before boxesAPI + data model first; derive the schema and shard key from access patterns.
06Sketch, then narrateSix or seven boxes, one happy path walked end-to-end out loud.
07Deep dive on one thingTrade-off, bottleneck, failure mode, scaling — commit and defend.
08Land the planeRecap, own your weaknesses, monitoring, edge cases, what breaks at 10×.
09Communicate & driveThink aloud, propose-then-justify, commit, manage the clock, no silence.
Self-test before you move on

Cover the notes and answer aloud:
(1) What is the single bottleneck this framework exists to prevent, and how does ordering the steps prevent it?
(2) Name the four steps and the two phases hidden inside Step 1 on the 45-minute clock.
(3) Give three non-functional requirements and explain how each one would change your storage choice.
(4) Walk a back-of-envelope estimate for a Shopee flash sale from DAU to peak write QPS, and name the design consequence.
(5) Why sketch the API and data model before the box diagram, and how does the data model decide your shard key?
(6) Name three deep-dive lenses and two communication anti-patterns.
Any fuzzy answer → re-read that section, replay the loop diagram, and redraw the 45-minute timeline from memory on paper.
