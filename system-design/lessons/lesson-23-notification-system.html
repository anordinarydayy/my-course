23Lesson · System Design Interview

Design a Notification System

A notification system takes a single event — "your GrabFood order is on the way" — and fans it out to the right people across push, SMS, and email. The deceptively simple ask hides the real work: per-channel queues that absorb bursts, the reliability machinery that guarantees delivery without double-paging anyone, and a preference layer that keeps you from spamming the very users you're trying to serve.

About these notes: original study material on designing a notification system — a standard, widely-documented engineering topic. The explanations, examples, numbers, and diagrams are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Three diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow.
00 The problem

Why it is hard

On the surface, "send a notification" is one HTTP call to Apple or Google and you are done. That intuition survives until the first real day of traffic. The hard parts of a notification system — a service that delivers messages to users across multiple channels in response to events — are not about sending one message; they are about sending millions of them, through providers you do not control, without ever lying to the user (claiming delivery that never happened) or annoying them (sending the same alert twice).

Consider what goes wrong without a deliberate design:

  • Bursts overwhelm providers. A flash sale on Shopee fires a "5-minute deal!" push to 8 million users at 8:00:00pm. If you call Firebase as fast as your servers can loop, you blow past the provider's rate limit and get throttled — or worse, silently dropped.
  • One slow channel blocks the others. If email and push share one worker pool and the email provider stalls, push notifications queue up behind it. The channels must be isolated.
  • Failures cause duplicates. A worker sends a push, then crashes before recording success. On restart it retries — and the user gets paged twice. For a payment confirmation, that is alarming; for a "Suspicious login" alert, it is a support ticket.
  • You spam people. Without preference checks, a user who muted marketing still gets the 2am promo. They uninstall. The most expensive notification is the one that makes someone churn.

So the system is really an exercise in decoupling (queues between you and bursts/providers), reliability (retries, dedup, dead-letter handling), and respect (preferences, quiet hours, frequency caps). The rest of this lesson builds it bottleneck by bottleneck.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

The interviewer drops a one-line prompt; your first job is to turn it into a bounded problem. Spend the opening two minutes asking questions and stating assumptions out loud — it shows judgment and stops you from designing the wrong thing.

Functional requirements

  • Channels. Deliver push (mobile via APNs for iOS and FCM for Android), SMS (via an aggregator like Twilio), and email (via a provider like Amazon SES). We will design so a new channel — say WhatsApp or in-app inbox — can be added later without touching the core.
  • Many event sources. Notifications are triggered by other services: an order service ("delivered"), a payments service ("GrabPay top-up succeeded"), a fraud service ("new device login"), and scheduled/marketing campaigns. We expose an internal API they all call.
  • Templating & localization. A notification is content + data; the system renders it from a versioned template in the user's language (English, Bahasa, Thai, Vietnamese for an SEA super-app).
  • Preferences. Users opt in/out per channel and per category, set do-not-disturb windows, and are protected by frequency caps.
  • Tracking. Record per-notification lifecycle: queued → sent → delivered → opened (or failed).

Non-functional requirements

  • Reliability. Critical notifications (OTP, payment receipt) must not be lost. We target at-least-once delivery with deduplication so the effective experience is exactly-once.
  • No double-paging. A user must never receive the same logical notification twice, even when retries and crashes happen.
  • High throughput, bursty. Campaigns produce sharp spikes; the system must absorb them without dropping or melting providers.
  • Low latency where it matters. An OTP should arrive in seconds; a weekly digest can take minutes. We will not give them the same priority.
  • Scalable & available. Horizontally scalable workers; no single point of failure; degrade gracefully when a provider is down.

Two scoping decisions worth stating explicitly: this is server-driven outbound notification (not real-time chat — that is a different lesson), and we treat the third-party gateways (APNs, FCM, Twilio, SES) as black boxes we must respect, not rebuild.

Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole design: "the interesting parts are the per-channel queues, the reliability machinery, and the preference layer — not the actual call to FCM." Say that early and the interviewer will let you drive.

02 Estimation

Back-of-envelope estimation

Numbers turn a hand-wave into a design. Assume a regional super-app with 50 million users, of whom 20 million are daily active. We size three things: send rate, fan-out, and storage.

Daily volume & average QPS

Suppose each active user receives on average 5 notifications a day across all channels — order updates, payment receipts, a promo or two. That is:

  • 20M × 5 = 100M notifications/day.
  • 100M ÷ 86,400s ≈ ~1,160 notifications/sec average.
  • Real traffic is spiky. A peak factor of 10× (dinner rush, a campaign) gives ~11,600/sec peak. Provider-bound bursts can be far higher for a few seconds.

The fan-out multiplier

One campaign event is not one notification. A marketing push to a 5M-user segment is a single API call that fans out to 5M deliveries. So the fan-out — deliveries produced per inbound event — ranges from 1 (a personal order update) to millions (a broadcast). The architecture must treat "expand the audience" as its own stage, because a single call can dwarf a whole day of organic traffic in seconds.

Per-channel split & provider limits

Roughly 80% push, 15% email, 5% SMS is typical (push is free and instant; SMS costs real money). The constraint that actually shapes the design is the provider rate limit:

  • FCM accepts very high throughput but rewards batching (up to 500 tokens per multicast request).
  • SMS aggregators are throttled hard — often a few hundred messages/sec per number/short-code, and each message costs cents.
  • Email (SES) has an account send-rate quota (e.g. a configured messages-per-second cap) that you must stay under or get throttled.

Storage

If we log every notification's metadata (~500 bytes: ids, channel, status, timestamps) and keep it 30 days: 100M/day × 500B × 30 ≈ 1.5 TB of hot tracking data. Device tokens for 50M users with ~1.5 devices each at ~200 bytes ≈ 15 GB — small and cacheable. The tracking log is the heavy table; it wants a write-optimized store and a TTL.

Why these numbers matter

The average QPS sizes your steady-state workers; the peak and fan-out size your queues. The whole reason queues exist here is the gap between "11,600/sec arrives" and "SMS provider accepts 300/sec." Estimation is how you justify that gap to the interviewer.

03 Contract

API & data model

Before drawing boxes, pin down the contract. This is where you go past the book: show a concrete request shape and the core tables.

The send API

Event sources call one internal endpoint. Crucially it accepts a caller-supplied idempotency key so a source that retries does not create a second notification.

# POST /v1/notifications  (called by order, payments, fraud services...)
{
  "idempotency_key": "order-evt-88231-shipped",   # dedup at ingest
  "recipient":       { "user_id": "u_50231" },     # or a segment_id for campaigns
  "category":        "order_status",             # used for preference + caps
  "priority":        "transactional",            # vs "marketing"
  "channels":        ["push", "email"],          # desired; filtered by prefs
  "template_id":     "order_shipped_v3",
  "data":             { "order_id": "88231", "eta_min": 25 }
}
# 202 Accepted -> { "notification_id": "n_af91c2" }  (async; not "delivered")

Note it returns 202 Accepted, not 200 OK — delivery is asynchronous and we are only promising to attempt it. We also expose GET /v1/notifications/{id} for status and PUT /v1/users/{id}/preferences for the preference center.

Data model sketch

Four entities carry the system. Two are write-heavy (notifications, device tokens), two are read-heavy and cacheable (preferences, templates).

-- device tokens: which devices to push to (cleaned up when stale)
device_token(user_id, token, platform, app_version, last_seen_at, valid)

-- per-user, per-category channel preferences + quiet hours
preference(user_id, category, channel, enabled, dnd_start, dnd_end, tz)

-- versioned, localized message templates
template(template_id, version, channel, locale, subject, body, vars)

-- the tracking log: one row per delivery attempt outcome
notification(notification_id, user_id, channel, category, status,
             dedup_key, provider_msg_id, created_at, sent_at, opened_at)

The dedup_key column on notification carries a uniqueness constraint — it is the backstop that makes duplicate delivery impossible even when everything upstream misbehaves (Step 06). The notification table is the 1.5 TB monster from estimation; it belongs in a write-scalable store like Cassandra or DynamoDB, partitioned by user_id with a TTL. Preferences and templates fit comfortably in PostgreSQL fronted by a Redis cache.

04 The skeleton

High-level architecture

Now the picture. The flow has five logical stages, and the ordering is deliberate — each stage exists because the previous one created a problem.

  • Event sources call the send API. They should not know anything about channels or providers.
  • The Notification Service (the brain) does ingest-dedup, expands the audience (fan-out), checks preferences, picks templates, and routes each delivery to the right channel. It owns no provider logic.
  • Per-channel queues sit between the brain and the workers. They decouple the spiky producer from the rate-limited consumer — this is the single most important structural decision.
  • Channel workers pull from their queue, render the final payload, and call the gateway. Each pool scales independently.
  • Third-party gateways (APNs, FCM, Twilio, SES) do the last-mile delivery to the device or inbox.

Walk the diagram below step by step: an event enters, the service builds and checks it, it is dropped onto a channel queue, a worker drains the queue at a safe rate, and the provider delivers. Pause on the queue step — that buffer is what lets the producer spike to 11,600/sec while the SMS worker calmly sips at 300/sec.

Under the hood

The Notification Service is stateless and horizontally scaled behind a load balancer. All durable state lives in the datastores (tokens, prefs, tracking) and the queues. That statelessness is what lets you add workers during a campaign and remove them after, without coordination.

05 The core decision

Per-channel queues & the fan-out

Here is the bottleneck that justifies the whole shape of the design. The producer (campaigns, event spikes) is bursty and fast; the consumers (providers) are steady and rate-limited, and each at a different rate. If you call providers synchronously inside the request, three things break: the source's request hangs for seconds, a provider outage takes your service down with it, and a burst slams straight into the provider's rate limit.

Why a queue, and why one per channel

A message queue between service and worker fixes all three. It is durable (survives a worker crash), it lets the producer fire-and-forget, and it lets the consumer pull at whatever rate it can safely sustain. We give each channel its own queue so they are isolated: if SES is throttling, email piles up in its queue while push and SMS keep flowing. One shared queue would let the slowest channel back up everyone — exactly the failure we are trying to avoid.

The same principle suggests a second axis of separation: a priority split. Transactional notifications (OTP, payment) get their own fast lane; marketing rides a separate, lower-priority queue so a 5M-user promo campaign cannot delay someone's login code.

Fan-out happens before the queues

A campaign event names a segment, not a user. The service (or a dedicated fan-out worker) expands the segment into individual deliveries and enqueues each one. For a 5M-user push that is 5M small messages dropped onto the push queue — which is precisely why the queue must be able to swallow a huge burst and meter it out. Walk the stepper: the service expands and routes; each channel queue buffers; each worker drains at its own safe pace.

What to build it on

Kafka shines as the durable backbone — high throughput, partitioned, retains messages so you can replay after a bug. RabbitMQ or AWS SQS are friendlier for simple per-channel work queues with built-in retry and dead-letter support. A common pattern: Kafka as the ingest/fan-out log, then SQS (with a DLQ) per channel for the worker stage. Workers apply per-provider rate limiting (a token bucket keyed by provider) so the queue, not the provider, holds the backlog.

One queue per channel

  • Channels are isolated — a slow SES never blocks push
  • Independent scaling and rate limits per channel
  • Clear, per-channel metrics and DLQs
  • More queues and workers to operate

One shared queue

  • Simpler to set up and operate
  • Fewer moving parts, one consumer group
  • Head-of-line blocking: slowest channel stalls all
  • Can't rate-limit or scale channels separately
Interviewer follow-up

"What happens when a campaign dumps 5 million messages into the push queue?" Answer: the queue absorbs them (that is its job), the worker drains at FCM's safe rate, transactional traffic rides a separate priority queue so it is unaffected, and queue depth / consumer lag is the metric you alarm on. The backlog is visible and bounded — it never reaches the provider as a flood.

06 Reliability

Exactly-once that you actually build as at-least-once + dedup

Distributed systems cannot give you true exactly-once delivery — networks drop acks, workers crash mid-send. What they give you is a choice between at-most-once (never retry; risk losing messages) and at-least-once (always retry; risk duplicates). For notifications, losing an OTP is unacceptable, so we choose at-least-once and then add deduplication to suppress the duplicates it inevitably produces. The combination looks exactly-once to the user.

Where duplicates come from

Two independent sources, each needing its own guard:

  • Source-side retries. The order service POSTs to our API, times out before the response arrives, and POSTs again. We block this with the caller's idempotency_key: on ingest we do an atomic "insert if not exists" on that key (a unique index, or SET key NX in Redis). The second POST sees the key exists and returns the original notification_id instead of creating a new notification.
  • Worker-side retries. A worker sends the push, then crashes before recording success or acking the queue message. The queue redelivers; a second worker is about to send again. We block this with a dedup key at the send step.

How the dedup key works under the hood

Construct a deterministic key per logical delivery — e.g. {notification_id}:{user_id}:{channel}. Before calling the provider, the worker does an atomic check-and-set against Redis: SET dedup:{key} 1 NX EX 86400. If the command succeeds (the key did not exist), this worker "won" and proceeds to send. If it fails (key already set), a previous attempt already sent — the worker drops this copy and acks the queue. The 24-hour TTL bounds memory and matches how long a duplicate could plausibly resurface.

The unique constraint on notification.dedup_key in the durable store is the second line of defense for when Redis itself is lost. Belt and suspenders, because the cost of a duplicate "your card was charged" alert is real user alarm.

At-least-once + dedup

  • Never loses a critical notification
  • Duplicates suppressed → effectively exactly-once
  • Needs a fast atomic store (Redis) on the send path

At-most-once

  • Simple — no dedup machinery
  • No risk of double-paging
  • Drops messages on any failure — unacceptable for OTP
Interviewer follow-up

"How do you avoid sending the same notification twice?" Land all three layers: idempotency key at ingest, dedup key with atomic SETNX before the provider call, and a unique constraint in the durable store as a backstop. Then note the honest caveat: if the provider did deliver but the ack was lost, you cannot perfectly know — the dedup key prevents a second send, which is the best a distributed system can do.

07 Reliability

Retries, backoff & the dead-letter queue

Providers fail transiently all the time: a timeout, a 503, a momentary rate-limit rejection. A worker must not give up on the first failure, nor hammer a struggling provider into the ground. The discipline is retry with exponential backoff and jitter, capped, with a dead-letter queue as the final resting place.

How the retry loop works

When a send fails with a retryable error, the worker does not block and sleep — that would waste a worker. Instead it re-enqueues the message with a computed delay: attempt 1 retries after ~1s, attempt 2 after ~2s, attempt 3 after ~4s, doubling each time (exponential backoff). To stop thousands of failed sends from retrying in lockstep and creating a synchronized thundering herd, each delay gets random jitter added. SQS supports this natively via per-message visibility timeout / delay; with Kafka you publish to a delay topic.

Critically, you distinguish error classes. A retryable error (network timeout, 429, 5xx) goes back for another attempt. A permanent error (FCM says the token is NotRegistered, SES says the address is invalid) is pointless to retry — you mark it failed immediately and, for a bad device token, trigger token cleanup (Step 09).

The dead-letter queue

After a capped number of attempts (say 5), the message is moved to a dead-letter queue (DLQ) — a separate queue for messages that could not be delivered. Nothing is silently dropped; the DLQ is monitored, alerts fire when it grows, and engineers can inspect, fix, and replay messages. The DLQ turns "lost notification" into "parked notification we can recover," which is the difference between a 3am page and a morning ticket.

Under the hood — backpressure

When a provider degrades, retries pile back into the queue and consumer lag grows. That lag is backpressure: the worker can detect a rising 429/timeout rate and deliberately slow its consumption (open a circuit breaker), letting the queue hold the backlog instead of flooding a sick provider. The queue depth, not lost data, is what absorbs the outage.

08 Respect the user

User preferences, quiet hours & frequency caps

A technically perfect delivery system that ignores preferences is a churn machine. The preference layer is a mandatory gate that runs before a notification is enqueued — it is cheaper to drop a message early than to render, route, and send it only to violate a user's wishes.

The three checks

  • Channel opt-in/opt-out per category. A user may want order updates on push but not email, and no marketing on any channel. The preference row keys on (user_id, category, channel). The service intersects the notification's requested channels with the user's enabled channels; if nothing remains for a non-critical category, it drops the notification entirely.
  • Do-not-disturb (quiet hours). A user in Singapore who set DND 22:00–08:00 (SGT) should not be buzzed at 3am about a promo. The service compares "now" in the user's timezone against their DND window. A marketing notification inside the window is deferred (scheduled for the window's end) or dropped; a transactional one (OTP, fraud alert) bypasses DND because safety beats silence.
  • Frequency capping. Even opted-in users tire of too many pings. A cap like "≤ 3 marketing pushes per user per day" is enforced with a Redis counter: INCR cap:{user_id}:{date} with a daily TTL; if it exceeds the cap, the message is suppressed. This is what stops a buggy campaign from sending one user forty pushes.

Transactional always wins

The recurring rule: priority decides who gets bypassed. Caps and DND apply to marketing/digest traffic. Transactional notifications — payment receipts, OTPs, security alerts — skip caps and DND, because their value is in arriving, not in being polite. Encoding priority on the request (Step 03) is what makes this distinction enforceable.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you honor user preferences and rate caps at scale?" Key points: check before enqueue (cheap), cache preferences in Redis (read-heavy, rarely changing), enforce caps with an atomic INCR + daily TTL, store DND in the user's timezone, and let transactional priority bypass both. Mention the failure mode: if the preference store is unreachable, you fail closed for marketing (don't risk spamming) but fail open for transactional (an OTP must go out).

09 The details that bite

Device tokens, templating & tracking

Three supporting concerns separate a toy from a production system. Each has a sharp failure mode interviewers probe.

Device-token management

To push to a phone you need its device token — an opaque per-app-install address issued by APNs or FCM. The app registers its token on login and re-registers when it rotates (OS updates, reinstall). The pain is stale tokens: a user uninstalls, but you still hold the old token and keep trying to push to a dead address. The fix is feedback-driven cleanup: when the provider responds with a permanent error like FCM's NotRegistered or APNs' Unregistered, the worker marks that token valid = false and stops using it. Skipping cleanup means a slowly rising share of your push volume is wasted on phantom devices — and your "delivered" rate quietly rots.

Templating & localization

You do not hand-write copy per notification; you store a versioned, localized template and render it with the event's data. order_shipped_v3 for locale vi-VN reads "Đơn {{order_id}} đang giao, dự kiến {{eta_min}} phút". Versioning lets you change wording without redeploying senders, and lets analytics compare v2 vs v3 open rates. The render step happens in the worker, just before the provider call, so templates can be edited live and only ever applied to messages not yet sent.

Tracking the lifecycle

Each notification moves through states: queued → sent → delivered → opened, or failed. The transitions come from different sources — the worker records sent when the provider accepts; delivery/open receipts arrive asynchronously via provider webhooks/callbacks (an email open pixel, an APNs delivery receipt, an SMS DLR). These events flow back into the notification tracking table (and a stream like Kafka → a warehouse for analytics). This is how you answer "what's our delivery rate by channel?" and detect a provider silently dropping messages.

Under the hood — sent ≠ delivered

Be precise about vocabulary in the interview. Sent means the provider accepted the request from us. Delivered means it reached the device/inbox — confirmed only by a later callback. Opened means the user engaged. Conflating them is the most common mistake; a high "sent" rate with a low "delivered" rate usually means stale tokens or a provider problem.

10 Wrap-up

Scaling & failure: what breaks at 10x

Take the design to 1 billion notifications a day and ask what gives first. Each answer reuses a piece we already built.

A provider goes down

APNs has an outage. Workers see timeouts; the retry+backoff loop (Step 07) keeps re-enqueuing, the circuit breaker slows consumption, and messages accumulate in the push queue rather than being lost. When APNs recovers, the workers drain the backlog. If you run multiple SMS aggregators, a worker can failover to a secondary provider on sustained errors. The user-visible effect is delay, not loss — exactly what queues buy you. "What happens when APNs is down?" → the queue absorbs, retries ride it out, optionally failover; nothing drops.

A worker dies

Because workers are stateless and pull from a durable queue, a dead worker just means its in-flight messages become visible again (visibility timeout expires) and another worker picks them up. The dedup key (Step 06) ensures the redelivered message is not double-sent if the original actually went out. Autoscaling on queue depth spins up replacements.

Hot partitions & the tracking firehose

At 10x, the notification table takes ~10B writes/day. Partition it by user_id (or a hash) in Cassandra/DynamoDB so writes spread evenly; a TTL expires old rows automatically. Beware a celebrity/hot-segment problem: a single huge segment fanned out can hot-spot one fan-out worker — shard the fan-out work itself by splitting the segment into chunks processed in parallel.

The thundering-herd campaign

A "11.11 sale starts NOW" push to 50M users is the worst case. Mitigations: pre-expand the segment ahead of time, enqueue with the campaign onto the low-priority queue so transactional traffic is untouched, meter delivery with per-provider rate limiting, and consider deliberate jittered scheduling (spread the 50M sends over a few minutes) so you neither melt the provider nor self-DDoS your own app when everyone taps at once.

The follow-ups, in one breath

Avoid duplicates? idempotency key + dedup SETNX + unique constraint. APNs down? queues absorb, retry/backoff, optional failover, no loss. Honor preferences? gate before enqueue, cached prefs, atomic cap counters, DND in user tz, transactional bypasses. Absorb a spike? per-channel + priority queues meter bursts into rate-limited providers. If you can rattle those off, you have the lesson.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole system compresses to one sentence: fan one event out to many users across isolated, rate-limited channels — with queues to absorb bursts, at-least-once + dedup so nobody is paged twice, and a preference gate so nobody is spammed. If you can draw the five-stage pipeline and name the bottleneck each piece solves, you can carry the interview.
