24Lesson · System Design Interview

Design a News Feed System

The home feed is the canonical fan-out problem. When someone posts, do you precompute the feed of every follower right away — fast reads, brutal writes — or do you assemble the feed fresh on each read — cheap writes, slow reads? The senior answer refuses to pick: it pushes for ordinary accounts, pulls for celebrities, and merges the two at read time.

About these notes: original study material on news-feed system design — a standard, widely-documented interview topic. The explanations, numbers, examples, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Singapore super-app context (TikTok, Shopee, Grab) is woven in for flavor. Three diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow.
00 The problem

What a feed really is

Open the TikTok "Following" tab, the Shopee Feed, or any home timeline and you see a list: the recent posts from accounts you follow, newest-ish first. That list is the news feed — a per-user, continuously updating view assembled from the activity of everyone in your social graph (the network of who-follows-whom). It looks trivial. It is one of the hardest read-scaling problems in consumer software.

The difficulty is a mismatch of fan-out. One post by one user must eventually appear in the feeds of all of that user's followers. If you have 300 followers that is 300 places the post might need to land; if you are a creator with 30 million followers it is 30 million places. Meanwhile the read side is relentless: hundreds of millions of users refresh their feed many times a day, and each refresh must feel instant. So the system is extremely read-heavy, the work-per-write is wildly uneven across users, and the whole thing has to return in tens of milliseconds.

The entire interview is a fight over when you do the expensive merge work — at write time (when content is posted) or at read time (when a feed is requested) — and how you special-case the users who break whichever choice you made. Everything below is built from that single tension.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Before drawing boxes, narrow the problem out loud. "News feed" hides a dozen products; the interviewer wants to see you carve out a tractable v1 and name what you are deferring.

Functional requirements (what it does)

  • Publish a post. A user creates a post — text, plus maybe an image, video, or product link. It is durably stored and timestamped.
  • Follow / unfollow. A user can follow another user. Following is directed (asymmetric): you can follow an account that does not follow you back. This defines the social graph that drives every feed.
  • View the home feed. A user opens the app and gets a list of recent posts from the people they follow, in reverse-chronological or ranked order, paginated as they scroll.

Things I will explicitly defer so v1 stays sharp: comments and reactions, notifications (a separate lesson), media transcoding and storage internals, the recommendation/"For You" engine, and direct messaging. I will gesture at ranking at the end because it changes the read path, but the core design is the fan-out.

Non-functional requirements (what makes it hard)

  • Low-latency reads. Opening the feed should feel instant — a p99 of well under ~200 ms for the first page. This is the dominant constraint and it pushes us toward precomputation.
  • Read-heavy. People look far more than they post. A read:write ratio of roughly 100:1 is typical, so we optimize the read path hard and are willing to do more work on write.
  • High availability. A feed that is a few seconds stale is fine; a feed that fails to load is not. We will favor eventual consistency — a new post showing up a second or two late is acceptable.
  • Scale. Tens of millions of daily users, and — critically — a follower distribution with an extreme long tail: most accounts have a few hundred followers, a handful have tens of millions.
Interview tip

Say the magic sentence early: "this is read-heavy and latency-sensitive, so I'll precompute feeds — but I have to handle celebrity accounts specially or precomputation explodes." That single line tells the interviewer you already see the trap that the rest of the interview is about, and it earns you room to build toward the hybrid.

02 Estimation

Back-of-envelope estimation

Numbers turn vague intuitions into design constraints. Pick round figures and state them so the interviewer can follow your arithmetic. Imagine a regional super-app feed, say at Shopee scale.

Traffic: reads dwarf writes

  • DAU: 100 million daily active users.
  • Posts (writes): assume each user posts on average twice a day. 100M × 2 = 200M posts/day → ≈ 2,300 writes/sec average, call it ~7,000 writes/sec at peak (a 3× peak factor).
  • Feed views (reads): assume each user opens the feed ~10 times a day. 100M × 10 = 1B feed reads/day → ≈ 11,600 reads/sec average, ~35,000 reads/sec at peak.

So the read:write ratio is about 5:1 in request volume — and if you count the posts surfaced rather than requests, far higher, because one feed read returns dozens of posts. This is why we are willing to pay extra at write time to make reads cheap.

The follower distribution is the real story

Averages lie here. Suppose the median account has ~200 followers, so an average post fans out to a couple hundred feeds — totally fine. But a top creator has 30M+ followers. When that one account posts, a naive precompute design must write that one post into 30 million feeds. At a few thousand celebrity posts a day across the platform, that is the difference between a calm system and a write tsunami. Hold onto this number; it is what forces the hybrid.

Storage: feeds are cheap, posts are not

  • A precomputed feed stores post IDs, not post bodies. An ID is ~8 bytes; cache ~500 IDs per user → ~4 KB/user. For 100M users that is ~400 GB of feed index — a comfortable fit in a Redis cluster.
  • The posts themselves (text + metadata) at ~1 KB each × 200M/day ≈ 200 GB/day of new post data, before media. Media (images/video) goes to object storage (S3-style) plus a CDN and dwarfs everything else, but it is out of the feed's hot path — the feed only stores references.
Why this matters

The estimation already dictates the architecture: store the feed as a small list of IDs in fast memory (Redis), keep post bodies and media in durable stores reached by ID, and design the write path around the worst-case fan-out, not the average. We separate "what order do I show?" (the feed index) from "what is the content?" (the post store) — and that separation is the backbone of every design that follows.

03 Contracts

API & data model

Sketch the contract before the architecture so everyone agrees on the shapes moving around. Three operations cover v1: post, follow, and read-feed.

The API

POST /v1/posts
  Authorization: Bearer <token>        # author = current user
  { "text": "GrabFood lunch run, who's in?",
    "media_ids": ["m_8842"] }           # media uploaded separately
  -> 201 { "post_id": "p_91f3", "created_at": 1717200000 }

POST /v1/users/{target_id}/follow      # current user follows target
  -> 204

GET  /v1/feed?limit=20&cursor=<opaque>  # the home feed, paginated
  -> 200 {
       "items": [ { "post_id": "p_91f3", "author_id": "u_77",
                    "text": "...", "created_at": 1717200000,
                    "media": [...] }, ... ],
       "next_cursor": "eyJ0cyI6MTcxNzE5..." # null when no more
     }

Note the read endpoint takes a cursor, not a page number — we will see in step 09 why offset pagination breaks on a feed that is constantly changing.

The data model

Three logical stores, each chosen for what it must do well:

  • Posts — the source of truth for content. Keyed by post_id, holding author_id, text, media_ids, created_at. This is append-heavy and read-by-ID, a great fit for a wide-column store like Cassandra or a key-value store like DynamoDB, partitioned by post_id.
  • Social graph (follows) — who follows whom. The two queries that matter: "give me everyone U follows" (the read/pull path) and "give me everyone who follows U" (the write/fan-out path). You need both directions, so store the edge twice or use a graph/adjacency design.
  • Feed cache — the precomputed per-user list of post_ids, newest first. This is the thing that makes reads instant. Natural fit: a Redis sorted set or list keyed feed:{user_id}, capped at ~500 entries.
-- social graph, stored in BOTH directions for O(1) lookups
followers(user_id, follower_id, created_at)   -- "who follows user_id?"  (fan-out)
following(user_id, followee_id, created_at)   -- "who does user_id follow?" (pull)

-- feed cache (Redis), per user, capped & sorted by time
feed:{user_id}  ->  ZSET { post_id : created_at_score }   -- newest-first
Under the hood — why store follows twice

A relational follows(follower, followee) table can answer both questions, but only one of them with an index hit; the other needs a second index or a scan. At feed scale you query both directions millions of times a second, so you denormalize: maintain a followers list and a following list and update both on every follow/unfollow. You trade write amplification and a consistency burden (keep the two in sync) for two cheap, symmetric reads. That trade — pay on write to make reads cheap — is the exact pattern the whole feed is built on.

04 The skeleton

High-level architecture

Split the system cleanly into two flows, because they have opposite cost profiles. The write path (posting) and the read path (viewing) share storage but otherwise barely touch — and decoupling them is what lets us scale each independently.

On the write path, a POST /posts hits the Post service, which persists the post, then hands off to a Fanout service via a message queue (Kafka). The queue matters: fan-out can be slow and bursty, so we do not make the user wait for it. The author gets their 201 the moment the post is durably stored; the fan-out happens asynchronously behind the queue.

On the read path, GET /feed hits the Feed service, which reads the precomputed list of post IDs from the Feed cache (Redis), then hydrates them — fetches the actual post bodies and author info from the post store (with its own cache) — and returns the assembled page. The two paths meet only at storage.

Interviewer follow-up

"Why a queue between Post and Fanout?" Because fan-out for a popular author can take seconds and touch millions of keys — you cannot block the author's request on it. The queue gives you async decoupling (the post is acknowledged immediately), buffering (absorb spikes), retry (if a fan-out worker dies, the message is redelivered), and independent scaling (add fan-out workers without touching the post API).

05 Strategy A · push

Fan-out on write (push)

Fan-out on write, also called the push model, does the merge work the instant a post is created. When user U posts, the fan-out service looks up every follower of U and writes the new post_id into each follower's precomputed feed list. The feed is fully assembled and waiting before anyone reads it.

How it works, step by step

  • Post service persists the post in the post store and emits a post_created event onto Kafka.
  • A fan-out worker consumes the event, reads followers(U) from the social graph.
  • For each follower F, it prepends post_id to feed:{F} in Redis and trims the list back to the cap (say 500). This is a tight loop of cheap Redis writes — often pipelined or batched.
  • Later, when F opens the app, the feed read is almost free: read feed:{F}, hydrate the IDs, done. No graph traversal at read time.

Worked example

A home cook on the Shopee Feed with 800 followers posts a recipe. Fan-out writes 800 entries across the Redis cluster — a few milliseconds of background work spread over the shards. When any of those 800 followers later refreshes, their feed is already built; the read is a single list fetch plus hydration. For the overwhelming majority of accounts, this is exactly what you want: reads are instant and writes are trivially cheap.

The tools, and the knobs that matter

In practice the fan-out workers consume from Kafka (partitioned by author so ordering per author is preserved) and write to a Redis cluster. The knobs you tune: the feed cap (how many IDs to retain — bigger caps cost memory but let users scroll deeper before a rebuild); pipelining / batching of the Redis writes (one round trip carrying many ZADDs instead of one round trip each, which is the difference between a fan-out worker keeping up and falling behind); the worker pool size and Kafka partition count (your fan-out throughput ceiling); and the active-follower optimization — only fanning out eagerly to recently-active followers and lazily rebuilding feeds for users who return after a long absence, so you stop burning writes on dormant accounts.

Strengths

  • Reads are blazing fast — just fetch a precomputed list, no graph work
  • Read cost is constant regardless of how many people you follow
  • The expensive work runs async, off the user's critical path

Watch-outs

  • Writes are expensive and amplify by follower count
  • Wasted effort fanning out to inactive followers who may never read
  • Catastrophic for celebrities — see the next step
  • New post takes a moment to appear (fan-out lag)
06 Where push breaks

The celebrity write storm

Push is wonderful until a popular account posts. Recall the estimation: a top creator has 30 million followers. Under pure fan-out-on-write, that single post must be written into 30 million feed lists. This is the celebrity problem (sometimes the "hot key" or "thundering herd" of fan-out), and it is the single most important thing to name in this interview.

Why it is catastrophic, concretely

  • Write amplification. One logical write becomes 30M physical writes. Even at 100k Redis writes/sec per node, a 30M fan-out monopolizes serious capacity, and you may have several celebrities posting in the same minute.
  • Latency & staleness. Fan-out that takes tens of seconds means a follower who refreshes early misses the post until their slice of the loop completes — the feed becomes inconsistent across followers.
  • Wasted work at scale. Of those 30M followers, perhaps a few percent are active today. You burned millions of writes for feeds nobody will read before the entry is trimmed away.
  • Queue backpressure. A burst of celebrity posts floods the fan-out queue, delaying fan-out for ordinary users stuck behind them in line.

Failure mode

When a mega-creator and several large accounts post within seconds — say during a live shopping event on TikTok Shop — the fan-out workers saturate, the Kafka lag climbs into the minutes, hot Redis shards holding popular feeds get CPU-bound, and every user's new posts are delayed because the workers are busy servicing the giants. One account's popularity degrades the whole platform's freshness. Pure push does not gracefully degrade here; it falls over.

Interviewer follow-up

"What happens when a user with 30 million followers posts?" The wrong answer is "the fan-out service writes 30 million entries." The right answer names the write storm, the queue backpressure, and the wasted work on inactive followers — and then pivots: "so for high-fan-out accounts I won't push at all. I'll pull their posts in at read time. Let me show the pull model, then the hybrid that combines both."

07 Strategy B · pull

Fan-out on read (pull)

Fan-out on read, the pull model, is push's mirror image: do nothing special at write time, and assemble the feed fresh when it is requested. When U posts, you simply store the post. Done. When a viewer opens their feed, the feed service looks up everyone the viewer follows, fetches each of those authors' recent posts, merges them by time, and returns the top slice.

How it works, step by step

  • On write: persist the post. No fan-out, no follower lookup, no Redis writes. Posting a celebrity update costs exactly the same as posting from a brand-new account.
  • On read: read following(viewer) — the list of accounts the viewer follows.
  • For each followee, fetch their N most recent posts (ideally from a per-author recent-posts cache).
  • Merge all those posts by created_at (a k-way merge of sorted lists), take the top page, hydrate, return.

Why pull is great for celebrities and bad for everyone reading

A celebrity's post is now free to publish — that completely kills the write storm. But the read becomes expensive and grows with how many accounts you follow. A user following 2,000 accounts triggers, in the worst case, 2,000 lookups merged on every single refresh — multiplied by 35,000 reads/sec. The read path is now the bottleneck, and reads are exactly what we promised would be fast. Pull also wastes effort: you re-merge the same feed every refresh even when nothing changed.

Strengths

  • Writes are cheap and constant — celebrity posts cost nothing extra
  • No wasted work for inactive users (you only build a feed someone asks for)
  • Always fresh — you see the latest posts the moment you refresh
  • No storage for precomputed feeds

Watch-outs

  • Reads are slow and scale with how many accounts you follow
  • Repeated, redundant merging — same feed rebuilt every refresh
  • Hard to hit a tight read p99 — the opposite of our top requirement
08 The senior answer · hybrid

The hybrid & read-time merge

Neither pure model wins. Push gives fast reads but dies on celebrities; pull survives celebrities but gives slow reads. The senior answer is the hybrid: push for ordinary accounts, pull for celebrities, and merge the two at read time.

How the hybrid works

  • Classify authors. Tag each author with a follower count. If they sit above a fan-out threshold (say 100k–1M followers), mark them a celebrity. This flag lives on the user record and is cheap to check.
  • On write — if the author is ordinary, fan out on write (push) as in step 05. If the author is a celebrity, skip fan-out entirely; just store the post in the author's own recent-posts list. No write storm.
  • On read — start from the viewer's precomputed feed:{viewer} (which already contains all the pushed posts from ordinary accounts they follow). Then look at which celebrities the viewer follows, pull those celebrities' recent posts, and merge them by time into the precomputed list. Return the top page.

This gets the best of both: the precomputed base makes reads mostly cheap, the celebrity pull is bounded (a viewer follows only a handful of celebrities, not thousands), and the catastrophic write storm is gone because nobody fans out a giant account.

Bounding and caching the celebrity pull

The pull half could still get heavy if a viewer follows dozens of celebrities, so two refinements keep it tight. First, shared per-celebrity lists: each celebrity's recent posts are cached once in a single recent:{celeb_id} list, and every viewer who follows that celebrity reads the same cached list. One pull serves millions of viewers — the merge cost is per-read, but the underlying data fetch is amortized across everyone. Second, a cap on celebrity pulls per read: if a viewer somehow follows 200 celebrities, you do not pull all 200 synchronously on a tight feed request; you pull the most relevant subset and accept that an obscure celebrity's brand-new post may surface a beat late. Both refinements convert an unbounded read into a bounded, cacheable one.

Failure mode of the hybrid

The subtle risk is the threshold boundary. An account that crosses from ordinary to celebrity (or back) changes how its posts are delivered mid-stream — followers who were getting pushed copies suddenly need to pull instead, and old pushed entries linger in their feed. The fix is to reconcile at hydration: a post is shown if it is either in the precomputed list or in a followed celebrity's recent list, deduplicated by post_id. Because dedup happens at read time on IDs, a brief window where a post exists in both places is harmless — the viewer sees it exactly once.

Worked example — TikTok-style

You follow 480 ordinary creators and 6 mega-celebrities. The 480 ordinary creators push into your feed continuously, so their posts are already sitting in feed:{you}. When you open the app, the feed service grabs that precomputed list, then pulls recent posts from just those 6 celebrities and merges. Six small reads, not 486 — and zero celebrity fan-out at write time.

Push (write)

  • + Instant reads
  • + Cheap, constant-cost reads
  • − Write storm for celebrities
  • − Wasted fan-out to inactive users

Pull (read)

  • + Cheap, constant-cost writes
  • + Always fresh, no waste
  • − Slow reads, scales with following
  • − Redundant repeated merges

Hybrid

  • + Fast reads (push base)
  • + No celebrity write storm (pull)
  • + Bounded read merge
  • − More moving parts; pick the threshold
Interviewer follow-up

"Where exactly is the cutover?" There is no universal number — it is a tuned fan-out threshold (often somewhere between 100k and a few million followers) chosen so that the cost of fanning the account out exceeds the cost of pulling it on read for its active followers. Be ready to say it is configurable, can be adjusted per-author, and that a tiny fraction of accounts cross it — but those accounts cause the overwhelming majority of fan-out load, which is exactly why special-casing them pays off.

09 The read side

Feed store & pagination

The precomputed feed lives in Redis, and the natural structures are a list (LPUSH + LTRIM) or, better, a sorted set (ZADD with the timestamp as the score). The sorted set is nice because it keeps entries ordered by time automatically and lets you slice a range cheaply. We cap each feed at a few hundred IDs — nobody scrolls past that in a session, and capping bounds memory.

Hydration

The feed list holds only post_ids. To return a page you hydrate: take the top ~20 IDs and batch-fetch the post bodies and author info — first from a post cache, falling back to the post store on a miss. Hydration is a MGET-style multi-get, one round trip for the whole page. Keeping the feed as IDs (not full posts) means an edited or deleted post is reflected at hydration time, and a popular post's body is cached once and reused across millions of feeds instead of copied into each.

Cursor pagination — and why offset breaks

The classic LIMIT 20 OFFSET 40 pagination assumes the list is stable between page requests. A feed is the opposite: new posts arrive at the top constantly. If you fetch page 1, then someone you follow posts three times, then you fetch "page 2 = offset 20," everything shifted down by three — you re-see items from page 1 (duplicates) or skip items (gaps). The fix is cursor-based pagination: the cursor encodes a stable position — typically the created_at timestamp (and post_id tiebreaker) of the last item you saw. Page 2 asks for "items older than this cursor," so new arrivals at the top never disturb your scroll.

# write: prepend new post, keep newest 500
ZADD  feed:u_77  1717200000  p_91f3
ZREMRANGEBYRANK  feed:u_77  0  -501      # trim the tail

# read page: items strictly older than the cursor score, newest first
ZREVRANGEBYSCORE  feed:u_77  (1717199990  -inf  LIMIT 0 20
# next_cursor = created_at of the 20th item returned
Under the hood — the empty-feed bootstrap

What about a brand-new user, or a feed that was evicted from cache? Their feed:{user} is empty, so push has nothing to show. You rebuild on demand: fall back to the pull path once — query who they follow, merge recent posts, populate feed:{user}, and serve from it thereafter. This same rebuild path is your recovery mechanism when the feed cache loses data, which is why pull never fully goes away even in a push-first design.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you paginate a feed that is changing under the user?" Name offset's duplicate/gap problem, then describe a cursor that pins a stable position by timestamp + ID. Bonus: explain that new posts appearing above your scroll position are surfaced by a separate "new posts" pill at the top, not by shifting the page you are reading.

10 Ordering

Ranking: chronological vs ML

So far the feed is reverse-chronological — newest first, full stop. That is simple, predictable, and easy to reason about, and it is exactly what a "Following" tab usually is. But the modern default for an engagement-driven home feed (TikTok's "For You," the main Instagram/Facebook feed) is ML-ranked: instead of pure time order, a model scores each candidate post for this viewer by predicted engagement (likelihood of watch, like, comment, share) and reorders accordingly.

How ranking layers onto the design

Crucially, ranking does not replace the fan-out architecture — it sits on top of it. The feed store becomes a candidate generator: it produces the pool of posts you could see (from push + celebrity pull). A separate ranking service then takes that candidate set, computes features (post age, author affinity, the viewer's past behavior, content signals), runs the model, and returns the reordered top slice. The split is "retrieve candidates cheaply, then rank them" — and the fan-out work we designed is the retrieval half.

Reverse-chronological

  • + Simple, cheap, predictable
  • + Easy to cache and paginate by time
  • + Transparent — users trust the order
  • − Lower engagement; misses content you'd love but posted hours ago

ML-ranked

  • + Higher engagement & relevance per view
  • + Surfaces the best content regardless of post time
  • − Heavy infra: feature store, model serving, training pipelines
  • − Harder to cache (score is per-viewer, time-varying) and to debug
Bridge to L31 (Ranking)

In an interview, build the chronological hybrid first and get it solid — then say "if we want a 'For You'-style ranked feed, I'd add a ranking service that consumes this same candidate set." That separation of retrieval (this lesson) from ranking (a feature-store + model-serving problem) is the senior framing, and it keeps the scope honest. The deep dive on scoring, feature stores, and online model serving is its own lesson.

11 Resilience

Scale, failure & what breaks at 10x

The hybrid is the answer; now defend it under stress.

Cache sizing & hot keys

Cap feeds (~500 IDs) and shard Redis by user_id so feeds spread evenly across the cluster. The danger is a hot key: a single post by a giant account, or a viral post, gets hydrated by millions of feeds at once, hammering whichever shard holds that post's cached body. Mitigate by caching popular post bodies behind a read-through cache with replicas, and by adding a small local in-process cache on the feed servers for the hottest posts so the same body is not re-fetched from Redis on every request.

Rebuilding a feed

Feeds in Redis are a cache, not the source of truth — the post store and social graph are. If a Redis shard fails or a feed is evicted, you do not lose data; you rebuild the affected feeds from the durable stores using the pull path (query the user's follows, merge recent posts, repopulate). This is why we keep both code paths: pull is simultaneously a strategy for celebrities and the disaster-recovery mechanism for push. Rebuilds are also how you backfill when a user follows someone new — you can lazily pull that author in on the next read rather than retroactively fanning out their history.

Failure modes to name

  • Fanout worker dies mid-loop. Because fan-out is driven off Kafka with consumer offsets, the message is redelivered and reprocessed. Make the feed write idempotent (a ZADD of the same post_id is a no-op), so a partial-then-retried fan-out is safe.
  • Feed cache (Redis) outage. Reads fall back to pull-and-rebuild — slower, but available. Favor availability: a degraded, slightly slow feed beats an error page.
  • Post store unavailable. Writes are buffered in the queue and applied when it recovers; reads serve whatever is already hydrated/cached. The async decoupling we built in step 04 is what makes this survivable.

What breaks at 10x

Push the platform to a billion DAU and the strains shift. The fan-out fleet becomes the dominant cost — more accounts cross the celebrity threshold as the network densifies, so you may lower the threshold and shift more authors to pull. Hydration bandwidth balloons (you are fetching far more post bodies), so the post cache and CDN for media matter more than the feed index itself. And the k-way merge at read time for users who follow thousands of accounts plus dozens of celebrities gets heavy — you cap the number of celebrity pulls per read and pre-merge celebrities' posts into shared, cacheable per-celebrity lists so many viewers reuse the same pulled data. The architecture holds; you re-tune the threshold and add caching layers rather than redesigning.

Deep-dive the interviewer may push for

Consistency & ordering: two posts created milliseconds apart on different servers can land out of order — use a monotonic timestamp source or a snowflake-style ID so ordering is stable. Deletes & privacy: a deleted or now-private post must vanish from feeds — easy if feeds store IDs and you check visibility at hydration time, painful if you copied bodies. Unfollow: you don't scrub the unfollowed author's posts from the feed list eagerly; you filter them at hydration or let them age out. Each of these is a reason the feed stores IDs and resolves at read time, not full content.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: precompute feeds at write time for fast reads, but special-case the celebrities whose follower counts would make that precomputation explode — push for the masses, pull for the giants, merge at read time. If you can derive the hybrid from the push and pull failure modes, and explain cursor pagination and feed rebuilds, you can carry the interview.
