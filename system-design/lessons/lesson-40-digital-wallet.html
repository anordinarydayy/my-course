40Lesson · System Design Interview

Design a Digital Wallet

A wallet stores balances and moves money between accounts that may live on different shards — so a single transfer becomes a distributed transaction that must stay exactly correct and fully auditable, even when nodes crash mid-flight. Event sourcing plus a two-phase reservation protocol (TCC or saga) is the backbone that makes that possible.

About these notes: original study material on designing a digital wallet — a standard, widely-documented backend topic (think GrabPay, SeaMoney/PayLah, Wise). The explanations, numbers, schemas, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Two diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the money move.
00 The problem

Why a wallet is hard

A digital wallet is the ledger at the heart of any payments product: GrabPay topping up from a bank card, a SeaMoney transfer between two Shopee users, a PayLah split-bill among friends. On the surface it looks trivial — store a number, add to it, subtract from it. The difficulty is that money has rules that ordinary CRUD apps do not, and an interviewer is testing whether you respect them.

Three properties make a wallet genuinely hard:

  • Exactness. A social-feed "like" count can be off by one and nobody cares. A balance that is off by one cent is a regulatory incident. There is zero tolerance for lost or duplicated money.
  • Auditability. A finance team, an external auditor, and the Monetary Authority of Singapore can all ask "show me every cent that ever moved through this account, and prove the balance follows from it." You must be able to reconstruct any balance at any past instant.
  • Atomicity across boundaries. A transfer debits one account and credits another. If those two accounts live on different database shards or different services, you are running a distributed transaction — and the source must never be debited unless the destination is credited, even if a server dies between the two steps.

Everything in this lesson exists to satisfy those three properties under high throughput. The chain of reasoning is: we need an audit trail, so we event-source the ledger; that ledger is the source of truth, so reads need a projection (CQRS); balances are huge, so we shard by account; sharding breaks single-database atomicity, so transfers become a two-phase reservation (TCC/saga); two-phase protocols retry on failure, so every step must be idempotent; and money can still drift, so we reconcile by replaying the log.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Before drawing anything, narrow the problem out loud. A "wallet" can mean very different systems, and the interviewer wants to see you pin the scope and surface assumptions.

Functional requirements

  • Store balances for many users, possibly in multiple currencies (SGD, USD, IDR for a regional super-app).
  • Transfer funds wallet-to-wallet (the core operation).
  • Top-up from an external funding source (bank card, FAST/PayNow bank transfer) and withdraw back out.
  • Query current balance and transaction history.

Non-functional requirements

  • Exact balances — strong consistency on writes; never lose or invent a cent; never go negative.
  • Full auditability — every balance must be explainable from an immutable record, and reconstructable at any past time.
  • High throughput — peak campaigns (Shopee 11.11, Grab surge) drive bursty write load.
  • High availability with bounded latency on the read path; the write path may trade a little latency for correctness.
  • Idempotency — clients and networks retry; a retried top-up must not double-credit.

Out of scope (state your assumptions)

To keep the interview focused, explicitly punt on: FX rate determination (assume an external rate service), KYC/onboarding, fraud scoring, the card-acquiring rails themselves, and interest/lending. Say "I'll treat top-up and withdraw as integrations with an external payment gateway and focus the design on the internal ledger and transfers." That single sentence buys you the rest of the hour.

Interview tip

The phrase that frames the entire design: "The ledger is the source of truth, and a transfer is a distributed transaction — so my two hardest problems are exactly-correct cross-shard atomicity and full auditability." Land that early and every later decision has an obvious motivation.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope

A rough sizing tells you whether a single Postgres node is enough or whether you must shard from day one. Assume a regional wallet at super-app scale.

Throughput

  • 100 million registered wallets; say 20 million daily active.
  • Each active user does ~5 money movements per day (a top-up, a couple of payments, a transfer). That's 100 million transactions/day.
  • Average write rate: 100M / 86,400s ≈ ~1,200 transfers/sec. Each transfer is a debit + a credit, so ~2,400 ledger appends/sec average.
  • Peak is the number that matters. A 11.11 flash sale or payday can push 5–10× the average → plan for ~15,000–25,000 ledger writes/sec at peak.
  • Reads (balance checks, history views) dominate volume — easily 10–50× the writes. A read-heavy ratio is the headline justification for CQRS.

Storage

  • A ledger entry is small — id, account, amount, currency, type, references, timestamp — call it ~300 bytes on disk with indexes.
  • 100M txns/day × 2 entries × 300 B ≈ ~60 GB/day of immutable ledger, ~22 TB/year.
  • This is append-only and never updated, which is exactly what cheap, replicated, partitioned storage and cold-tier archival are good at.

The conclusion writes itself: ~15k+ writes/sec of strongly-consistent money data, tens of TB/year, billions of immutable rows. No single node survives that. You will shard, and because money is involved you cannot tolerate the usual NoSQL "eventually it'll be fine." That tension drives the rest of the design.

03 Contract

The API and data model

Sketch the external contract and the storage entities before architecture — it forces the consistency and idempotency requirements to become concrete.

The API surface

Keep it small and make every mutating call carry an idempotency key: a client-generated unique id (often a UUID) that the server uses to deduplicate retries. The same key replayed must return the same result, never a second movement of money.

POST /v1/transfers
  Idempotency-Key: 9f1c-ab33-...      # client-generated, unique per intent
  {
    "from_wallet": "wlt_alice",
    "to_wallet":   "wlt_bob",
    "amount":      "20.00",            # decimal string, never a float
    "currency":   "SGD",
    "reference":   "split dinner"
  }
  → 201 { "transfer_id": "txf_8821", "status": "COMPLETED" }
  → 409 { "error": "INSUFFICIENT_FUNDS" }

POST /v1/topups       { wallet, amount, currency, funding_source }  + Idempotency-Key
POST /v1/withdrawals  { wallet, amount, currency, destination }     + Idempotency-Key
GET  /v1/wallets/{id}/balance        → { available, ledger_balance, currency }
GET  /v1/wallets/{id}/transactions   → paginated, newest first

Note amount is a decimal string. Money is stored as integer minor units (cents) or a fixed-precision decimal — never a binary float, because 0.1 + 0.2 != 0.3 in floating point and that rounding error is real lost money. Mention this; it signals you've worked on payments.

The data model

Three core entities. The account (wallet) is the thing balances belong to. The ledger entry is one immutable line of the double-entry ledger. The transaction groups the entries of one logical movement so they balance to zero.

accounts                          ledger_entries  (append-only, immutable)
  account_id   PK                   entry_id      PK
  user_id                           account_id    FK  (shard key)
  currency                          txn_id        FK
  created_at                        direction     DEBIT | CREDIT
                                    amount_minor  BIGINT   (cents)
transactions                       balance_after BIGINT   (running balance)
  txn_id       PK                   created_at    TIMESTAMP
  type   TRANSFER|TOPUP|WITHDRAW
  status PENDING|COMPLETED|FAILED  idempotency_keys
  idempotency_key  UNIQUE            key          PK
  created_at                        txn_id, result, created_at

Two invariants the schema enforces. First, double-entry: every transaction's entries sum to zero — a S$20 transfer is a −2000 debit on Alice and a +2000 credit on Bob. If the sum of all entries in the system is ever non-zero, money was created or destroyed and you have a bug. Second, idempotency_key is unique, so a replayed request collides at the database level and is safely rejected rather than re-executed.

Why double-entry

Accountants settled this 500 years ago: record every movement twice, once as a source and once as a destination, so the books always balance. In software it gives you a free, continuous integrity check — SELECT SUM(amount_minor) FROM ledger_entries must equal zero (or the total of external top-ups). It also makes the meaning of every entry unambiguous, which is what an auditor needs.

04 The core decision

The balance model: event-sourced ledger

Here is the heart of the design. How do you store a balance? There are two camps, and choosing event sourcing is the answer the interviewer is fishing for.

The naive way: a mutable balance column

The obvious design stores balance as a single mutable number on the account row. A transfer does UPDATE accounts SET balance = balance - 20 WHERE id = alice. Reads are a single fast lookup. The fatal problem: the balance has no history. When a customer disputes a charge or an auditor asks how the number got to S$38.40, you can only shrug — the previous values were overwritten. A bug that corrupts a balance is unrecoverable, because there's no record to recompute from. Money systems cannot live like this.

The right way: event sourcing

Event sourcing flips the model: you never store the balance as a primitive fact. Instead you store an append-only, immutable log of events — every debit and credit that ever happened — and you derive the balance by folding over them. The log is the single source of truth; the balance is a computed view of it.

How it works under the hood, step by step:

  • A transfer doesn't mutate a balance. It appends two ledger entries: (alice, DEBIT, 2000) and (bob, CREDIT, 2000), both tagged with the same txn_id.
  • Entries are never updated or deleted. Made a mistake? You don't edit the wrong entry — you append a compensating reversal entry. The log only ever grows.
  • An account's balance at any time T is the sum of all its entries with created_at ≤ T. Want last Tuesday's balance for a dispute? Sum up to last Tuesday. The past is always available because the past was never overwritten.

Worked example. Alice tops up S$100, pays S$30 to a hawker via SeaMoney, receives S$10 from Bob. The log holds three entries: +10000, −3000, +1000. Her balance is the fold: 0 → 10000 → 7000 → 8000 = S$80.00. If a deploy bug accidentally double-applies the hawker payment, you don't panic-patch a number — you replay the (correct) log and the right balance reappears.

Mutable balance

  • + Reads are one fast lookup
  • + Tiny storage footprint
  • − No history — can't audit or explain a balance
  • − Corruption is unrecoverable; nothing to recompute from
  • − Disputes and reversals are awkward

Event-sourced ledger

  • + Full audit trail by construction
  • + Any past balance reconstructable; replayable after bugs
  • + Natural fit for double-entry and regulators
  • − Reads need a projection (folding is slow at scale)
  • − More storage; the log grows forever
Interviewer follow-up

"Why event-source the ledger instead of just storing a balance?" Answer: auditability and recoverability. The mutable balance discards the very information regulators and dispute teams require, and leaves no way to recover from corruption. With an immutable log, the balance is always provable and always recomputable — and you'll see in the next step how to keep reads fast despite the fold.

05 Fast reads

CQRS: write events, project a balance

Event sourcing creates a problem it must then solve. If a balance is a fold over the whole log, then a chatty user with 50,000 lifetime entries would force you to sum 50,000 rows on every balance check. At tens of thousands of reads per second that is hopeless. The fix is CQRS — Command Query Responsibility Segregation: split the system into a write side that appends events and a read side that serves a pre-computed view.

How the projection works

  • The command side validates and appends ledger entries (the events). This is the source of truth and is strongly consistent.
  • A projector consumes the event stream and maintains a read model — a denormalized balances table holding the current number per account, plus convenient indexes for the transaction-history view.
  • The query side reads that table directly: a balance check is now a single indexed lookup, not a fold.

Crucially, the read model is a cache you can throw away. If it's ever wrong — a projector bug, a bad deploy — you delete it and rebuild it by replaying the log from the beginning. The truth was never in the read model; it was always in the events.

Synchronous vs asynchronous projection

You have a choice with real consequences. Synchronous: in the same database transaction that appends the entries, update the balances row. The read model is always consistent, but you pay the cost on every write. Asynchronous: append the events, then a separate consumer (off a Kafka topic or a change-data-capture stream like Debezium) updates the read model a few milliseconds later. This scales the read side independently but introduces a brief window where the projected balance lags the truth — eventual consistency on reads.

The pragmatic answer for a wallet: keep the authoritative available balance used for the negative-balance check strongly consistent on the write shard (you'll read it inside the same transaction in step 09), but let secondary read views — history feeds, monthly statements, analytics — be projected asynchronously where a small lag is harmless.

Interviewer follow-up

"If the projection is async and a user tops up then immediately checks their balance, they might see the old number — how do you handle that?" Two moves:
(1) serve the negative-balance check and the just-completed transfer from the strongly-consistent write path, and
(2) use read-your-writes — after a successful write, return the freshly computed balance directly rather than re-querying the lagging projection.

06 Scale the store

Sharding balances by account

From the estimation, the ledger is tens of TB/year and 15k+ writes/sec at peak — far past one node. So we shard: partition accounts across many database nodes, each owning a slice of the keyspace. The natural shard key is account_id, typically hash(account_id) mod N or, better, consistent hashing so adding a shard only remaps a fraction of keys.

Sharding by account has a lovely property: all of one account's entries live on one shard. So computing or rebuilding a single balance never crosses the network — the fold is local. Top-ups and withdrawals (which touch one wallet plus an external system) are single-shard operations and can run as ordinary local ACID transactions on Postgres or a similar engine.

The bottleneck sharding creates

Here is the problem that defines the rest of the lesson. A transfer touches two accounts. If Alice is on shard A and Bob is on shard B — which, with random hashing, they usually are — then the debit and the credit live in two different databases. A single local transaction can no longer wrap both. You've lost the one mechanism that guaranteed all-or-nothing.

The textbook answer would be a two-phase commit (2PC) coordinated across both databases. In practice payments teams avoid classic 2PC: it holds locks across the network for the whole protocol, and if the coordinator dies after PREPARE, participants are stuck holding locks indefinitely (the blocking problem). At thousands of transfers/sec that lock contention is a non-starter. Instead we move the atomicity up into the application with a reservation protocol — TCC or saga — which is the next step.

07 The cross-shard transfer

TCC: Try, Confirm, Cancel

TCC (Try–Confirm–Cancel) solves cross-shard atomicity without distributed locks by splitting each operation into three local steps, each of which is a normal ACID transaction on its own shard. A coordinator (the transfer service) drives them.

  • Try — reserve resources, don't commit the final effect. On shard A, atomically check Alice has S$20 available and move it from available into a held / reserved bucket (a pending debit). On shard B, prepare to receive — record a pending credit. Funds are now earmarked but not yet spendable or received.
  • Confirm — if both Trys succeeded, commit both legs: finalize the debit on A (the held amount leaves Alice for good) and finalize the credit on B (Bob can now spend it). Each Confirm is a small local transaction.
  • Cancel — if any Try fails or times out, release: return the held amount to Alice's available balance on A, and drop the pending credit on B. The system is back exactly where it started.

How it works under the hood

The coordinator persists the transfer's state machine (TRYING → CONFIRMING → DONE or → CANCELLING → CANCELLED) in its own durable store before acting. After a crash it reads that state and resumes — re-driving Confirm or Cancel until both shards acknowledge. Because every step is idempotent (step 09), re-driving is safe: a duplicate Confirm on a leg already confirmed is a no-op. The key invariant: once both Trys succeed, the transfer can only roll forward to Confirm; it never silently disappears.

Worked example. Grab passenger pays a driver S$24 via GrabPay. Try: hold S$24 from the passenger (shard A), stage a S$24 inbound for the driver (shard B). Both succeed → Confirm both → driver's available balance rises by S$24, passenger's held S$24 is gone. If the driver's shard is briefly unreachable during Try, the coordinator Cancels — the passenger's S$24 is released back, and the app shows "payment failed, try again." No money ever sat in limbo.

Under the hood: why "held" matters

The reservation bucket is what makes TCC safe under concurrency. Between Try and Confirm, Alice's S$20 is in held, not available. A second concurrent payment sees only her available balance, so she can't double-spend the same funds. Two balances are tracked per account: ledger balance (committed) and available balance (ledger minus active holds). The negative-balance check always tests available.

08 The trade-off

Saga vs TCC

TCC is one of two common patterns for application-level distributed transactions. The other is the saga. Both abandon global locking and instead chain local transactions, but they differ in how they handle the "undo."

A saga executes each step's real effect immediately and, on failure, runs a compensating transaction to undo the steps already committed. For a transfer: actually debit Alice (committed), then try to credit Bob; if the credit fails, run the compensation — credit Alice back. There is no reservation phase; effects are visible as they happen, and "undo" is a semantic reversal after the fact.

TCC instead reserves first (Try) and only commits visible effects in Confirm. The money is held but never prematurely visible, so there's no window where Alice's balance is wrong from her own perspective.

Saga (do + compensate)

  • + Simple to bolt onto existing services — no Try API needed
  • + Good for long, multi-step business workflows
  • − Effects are visible mid-flight; an intermediate state can be read (no isolation)
  • − Compensation can be messy: what if Alice already spent the credited money before the reversal?
  • − Harder to reason about correctness across many steps

TCC (reserve + commit)

  • + Reservation gives isolation — held funds aren't spendable, no dirty reads
  • + Clean money semantics; matches "hold then capture" in payments
  • + Confirm/Cancel are idempotent and easy to re-drive after a crash
  • − Every participant must implement three operations (Try/Confirm/Cancel)
  • − More upfront design and more state to track

For a wallet's core transfer, TCC wins: the reservation maps perfectly onto a money "hold," and isolation prevents the double-spend that a saga's visible-mid-flight model invites. Sagas are still useful for the outer orchestration — e.g. a top-up workflow that calls an external gateway, waits for settlement, then credits the wallet — where you genuinely can't reserve on a third party and must compensate instead.

Interviewer follow-up

"Why not just use two-phase commit?" Because classic 2PC holds locks across the network for the duration of the protocol and blocks if the coordinator dies after PREPARE — participants can't release locks and the shard stalls. TCC keeps each phase a short local transaction, persists the coordinator's intent so it can resume after a crash, and never holds a cross-shard lock. That's why payments systems reach for TCC/saga, not 2PC.

09 Correctness

Never negative, exactly once

Two correctness hazards can still bite even with TCC in place: a balance going negative under concurrency, and an operation taking effect more than once. Both are interview favorites.

Preventing a negative balance under concurrency

Imagine Alice has S$20 and fires two S$20 payments at the same instant onto different app servers. The naive flow — read balance, check ≥ 20, then write the debit — has a classic race: both reads see S$20, both checks pass, both debit, and Alice ends at −S$20. The fix is to make the check and the debit a single atomic, conditional operation guarded by the database, not by application logic.

The cleanest form is a conditional UPDATE that only succeeds if funds remain, executed inside the Try on the source shard:

UPDATE balances
   SET available = available - 2000,
       held      = held      + 2000
 WHERE account_id = 'wlt_alice'
   AND available  >= 2000;        -- the guard

-- rows_affected == 1  → reservation succeeded
-- rows_affected == 0  → insufficient funds, fail the Try

The database's row lock serializes the two concurrent statements: the first to acquire the lock subtracts and commits; the second now reads available = 0, the WHERE guard fails, zero rows change, and that payment is rejected. There is no window between the check and the write because they are the same statement. (Alternatives: SELECT ... FOR UPDATE to lock the row first, or optimistic concurrency with a version column and retry. The conditional UPDATE is simplest and lock-cheap.)

Idempotency and exactly-once effects

Networks retry. A client whose connection drops mid-request will re-send; the TCC coordinator re-drives Confirm after a crash. Without protection, a retried top-up double-credits. Idempotency means applying the same operation N times has the same effect as applying it once.

How it works under the hood: the unique idempotency_key from the API is recorded in the same transaction that performs the effect. On a retry, the insert of the key collides with the unique constraint, the transaction aborts, and the server returns the stored original result instead of moving money again. For internal TCC legs, the (txn_id, leg, phase) triple is the dedup key — a second Confirm for an already-confirmed leg finds the marker and no-ops.

True exactly-once across a network is impossible (you can always lose the acknowledgement); what you build is at-least-once delivery + idempotent effects = effectively-once. State it that way; it shows precision.

Interviewer follow-up

"Two requests try to spend the last S$20 simultaneously — what happens?" Walk the conditional UPDATE: the row lock serializes them, the guard available >= 2000 lets exactly one through, the other gets zero rows affected and a clean INSUFFICIENT_FUNDS. No negative balance, no lost update. Then add: "and the idempotency key makes the client's retry of the winner a safe no-op."

10 Trust but verify

Reconciliation and replay

Even with a careful design, you must prove the books are right, not just hope. That proof is reconciliation: independent processes that verify the system's money adds up, and that it matches the outside world.

Internal reconciliation: the books must balance

Because every transaction is double-entry, an invariant holds at all times: the sum of all ledger entries equals the net of external money in (top-ups) minus money out (withdrawals). A scheduled job sums entries per shard and globally and checks the identity. If it drifts by even one cent, an alert fires and an engineer investigates before customers do. This is the safety net that catches a projector bug, a half-applied compensation, or a TCC leg that confirmed on one shard but not the other.

External reconciliation: match the bank

Top-ups and withdrawals cross into bank rails (PayNow/FAST, card networks). Daily, you pull the bank's settlement file and match each external movement to a wallet transaction. A top-up the bank confirmed but the wallet never credited (a Confirm that was lost) is found here and re-driven; a wallet credit with no matching bank settlement is flagged as a potential leak. Building this from day one is what separates a toy from a real fintech.

Replay as the recovery tool

This is event sourcing's superpower paying off. When reconciliation finds a wrong projected balance, you don't hand-edit it. You replay: re-fold the immutable event log from the start (or from the last verified snapshot) to recompute the correct balance, then atomically swap the read model. The log is intact, so the truth is always recoverable. The same replay rebuilds a read model from scratch after a schema change, or seeds a new analytics store.

To keep replay fast, take periodic snapshots: persist "balance at entry #5,000,000 = X" so a rebuild folds only the tail since the last snapshot rather than years of history.

11 Scale & failure

Hot accounts, failure modes, and 10x

The hot-account bottleneck

Sharding by account spreads load evenly — until one account is a hotspot. A hot account is one with a torrent of concurrent writes: a Grab merchant settlement account taking thousands of ride payments a minute, or a Shopee 11.11 promotion float crediting cashback to millions. Every write to it serializes on that single row's lock (from the conditional UPDATE), so the row becomes the bottleneck and latency spikes.

Mitigations, in increasing order of effort:

  • Batching. Aggregate many small credits to the merchant into one periodic netted entry instead of thousands of individual row updates.
  • Sub-balances / striping. Split the hot account into N child sub-accounts (merchant#0..merchant#15); route each write to a random sub-account so the lock contention spreads across N rows. The true balance is the sum of the children. This is the same "shard the hotspot" trick used for hot counters.
  • Asynchronous credit. For incoming credits (which can't make a balance negative, so they need no guard), queue them and apply via a single-writer consumer that batches updates to the hot row.

Failure modes — what happens when a node dies?

  • Coordinator crashes mid-TCC. Its durable state machine (persisted before each action) is read on restart; it re-drives Confirm or Cancel to completion. Idempotency makes re-driving safe. A timeout on a stuck Try triggers Cancel so funds are never held forever.
  • A shard's primary dies. Run each shard as a replicated group (e.g. Postgres streaming replication, or a Raft-based store) with synchronous replication for the money tables; a standby is promoted. Synchronous replication means a committed entry is on at least two nodes before acknowledgement, so failover loses no entries.
  • A Confirm lands on shard A but the message to B is lost. The transfer is "half-confirmed." Reconciliation and the coordinator's retry loop detect the missing leg and complete it; until then Bob simply hasn't been credited (a delay, not lost money).
  • The async projector falls behind. Reads lag, but the write-path balance used for the negative check is still correct, so no overspend. The projector catches up; if it's corrupt, replay rebuilds it.

What breaks at 10x

Push the design to 150k+ writes/sec and several problems surface. The coordinator becomes a bottleneck — shard it too, partitioning transfers by a hash of the source account, and make each coordinator instance stateless over a shared durable state store. Cross-shard transfers dominate — with random hashing, almost every transfer spans two shards; consider co-locating frequently-interacting accounts (a merchant and its regular customers) on the same shard to turn cross-shard transfers back into cheap local ones. The event log's size stresses storage — tier cold history to object storage (S3) behind snapshots, keeping only recent entries on hot disks. Reconciliation can't scan the whole ledger nightly — run it incrementally per shard off the change stream rather than as one global table scan.

Deep-dive the interviewer may push for

Multi-currency transfers. A SGD→IDR transfer isn't a single double-entry pair — it's two transactions linked by an FX leg: debit the sender in SGD, credit an FX intermediary, debit the intermediary in IDR, credit the receiver in IDR, with the rate locked at Try time. Each currency's books must independently balance to zero. Mentioning this shows you understand that "transfer" hides real complexity once money crosses a currency boundary.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: make the immutable ledger the source of truth, project a fast read balance from it, shard by account, and move money across shards with an idempotent reserve-then-commit protocol — then prove it all adds up by reconciliation and replay. If you can whiteboard the event log, the TCC three-phase flow, and the negative-balance guard, you can carry this interview.
