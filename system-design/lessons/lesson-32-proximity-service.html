32Lesson · System Design Interview

Design a Proximity Service

"What's within 5 km of me?" looks innocent, but it's a 2D range query — and answering it by measuring the distance to every point on the map is hopelessly slow. The trick is a spatial index: geohash or quadtree turns nearness into a cheap prefix or tree lookup. It's the engine behind nearby restaurants on Shopee Food and nearby drivers on Grab.

About these notes: original study material on building a proximity (nearby-search) service — a standard, widely-documented system-design topic. The explanations, numbers, schemas, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. One diagram is interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the read path execute.
00 The problem

What's near me?

Open the Grab app in Singapore and tap the map. Within a fraction of a second you see the dozen drivers idling near you, or the restaurants delivering to your block. A proximity service is the component that answers one question fast: given my coordinates and a radius, which entities fall inside that circle? The entities might be static — restaurants, ATMs, MRT stations, GrabKiosks — or moving, like drivers. This lesson focuses on the static and slowly-moving case; the high-churn "live driver location" variant is its own beast (the bridge to that comes at the end).

The reason this is an interview favourite is that the obvious solution is a trap. Your data is a list of (lat, lng) pairs in a database. The instinct is: loop over them, compute the distance to each, keep the ones under 5 km. That works on a laptop with a thousand rows. It collapses the moment you have millions of points and thousands of queries per second. Everything interesting in this design is about replacing that linear scan with a spatial index — a data structure that lets you find points by location without examining points that are obviously far away.

Hold that picture. The whole lesson is about how to find the green dots without ever looking at the grey ones.

01 Scope it

Clarify & scope the requirements

Before drawing a single box, narrow the problem out loud. "Proximity" hides several different products, and the right index depends on the answers.

Functional requirements

  • Search by radius. Given (lat, lng) and a radius (or a fixed set of radii like 1/3/5 km), return the entities inside that circle. We'll assume the client picks a radius rather than asking for "the nearest 20 regardless of distance" — though we'll note how to support that too.
  • Return useful results. Not just IDs — the caller wants the entity's name, location, and enough to rank, sorted by distance ascending.
  • Manage entities. Add, update, and remove businesses. A restaurant opens, changes address, or shuts down.
  • Handle density skew. Orchard Road and the CBD are packed; Pulau Ubin is nearly empty. The design must not choke where points cluster.

Non-functional requirements

  • Low latency. Search sits in front of a map the user is staring at. Target p99 well under ~200 ms.
  • Read-heavy. People search constantly; businesses change rarely. This shapes everything — we optimise reads and tolerate slightly stale writes.
  • High availability. "No restaurants near you" because the service is down is a lost order. Degrade, don't disappear.
  • Eventual consistency is fine. A restaurant appearing in search 30 seconds after it's added is acceptable. We are not moving money here.

Assumptions to state

Make your scope explicit so the interviewer can correct you early: a Southeast-Asia footprint (think one region, multi-city); roughly tens of millions of businesses; radius queries dominate; and entities are mostly static with occasional edits. Park the moving-driver case — it changes the storage strategy entirely, and conflating the two is the classic way to lose the thread.

Interview tip

The single most valuable clarifying question is "are the entities static or moving?" Static businesses can be indexed once and cached aggressively; moving drivers churn their location every few seconds and break any disk-based index. Naming this fork early shows you understand where the real difficulty lives.

02 Estimate

Back-of-the-envelope

Quick numbers anchor every later decision. Use round figures and say them aloud — the interviewer wants the reasoning, not the third decimal place.

Storage

Say 100 million businesses across the region. Each row is small: an ID, a name, (lat, lng), a geohash string, plus a handful of attributes — call it ~400 bytes. That's 100M × 400 B = 40 GB. Tiny by modern standards. The point of the estimate isn't that storage is hard (it isn't) — it's that 40 GB fits comfortably on a single replicated SQL box, which means our scaling problem is about query speed and read fan-out, not disk capacity.

Read throughput

Suppose 100 million daily active users, each doing ~5 nearby searches a day: 500M searches/day ≈ 5,800 QPS averaged. But traffic isn't flat — Grab's lunch and dinner peaks easily push 5–10× the average, so design for ~50,000 QPS at peak. That number is what forces caching and read replicas; a single database answering 50k geospatial lookups per second is not happening.

Write throughput

Businesses change slowly — new listings, edits, closures. Even a generous 1 million updates a day is ~12 writes/sec. The read:write ratio is roughly 4,000:1. This lopsidedness is the headline of the whole design: we can pre-compute, denormalise, and cache to our heart's content, because writes are cheap and rare.

What the numbers tell us

Data fits on one node; reads dwarf writes by thousands to one. Conclusion: this is a read-optimisation problem. The architecture will be "index once, cache hard, scale reads horizontally," not "shard a write firehose." Keep this in your pocket — every later trade-off resolves in favour of read speed.

03 Contract

API & data model

Pin down the interface before the internals. Two surfaces matter: the read API the app calls, and the write API an internal business-management tool calls.

The search endpoint

# Find businesses near a point, within a radius
GET /v1/search/nearby?lat=1.3048&lng=103.8318&radius=2000&category=food&limit=20

# 200 OK
{
  "total": 14,
  "results": [
    { "id": "biz_8841", "name": "Maxwell Food Centre",
      "lat": 1.2803, "lng": 103.8447, "distance_m": 412 },
    { "id": "biz_2207", "name": "Tiong Bahru Bakery",
      "lat": 1.2856, "lng": 103.8291, "distance_m": 1180 }
  ]
}

radius is in metres; distance_m is the true ground distance, already computed and used for sorting. We cap limit so a query in a dense area can't try to return ten thousand rows. Note what's not here: no pagination cursor crossing the spatial boundary, because spatial pagination is genuinely awkward — most apps re-query as the user pans the map instead.

The business management endpoints

POST   /v1/business            # create — server computes the geohash
PUT    /v1/business/{id}       # update (may change lat/lng → recompute geohash)
DELETE /v1/business/{id}       # remove
GET    /v1/business/{id}       # fetch one

The data model

Split the design into two tables. One holds the authoritative business record; the other is the spatial index — the structure we query by location. Keeping them separate lets the index be rebuilt or re-sharded without touching the source of truth.

business
  id            BIGINT  PRIMARY KEY
  name          TEXT
  lat           DOUBLE
  lng           DOUBLE
  category      TEXT
  address       TEXT
  updated_at    TIMESTAMP

geo_index
  geohash       VARCHAR(8)   -- e.g. "w21z3wc" (precision 6–7)
  business_id   BIGINT
  INDEX (geohash)             -- the prefix/range query rides this B-tree

The key design choice is in geo_index: we store a geohash string per business and put a B-tree index on it. Why a string and not the raw (lat, lng)? Because — as the next sections show — a geohash turns "find nearby points" into "find rows whose geohash starts with this prefix," and a B-tree answers prefix/range queries blazingly fast. The raw coordinates can't be indexed for 2D nearness with one ordinary B-tree; that's the whole problem we're about to unpack.

Going past the book

An interviewer may ask "what is geo_index actually stored in?" Strong answers: a B-tree index on a geohash column in PostgreSQL or MySQL; or skip the manual geohash and use PostGIS with a GiST index on a geometry column; or use Redis Geo commands (GEOADD / GEOSEARCH), which store points in a sorted set keyed by a 52-bit geohash interleave. Knowing more than one option, and when each fits, is the differentiator.

04 The core bottleneck

Why the naive scan fails

Start with the simplest thing that could possibly work, then watch it break — that's how you motivate the index. The naive query is a full scan with a distance filter:

SELECT id, name, lat, lng
FROM   business
WHERE  haversine(lat, lng, :userLat, :userLng) < :radius
ORDER BY distance;

The haversine formula computes the great-circle distance between two latitude/longitude points on a sphere. It's the correct way to measure ground distance, and we'll absolutely use it — but not like this. Here it runs once per row, so the database must touch all 100 million rows, do 100 million trig calculations, and only then discard the 99.99% that were nowhere near you. That's O(n) per query, and no index helps, because the filter is a function of two columns, not a value the B-tree can seek to.

What breaks, concretely

At our peak of 50,000 QPS, each query scanning 100M rows is 5 × 10¹² distance computations per second across the fleet. Even if one box could scan a few million rows per second, a single query takes tens of seconds — and they pile up, connection pools saturate, and the service falls over. The naive scan doesn't just get slow at scale; it stops responding entirely.

Why a regular index doesn't save you

"Just index lat and lng!" A B-tree on lat can quickly find everything in a latitude band — a horizontal strip across the whole planet. A separate B-tree on lng finds a vertical strip. Intersecting two strips still drags in a huge cross-shaped region far larger than your little circle, and the database can typically only use one of the two indexes efficiently for a range scan. The fundamental issue: a B-tree is one-dimensional, and nearness is two-dimensional. We need to map 2D location down to a single sortable dimension such that points close in space are close in that dimension. That mapping is exactly what a spatial index provides.

05 Spatial index · the workhorse

Geohash

The geohash is the elegant answer to "collapse 2D into 1D." It encodes a (lat, lng) pair into a short string (base-32) with a beautiful property: two locations that are physically close share a long common prefix. So "find nearby points" becomes "find points whose geohash starts with the same prefix" — a range scan a plain B-tree handles in milliseconds.

How it works under the hood

Geohashing is recursive bisection of the world. Start with longitude spanning [-180, 180] and latitude [-90, 90]. To encode a point:

  • Halve the longitude range. If the point is in the upper half, emit a 1 bit; lower half, a 0. Keep the half that contains the point and repeat.
  • Do the same for latitude, alternating: longitude bit, latitude bit, longitude bit, and so on. Each bit doubles the precision of one axis.
  • Group the interleaved bits into chunks of 5 and map each chunk to one base-32 character. A 6-character hash is 30 bits; a 7-character hash is 35 bits.

Because the bits encode "which half, then which quarter, then which eighth…", the prefix of the string corresponds to a containing rectangle (a "cell"). A 5-char hash is a roughly 5 km × 5 km cell; 6 chars is ~1.2 km × 0.6 km; 7 chars is ~150 m × 150 m. Drop characters off the end and you zoom out to a coarser cell; add characters and you zoom in. Sharing a prefix literally means "inside the same box."

Worked example — Tanjong Pagar

A café near Tanjong Pagar might encode to w21z3w at precision 6. A second café one street over encodes to w21z3x — same five-character prefix w21z3, differing only in the last character, because they sit in adjacent sub-cells of the same parent. A hawker stall out in Jurong, far away, encodes to something like w21yqf: the shared prefix collapses to just w21. The length of the agreement is the closeness. To find everything near the café, you query WHERE geohash LIKE 'w21z3%' — and the B-tree seeks straight to that contiguous block of rows.

The boundary problem — and why you need 9 cells

Here's the subtlety interviewers probe. The user might stand right at the edge of their geohash cell. A restaurant 50 m away, just across the cell border, has a different geohash prefix — so a single-cell query would miss it even though it's clearly "nearby." Worse, geohash has a quirk: two points can be physically adjacent yet share almost no prefix, because the bisection boundary fell between them (the antimeridian and equator edges are the extreme cases).

The fix is the standard recipe: query the user's cell plus all 8 surrounding neighbour cells — a 3×3 grid. You compute the eight neighbours arithmetically from the centre hash (libraries expose a neighbors() call), then issue one query covering all nine prefixes. This guarantees you can't miss a point near a border, because any point within the radius must fall in the centre cell or one touching it (provided you pick a cell size at least as large as the radius).

Choosing the precision to match the radius

The cell length must be at least the query radius, so that the 3×3 block fully contains the search circle. For a 5 km radius, pick precision 4–5 (cells ~5 km+); for a 500 m radius, precision 6; for "drivers within 200 m," precision 7. If you support several fixed radii, store one geohash but truncate it to the right length at query time — the prefix property means a precision-7 hash contains its precision-5 ancestor for free.

Under the hood — tools that speak geohash

Redis GEOADD/GEOSEARCH internally interleaves lat/lng into a 52-bit geohash and stores it as the score in a sorted set, so a radius query becomes a set of ZRANGEBYSCORE reads over the covering cells. Elasticsearch offers a geohash_grid aggregation. In plain PostgreSQL/MySQL you store the string and run LIKE 'prefix%' against a B-tree. All three exploit the same prefix-equals-proximity trick.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you handle the radius crossing a cell boundary?" The answer they want, crisply: query the centre cell and its 8 neighbours, with a cell size ≥ the radius, then filter by exact haversine distance. If you can also name the equator/antimeridian edge cases where adjacent points lose their shared prefix, you're clearly past surface level.

06 Spatial index · the alternative

Quadtree, and a note on S2 & H3

Geohash splits the world into a fixed grid: every cell at a given precision is the same size, whether it covers empty sea or the dense CBD. That's its weakness. A quadtree is the density-adaptive alternative.

How a quadtree works

A quadtree is a tree where each node represents a square region of the map and has exactly four children — the four quadrants (NW, NE, SW, SE). The rule: a node only subdivides when it holds more than some threshold of points (say 100). So empty regions stay as a single shallow node, while a packed area like Orchard Road keeps splitting into finer and finer quadrants until each leaf holds a manageable handful. The tree is built in memory by inserting points one at a time, splitting any leaf that overflows.

To query, you descend from the root, following only the quadrants that intersect your search circle, and collect the points in the leaves you reach. Because dense areas are deeper and sparse areas are shallow, you naturally examine roughly the same small number of candidate points regardless of where the query lands. That's the headline advantage: the quadtree adapts to density, so a query in the CBD doesn't drown in points while a query in Pulau Ubin descends a near-empty tree.

Geohash vs quadtree

Geohash

  • Dead simple: just a string column + B-tree, fits any SQL/NoSQL store
  • Stateless — encode on the fly, no tree to hold in memory or rebuild
  • Trivially shardable and cacheable by prefix
  • Fixed grid — a dense cell can return huge candidate sets; uneven query cost
  • Boundary handling needs the 3×3 neighbour trick

Quadtree

  • Adapts to density → even, predictable query cost everywhere
  • Naturally supports "k nearest" and variable radii
  • Usually lives in memory → must be built on startup and rebuilt/updated carefully
  • Harder to distribute; a moving point can force expensive node splits/merges
  • More code and more operational care than a geohash column

S2 and H3 — the production-grade options

Two libraries you should name. Google S2 projects the sphere onto a cube and uses a Hilbert curve to number cells; the Hilbert curve preserves locality even better than geohash's Z-order, and it has no nasty antimeridian discontinuity. Uber H3 tiles the world in hexagons; hexagons have the pleasant property that all six neighbours are equidistant from the centre — unlike a square's mix of edge and diagonal neighbours — which makes radius queries and "ring" expansions cleaner. Uber uses H3 heavily for exactly this kind of geospatial work. Mentioning that you'd reach for S2 or H3 in production, rather than hand-rolling geohash math, signals real-world awareness.

Interviewer follow-up

"Geohash or quadtree — which and why?" The crisp answer: geohash for a static, read-heavy business directory because it's simple, cacheable, and trivially shardable; a quadtree (or H3) when density is extreme or you need adaptive cell sizes and k-nearest. Then add: "in production I'd use S2 or H3 rather than reimplement either." That sequence — pick, justify, then upgrade — is exactly what they're listening for.

07 Assemble it

High-level architecture

Now wire the pieces into a system, keeping the read:write asymmetry front of mind. Two distinct paths run through it.

The read path: client → load balancer → a stateless Search service that does the geohash math and candidate fetch, sitting behind a Redis cache, falling through to the geo-index database (with read replicas). The write path: an internal tool → Business service → writes to the primary database and recomputes the geohash. Splitting read and write services lets each scale independently — and reads need far more instances than writes.

Why a cache, and what's the key

Because reads outnumber writes ~4,000:1, most searches in a given area are repeated by many users. Cache the result of a covering cell: key the cache by geohash prefix (and category), value = the list of businesses in that cell. A Redis cache fronting the database absorbs the bulk of the 50k QPS. Since businesses rarely change, a TTL of a few minutes is fine, and a write can proactively invalidate the affected cell's key.

The Search service is deliberately stateless — it holds no per-user data, so the load balancer can spray requests across any instance and you scale out by adding boxes. State lives in Redis and the database, not in the app tier.

08 The read path

End-to-end: answering "what's near me?"

This is the sequence to be able to recite. It's also where the geohash and haversine pieces finally click together. Watch it play through, then redraw it from memory.

Five stages: (1) take the user's (lat, lng);
(2) encode it to a geohash at the precision matching the radius, and compute its 8 neighbour cells;
(3) fetch all candidate entities sitting in those 9 cells — from the cache, or the index on a miss;
(4) filter the candidates by true haversine distance, dropping any that are inside the 3×3 box but outside the actual circle;
(5) sort by distance and return the top N.

The crucial insight is the division of labour. The geohash does the cheap coarse filter — it slashes 100 million points down to maybe a few hundred candidates using a fast prefix lookup. Haversine does the exact fine filter — it runs only on those few hundred, turning "in the square" into "in the circle," and producing the precise distances we sort on. You get the best of both: index speed and geometric correctness.

Why filter with haversine at all?

The 3×3 geohash block is a square region; the user asked for a circle. The corners of the square stick out beyond the radius, so without the haversine pass you'd return points up to ~40% farther than requested. Running haversine only on the small candidate set keeps results geometrically honest and gives you the exact distance_m the API promised — at negligible cost, since you're computing it a few hundred times, not a hundred million.

Interviewer follow-up

"Walk me through one query." The five-step recital above is the answer. The line that lands the point: "the spatial index is a coarse pre-filter to get candidates cheaply; haversine is the exact post-filter to get correctness." Say that and the read path is locked.

09 Scale & the hot cell

Scaling reads and taming hot cells

The system works; now make it survive peak load and density skew. Three pressures show up.

1 — Read fan-out: cache and replicas

We've covered the cache, but the layering matters. A search first hits Redis keyed by covering cell; on a miss it reads a database read replica; the primary handles only writes. Because the data set is just ~40 GB, you don't shard for capacity — you replicate for read throughput, adding replicas until 50k QPS is comfortable. This is the payoff of the estimation step: a capacity problem would force sharding, but a throughput problem is solved with replicas and cache.

2 — Sharding by region

If you do outgrow a single replicated dataset (multi-country expansion), shard by geographic region — partition on a coarse geohash prefix so each shard owns a contiguous chunk of the map. A query in Singapore touches only the Singapore shard. This keeps a single radius query on one shard most of the time, since the 3×3 cell block is geographically tiny. The edge case: a query right on a shard boundary may need to read two shards — handle it the same way as the cell-boundary case, by fanning out to both.

3 — The hot cell — dense city centres

This is the real bottleneck for this system, and the one interviewers love. Geohash uses a fixed grid, so the cell covering Orchard Road at lunchtime might contain tens of thousands of businesses (and, in the moving-driver variant, thousands of drivers), while a cell over the Causeway holds a handful. That hot cell causes three problems: huge candidate sets to haversine-filter, a fat cache value that's expensive to store and invalidate, and a single Redis key that becomes a traffic magnet — a hot key hammering one shard.

Mitigations, roughly in order of reach:

  • Use a finer precision in dense areas. A smaller cell holds fewer points. Either globally pick a precision suited to your densest city, or use a variable scheme — which is precisely what a quadtree or H3 gives you for free, since they subdivide where it's crowded.
  • Cap and rank. No user scrolls 8,000 restaurants. Return the closest N (and the freshest/highest-rated), bounding both the candidate work and the payload.
  • Replicate the hot key. Spread a popular cell's cache entry across several Redis nodes (key suffixing / client-side fan-out) so the read load isn't pinned to one box.
  • Add local in-process caching. A short-lived LRU on each Search instance absorbs repeated identical hot-cell queries before they even reach Redis.
What breaks at 10×

At 10× traffic (~500k QPS) and 10× data, the flat dataset no longer comfortably fits one replicated box and the hot cell turns molten. The fixed-grid geohash starts to hurt: you'd migrate the index to H3 or S2 for adaptive cells, shard by region, and lean far harder on multi-tier caching (in-process LRU → Redis → replica). The read path logic is unchanged — coarse spatial filter, then haversine — but every layer gets replicated and the cell sizing becomes density-aware.

Interviewer follow-up

"What about a dense city centre?" Lead with the diagnosis — "a fixed geohash grid makes the CBD cell a hot key with an enormous candidate set" — then offer the menu: finer precision or an adaptive index (quadtree/H3), cap-and-rank, hot-key replication, and a local LRU. Diagnosing the hot cell unprompted is what separates a strong answer from a memorised one.

10 Updates & failure

Updates, failure modes, and the moving-driver bridge

Static businesses are easy

An update is rare and simple: write the new record to the primary, recompute the geohash if the location changed, update geo_index, and invalidate the affected cell's cache key. At ~12 writes/sec this is trivial. Replication to read replicas is asynchronous, so a brand-new restaurant may take a few seconds to appear in search — which we already declared acceptable. No part of the write path is on the user's latency-critical read path.

Moving drivers are a different problem (bridge to L33)

If the entity is a Grab driver pinging location every 4 seconds, the picture inverts. Now writes are a firehose, not a trickle: hundreds of thousands of location updates per second. A disk-based geohash index can't absorb that write rate, and every move potentially changes the driver's cell. The standard answer flips the storage: keep driver locations in an in-memory store (Redis), accept some staleness, and don't durably persist every ping. The matching service (rider ↔ driver) is a separate concern layered on top. That's a whole lesson of its own — but the proximity query mechanism you learned here (cell + neighbours + haversine) is reused unchanged; only the storage and update strategy differ.

Failure modes

  • Cache (Redis) down. Searches fall through to the read replicas. Latency rises, but results stay correct — degrade, don't fail. Guard the replicas with a request-coalescing / circuit-breaker layer so a cache outage doesn't instantly stampede them.
  • A read replica dies. The load balancer drops it from rotation and routes to healthy replicas; you provision enough headroom that losing one doesn't tip the rest over. Reads are unaffected beyond a brief blip.
  • The primary dies. Reads keep working off replicas (this is why splitting read/write paths is so valuable). Writes pause until a replica is promoted — acceptable, since a few minutes without new business listings hurts nobody.
  • Stale index. If a geohash recompute is missed on a location edit, a business shows in the wrong cell. A periodic background job that rebuilds geo_index from the authoritative business table is your safety net — cheap insurance given how small the data is.
Interviewer follow-up

"How would this change for live drivers?" Don't redesign the query — reuse it. Say: "the cell-plus-neighbour-plus-haversine lookup is the same; what changes is storage — move to an in-memory Redis index, tolerate staleness, and stop persisting every ping, because the write rate explodes." Recognising that the read algorithm is stable while the write strategy flips is the senior-level observation.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: turn a 2D range query into a 1D prefix or tree lookup with a spatial index to find candidates cheaply, then refine with exact haversine distance — and since reads dwarf writes, cache hard and replicate. If you can sketch the read path and explain why each layer exists, you can carry this interview.
