41Lesson · System Design Interview

Design a Distributed Email Service

An email service has to do four hard things at once: speak SMTP to send and receive across the open internet, store mailboxes that grow without bound, keep spam and malware out without dropping real mail, and let a user search years of messages in milliseconds. It's the broadest systems drill there is — a tour through object storage, queues, full-text search, and the dark art of staying deliverable.

About these notes: original study material on building a large-scale email system — a standard, widely-documented engineering topic. The explanations, numbers, schemas, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. One diagram is interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch an incoming message flow through filtering, storage, and indexing.
00 The problem

Why email is hard

Email looks deceptively simple — a textbox, a Send button, an inbox. Under the hood it is one of the oldest and least forgiving distributed systems still in daily use. The reason it makes a great interview is that it forces you to combine almost every building block you have studied: a protocol you do not control (SMTP, the Simple Mail Transfer Protocol), petabyte-scale storage, asynchronous queues with retries, a full-text search engine, and a real-time adversary (spammers) trying to abuse the whole thing.

Three properties make email uniquely painful compared to a chat app or a feed:

  • You federate with strangers. When SeaMoney sends a receipt to a customer on Gmail, your servers talk directly to Google's servers over SMTP. You don't control the other side, can't retry forever, and your sender reputation with them decides whether your mail lands in the inbox or the spam folder.
  • Storage only grows. Users delete almost nothing. A ten-year-old Shopee account might hold 80,000 messages and several gigabytes of attachments. Mailboxes are append-heavy and read-skewed toward recent mail, but the long tail must stay cheap.
  • Delivery must be durable, not fast. Losing a payment confirmation email is unacceptable, but taking thirty seconds to deliver it is fine. That inverts the usual latency-first instinct and pushes you toward queues and idempotent retries.

So the lesson is bottleneck-driven: we start with a single send and a single receive, then watch each naive choice break under volume, adversaries, and storage cost — and add the component that fixes it.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Before drawing a box, narrow the problem. "Design email" is enormous — calendars, contacts, threading, encryption, and labels could each be a lesson. State out loud what you will and won't build.

Functional requirements

  • Send a message to one or more recipients, including external domains, over SMTP — with attachments.
  • Receive mail from external senders over SMTP and deliver it into the right mailbox.
  • Store mailboxes durably and let users organize mail into folders/labels with read/unread and starred flags.
  • Search a mailbox by sender, subject, body text, and date, returning results in well under a second.
  • Filter spam and malware on the inbound path, and stay deliverable on the outbound path.
  • Sync the same mailbox across a web client and a phone, keeping flags consistent.

Out of scope (say so explicitly)

  • End-to-end encryption, calendaring, contacts, and rich threading UI. Mention them as extensions, then set them aside.

Non-functional requirements

  • Durability above all. Once we accept a message, we must never lose it. Target eleven nines of storage durability (the S3-class bar).
  • Availability over latency. The send pipeline is asynchronous; a few seconds of delivery latency is acceptable, an outage that loses mail is not.
  • Read-your-own-writes for the owner. When I send mail, it must appear in my Sent folder immediately and consistently; cross-device flag updates can be slightly eventual.
  • Scalable, cheap storage. The bulk of bytes are old, rarely-read attachments. Cost per stored gigabyte dominates the bill.
Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole design: "Email is a durability-and-deliverability problem far more than a latency problem — so I'll lean on queues, object storage, and reputation management rather than chasing milliseconds." Say that early and the rest of the interview follows naturally.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope

Numbers turn vague hand-waving into concrete design pressure. Assume a regional provider — think the email backbone behind a Singapore super-app's transactional and consumer mail — with 100 million users, half active daily.

Traffic (QPS)

  • Suppose each active user sends ~10 emails/day and receives ~40 (most mail is received: notifications, receipts, newsletters). With 50M active users that's 50M × 50 = 2.5 billion messages/day.
  • 2.5B / 86,400s ≈ ~29,000 messages/sec average. Mail is peaky — business hours in one timezone push the peak to maybe 5× average ≈ 145,000 msg/sec.
  • Reads dominate interaction: opening the inbox, paging, searching. Call it 10× the message rate, so design the read path for ~300k+ QPS.

Storage

  • Average message with headers and a small body: ~50 KB. Attachments are the killer — assume 20% of mail carries an attachment averaging 1 MB, so blended size ≈ 50 KB + 0.2 × 1 MB ≈ ~250 KB/message.
  • Daily ingest: 2.5B × 250 KB ≈ ~625 TB/day of new mail. Over a year that's ~228 PB before dedup/compression.
  • This is why the body and attachments cannot sit in a relational database — they go to object storage, and we deduplicate identical attachments (the same 5 MB Shopee promo image sent to a million inboxes is stored once).

Metadata

  • Headers + flags + folder + index pointers ≈ ~1 KB/message. 2.5B/day × 1 KB ≈ ~2.5 TB/day of metadata — small enough for a partitioned, indexed store but far too hot for a single node.
What the numbers tell us

Three orders of magnitude separate blob bytes (hundreds of PB) from metadata bytes (single-digit PB). That gap is the design's load-bearing insight: split metadata from blobs so each lands on the storage tier that fits its access pattern and cost. Everything in step 07 follows from this one ratio.

03 Contract

API & data model

Two interface surfaces matter. First, the internal/client API our own web and mobile apps call (HTTPS/JSON). Second, the SMTP wire protocol we must speak to the rest of the world — that one we don't get to design, only implement.

Client-facing REST API

# Submit a message for delivery (returns immediately; sending is async)
POST /v1/messages
  { "from": "shop@seamoney.com", "to": ["buyer@gmail.com"],
    "subject": "Your receipt", "body_html": "...",
    "attachments": ["blob://att/9f2c..."] }
  → 202 Accepted { "message_id": "msg_01HXY...", "status": "queued" }

# List a folder, newest first, cursor-paginated
GET  /v1/folders/INBOX/messages?limit=50&cursor=...
  → 200 { "messages": [ {meta}, ... ], "next_cursor": "..." }

# Full-text + structured search
GET  /v1/search?q=receipt+from:shopee&after=2026-01-01
  → 200 { "hits": [ {message_id, snippet, score}, ... ] }

# Mutate a flag (read/unread/star) — idempotent
PATCH /v1/messages/{id}  { "flags": { "seen": true } }
  → 200 { "message_id": "...", "flags": {...}, "mod_seq": 4821 }

Note the 202 Accepted on send: the API does not wait for SMTP delivery, it just durably enqueues the message and returns. Note also mod_seq on the flag update — a monotonically increasing per-mailbox sequence number that step 09 uses to sync devices.

Data model: metadata vs blob

The schema encodes the split from step 02. Message metadata lives in a partitioned wide-column / NoSQL store (Cassandra-style), partitioned by user_id so one user's whole mailbox is co-located. The body and attachments live in object storage, addressed by content hash.

-- Metadata row (partitioned by user_id, clustered by folder + received_at)
mailbox_message {
  user_id         PARTITION KEY   // shard / locality key
  folder_id       CLUSTER KEY
  received_at     CLUSTER KEY DESC // newest first
  message_id      uuid
  thread_id       uuid
  from, to, subject, snippet   text
  flags           map<string,bool> // seen, starred, answered
  size_bytes      int
  blob_ref        text          // pointer into object store
  mod_seq         bigint        // per-mailbox change counter
}

-- Blob, content-addressed & deduplicated in object storage
S3 key:  /blobs/<sha256-of-content>
value:   raw MIME (headers + body + base64 attachments)
refcount table tracks how many mailboxes point at each blob

Storing the blob under its own SHA-256 means two identical attachments collapse to one object automatically — the dedup win we sized in step 02. The metadata row is tiny and indexable; the heavy bytes never touch the hot path.

Interview follow-up

"Why not keep everything in Postgres?" Because the blob bytes are 100× the metadata and have a totally different access pattern — write-once, read-rarely, never queried by content. A relational row store would make every mailbox scan drag megabytes off disk. Splitting lets metadata stay in a fast, indexable store and blobs sit on the cheapest durable tier. Name the trade now; you'll defend it in step 07.

04 The whole board

High-level architecture

Step back and draw the full system before diving in. There are two largely independent data planes — send (outbound) and receive (inbound) — that meet only at the shared mailbox store and search index. Decoupling them with queues is the single most important structural decision: it lets each side absorb spikes, retry independently, and scale on its own.

Read the diagram as two pipelines that share a tail. Send: client → API → durable send queue → outbound MTA → the internet. Receive: the internet → inbound MTA → spam/virus filter → delivery worker → mailbox store. Both delivery and submission write metadata to the store, push heavy bytes to the object store, and emit a copy to the search indexer.

The components in green are stateless app servers you scale horizontally; blue is the partitioned metadata store; amber is object storage; teal is the search cluster; violet is the queue. Each gets its own deep-dive below.

05 Outbound

The send path: submission, MTA, queue & retry

Sending is where most candidates underthink the design. The naive version — "the API opens an SMTP connection to Gmail and waits" — collapses the moment the recipient server is slow, busy, or temporarily refusing. You must decouple submission from delivery.

Submission vs transfer

Message submission is the act of a client handing us a message to send (authenticated, over our API or submission port 587). Mail transfer is our server relaying that message to the recipient's server over SMTP (port 25). The MTA (Mail Transfer Agent) is the daemon that performs transfer — real-world implementations are Postfix, Exim, or Haraka. Crucially, submission returns to the client immediately after we durably persist the message; transfer happens asynchronously behind a queue.

How transfer actually works, step by step

When the outbound MTA pulls a queued message for buyer@gmail.com:

  • It extracts the domain gmail.com and does a DNS MX lookup to find the recipient's mail exchanger hostnames, sorted by priority.
  • It opens a TCP connection to the highest-priority MX, performs the SMTP handshake (EHLO, optional STARTTLS to encrypt), then MAIL FROM, RCPT TO, DATA.
  • The remote server answers with a status code. 2xx = accepted (we mark delivered). 4xx = temporary failure ("try later" — greylisting, full mailbox, rate-limited). 5xx = permanent failure (no such user) → we generate a bounce back to the sender.

Why the queue + retry is non-negotiable

A 4xx is normal, not exceptional — many receiving servers greylist first-time senders, deliberately returning a temporary failure to see if a legitimate server retries (spammers usually don't). So the outbound MTA must re-enqueue with exponential backoff: retry after 1 min, 5 min, 30 min, a few hours, then give up after a deadline (typically a few days) and bounce. The send queue (Kafka, or SQS/RabbitMQ) is what makes this durable across MTA restarts.

Sender reputation & rate control

The outbound side has a business-critical concern most candidates miss: deliverability. Receiving providers throttle or block senders they don't trust. To stay in the inbox you must: warm up sending IPs gradually, keep complaint and bounce rates low, sign every message (DKIM, covered in step 06), and separate traffic streams — a SeaMoney one-time-password email should not share a sending IP pool with a Shopee marketing blast, because a high complaint rate on marketing would poison delivery of the critical OTP.

Synchronous send (naive)

  • Simple to reason about — call SMTP, get a result
  • Client blocks on every slow recipient
  • A single greylisting recipient stalls the request
  • No durability: an MTA crash mid-send loses the mail

Queue + async MTA (chosen)

  • Submission is fast and durable; delivery is best-effort with retries
  • Absorbs spikes; per-recipient backoff doesn't block others
  • Survives MTA restarts — the queue is the source of truth
  • More moving parts; needs dedup to avoid double-send on retry
Interview follow-up

"What happens if the MTA crashes after sending to the remote server but before recording success?" On restart it retries from the queue and may double-send. You bound this with an idempotency key (message-id + recipient) and accept that SMTP gives at-least-once, not exactly-once, delivery — duplicates are rare and far better than lost mail. Be ready to say "email is fundamentally at-least-once."

06 Inbound & defense

The receive path & the spam pipeline

Inbound is where the adversary lives. The inbound MTA listens on port 25 for connections from the world, but accepting raw SMTP without defense means a flood of spam, phishing, and malware lands in mailboxes. So between "accepted at the door" and "delivered to the mailbox" sits a filtering pipeline — and getting it right is the heart of an email interview.

Authentication: SPF, DKIM, DMARC

Before scoring content, verify the sender is who they claim to be. Three DNS-backed standards stack up:

  • SPF (Sender Policy Framework): the sending domain publishes, in DNS, which IPs are allowed to send for it. We check whether the connecting IP is on that list.
  • DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail): the sender signs the message with a private key; the public key is in DNS. A valid signature proves the body wasn't tampered with and really came from that domain.
  • DMARC: a policy tying SPF and DKIM to the visible From: domain, telling receivers what to do on failure (none / quarantine / reject) and where to send reports. DMARC is what stops someone spoofing From: support@grabpay.com.

Greylisting: a cheap, brutal first filter

Greylisting temporarily rejects (4xx) the first delivery attempt from an unknown sender triple (IP, from, to). Legitimate MTAs retry minutes later and pass; most spam cannons fire once and never come back. It costs you a small delay on first contact in exchange for cutting a large fraction of junk before you even score it.

Scoring: rules + ML

Messages that pass authentication go to the scorer, which blends two approaches. Rule-based checks (think SpamAssassin-style) award points for known-bad patterns: suspicious URLs, mismatched headers, blocklisted IPs. A machine-learning classifier (a model trained on labeled spam/ham, scoring features like token distributions, sender history, and embedded-link reputation) produces a probability. Above a high threshold → reject or drop; medium → spam folder; low → inbox. A separate antivirus scanner detonates or signature-checks attachments.

Under the hood: false positives are the real cost

Spam filtering is an asymmetric problem. Letting one spam through annoys a user; blocking one legitimate email (a false positive) can mean a missed payment, a lost booking confirmation, a furious customer. So thresholds skew conservative, borderline mail goes to a recoverable spam folder rather than being dropped, and you keep a feedback loop: when users mark "not spam" / "spam," those labels retrain the model. Quarantine, never silently discard.

Interview follow-up

"How do you stay deliverable AND keep spam out?" — the two directions are mirror images. Inbound, you verify SPF/DKIM/DMARC and score. Outbound, you publish your own SPF/DKIM/DMARC records, sign mail, warm IPs, and watch complaint rates so other providers trust you. The same three acronyms appear on both sides, which is the clean way to tie the answer together.

07 Storage at scale

Storing huge mailboxes cheaply

This is the interviewer's favorite probe — "how do you store huge mailboxes cheaply?" — and step 02 already handed us the answer: split metadata from blobs, then partition by user. Let's make it concrete.

Metadata in a partitioned store

Mailbox metadata — headers, subject, snippet, folder membership, flags, the pointer to the blob — is small, frequently read, and queried by user and date. It belongs in a horizontally partitioned store: Cassandra or DynamoDB keyed by user_id as the partition key, clustered by folder_id then received_at DESC. The huge win: every operation a user performs — list inbox, page back, fetch flags — hits a single partition, so it's a fast, local read with no scatter-gather. We co-locate a user's entire mailbox on one shard.

Blobs in object storage, deduplicated

The body and attachments — the hundreds of PB from step 02 — go to object storage (Amazon S3, GCS, or an internal blob store), addressed by content hash. Object storage gives eleven-nines durability, tiered pricing, and effectively infinite scale, at a fraction of database cost per byte. Because the key is the content's SHA-256, identical attachments deduplicate for free: the same Shopee promotional PDF mailed to a million users is stored once, with a refcount of a million; each mailbox metadata row just points at the same blob.

Tiering hot vs cold

Access is heavily skewed to recent mail. So tier the blobs: recent attachments on standard (fast) storage, anything older than ~90 days transitioned automatically to a cold tier (S3 Glacier / Infrequent Access) via a lifecycle policy. A user opening a five-year-old attachment tolerates a few seconds of retrieval latency; you pay a fraction of the storage cost for the 95% of bytes that are rarely touched.

One big relational DB

  • Simple: one query reaches everything
  • Blobs bloat the table; every scan drags MB off disk
  • Vertical scaling hits a wall at this byte volume
  • Expensive per-GB; no automatic tiering or dedup

Metadata store + object store (chosen)

  • Metadata reads are tiny, indexed, single-partition
  • Blobs sit on the cheapest durable tier and dedup for free
  • Each tier scales independently to its own pattern
  • Two systems to keep consistent (write blob first, then row)

Failure mode: a metadata shard dies

If the node owning a user's partition fails, the affected users can't read their mailbox until failover. The fix is the standard one: replication (Cassandra/DynamoDB keep N replicas across availability zones), so a replica is promoted and reads continue with a brief blip. Because blobs are content-addressed and immutable, the object store's own cross-AZ replication protects them independently; a lost metadata row can even be rebuilt from the raw MIME blob if needed. The two stores fail independently, which is exactly what we wanted.

Interview follow-up

"Write the blob or the metadata row first?" Always blob first, metadata second. If you crash between them, you have an orphan blob (harmless, a background GC reclaims unreferenced objects) rather than a metadata row pointing at a blob that doesn't exist (a broken mailbox). Ordering writes so the dangling artifact is the harmless one is a reusable distributed-systems instinct worth naming.

08 Search

Searching mail fast: the inverted index

The next probe: "how do you search mail fast?" A user types receipt from:shopee and expects results across 80,000 messages in under a second. You cannot scan the mailbox — at that size a linear scan is hopeless. You need an inverted index.

What an inverted index is and how it works

An inverted index maps each term (a normalized word) to the list of documents containing it — the inverse of "document → its words." Building one is a pipeline: tokenize the subject and body into words, normalize them (lowercase, strip punctuation, sometimes stem so "receipts" matches "receipt"), then for each term append the message's id to that term's postings list. A query for receipt invoice looks up both postings lists and intersects them — an operation that's near-instant because the lists are pre-built and sorted.

// inverted index (conceptual)
"receipt"  → [ msg_8821, msg_9043, msg_9911, ... ]
"shopee"   → [ msg_8821, msg_9911, msg_10240, ... ]
"refund"   → [ msg_7702, msg_9911, ... ]

// query: receipt AND from:shopee  → intersect postings
intersect("receipt", "shopee") = [ msg_8821, msg_9911 ]

Structured fields like from:, has:attachment, and date ranges become filters applied alongside the text terms, and results are ranked (recency-weighted relevance) before returning the top hits with snippets.

Per-user partitioning is the key trick

A global index over everyone's mail would be enormous and would leak one user's terms into another's search. Instead, partition the index by user: each user has their own logical index (or shares a shard keyed by user_id). A search only ever touches one user's postings, which keeps both the index small per-shard and the query fast and private. This mirrors the metadata partitioning exactly — the same locality key, user_id, governs both.

Tools and the async build

In practice the search layer is Elasticsearch / OpenSearch or Apache Lucene-based. Indexing happens asynchronously: when delivery writes a message, it emits an event (often onto the same Kafka backbone) and an indexer consumes it. This decouples write latency from index latency — mail appears in the inbox instantly even if it becomes searchable a second or two later. That's an acceptable trade: eventual consistency for search, strong consistency for the inbox listing.

Interview follow-up

"What if the indexer falls behind or a shard's index is corrupted?" Because the index is derived data, it's always rebuildable from the durable mailbox store — you replay the delivery events for that user/shard to reconstruct postings. Stating "search is a derived, rebuildable view, so I optimize it for query speed and treat the mailbox store as the source of truth" is the senior-level framing.

09 Access & sync

Sync, IMAP & flag consistency

Users read mail on a phone and a laptop at once. If they read a message on the phone, the laptop should show it as read too. This is a state synchronization problem layered on top of storage, and it's subtle because the flag updates fly in from multiple devices concurrently.

The access protocols

Classic clients speak IMAP (Internet Message Access Protocol), which keeps mail server-side and syncs folder state — the right model for multi-device. (Its ancestor POP3 downloads-and-deletes, a single-device model we'd avoid.) Modern apps mostly use our own HTTPS API plus a push channel — a long-lived connection or websocket, falling back to mobile push notifications — so a newly delivered or newly-read message propagates to other devices in near real time instead of waiting for a poll.

How flag consistency works: a per-mailbox change counter

The mechanism is a monotonically increasing modification sequence (the mod_seq from step 03), one per mailbox. Every change — new mail, a flag flip, a move — bumps mod_seq and stamps the affected row with the new value. A device remembers the highest mod_seq it has seen and, on reconnect, asks "send me everything changed since mod_seq = N." The server returns only the delta. This is exactly how IMAP's CONDSTORE/QRESYNC extensions work, and it makes sync an O(changes) operation rather than re-downloading the folder.

# device knows it last synced at mod_seq 4810
GET /v1/folders/INBOX/changes?since_mod_seq=4810
  → { "changes": [
        {message_id: m1, flags:{seen:true},  mod_seq:4811},
        {message_id: m9, "new":true,          mod_seq:4820}
      ], "highest_mod_seq": 4820 }

Concurrency: two devices, one flag

What if the phone marks a message read at the same instant the laptop stars it? Each update targets a different flag, so they merge cleanly. The harder case — both toggling the same flag — is resolved last-writer-wins by mod_seq order, which for boolean read/unread is benign. Flags are intentionally modeled as a small map of independent booleans precisely so concurrent edits commute rather than conflict.

Under the hood: why eventual consistency is fine here

The inbox listing needs read-your-writes for the owner (you must see mail you just received, and the Sent copy of mail you just sent). But a flag flip propagating to your other device a second late is invisible in practice. So we spend strong consistency where it's felt and accept eventual consistency on cross-device flag fan-out — a deliberate, defensible split rather than a uniform guarantee.

Interview follow-up

"How does a device efficiently resync after being offline for a week?" Answer with the change counter: it sends its last mod_seq, gets only the delta, and never re-downloads unchanged mail. If you also support offline composition, mention that queued sends carry a client-generated idempotency key so a flaky reconnect doesn't double-send — tying back to the at-least-once theme from step 05.

10 Wrap-up

Scale, failure modes & what breaks at 10x

Pull the threads together. The system survives scale because every plane is independently horizontally scalable and decoupled by durable queues. Walk an interviewer through where it bends at 10× — say, a Black-Friday-scale Shopee campaign hammering both planes.

The send plane at 10x

A marketing blast spikes outbound volume. The send queue absorbs it (that's its job), and outbound MTAs scale out as queue consumers. The real limit is downstream throttling: Gmail and others rate-limit per sending IP, returning 4xx if you push too fast. So you add more sending IPs, spread load across them, and crucially isolate streams — keep the OTP/transactional pool separate from the marketing pool so a complaint surge on marketing can't sink delivery of critical mail. The failure mode to name: a poorly-warmed IP pool gets the whole campaign deferred.

The receive plane at 10x

An inbound flood (a spam wave, or just heavy legitimate traffic) is shaped by the front door. The inbound MTA applies connection rate limits and greylisting at the edge; accepted mail lands on an ingest queue so the spam-scoring and delivery workers consume at their own pace. If scoring falls behind, mail queues rather than drops. The failure mode: if the spam model node dies, you fail safe by quarantining unscored mail (delay it) rather than letting unfiltered junk through or, worse, dropping good mail.

Storage and hotspots at 10x

Object storage scales effectively without bound, and dedup plus cold-tiering keep the bill sane. The metadata store's danger is a hot partition — a single mega-mailbox (a shared support@ address receiving millions of messages) overwhelming one shard. The fix is to detect such accounts and shard them further (e.g. by folder or time bucket), or route shared inboxes through a different model entirely. Always name hot-partitioning as the NoSQL failure mode; it's the question behind the question.

Search at 10x

Index volume grows linearly; you add search shards and rebalance by user_id. Because the index is derived, a hot shard can be split and rebuilt from the mailbox store without data loss. Stale or lagging indexes degrade gracefully — search is a second behind, the inbox is not.

The three questions to have crisp answers for

Every email interview circles back to the same trio:
(1) store huge mailboxes cheaply → split metadata from blobs, object storage with dedup and cold-tiering;
(2) search fast → per-user inverted index, built async, rebuildable from the store;
(3) keep spam out and stay deliverable → SPF/DKIM/DMARC both directions, greylisting + rules + ML scoring inbound, IP warm-up and stream isolation outbound.
Land those three and you've covered 80% of the rubric.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole design compresses to one sentence: treat email as a durability-and-deliverability problem — decouple send and receive with durable queues, split tiny metadata from cheap dedup'd blobs, build a per-user inverted index off to the side, and defend both directions with SPF/DKIM/DMARC. If you can whiteboard the two planes and name the bottleneck each component solves, you can carry the interview.
