28Lesson · System Design Interview

Design Video Streaming (YouTube)

A video platform must swallow enormous uploads, grind each one into a fan-out of resolutions and bitrates, and then push it to phones, laptops, and TVs on every kind of network without the dreaded spinning buffer wheel. The twist that shapes the whole design: bandwidth — not compute — is where the money goes. The interesting engineering lives in the upload-process-serve pipeline and in adaptive bitrate delivery.

About these notes: original study material on designing a large-scale video streaming service, a standard and widely-documented system-design topic. The explanations, numbers, examples, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Three diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the pipeline flow.
00 The problem

Why video is its own beast

Most "design a system" questions are dominated by request count: how many writes per second, how many reads, how do you shard the keyspace. Video flips that intuition. A single one-hour 1080p upload is on the order of a few gigabytes of source data; serving it to a million viewers at once is hundreds of gigabits per second of egress — the bytes leaving your network toward users. Compared to that, the database queries that look up a title or a view count are a rounding error.

So the design pressure comes from three places that ordinary CRUD systems never feel:

  • Ingest is heavy. Uploads are huge, slow, and flaky. A creator on hotel Wi-Fi uploading a 2 GB clip cannot afford to restart from byte zero when the connection drops at 90%.
  • Processing is expensive and parallel. One source video must become many renditions — different resolution/bitrate combinations — and often multiple codecs. That is a CPU-and-GPU-bound fan-out, not a quick write.
  • Delivery is bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. Once a popular video exists, the cost is almost entirely the bytes you ship. This is why a CDN (content delivery network) is not optional decoration — it is the load-bearing wall of the whole design.

Think of TikTok in Singapore: a creator films a 60-second clip, uploads it over mobile, and within minutes it can be playing on millions of devices across Southeast Asia at whatever quality each phone's network can sustain. Every architectural choice below exists to make that loop fast, cheap, and resilient.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Before drawing boxes, narrow what we are building. "YouTube" can mean a hundred features; the interviewer wants to see you carve out a coherent core and state assumptions out loud.

Functional requirements

  • Upload a video from any device, reliably, even on a weak connection.
  • Process each upload into multiple qualities so it plays smoothly on a 4K TV or a 3G phone.
  • Stream a video on demand with minimal startup delay and minimal mid-playback buffering (the player stalling while it waits for data).
  • Browse and search by title, fetch metadata (owner, description, thumbnail), and show a view count.

Out of scope (say so explicitly)

  • Live streaming — real-time ingest changes the pipeline materially; we focus on video-on-demand (VOD).
  • Comments, the recommendation feed, monetization, and DRM — acknowledge them, then set them aside to protect time.

Non-functional requirements

  • High availability for playback — a viewer should almost never see "video unavailable."
  • Low startup latency and smooth playback across a wide range of network conditions.
  • Cost efficiency — because egress dominates, the design must aggressively cache and avoid re-shipping bytes from origin.
  • Scalability — read-heavy by a huge margin (far more views than uploads), so reads and writes scale independently.
  • Durability — never lose an uploaded master; creators trust us with the only copy.
Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole answer: "This is overwhelmingly read-heavy and bandwidth-bound, so the pipeline turns one expensive upload into many cheap-to-serve segments, and a CDN absorbs the read load." Say it early; everything downstream is justified by it.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope estimation

Numbers force the architecture. Pick round figures and reason aloud — precision matters less than the order of magnitude and the conclusion it drives.

Uploads and storage

Assume 500 million daily active users and that 1 in 1,000 uploads on a given day — so 500,000 uploads/day, roughly 6 uploads/second on average (and call it ~3x that at peak, ~18/s). Say the average upload is 300 MB of source video. Raw ingest is then:

  • 500,000 × 300 MB ≈ 150 TB/day of master files.
  • Transcoding multiplies that. If all renditions plus the codecs together total roughly 1.5× the master size, processed output is another ~225 TB/day. Combined, call it ~375 TB/day, or well over 100 PB/year. Storage is large but, on object storage, cheap and linear — it is not the scary number.

Egress bandwidth — the scary number

Now the read side. Suppose those 500M users watch on average 30 minutes/day, and the average serving bitrate (blending phones on mobile data with TVs on fiber) is 3 Mbps. Daily bytes served:

  • 500M users × 1,800 s × 3 Mbps ≈ 2.7 × 10¹⁸ bits/day ≈ 340 PB/day egress.
  • Spread over a day that averages ~31 Tbps, and peak is several times higher.

Compare: ~375 TB/day written versus ~340 PB/day read out — reads outweigh writes by roughly 900× in bytes. That ratio is the entire justification for a CDN. Serving even a fraction of 30+ Tbps directly from origin data centers would be ruinous; you push the bytes to edge caches close to users and serve the same popular segment millions of times from there.

Why this matters

Two takeaways to state:
(1) storage is large but boring — object storage scales linearly and is cheap per byte;
(2) egress is the cost center, so the design optimizes for cache hit ratio at the edge. If 95% of viewing concentrates on a small set of popular videos, a CDN serving 95%+ from cache turns a 30 Tbps origin problem into a 1.5 Tbps one.

03 Contract

API & data model

Sketch the contract so the components have something concrete to talk to. Two surfaces matter: the upload API (write path) and the playback API (read path). The actual video bytes never travel through your application servers as request bodies — they go straight to object storage and are served by the CDN. Your API moves metadata and URLs, not media.

Upload API

# 1. Reserve an upload — get back an id and a place to put bytes
POST /v1/videos
  body: { title, description, visibility }
  → 201 { video_id, upload_id,
          upload_url:  # pre-signed, resumable target in object storage
          "https://uploads.vid.sg/u/abc123?token=..." }

# 2. Client PUTs the file in chunks to upload_url (resumable, see step 05)
PUT {upload_url}
  Header: Content-Range: bytes 8388608-16777215/314572800
  → 308 Resume Incomplete   # or 200/201 when the last chunk lands

# 3. Finalize — triggers the transcoding pipeline
POST /v1/videos/{video_id}/complete
  → 202 Accepted { status: "PROCESSING" }

Playback API

# Fetch metadata + the manifest URL (a tiny JSON response)
GET /v1/videos/{video_id}
  → 200 {
      video_id, title, owner_id, duration_s, view_count,
      thumbnail_url: "https://cdn.vid.sg/t/abc123/hq.jpg",
      status: "READY",
      manifest_url: "https://cdn.vid.sg/v/abc123/master.m3u8"
    }

# The PLAYER then talks to the CDN directly, not to your API:
GET https://cdn.vid.sg/v/abc123/master.m3u8     # the manifest
GET https://cdn.vid.sg/v/abc123/720p/seg_0007.ts # one ~4s segment

Data-model sketch

Metadata is small, structured, and read constantly, so it lives in a database; the media lives in object storage and is referenced by key. A first cut:

videos          # the catalog row, read on every watch page
  video_id PK · owner_id · title · description
  duration_s · visibility · status (UPLOADING|PROCESSING|READY|FAILED)
  thumbnail_key · created_at

renditions      # one row per quality produced by the pipeline
  video_id FK · resolution (1080p|720p|480p|...) · codec (h264|av1)
  bitrate_kbps · segment_count · manifest_key · storage_class (HOT|COLD)

view_counts     # write-heavy, eventually consistent (see step 09)
  video_id PK · count (approx) · updated_at

The videos table is read-mostly with simple key lookups, so a wide-column store like Cassandra (or DynamoDB) partitioned by video_id fits the access pattern and scales horizontally; a relational store like PostgreSQL is perfectly fine until the read fan-out forces partitioning. The choice is secondary — the point in the interview is recognizing that metadata and media have completely different storage needs.

Follow-up they'll ask

"Why a pre-signed upload URL instead of uploading through your servers?" Because routing gigabytes through application servers wastes their bandwidth and memory and makes them a bottleneck. A pre-signed URL lets the client write bytes directly to object storage with a time-limited, scoped credential, keeping your API servers thin. We expand on the upload mechanics in step 05.

04 The blueprint

High-level architecture

Now assemble the pieces into one picture and trace the end-to-end flow. The system splits cleanly into a write path (upload → store → transcode) that runs once per video, and a read path (manifest → segments → CDN) that runs millions of times. Keeping them separate is what lets reads scale independently of writes.

Walk the diagram below with the controls. A client uploads to object storage via a pre-signed URL; finalizing the upload drops a job on a message queue; a fleet of transcoding workers fans the master out into renditions and segments, writes them back to storage, and publishes a manifest; the metadata service flips the video to READY; and from then on viewers pull the manifest and segments through the CDN, which caches them at the edge.

The cleanest way to describe this to an interviewer is "asynchronous, queue-driven processing decouples a slow, expensive transcode from a fast user response." The uploader gets a 202 Accepted immediately; the heavy work happens out of band, and the video becomes watchable when the pipeline finishes.

05 Bottleneck · ingest

Resumable upload

The first bottleneck is getting the bytes in at all. A naive single POST of a 2 GB file has two fatal problems: if the connection drops at 95% you start over, and the upload ties up a server socket for minutes. On Singapore mobile networks, where a creator might be on a train switching cells, that failure is the common case, not the edge case.

How resumable upload works under the hood

The fix is chunked, resumable upload: split the file into fixed-size chunks (say 8 MB) and upload them independently, each addressed by a byte range.

  • The client first asks the API to initiate an upload session, receiving an upload_id and a pre-signed URL.
  • Each chunk is sent with a Content-Range: bytes 8388608-16777215/314572800 header. The storage service acknowledges each chunk it has durably received.
  • If the connection drops, the client asks "how far did you get?" — the server replies with the highest contiguous byte received (an HTTP 308 Resume Incomplete with a Range header), and the client resumes from there instead of byte zero.
  • When the final chunk lands, storage assembles the object and the client calls /complete, which enqueues the transcode job.

This is exactly the resumable object-upload model offered by Amazon S3 multipart upload and Google Cloud Storage resumable uploads, and what the tus protocol standardizes. The chunk size is the key knob: larger chunks mean fewer round trips but more to re-send on a failure; 5–16 MB is a common sweet spot.

Two refinements matter in practice. First, chunks can upload in parallel — because each is an independent PUT against a byte range, a client on a good connection can saturate its uplink with several in flight at once, and storage assembles them in order at the end. Second, attach a checksum (e.g. an MD5/ETag) per chunk so a silently corrupted chunk is detected and re-sent rather than baked into the master; the final assembled object also carries a checksum the pipeline verifies before transcoding. This protects against the subtle failure where bytes arrive but are wrong, which a naive "did we receive enough bytes?" check would miss.

Failure mode

If the client crashes mid-upload, the partial object simply expires — set a lifecycle rule to abort incomplete multipart uploads after, say, 24 hours so half-finished masters don't accumulate cost. If the /complete call is lost, the client retries it; making /complete idempotent on upload_id means a duplicate retry doesn't enqueue two transcode jobs.

Follow-up they'll ask

"How do you stop someone uploading a 50 GB junk file or a virus?" The pre-signed URL is scoped and size-capped; after upload, the pipeline runs validation (container/codec sniffing, a malware scan, and content checks) before the video is published. A rejected upload flips status to FAILED and never reaches the CDN.

06 Bottleneck · processing

The transcoding pipeline

The master file is in storage, but it is useless for streaming as-is — it is one giant file in one quality. The second bottleneck is turning it into something every device can play smoothly. This is the job of transcoding: decoding the source and re-encoding it into many renditions, then chopping each into small segments and writing a manifest.

Why a DAG of jobs, not one big job

Processing a video is not a single step — it is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of dependent tasks: inspect the file, then in parallel produce each rendition, then per rendition split into segments, then assemble the manifest, then a final "publish" step that flips the video to READY. Modeling it as a DAG lets independent branches (e.g. the 1080p and 480p encodes) run on different workers at the same time, and lets you retry just the branch that failed instead of redoing everything.

How the fan-out works under the hood

  • /complete publishes a job message onto a queue (e.g. Kafka or SQS) — this is the decoupling point from L15's async-processing pattern. The API returns instantly; the queue buffers bursts.
  • A fleet of stateless transcoding workers pulls jobs. Each worker runs an encoder — in practice FFmpeg with codecs like H.264, VP9, or AV1, often GPU-accelerated for speed.
  • For each target rendition (e.g. 144p, 240p, 480p, 720p, 1080p, 4K, each at a chosen bitrate), the worker re-encodes the video, then runs the segmenter to cut it into ~2–6 second segments (small media files, e.g. seg_0001.ts).
  • Crucially, segments are aligned on keyframe (GOP) boundaries so the player can switch between renditions cleanly at any segment edge. This alignment is what makes adaptive bitrate (step 07) possible.
  • A final step writes the manifest files (one per-rendition playlist plus a master that lists all renditions) and publishes a "done" event that updates metadata to READY.

Step through the fan-out below: one master explodes into parallel renditions, each segmented, all stitched together by a single manifest.

Transcode on upload (eager)

  • Video plays smoothly the instant it goes READY
  • Predictable: every rendition exists before first view
  • Wastes compute & storage on videos nobody watches
  • The long tail (most uploads get <100 views) is pure cost

Transcode on demand (lazy)

  • Only pay to encode renditions that are actually requested
  • Cheaper for the huge long tail of unpopular videos
  • First viewer of a rare rendition waits for encode (latency)
  • More complex: needs a just-in-time encode + cache layer

Failure mode & retries

Transcoding is long-running and fails for ordinary reasons — a worker crashes, an OOM on a 4K frame, a corrupt source. Because the job lives on a durable queue, a failed or timed-out job is simply re-delivered to another worker. Jobs must be idempotent (writing rendition outputs to deterministic keys so a re-run overwrites cleanly) and bounded by a retry limit; after N failures the job goes to a dead-letter queue for inspection and the video is marked FAILED. Because the DAG isolates branches, a 4K encode failing doesn't block the 480p rendition that most viewers will actually use — you can publish partial renditions and backfill.

Follow-up they'll ask

"How do you transcode at scale without a giant idle fleet?" The queue depth is your autoscaling signal: scale the worker pool up when the backlog grows and down when it drains, and run non-urgent encodes on cheap spot/preemptible instances (the durable queue makes interruptions harmless). Split long videos into independent chunks transcoded in parallel to cut wall-clock time for a single big upload.

07 Bottleneck · smooth playback

Adaptive bitrate streaming

We now have many renditions and a manifest. The third bottleneck is playing back smoothly when the viewer's bandwidth is unknown and constantly changing — a Grab driver watching between rides drops from 5G to a congested cell in seconds. Pick a fixed quality and you either buffer constantly (too high) or look terrible (too low). The answer is adaptive bitrate streaming (ABR).

What the manifest contains

A manifest (a playlist file) is the table of contents the player reads first. There are two industry formats — HLS (Apple's, uses .m3u8 playlists and .ts/fMP4 segments) and MPEG-DASH (an open standard, uses an .mpd XML manifest). Both express the same idea: a master manifest lists every available rendition with its bandwidth and resolution, and each rendition has its own media playlist listing that quality's segments in order.

# master.m3u8 — the player reads this first
#EXTM3U
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=6000000,RESOLUTION=1920x1080
1080p/index.m3u8
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=3000000,RESOLUTION=1280x720
720p/index.m3u8
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=1200000,RESOLUTION=854x480
480p/index.m3u8

# 720p/index.m3u8 — that rendition's ordered segment list
#EXTM3U
#EXT-X-TARGETDURATION:4
#EXTINF:4.0,
seg_0001.ts
#EXTINF:4.0,
seg_0002.ts   # ...one entry per ~4s segment

How ABR decides, segment by segment

The intelligence is in the player, not the server — the server just hosts static files. The loop runs continuously:

  • The player fetches the master manifest and starts at a conservative rendition to begin playback fast.
  • After downloading each segment, it measures throughput (segment bytes ÷ download time) and watches its buffer level (how many seconds of video are queued ahead).
  • For the next segment, it picks the highest rendition whose bitrate the recent throughput can sustain with margin. Throughput rising and buffer healthy → step up a quality. Throughput dropping or buffer draining → step down before the buffer empties.
  • Because every rendition's segments are cut on the same boundaries, the player can request 720p/seg_0008.ts right after 1080p/seg_0007.ts and the video plays seamlessly across the switch.

The goal is to keep the buffer non-empty at all times. A stall (rebuffer) is the worst outcome for perceived quality — viewers tolerate a momentary dip in sharpness far better than a frozen frame, so good ABR errs toward stepping down early.

Concretely, picture a Grab driver on the MRT watching a 720p stream (3 Mbps) with 12 seconds buffered. The train enters a tunnel and measured throughput collapses to 0.5 Mbps. A throughput-only player would keep requesting 720p, drain the 12-second buffer in a few segments, and stall. A buffer-aware player sees the buffer falling and immediately requests the next segment at 240p (0.4 Mbps) — which downloads faster than it plays — so the buffer refills even on the weak link, and playback continues, just softer. When the train exits and throughput recovers, the buffer climbs back above the high-water mark and the player ramps back to 720p, then 1080p. The viewer saw a few seconds of lower sharpness; they never saw a spinner.

Under the hood — startup vs steady state

The player tunes two things differently. Startup favors fast time-to-first-frame: begin low so playback starts in well under a second, then ramp up once throughput is measured. Steady state favors stability: avoid flip-flopping quality every segment (visually jarring) by using a hysteresis margin and the buffer level, not just instantaneous throughput. Real players (hls.js, ExoPlayer, AVPlayer, dash.js) implement variants of this — throughput-based, buffer-based, or hybrid algorithms.

HLS

  • Default on Apple devices; the most broadly supported
  • Simple text .m3u8 playlists, easy to debug
  • Historically tied to .ts; now supports fMP4

MPEG-DASH

  • Open standard, codec-agnostic, very flexible
  • Not natively supported on Apple Safari/iOS
  • XML .mpd manifest is more verbose
Follow-up they'll ask

"How does adaptive bitrate actually work?" is the single most common probe on this topic. Nail it in one breath: "The video is pre-segmented at every quality and described by a manifest; the player measures throughput and buffer after each segment and chooses the next segment's rendition, stepping down before the buffer empties so it never stalls. The server is dumb static files; the smarts are in the client."

08 Bottleneck · the bill

CDN delivery & origin shielding

The estimation said egress is 900× the ingest. The fourth bottleneck is serving that flood affordably and fast. Serving every segment from your origin data center would be slow (a viewer in Jakarta hitting a Singapore origin pays the round-trip every segment) and catastrophically expensive. The answer is to put the bytes physically close to viewers with a CDN.

How a CDN serves a segment

  • Segment URLs point at the CDN (cdn.vid.sg/v/abc123/720p/seg_0007.ts). DNS/anycast routes the viewer to the nearest edge point of presence.
  • On a cache hit, the edge returns the segment immediately — no origin involved. Because segments are immutable, they cache for a long TTL.
  • On a cache miss, the edge fetches the segment from origin once, stores it, and serves it; every later viewer in that region gets a hit.
  • Real CDNs (CloudFront, Akamai, Fastly, Cloudflare) do exactly this; the platform owns the origin (object storage) and pays the CDN for edge bandwidth.

Origin shielding and tiered caching

A subtle problem: a brand-new viral video is a miss at every edge at once, so all edges stampede the origin simultaneously — the thundering herd. Origin shielding inserts a mid-tier cache between the edge layer and origin: edges miss to a regional shield, the shield fetches from origin once, and serves all the edges. This collapses thousands of concurrent origin requests for the same hot segment into one, protecting the origin and your bandwidth bill.

Step through the delivery path: a hit at the edge, a miss that walks back through the shield to origin, then warm for everyone after.

Where the bandwidth cost actually goes

The dominant cost is CDN egress to viewers — the bytes that leave edge nodes toward devices, which you pay per gigabyte. Origin-to-CDN transfer is a smaller, secondary cost that a high cache-hit ratio keeps tiny. So the levers that move the bill are: maximize cache-hit ratio (cache popular segments aggressively, use long TTLs on immutable segments), serve more-efficient codecs (AV1/VP9 cut bytes-per-pixel versus H.264, trading more encode compute for less egress), and avoid shipping renditions higher than the device needs.

Follow-up they'll ask

"Where does the bandwidth cost go, and how do you cut it?" Answer: almost entirely CDN egress to viewers. Cut it by raising the edge cache-hit ratio (popular content is highly cacheable), adopting denser codecs (spend encode CPU once to save egress millions of times), and right-sizing renditions to devices. This is the classic "spend cheap, reusable compute to save expensive, repeated bandwidth" trade.

09 Supporting cast

Metadata, view counting & storage tiers

The media pipeline is the hard part, but a watch page needs more than segments. Three smaller services round out the design, each with its own twist.

Metadata service

The metadata service serves the small, structured data behind a watch page: title, owner, description, duration, status, and the manifest URL. It is read on every view and is tiny per response, so it scales with a read replica fleet plus a cache (Redis) in front for the hottest videos. Writes are rare (upload, edits), reads are enormous — a textbook read-heavy workload. Thumbnails are just small images served, like segments, from the CDN.

View counting at scale

A naive UPDATE videos SET view_count = view_count + 1 per view is a write hotspot — a single viral video would funnel millions of writes onto one row and create lock contention. Instead, treat views as an aggregation problem:

  • Players emit lightweight view events to a queue (Kafka).
  • A stream processor (e.g. Flink) or periodic batch job aggregates events per video over a window and writes back an updated count.
  • The displayed count is eventually consistent and approximate — which is completely fine, because nobody needs the count exact to the unit, and "1.2M views" is what users see anyway.

This is the same pattern Shopee or TikTok would use for "items sold" or "likes": don't synchronously increment a hot row; ingest events, aggregate asynchronously, accept approximate.

Hot vs cold storage tiers

Storage grows forever, but access is wildly skewed: a small set of recent/popular videos gets nearly all the traffic, while an enormous long tail is watched rarely or never. Keeping everything on fast, expensive storage wastes money.

Hot tier

  • Standard object storage; instant access
  • Holds recent & popular videos and their segments
  • Higher per-GB cost, but it is what the CDN pulls from

Cold tier (archive)

  • Cheap archival storage (e.g. S3 Glacier-class)
  • Holds the long tail and the original masters
  • Retrieval is slow/charged — fine for rarely-watched content

A lifecycle policy migrates a video's renditions to the cold tier after it has been cold (unwatched) for a while, and promotes it back if it suddenly trends. The master file is precious — it is the source of truth for any future re-encode (a new codec, a new resolution) — so keep it durably, typically in cheap cold storage, effectively forever.

Follow-up they'll ask

"What happens when a long-tail video suddenly goes viral?" Its segments are cold and uncached. The first viewers trigger a cold-tier retrieval into hot storage and a cache fill at the edge (protected by the shield from step 08); after that brief warm-up the video serves like any popular one. The design degrades gracefully: a slightly slower first play, never an outage.

10 Resilience

Failure modes & what breaks at 10x

Close by walking the failure modes — interviewers always ask "what happens when X dies?" — and by stress-testing the design at ten times the load.

What happens when a node dies

  • A transcoding worker dies mid-job. The job is still on the durable queue; its visibility timeout expires and another worker picks it up. Idempotent output keys make the re-run safe. No data lost.
  • The CDN edge or a whole PoP fails. Anycast/DNS reroutes viewers to the next-nearest edge; they re-fetch from origin/shield and continue. Playback may briefly drop a quality level, not stop.
  • The metadata DB primary fails. Promote a replica; reads continue from the remaining replicas throughout. Because playback URLs are static CDN paths, even a metadata blip doesn't stop in-flight playback.
  • Object storage degrades. Object stores are designed for very high durability via cross-AZ/region replication; a single AZ outage doesn't lose data, and segments already at the edge keep serving.

What breaks at 10x

  • Transcoding compute is the first wall — 10x uploads means 10x encode hours. Mitigate with on-demand/lazy transcoding for the long tail, denser-but-slower codecs only where they pay off, chunked parallel encoding, and elastic spot fleets driven by queue depth.
  • CDN egress scales with viewers, so 10x viewers is ~10x bandwidth bill. The cache-hit ratio is the lever: push it higher with origin shielding and longer TTLs, and pre-warm caches for predictable spikes (a creator's scheduled premiere).
  • Metadata reads grow with views; add read replicas and a larger cache tier. The view-event queue must scale horizontally (more Kafka partitions) so a viral spike doesn't back it up.
  • Storage is the easy one: object storage scales near-linearly, and tiering keeps the cost curve sane. It is rarely the bottleneck.
The shape of the answer

Notice every 10x mitigation is the same move applied in a new place: decouple with a queue so you can scale the consumers elastically, and cache aggressively so you serve the same bytes many times. If you can name those two principles and point to where each applies, you have demonstrated you understand the system rather than memorized a diagram.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole design compresses to one arc: ingest a huge upload reliably, fan it out asynchronously into many segmented renditions described by a manifest, then let the client adapt quality per segment while a CDN serves the bytes from the edge.
