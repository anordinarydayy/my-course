Message Queues & Async Processing

Synchronous call chains tie every service together in time: one slow dependency stalls the whole request, and a single traffic spike can topple the entire chain at once. A message queue breaks that coupling — it buffers work between a producer and a consumer so each side can scale, stall, and recover on its own clock.

About these notes: original study material on message queues and asynchronous processing — a standard, widely-documented engineering topic. The explanations, examples, numbers, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Two diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow.
00 The problem

The synchronous trap

Start with the most natural way to build anything: a request comes in, and your service does all the work before replying. A user taps Place Order in the Shopee app; the order service writes the order, charges the wallet, reserves inventory, sends a push notification, fires off a confirmation email, and updates the analytics pipeline — and only then returns 200 OK. Every one of those calls is synchronous: the caller blocks, holding the request thread open, until the callee answers.

This works beautifully on day one and breaks in three predictable ways as you grow.

It couples services in time

A synchronous call creates a temporal coupling: the producer and the consumer must both be healthy, at the same instant, for the request to succeed. If the email provider is having a slow day and takes 4 seconds to respond, the customer waits 4 extra seconds to see their order confirmed — even though the email has nothing to do with whether the order is valid. The slowest link in the chain sets the latency of the whole thing.

It fails as a unit

Worse, if the email provider is fully down, the naive code throws, and the whole order fails — the customer sees an error for an order that was actually fine. You have let a non-critical dependency (email) hold a critical path (checkout) hostage. This is cascading failure in miniature: one component's outage propagates outward into every caller that was waiting on it.

It cannot absorb spikes

Now imagine a Shopee 11.11 flash sale. Traffic jumps 20x in ten seconds. Each request still holds threads open across the whole call chain, so threads pile up waiting on the slowest downstream service. Connection pools drain, queues of pending work form inside each service's memory, and the system tips over all at once. Synchronous chains have no shock absorber — the load that arrives is exactly the load every service must handle right now.

The thread you should ask yourself: which of these steps does the customer actually need to wait for? The order must be written and the wallet charged before you can honestly say "ordered." But the email, the analytics event, and arguably the push notification are things that merely need to happen eventually. Forcing them onto the synchronous path is the root mistake.

01 The fix

Decouple with a queue

A message queue is a durable buffer that sits between two parties. A producer (also called a publisher) writes a message — a small, self-contained record of work to be done — into the queue and immediately gets an acknowledgement. A consumer (or subscriber) reads messages from the queue, on its own schedule, and processes them. The two never talk directly and never need to be alive at the same moment.

How it actually works under the hood

The mechanism is simpler than it sounds. The queue is essentially a server (or cluster) that owns a persistent data structure — conceptually an append-only list, often backed by disk for durability. Walk the flow step by step:

  • Publish. The producer opens a connection to the broker and sends a message: a payload plus metadata (a key, a timestamp, headers). The broker writes it to storage and returns an ack. From the producer's view, the work is now "handed off" — it returns to the caller in single-digit milliseconds, regardless of how long the actual processing will take.
  • Buffer. The message sits in the queue. If consumers are keeping up, it sits there for milliseconds. If they have fallen behind or are temporarily down, it sits there for minutes or hours without being lost. The queue is the shock absorber.
  • Deliver. A consumer asks the broker for the next message (a pull model, like Kafka and SQS) or the broker hands one to a connected consumer (a push model, like classic RabbitMQ). The message becomes invisible to other consumers while one works on it.
  • Acknowledge. When the consumer finishes successfully, it sends an ack back to the broker, which then deletes the message (or advances the consumer's read position). If it never acks, the broker assumes failure and re-delivers later — the safety net we will build out in step 06.

Rewriting our Shopee checkout with this in mind: the order service now writes the order, charges the wallet (both still synchronous — the customer genuinely needs them), and then publishes three messages — send_confirmation_email, emit_analytics, update_recommendations — and returns 200 OK. The customer sees their confirmation in ~120 ms instead of ~4 s. The email is sent a moment later by a separate worker reading the queue. If the email provider is down, the message simply waits; the order was never at risk.

Under the hood

The word "queue" hides two design families. A broker-with-deletion system (RabbitMQ, Amazon SQS) treats messages as transient: once acked, they are gone. A log-based system (Apache Kafka, AWS Kinesis, Apache Pulsar) keeps every message on disk for a retention window (say 7 days) and tracks each consumer's offset — its read position — separately. The log model lets you replay history and have many independent readers; we build one from scratch in Lesson 17.

02 Why it pays off

What you buy: five benefits

The queue is not free — it adds a moving part, operational burden, and a new failure surface. So it earns its place by delivering five concrete benefits. An interviewer wants to hear you name them and tie each to a problem from step 00.

1 — Decoupling

The producer knows only the queue, not who consumes the message or how many consumers there are. You can add a new consumer — say a fraud-scoring service that also wants every order event — without touching the order service at all. The two sides evolve independently. This is the single biggest architectural win.

2 — Load leveling (buffering spikes)

This is the shock absorber. During the 11.11 flash sale, the producer might publish 50,000 messages per second for a few minutes, while the consumers can only process 8,000 per second. Without a queue, the extra 42,000/s would crush the downstream services. With a queue, the surplus simply accumulates as queue depth and drains over the following minutes. You have converted a fatal spike into a tolerable backlog. The system is provisioned for the average rate, not the peak.

3 — Independent scaling

Because consumers pull work at their own pace, you scale them independently of producers. If the backlog grows, add more consumer instances — they are competing consumers on the same queue, so each grabs different messages and total throughput rises roughly linearly. When the spike passes, scale them back down. Producers are unaffected either way.

4 — Resilience

If a consumer crashes, its unacked messages return to the queue and another consumer picks them up. If all consumers are down, the producer keeps publishing and the queue keeps the messages safe; processing simply resumes when a consumer comes back. A down consumer now means delayed work, not lost work or a failed request. The blast radius of a failure shrinks dramatically.

5 — Offloading slow async work

Anything that does not need to finish before you answer the user belongs off the critical path: sending email and push notifications, generating thumbnails or transcoding a video, recomputing a recommendation feed, writing to an analytics warehouse, syncing data to a search index. Move each behind a queue and the user-facing latency collapses to just the work that genuinely matters.

Interview tip

Do not reflexively add a queue to every diagram. The honest follow-up is: "what does this queue cost you?" Answer: an extra hop, eventual (not immediate) consistency for the offloaded work, harder end-to-end debugging, and a new system to operate. A queue is right when the work can tolerate a short delay and you value resilience over instant feedback — and wrong when the caller genuinely needs the result now.

03 Two topologies

Queue vs pub/sub

There are two fundamentally different distribution patterns, and choosing the wrong one is a common interview slip. The difference is who gets each message.

Point-to-point queue (competing consumers)

In a point-to-point queue, each message is delivered to exactly one consumer. Many consumers may be attached, but they compete for messages — the broker hands message A to consumer 1, message B to consumer 2, and so on. This is the pattern for work distribution: you have a pile of independent tasks (resize these 10,000 images) and you want them spread across a worker pool so the job finishes faster. Adding workers increases throughput; each unit of work is done once.

Publish/subscribe (fan-out)

In publish/subscribe (pub/sub), each message is delivered to every interested subscriber. The producer publishes to a topic; multiple subscriptions each get their own independent copy of the stream. This is the pattern for event broadcasting: one order.placed event needs to reach the email service and the analytics service and the loyalty-points service and the fraud detector — each doing something completely different with the same fact.

The two compose. A single GrabPay payment.completed event can fan out via pub/sub to four subscriptions, and within each subscription a pool of competing consumers shares that subscription's copies. That is exactly how Kafka consumer groups work: each group is an independent subscriber (fan-out across groups), and within a group, partitions are split among members (competing consumers inside the group).

Point-to-point queue

  • Each message processed exactly once across the consumer pool
  • Adding consumers raises throughput (work is split)
  • Best for task distribution: image resize, send-this-email, charge-this-card
  • Tools: RabbitMQ queue, Amazon SQS, a single Kafka consumer group

Publish / subscribe

  • Each message delivered to every subscriber independently
  • Adding subscribers does not split work — it adds reactions to the same event
  • Best for event broadcast: one fact, many independent consequences
  • Tools: Kafka topics + consumer groups, SNS→SQS, Google Pub/Sub, NATS, Pulsar
04 Guarantees

Delivery semantics

Now the question interviewers love most: how many times will a message be delivered? The honest answer involves a deep trade-off between losing messages and duplicating them, and it falls into three named levels.

At-most-once

At-most-once delivery means a message is delivered zero or one times — it is never duplicated, but it may be lost. Mechanically, the producer fires the message and does not wait for confirmation, or the consumer acks before processing. If anything crashes at the wrong moment, the message vanishes silently. This is the cheapest and lowest-latency option, and it is fine for data where the occasional gap is harmless — high-frequency metrics, non-critical telemetry, a "user is typing…" indicator. It is unacceptable for an order or a payment.

At-least-once

At-least-once delivery means a message is delivered one or more times — it is never lost, but it may be duplicated. The consumer acks only after it finishes processing. If the consumer processes the message but crashes before the ack lands, the broker never hears the ack, assumes failure, and re-delivers — so the work runs twice. This is the common default in SQS, Kafka, RabbitMQ, and most real systems, because losing a message is usually far worse than handling it twice. The catch: you must make duplicates harmless, which is the entire subject of step 05.

Exactly-once

Exactly-once delivery — each message processed once and only once — is the holy grail and is genuinely hard in a distributed system. The fundamental obstacle: the consumer's "process the work" and "ack the broker" are two separate actions, and any crash between them forces a choice between re-delivering (at-least-once) or risking loss (at-most-once). Some systems offer exactly-once within their own boundary (Kafka's transactional producer + idempotent writes to Kafka), but the instant your consumer touches an external system — a database, a payment API — that guarantee evaporates. So in practice the production recipe for "effectively exactly once" is: at-least-once delivery + idempotent consumers. You accept duplicates at the transport layer and make them no-ops at the application layer.

At-least-once (the default)

  • Never loses a message — safe for orders, payments, events
  • Ack after processing; broker re-delivers on missing ack
  • Cost: duplicates happen, so consumers must be idempotent
  • What everyone ships unless they have a strong reason not to

At-most-once

  • Never duplicates — simplest, lowest latency
  • Ack before processing (or fire-and-forget)
  • Cost: a crash silently drops the message
  • Only for loss-tolerant data: metrics, telemetry, presence
Interview tip

If asked "can you give me exactly-once?", the strong answer is: "Not end-to-end across external systems — that's a distributed-consensus problem. I'll use at-least-once delivery and make the consumer idempotent, which gives exactly-once effects, which is what the business actually cares about." Naming the distinction between exactly-once delivery and exactly-once processing signals real experience.

05 Surviving duplicates

Idempotency & dedup keys

Since the default is at-least-once, duplicates are not a rare edge case — they are a guarantee you signed up for. The discipline that makes them harmless is idempotency: an operation is idempotent if applying it many times has the same effect as applying it once. SET balance = 100 is idempotent; balance = balance - 50 is not. Your job as the consumer author is to turn the second kind into the first.

How idempotency is actually implemented

The standard mechanism is a dedup key (idempotency key): a unique identifier the producer attaches to each message — often a UUID or a natural business key like order_id. The consumer records which keys it has already processed and skips repeats. Concretely, for a GrabPay top-up worker:

def handle(msg):
    key = msg.idempotency_key          # e.g. "topup:txn-8842177"
    # atomic claim: succeeds only the FIRST time this key is seen
    claimed = db.insert_if_absent("processed_keys", key)
    if not claimed:
        return ack()                   # duplicate — already done, no-op

    apply_topup(msg.wallet_id, msg.amount)   # the real, non-idempotent work
    ack()

The load-bearing detail is that the "have I seen this key?" check and the "mark it seen" write must be atomic — otherwise two duplicate deliveries racing on two workers can both pass the check. You get atomicity from a unique constraint (INSERT ... ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING in PostgreSQL), a conditional write (PutItem with a condition expression in DynamoDB), or SETNX in Redis. Even better, where the database supports it, fold the dedup row and the business write into one transaction, so they commit or fail together.

The worked scenario

A customer tops up their GrabPay wallet with S$50. The message is delivered, the worker credits S$50, but the worker's node dies a millisecond before the ack reaches the broker. The broker re-delivers. Without idempotency, the customer is now credited S$100 — a real-money bug. With the dedup key, the second delivery finds topup:txn-8842177 already in processed_keys, acks immediately, and credits nothing. The duplicate became a harmless no-op.

Why this matters more in fintech

In a payments or wallet context, an idempotency key is not optional polish — it is the line between a balanced ledger and a financial incident. This is why Stripe, SeaMoney, and Wise expose an Idempotency-Key header on their write APIs: the caller supplies the key so a network retry of "charge S$50" can never double-charge. The same principle flows straight through into how their internal queues are consumed.

06 Failure handling

Retries, ack & the dead-letter queue

A consumer will sometimes fail to process a message: a downstream API times out, the database is briefly unavailable, or the message itself is malformed. The reliability machinery that handles this cleanly is built from four pieces working together.

Visibility timeout & explicit ack

When a consumer pulls a message, the broker does not delete it — it hides it from other consumers for a window called the visibility timeout (SQS's term) or holds it as unacked (RabbitMQ). If the consumer acks within the window, the message is deleted. If the window expires with no ack — because the consumer crashed or hung — the broker makes the message visible again and another consumer retries it. This is what turns "consumer died mid-work" into "the work just runs again elsewhere." Set the timeout longer than your worst-case processing time, or the broker will redeliver a message that is still being worked on, creating needless duplicates.

Retries with exponential backoff and jitter

For transient failures, retrying is the right move — but retrying immediately and forever is a trap. If a downstream service is overloaded, a wall of instant retries makes it worse (a retry storm). The fix is exponential backoff: wait 1 s, then 2 s, then 4 s, then 8 s between attempts, giving the dependency room to recover. And add jitter — a random offset — so that thousands of consumers that failed at the same instant don't all retry at the same instant and create a synchronized thundering herd. The delay for attempt n is roughly random(0, base · 2^n), capped at some maximum.

Dead-letter queues for poison messages

Some messages will never succeed no matter how often you retry: a malformed payload, a reference to a deleted record, a bug that only that message triggers. This is a poison message, and if you retry it forever it blocks the queue and burns resources. The answer is a dead-letter queue (DLQ): after a message has been retried a maximum number of times (say 5), the broker moves it to a separate queue instead of the main one. The DLQ quarantines the poison so healthy messages keep flowing, and gives your team a place to inspect, fix, and replay the failures. An alarm on DLQ depth is one of the most valuable alerts in a queue-based system — a rising DLQ usually means a bad deploy or a schema change broke the consumer.

Interview tip

Expect the direct question: "how do you handle a message that always fails?" The full answer names all four pieces: bounded retries with exponential backoff and jitter, a visibility timeout so a crashed worker's message comes back, a max-retry threshold, and a dead-letter queue to quarantine poison messages with an alarm on its depth. Bonus: mention that the DLQ is for operators, not customers — someone has to look at it.

07 Ordering

Ordering guarantees

A subtle question that trips people up: do messages come out in the order they went in? The instinct is "of course," but global ordering is surprisingly expensive, and most systems deliberately give it up.

Why global FIFO is costly

To guarantee a single, total FIFO order across an entire queue, every message must pass through one serialized point — you cannot process message #5 before #4 is confirmed done. That throttles you to a single consumer working one message at a time, destroying the independent-scaling benefit that justified the queue in the first place. Strict global ordering and high parallel throughput are fundamentally at odds.

The usual compromise: partition / key-based ordering

The practical middle ground is per-key ordering. You split the queue into independent partitions (Kafka) or message groups (SQS FIFO's MessageGroupId), and route each message to a partition by hashing a partition key. Ordering is then guaranteed within a partition but not across partitions. Choose the key so that messages which must stay ordered share a key.

For Grab driver-location updates, key by driver_id: every update for one driver lands in the same partition and is processed strictly in order, so you never apply a stale position after a fresh one — but updates for different drivers run fully in parallel across partitions. You get the ordering that matters and keep nearly all the throughput. The number of partitions caps your maximum parallelism for that topic, so pick it with growth in mind.

Interview tip

The question to anticipate is simply "do you need ordering here?" — and the strong move is to push back on whether you need global order at all. Most of the time you only need order per entity (per user, per account, per driver), which partition keys give you cheaply. Reserve strict global FIFO for the rare case that truly demands it, and call out the throughput cost when you do.

08 Operating it

Backpressure & queue depth as a signal

A queue's greatest strength — absorbing surges — hides its greatest operational risk. If consumers are persistently slower than producers, the queue does not magically fix that; it just delays the reckoning. The backlog grows without bound, memory or disk fills, and message latency climbs from milliseconds to hours. The buffer bought you time, not a free lunch.

Queue depth is your health signal

The single most useful metric in a queue system is queue depth (backlog size), and its first derivative — is the depth growing or shrinking? A flat-or-falling depth means consumers are keeping up. A steadily rising depth means you are falling behind and will eventually fall over. Two close cousins matter too: consumer lag (in Kafka, how far behind the latest offset a consumer group is) and message age (how old the oldest unprocessed message is — this directly bounds your end-to-end latency).

Autoscaling on the backlog

Because queue depth is such a clean signal, it is the ideal trigger for autoscaling consumers. The control loop: depth above a high-water mark → add consumer instances; depth drained below a low-water mark → remove them. This is far better than scaling on CPU, because it reacts to the actual work waiting, not a proxy for it. During the 11.11 sale the worker fleet scales out automatically as the backlog builds and scales back in as it drains — the system right-sizes itself to demand.

Backpressure: pushing the problem upstream

Backpressure is the mechanism by which a system that cannot keep up signals "slow down" to whatever is feeding it, rather than silently collapsing. When you cannot add consumers fast enough — say a downstream database is the true bottleneck — the queue can apply backpressure: reject or throttle new publishes (returning an error or a "try later" to producers), or pause pulling from upstream so the pressure surfaces at the edge where you can shed load deliberately. The alternative to backpressure is an unbounded queue that eventually exhausts memory and takes down the broker — converting a slowdown into an outage. A bounded queue that pushes back is healthier than an unbounded one that pretends everything is fine.

Failure mode to name out loud

"What happens when the consumers can't keep up?" Walk it through: queue depth climbs, message age grows, end-to-end latency degrades (the confirmation email arrives an hour late), and eventually the queue hits its size limit. At that point a good design applies backpressure — throttling producers or shedding low-priority messages — instead of OOM-killing the broker. The recovery action is to autoscale consumers on depth and, if the bottleneck is downstream, to fix or scale that. The queue made the failure graceful and visible instead of sudden and silent.

09 Concretely

A concrete contract & what breaks at 10x

To whiteboard this convincingly, you need a message schema and an API shape, not just boxes. Here is an original sketch for the Shopee order-event contract — note the explicit idempotency key, the partition key, and a schema version so producers and consumers can evolve independently.

// Topic: order-events     partition key: order_id
{
  "schema_version": 2,
  "event_id":       "evt-7f3a91c2",      // dedup / idempotency key
  "event_type":     "order.placed",
  "order_id":       "ord-558203",        // partition key → per-order ordering
  "occurred_at":    "2026-05-31T09:14:22Z",
  "payload": {
    "buyer_id":  "usr-9921",
    "amount_sgd": 49.90,
    "items": [ { "sku": "SKU-771", "qty": 2 } ]
  }
}

// Producer side (publish is fire-and-confirm, ~few ms):
publish(topic="order-events", key=order_id, value=event, await_ack=true)

// Consumer side (at-least-once + idempotent):
on_message(msg):
    if seen(msg.event_id): ack(); return     // duplicate → no-op
    process(msg.payload)                      // do the real work
    mark_seen(msg.event_id); ack()            // commit, then ack

The real tools you'd name

For a high-throughput event backbone with replay and many independent consumers, reach for Apache Kafka (or managed: AWS MSK, Confluent Cloud) — log-based, partitioned, retains messages for days. For simple, fully-managed task queues without operational overhead, Amazon SQS (with SQS FIFO when you need per-group ordering) plus SNS for fan-out. For flexible routing and classic broker semantics, RabbitMQ. For cloud-native pub/sub, Google Pub/Sub; for low-latency lightweight messaging, NATS; for a unified queue+stream model, Apache Pulsar. The relevant knobs to mention: retention period, partition count, visibility timeout, max-receive-count (the DLQ trigger), consumer-group concurrency, and prefetch / batch size.

What breaks at 10x

  • Hot partition. If one partition key is far busier than the rest — a single mega-merchant during a sale — that one partition saturates while others idle, and per-key ordering means you cannot spread its load. Mitigate by choosing a higher-cardinality key or sub-keying the hot entity.
  • The broker itself. A single broker node is a bottleneck and a single point of failure; at 10x you need a replicated cluster (Kafka with replication factor ≥ 3) so a node loss does not lose messages or halt publishing.
  • The dedup store. The idempotency check is now on the hot path of every message. At 10x it must be fast and scalable (Redis or DynamoDB) and its keys must expire (a TTL) so it does not grow forever.
  • DLQ flood. A bad deploy at 10x can dump millions of messages into the DLQ in minutes. You need alarming and a tested replay path, or you will be reprocessing by hand at 3 a.m.
Where this goes next

Everything here treats the queue as a black box. Lesson 17 opens it up and builds a Kafka-like distributed log from first principles — partitions, the append-only commit log, offsets, replication, and consumer groups — so you understand exactly how the durable buffer keeps its promises under failure. ★ Review

Summary & self-test

Compress the whole topic to one sentence: a queue trades immediate consistency for decoupling, buffering, and resilience — and you pay for that with at-least-once delivery, which you make safe through idempotency, retries, and a dead-letter queue. If you can draw the producer → queue → consumer flow, branch it into the retry-and-DLQ path, and explain why the default is at-least-once, you own the interview.
