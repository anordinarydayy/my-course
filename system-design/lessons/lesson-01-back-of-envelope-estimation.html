01Lesson · System Design Interview

Back-of-the-Envelope Estimation

Estimation is how you turn a vague prompt like "design Twitter" into numbers you can defend out loud: queries per second, the read-to-write ratio, storage per year, bandwidth, and cache size. Done in two minutes, those numbers justify every component you reach for next — and getting an order of magnitude wrong is the fastest way to lose a senior interviewer's confidence.

About these notes: original study material on back-of-the-envelope estimation — a standard, widely-taught interview skill. The explanations, examples, tables, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. 00 The problem

Why estimate at all?

An interviewer says "design a news feed for 300 million daily users." You could jump straight to drawing boxes — load balancer, app servers, a database, a cache — but you'd be guessing. Back-of-the-envelope estimation is the practice of computing rough resource numbers (traffic, storage, bandwidth, memory) from a few stated assumptions, so that every box you draw afterward is justified by arithmetic instead of vibes.

The reason this matters is that architecture decisions are quantitative. The numbers decide the design:

  • How many app servers? If you compute 35,000 queries per second and one server handles ~1,000, you need roughly 35 plus headroom — not 3, and not 3,000.
  • Do we even need a cache? A read-heavy system with a 100:1 read/write ratio screams "cache." A write-heavy one does not.
  • Single database, replicas, or sharding? If a year of data is 50 GB, one PostgreSQL box is fine. If it's 50 TB, you're sharding.
  • Do we need a CDN? If you compute tens of gigabits per second of media egress, the answer is yes, and you should say so before the interviewer asks.

The crucial subtlety: the interviewer does not want a precise figure. They want the right order of magnitude, fast. "About 40,000 QPS" delivered confidently in ninety seconds beats "39,712.4 QPS" computed slowly on a calculator. Estimation is a thinking-out-loud exercise that proves you can reason about scale, round aggressively, and keep your units straight under pressure.

This lesson builds the toolkit in layers. First the three small reference tables you should memorize — data-size shorthand, latency numbers, and availability nines. Then the four core formulas — QPS, peak QPS, storage, and bandwidth — each motivated by a question the previous answer raised. Then a complete worked example for a Twitter-scale feed, computed end to end. We close with the discipline that keeps your math honest and the follow-up questions interviewers always ask.

Why it matters

Estimation is the bridge between the prompt and the diagram. Skip it and every component looks arbitrary; do it and your whole design reads as a chain of justified consequences. That chain is exactly what a senior interviewer is scoring you on.

01 Reference · data sizes

Powers of two & data-size shorthand

Half of estimation is converting between bytes, kilobytes, gigabytes, and so on without fumbling the zeros. The trick is to anchor on powers of two, because storage and memory are measured in binary multiples, and each step of ten bits is almost exactly a step of one thousand in decimal. Memorize this single mapping and the rest follows.

Power of 2Approx. decimalShort nameBytes shorthand
210~1 thousandkilo (K)1 KB ≈ 103 bytes
220~1 millionmega (M)1 MB ≈ 106 bytes
230~1 billiongiga (G)1 GB ≈ 109 bytes
240~1 trilliontera (T)1 TB ≈ 1012 bytes
250~1 quadrillionpeta (P)1 PB ≈ 1015 bytes

The mental model: every three zeros of decimal is roughly ten bits of binary. So 230 is "about a billion" and you should treat a gigabyte as 109 bytes when estimating. The ~7% gap between 210 (1,024) and 1,000 is real, but in estimation you round it away — being off by 7% never changes an order of magnitude, and order of magnitude is all that matters here.

The data-type sizes you build payloads from

To estimate storage you need a feel for how big the primitives are. These are the round numbers worth carrying in your head:

ThingRough sizeNote
ASCII character1 byteone keystroke of plain text

UUID16 bytes36 chars as a string
A short text post (e.g. a tweet)~300 bytestext + id + author + timestamps

One minute of HD video~50 MBstreaming bitrates vary widely

Worked conversion: how big is a year of GrabPay transactions?

Suppose GrabPay records 30 million transactions a day, each row about 500 bytes (amounts, two account ids, status, timestamps, a reference string). That's 3 × 107 × 500 = 1.5 × 1010 bytes per day = 15 GB/day. Over a year, 15 GB × 365 ≈ 5.5 TB. The instant you can say "about 5 terabytes a year for the ledger," you've justified a partitioned/sharded store and a cold-storage tier — without touching a calculator.

Interview tip

When you state a size, immediately translate it into a decision. "Five terabytes a year" should be followed by "so a single node won't hold a few years of history — we'll shard by account and archive old rows." Numbers without a consequence are just trivia; the consequence is the point.

02 Reference · latency

Latency numbers every engineer should know

Sizes tell you about storage; latency numbers tell you about time — specifically, the relative cost of reaching different layers of the memory hierarchy. They explain why caching exists, why cross-region calls are dangerous, and why "just read it from disk" is sometimes a thousand times slower than "read it from memory." You don't need exact figures; you need the ratios.

OperationRough timeIntuition
L1 / L2 CPU cache reference~1 nsessentially free
Main memory (RAM) reference~100 nsfast — the cache layer lives here
Read 1 MB sequentially from RAM~10 µsmemory bandwidth is huge
SSD random read~100 µs~1,000× slower than RAM
Round trip within one data center~0.5 msa network hop next door
Spinning-disk seek~10 msavoid on the hot path
Round trip across regions~tens–hundreds msSG ↔ US can be ~150–200 ms

The single lesson to extract: memory is fast, disk and network are slow, and crossing a region is very slow. The jumps are roughly ×100 from cache to RAM, ×1,000 from RAM to SSD, another ×5 to a same-data-center hop, and then orders of magnitude more once you leave the building. A request that touches RAM is a different animal from one that seeks a spinning disk or hops to another continent.

Why these ratios drive architecture

Concretely, for an Agoda hotel-search page that must respond in under 200 ms: a single cross-region database call to a US replica can eat your entire latency budget in one hop (~180 ms round trip from Singapore). That arithmetic is exactly why you place read replicas or caches in the same region as the user, and why a CDN edge for static assets matters — it turns a 180 ms fetch into a ~5 ms one by moving the bytes physically closer.

The same ratios justify caching in general. If a feed read costs ~10 ms from a database (disk + network) but ~0.5 ms from Redis in RAM, the cache isn't a nice-to-have — it's a 20× latency win on the hottest path in the system. You can defend the entire existence of your cache layer with two numbers from this table.

03 Reference · availability

Availability & the nines

Availability is the fraction of time a service is up and serving, usually written as a percentage of "nines." It shows up in interviews because it's a hard constraint that shapes architecture: a payments service like SeaMoney that promises four nines can tolerate far less downtime than a best-effort analytics dashboard, and that promise forces redundancy, failover, and multi-region replication. The skill is to convert a nines figure into real downtime per year instantly.

Availability"Nines"Downtime / yearDowntime / day
99%two nines~3.65 days~14.4 min
99.9%three nines~8.7 hours~1.4 min
99.99%four nines~52 minutes~8.6 sec
99.999%five nines~5 minutes~0.86 sec

The pattern is clean: each extra nine cuts allowed downtime by roughly a factor of ten. Three nines is about 8.7 hours a year — fine for many internal tools. Four nines is about 52 minutes — typical for a serious consumer product. Five nines is about 5 minutes — the gold standard reserved for payment rails and infrastructure, and expensive to reach because it leaves no room for a slow manual recovery.

How the math works

A year is about 525,600 minutes (365 × 24 × 60). Allowed downtime is just (1 − availability) × 525,600 minutes. For four nines: 0.0001 × 525,600 ≈ 53 minutes. You can reconstruct any row from that one expression, which is safer than memorizing the table blind.

Where the nines come from

Cloud providers state these in Service Level Agreements (SLAs). Note that availability multiplies down a dependency chain: if your API needs a database (99.99%) and a cache (99.99%) and a queue (99.99%), the combined ceiling is roughly 0.99993 ≈ 99.97%. More moving parts means a lower effective number, which is the quantitative argument for redundancy and graceful degradation.

Interview tip

If you claim a target like "four nines," be ready for "how do you actually achieve it?" The answer is no single points of failure: replicated databases with automatic failover, multiple stateless app instances behind a load balancer, health checks, and ideally multi-region deployment. Stating the number commits you to the architecture that earns it.

04 Core formula · traffic

QPS: turning users into traffic per second

Now the formulas. The most important number in any estimation is QPS — queries (or requests) per second — because it sizes your compute. You derive it from the user base and how active each user is. The base formula:

# average QPS for one type of action
QPS = DAU × actions_per_user_per_day ÷ seconds_per_day

DAU stands for daily active users — the count of distinct users who do something on a given day, and the standard starting point for traffic math. The key rounding trick: a day has 86,400 seconds, but in estimation you use ~100,000 seconds/day (105). It's about 16% high, which conveniently makes your QPS slightly conservative (lower), and it turns ugly division into shifting decimal points.

Worked example: writes vs reads for a Twitter-like feed

Take a feed product with 300 million DAU. Assume each user posts 2 items a day (a write) and reads 200 items a day across their scrolling (a read). Compute each stream separately:

  • Write QPS = 3 × 108 × 2 ÷ 105 = 6 × 103 = ~6,000 writes/sec.
  • Read QPS = 3 × 108 × 200 ÷ 105 = 6 × 105 = ~600,000 reads/sec.

The read/write ratio is 600,000 : 6,000 = 100 : 1. That single ratio is one of the most consequential numbers you'll produce, because it tells the interviewer — and you — that this is a read-heavy system. Read-heavy means: cache aggressively, add read replicas, and don't over-engineer the write path. A write-heavy system (say, an IoT ingest pipeline) would push you toward queues, batching, and write-optimized stores like Cassandra instead.

Read-heavy (ratio ≫ 1)

  • Cache the hot reads (Redis, CDN)
  • Add read replicas to fan out load
  • Eventual consistency is usually tolerable
  • Examples: news feed, Agoda search, product pages

Write-heavy (ratio ≪ 1)

  • Buffer writes through a queue (Kafka)
  • Use write-optimized stores (Cassandra, LSM-trees)
  • Batch and compact to amortize disk cost
  • Examples: telemetry, GPS pings, event logging
Interview tip

After computing QPS, the very next sentence should answer "is this read- or write-heavy?" — out loud, before being asked. It signals that you understand the numbers mean something. Stating the ratio is the move that separates a candidate who memorized a formula from one who reasons with it.

05 Core formula · peak

Peak vs average: provision for the spike

Average QPS hides a trap. Traffic is never flat — it clusters around mealtimes, commutes, paydays, and promotions. If you provision for the average, you'll be overwhelmed exactly when it matters most. So you scale the average by a peak factor to estimate the peak QPS you must actually handle:

peak_QPS = average_QPS × peak_factor   # peak_factor is usually 2–3×

A factor of 2–3× is the standard default for a consumer app with a clear daily rhythm. For our feed: average read QPS was ~600,000, so peak read QPS is roughly 600,000 × 3 = 1.8 million reads/sec. That's the number that decides how many servers you provision — not the average. You size for the spike and let autoscaling reclaim the slack when traffic falls.

When the peak factor is far higher

The 2–3× rule assumes organic, smoothly-varying traffic. Some events shatter it. A Shopee 11.11 sale or a flash voucher drop can drive traffic 10–50× the daily average in a single minute, because demand is artificially synchronized to a countdown. In those cases you don't just multiply by 3 — you say so explicitly: "this is event-driven, so I'd plan for a 20× spike, pre-scale capacity ahead of the sale, and put a queue in front of the checkout path to absorb the burst." Naming the unusual peak factor shows you understand the business shape of the traffic, not just the formula.

Sizing the fleet from peak QPS

Once you have peak QPS, sizing servers is a division. If one app instance comfortably serves ~1,000 requests/sec, then 1.8 million peak reads need 1,800,000 ÷ 1,000 = 1,800 instances on the read path. Add headroom (say 30%) for failover and uneven load, and round up. Whether the per-server number is 1,000 or 5,000 depends on your stack, but the method — peak QPS divided by per-node throughput, plus headroom — is always the same.

Failure mode if you ignore the peak

Provision for the average and the peak arrives as a brownout: request queues back up, latency climbs, health checks start failing, and the load balancer pulls "unhealthy" nodes — concentrating even more load on the survivors. That's a cascading failure, and it's almost always triggered by sizing to the average instead of the peak. The peak factor is cheap insurance against it.

06 Core formula · storage

Storage per year: where the bytes pile up

Compute tells you how many servers; storage tells you what kind of database and how many of them. Only writes accumulate (reads don't add bytes), so storage is driven by the write rate, the size of each item, the retention horizon, and the replication factor:

storage_per_year = writes_per_day × bytes_per_item × 365 × replication_factor

The replication factor is how many physical copies you keep for durability — typically 3 in systems like Cassandra, HDFS, or a primary-plus-two-replicas SQL setup. It's easy to forget and it triples your real footprint, so include it.

Worked example: a year of feed posts plus media

Our feed takes ~6,000 writes/sec, which is 6,000 × 86,400 ≈ 5 × 108 = roughly 500 million posts/day. Split the storage by type, because text and media behave completely differently:

  • Text + metadata at ~300 bytes/post: 5 × 108 × 300 = 1.5 × 1011 bytes/day = ~150 GB/day. Over a year, ~55 TB; with replication ×3, ~165 TB/year.
  • Media: assume only 1 in 10 posts carries a photo averaging 1 MB. That's 5 × 107 × 1 MB = 5 × 1013 bytes/day = ~50 TB/day. Over a year, ~18 PB; with replication, ~50+ PB/year.

The breakdown is the insight: media dwarfs text by roughly 300×. That single comparison justifies the entire architecture split — text lives in a sharded database (e.g. Cassandra or sharded PostgreSQL), while media goes to object storage (S3) fronted by a CDN. You'd never put 50 PB of images in your transactional database, and the numbers prove why without any hand-waving.

07 Core formula · bandwidth & cache

Bandwidth and cache size

Two more numbers round out the toolkit. Bandwidth is the data flowing per second; cache size is how much RAM you need to hold the hot subset of data. Both are simple multiplications once QPS is known.

Bandwidth = QPS × payload size

bandwidth = QPS × bytes_per_payload

For the read path, the heavy payload is media. Suppose each feed read that includes an image transfers ~1 MB, and the media-read QPS is ~60,000/sec (one tenth of the 600,000 total reads carry an image). Then egress is 6 × 104 × 106 = 6 × 1010 bytes/sec = ~60 GB/sec, which is ×8 bits = ~480 Gbps. That is enormously more than any single server's network card (typically 10–25 Gbps), which is the quantitative reason a CDN is mandatory: it serves those bytes from hundreds of edge locations instead of your origin. Text-only bandwidth, by contrast, is 600,000 × 300 bytes ≈ 180 MB/sec — trivial in comparison.

Cache size via the 80/20 rule

You can't cache everything, and you don't need to. The 80/20 rule (the Pareto principle) observes that roughly 80% of requests hit about 20% of the data — the hot set. So you size the cache to hold that hot 20% rather than the whole dataset.

Estimate it from the read volume you want to absorb in memory. Say you want to cache one day of hot reads. Daily reads are 600,000 × 86,400 ≈ 5 × 1010 read events; the distinct hot items behind them, at ~300 bytes each for text, might be on the order of a few hundred gigabytes once you take the 20% slice. A round answer: "a few hundred GB of Redis, spread across a cluster." The exact figure matters less than the conclusion — the hot set fits in a modest in-memory cluster, so caching is feasible and cheap relative to the latency it saves.

Under the hood: why the 80/20 slice works

Access patterns in social and commerce systems are heavily skewed — a Zipfian distribution where a few celebrities, trending posts, or best-selling SKUs draw a disproportionate share of reads. Caching the top slice gives you most of the hit-rate benefit for a fraction of the memory. Redis with an LRU or LFU eviction policy and a per-key TTL naturally keeps that hot set resident and lets cold items fall out. If you sized the cache for 100% of the data you'd be paying for RAM that almost never gets read.

Interview tip

When you hit a bandwidth figure that exceeds a single machine's NIC (like ~480 Gbps), say the consequence immediately: "no origin can serve this, so static and media traffic must go through a CDN; the origin only sees cache misses." Connecting the number to the component is the whole exercise.

08 Putting it together

The full worked example, end to end

Let's run the entire pipeline for our Twitter-like feed in one continuous pass, exactly as you'd do it on a whiteboard. State the assumptions first, then compute each number, and let each result point at an architecture decision. Step through the diagram below to watch every figure appear.

Stated assumptions: 300M DAU; each user posts 2 items/day and reads 200/day; a text post is ~300 bytes; 1 in 10 posts has a ~1 MB photo; peak factor 3×; replication factor 3.

Reading the result as an architecture

Lay the numbers side by side and the design writes itself:

  • ~600K avg / ~1.8M peak reads vs ~6K writes → 100:1 read-heavy → cache layer + read replicas; the write path stays simple.
  • ~165 TB/yr text, ~50 PB/yr media → sharded database for text, object storage (S3) for media. No single node holds it.
  • ~480 Gbps media egress → CDN is mandatory; the origin only serves cache misses.
  • Hot set fits in a few hundred GB of RAM → a Redis cluster is feasible and pays for itself in latency.

That is a complete, defensible high-level design — load balancer, stateless app tier sized to peak QPS, read replicas, a Redis cluster, a sharded post store, S3 + CDN for media — and every single component traces back to a number you computed in under five minutes.

09 Discipline

Estimation discipline: keeping the math honest

The formulas are easy; doing them live, out loud, without a calculator is the actual skill. A handful of habits keep you fast and credible.

Round aggressively, and round early

Use 105 for seconds/day, treat a GB as 109 bytes, and treat ×365 over a year as "roughly three more zeros." The interviewer is checking your order of magnitude, not your long division. Carrying exact figures slows you down and invites arithmetic mistakes that look worse than a clean approximation.

State every assumption out loud

"I'll assume 300M DAU, 2 posts each, 200 reads each" is not a side note — it is the answer's foundation, and saying it lets the interviewer correct you early ("actually assume 50 reads") instead of watching you build five minutes of math on a wrong premise. An estimate is only as good as its stated inputs, so make them explicit and adjustable.

Keep units consistent and visible

The most common live mistake is a units slip — mixing bits and bytes (a factor of 8), or seconds and days (a factor of ~105). Write the unit next to every number. When you compute bandwidth, decide up front whether you're reporting GB/s or Gbps and stick to it. A 480 Gbps answer accidentally stated as 480 GB/s is off by 8× and a sharp interviewer will catch it.

Sanity-check against intuition

After each result, ask "does this feel right?" 1.8 million reads/sec for a 300M-user feed is plausible. If you'd gotten 18 reads/sec or 18 billion, a zero went missing or got added — stop and recheck before building on it. A quick reasonableness pass catches the order-of-magnitude blunders that actually lose interviews.

Do

  • Round to one significant figure
  • Use 105 sec/day and powers of ten
  • Announce assumptions before computing
  • Label every number with its unit
  • Sanity-check each result against intuition

Don't

  • Reach for exact figures or a calculator
  • Mix bits and bytes, or seconds and days
  • Hide assumptions in your head
  • Provision for the average, not the peak
  • Forget the replication factor in storage
The follow-ups to rehearse

Three questions come up almost every time, so have the answers loaded: "Is that read- or write-heavy?" — state the ratio and the caching/replica consequence. "Where does the storage go?" — break it into text vs media and name the store for each. "What's your peak-to-average factor, and why?" — say 2–3× for organic traffic, much higher for sale events, and tie it to how you provision. Each follow-up is just one of your numbers, explained.
