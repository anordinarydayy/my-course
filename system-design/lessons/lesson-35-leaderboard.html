Design a Real-Time Leaderboard

A game leaderboard has to absorb a flood of score changes and still answer "who's in the top 10?" and "what's my rank?" the instant a player asks — for millions of players at once. A relational ORDER BY ... LIMIT buckles under that load; a single in-memory data structure turns the whole problem into a few logarithmic-time operations.

About these notes: original study material on designing a real-time leaderboard — a standard, widely-documented system-design exercise. The explanations, examples, numbers, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. The reference shorthand BBG V2·C10 is only a syllabus marker. 00 The problem

Why leaderboards are hard

A leaderboard looks trivial — it's just a sorted list. That intuition is exactly the trap the interview is built around. The difficulty is not sorting; it's keeping a list sorted while it is being mutated thousands of times per second and simultaneously queried for both a tiny slice (the top 10) and an exact position deep in the middle (a player's rank), with both answers expected back in single-digit milliseconds.

Think about a Free Fire tournament run by Garena out of Singapore. During a weekend event, tens of millions of matches finish, each producing a score delta for one or more players. Every one of those players, between matches, refreshes their profile screen — which shows "You are ranked #1,284,902 of 40 million" and "Top 100 this week." That single screen demands two fundamentally different operations against the same constantly-shifting dataset:

  • A range read — fetch the top K entries in score order.
  • A rank lookup — given one player, count how many players score higher.

The second one is the killer. Counting "how many rows are above me" naively means scanning a huge dataset on every profile open. Hold that thought — section 03 shows exactly why the obvious database approach collapses, and section 04 shows the data structure that makes both operations cost O(log n).

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Open by narrowing the problem out loud. Leaderboards come in many shapes, and the design hinges on the answers. Drive the conversation toward both the functional features and the non-functional constraints.

Functional requirements

  • Update a score. When a match ends, a player's score changes — usually an increment (you earned 50 points), sometimes an absolute set (you reached level 90). This is the write path.
  • Top-K query. Return the highest-scoring K players in order, with their scores. K is small and bounded — typically the top 10, 50, or 100.
  • A player's own rank. Given a user_id, return their exact (or near-exact) position, 1-indexed, among all players.
  • The neighborhood window. "Show me the 5 players above and 5 below me" — a range read centered on a rank, not on position 1. This is what makes the profile screen feel competitive.
  • Real-time freshness. A score change should be reflected in queries within roughly a second, not after a nightly batch job.

Out of scope (state it, then drop it)

  • How scores are computed — that is the game server's job. We receive a final delta.
  • Anti-cheat and score validation — assume an upstream trust boundary already verified the score.
  • Rich profile data (avatars, clan names) — the leaderboard stores user_id → score; everything else is a join the client does separately.

Non-functional requirements

  • Scale: tens of millions of players on a single board; tens of thousands of score updates per second at peak.
  • Latency: reads and writes in single-digit to low-double-digit milliseconds at p99.
  • Availability over strict consistency: if a rank is off by one for a fraction of a second, nobody is harmed. We can lean toward eventual consistency for ranks far from the top.
  • Durability: a score, once accepted, must not be silently lost — there must be a source of truth we can rebuild from.
Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole design: "the hard part is supporting both top-K and arbitrary-rank lookups in real time while the data is constantly mutating." Say it early. It tells the interviewer you've identified the real bottleneck instead of treating this as a sorting exercise.

02 Estimate

Back-of-the-envelope estimate

A quick sizing pass tells you whether this fits in memory and how much write throughput you must absorb. Use round numbers; the interviewer wants the reasoning, not precision.

Write throughput

Suppose 40 million monthly active players, and during a peak event 5 million are online at once. If an active player finishes a match every 5 minutes, that's 5,000,000 / 300s ≈ 16,600 score updates per second on average, spiking to perhaps 50,000 writes/sec at the top of the hour when matches cluster. That is a lot for a relational table to re-sort on, but trivial for an in-memory structure.

Read throughput

Players check rankings far more than they play. Say each online player opens the leaderboard or profile screen once a minute: 5,000,000 / 60 ≈ 83,000 reads/sec, and these spike harder than writes because everyone refreshes when a tournament ends. Reads dominate writes by roughly 5:1 — a crucial fact for section 08.

Storage

Each leaderboard entry is essentially a user_id (8 bytes) plus a score (8 bytes), but the sorted structure carries pointer overhead. Budget roughly ~100 bytes per member in a Redis sorted set. For 40 million members:

# memory for one global board
40,000,000 members × ~100 bytes  ≈  4 GB

# comfortably fits in RAM on a single modern node;
# a 50k writes/sec stream is well within one Redis instance's ceiling

The headline: the entire global board fits in a few gigabytes of RAM on one machine. That single fact is why an in-memory sorted structure is the answer and a disk-based sort is not. The bottleneck will not be storage size — it will be the read fan-out onto one hot key.

03 The wrong default

Why not just use SQL?

The reflexive answer is a relational table: players(user_id PK, score, updated_at), an index on score, and two queries. It works beautifully at small scale and falls apart at large scale in two distinct ways — understanding both is what separates a strong answer from a hand-wave.

Top-K is fine; rank is the problem

With a B-tree index on score, the top-K query is actually cheap:

SELECT user_id, score FROM players
ORDER BY score DESC LIMIT 100;

The database walks the tail end of the index for 100 rows and stops. That's logarithmic plus a tiny scan — not the issue. The killer is the rank query: "how many players score higher than me?"

SELECT COUNT(*) FROM players
WHERE score > (SELECT score FROM players WHERE user_id = :me);

A B-tree index lets you find a value in O(log n), but a standard index does not store how many entries precede a given key. So COUNT(*) WHERE score > X must scan every index entry above your score and tally them. For a player near the median of 40 million, that's counting ~20 million entries — per profile open. Multiply by 83,000 reads/sec and the database is doing trillions of index touches per second. It melts.

Constant re-sorting under write load

The second problem is the write side. Every score update changes a player's position, which means the score index must be re-balanced. At 50,000 writes/sec, the index is in perpetual churn, lock contention climbs, and the very reads above get slower still. The read and write pressures compound.

SQL table + index

  • Durable and familiar; great source of truth
  • Top-K via ORDER BY ... LIMIT is genuinely fast
  • Rank query is O(n) — a full count above your score
  • Index churns constantly under heavy writes
  • Cannot hold real-time rank for millions at 80k+ reads/sec

In-memory sorted set

  • Update, top-K, and rank are all O(log n)
  • Holds 40M members in ~4 GB of RAM
  • Absorbs 50k writes/sec on one node
  • Volatile — needs a persistence story (section 07)
  • A single global board is a hot key (section 08)
Under the hood — why no rank from a B-tree

You could make SQL fast by maintaining an order-statistics tree (each node stores the size of its subtree, so rank is a logarithmic walk). But standard SQL indexes are not order-statistics trees. Redis's sorted set essentially is one — which is exactly why we reach for it. Some teams approximate ranks in SQL with a periodically-refreshed materialized rank column, but that sacrifices real-time freshness.

Common follow-up

"Could you use SQL anyway?" Yes — for the durable source of truth. The trick (section 07) is to keep PostgreSQL as the system of record and use the sorted set purely as a rebuildable serving layer. You don't pick one; you layer them.

04 The core idea

The sorted set — one structure, three operations

The heart of the design is the sorted set (Redis calls it a ZSET). A sorted set is a collection of unique members — here, user_ids — each tagged with a floating-point score. The structure keeps members ordered by score at all times, and crucially it can answer "give me members ranked A through B" and "what rank is this member?" both in logarithmic time.

How it works under the hood

Redis implements a sorted set with two cooperating structures kept in sync:

  • A hash table mapping member → score, so "what is this player's score?" is O(1).
  • A skip list ordering members by score, which gives ordered traversal and range queries in O(log n).

A skip list is a stack of linked lists. The bottom list holds every member in score order. Each higher list is a sparse "express lane" that skips over many nodes, built probabilistically (each node has a coin-flip chance of being promoted to the next level up). To find a position, you start at the top express lane and drop down level by level, skipping huge spans cheaply — the same logarithmic search a balanced tree gives, but simpler to implement and easy to traverse in order.

The reason this matters for rank specifically: Redis augments each skip-list node with a span — the number of bottom-level nodes that the forward pointer jumps over. As the search descends, it sums those spans, so by the time it locates your member it has already counted how many members precede it. That sum is your rank, computed in the same logarithmic walk. This is the order-statistics trick that SQL's plain B-tree lacks.

The four operations you actually use

Map every leaderboard feature onto a sorted-set command. Using Redis names (the de-facto standard; most managed offerings — AWS ElastiCache, GCP Memorystore, Redis Cloud — expose the same API):

  • Update a score: ZADD board 990 user:42 sets an absolute score; ZINCRBY board 50 user:42 adds a delta. Both are O(log n) because they reposition one member in the skip list.
  • Top-K: ZREVRANGE board 0 99 WITHSCORES returns the 100 highest scorers, descending. O(log n + K) — locate the head, then walk K nodes.
  • A player's rank: ZREVRANK board user:42 returns the 0-indexed position from the top. O(log n), thanks to the span trick above.
  • The neighborhood window: get the rank with ZREVRANK, then ZREVRANGE board (rank-5) (rank+5) WITHSCORES to fetch the 11 entries centered on the player.

Every core feature is now a single, sub-millisecond, logarithmic-time command against ~4 GB of RAM. The SQL nightmare of section 03 has evaporated.

Why descending (REV) commands

A sorted set orders ascending by score, so rank 0 is the lowest score. Leaderboards want the highest score at rank 1, so you use the REV variants (ZREVRANGE, ZREVRANK) throughout. It's the most common off-by-one mistake people make at the whiteboard — the highest scorer is ZREVRANK = 0, which you present to the user as "#1".

05 Contracts

API and data model

Now make it concrete with the endpoints a client calls and the keys the storage layer holds. Going past a vague "we have a sorted set" earns real points.

The HTTP API

# write path — game server reports a finished match
POST /v1/leaderboards/{boardId}/scores
  { "userId": "u_8842", "delta": 50, "occurredAt": 1717142400 }
  → 202 Accepted

# top-K
GET /v1/leaderboards/{boardId}/top?limit=100
  → [ { "rank": 1, "userId": "u_19", "score": 99820 }, ... ]

# a single player's standing + neighbors
GET /v1/leaderboards/{boardId}/rank/{userId}?window=5
  → { "rank": 1284902, "score": 7310,
      "neighbors": [ {rank:1284897,...}, ... {rank:1284907,...} ] }

Two design choices worth defending. First, the write uses a delta, not an absolute score, so the leaderboard never needs to know the player's prior total — it just applies ZINCRBY. Second, the write returns 202 Accepted: the score is durably enqueued, and the sorted set may update a beat later. That deliberate looseness is what lets the write path scale (section 07).

The storage keys

The naming convention for keys is itself a design decision, because it's how you get multiple boards (section 09) for free:

# one ZSET per logical board
ZADD   lb:global              99820  u_19
ZADD   lb:region:SG           41200  u_8842
ZADD   lb:weekly:2026-W22     7310   u_8842

# the durable source of truth lives elsewhere (Postgres)
players(user_id PK, total_score BIGINT, region, updated_at)
score_events(event_id PK, user_id, delta, board_id, occurred_at)

The key prefix (lb:global, lb:region:SG, lb:weekly:...) encodes the board's identity. The same score event can fan out into several sorted sets — a global board, a regional board, and the current weekly board — each just another ZINCRBY against a different key. The relational tables are the system of record: score_events is an append-only log of every delta (so we can replay), and players holds the authoritative running total.

06 The happy path

Score update and rank query, end to end

Watch the central flow play out: a player earns points, the sorted set re-orders itself, and a top-K read and a my-rank read both come back instantly. This is the loop the whole system optimizes.

When ZINCRBY lb:global 50 u_8842 runs, Redis finds u_8842 via the member hash, removes its skip-list node, recomputes the new score, and re-inserts the node at its new position — adjusting the span counts of the nodes it passes. The member has physically moved up the board. A subsequent ZREVRANK for that player now walks the express lanes and returns the new, correct rank; a ZREVRANGE 0 9 walks the head and returns the fresh top 10. No re-sort, no scan — just two logarithmic traversals.

Common follow-up

"What if two matches for the same player finish at the same instant?" ZINCRBY is atomic and additive, so two concurrent +50 increments correctly land as +100 — unlike an absolute ZADD, where the later write would clobber the earlier one (last-writer-wins, a lost update). Prefer increments for additive scores; reserve absolute ZADD for "set to this exact value" semantics like a high-water-mark level.

07 Don't lose data

Architecture and persistence

The sorted set is in memory, and memory is volatile. The bottleneck the previous section created: if the Redis node restarts, does every player's rank vanish? The answer must be no — so we treat the sorted set as a fast, rebuildable serving layer sitting in front of a durable source of truth.

The write pipeline

A finished match doesn't write straight to Redis-and-pray. It flows through a pipeline that makes the score durable first:

  • The game server POSTs the delta to the leaderboard service, which appends it to a durable log — a Kafka topic (or writes the row to score_events in Postgres). This is the moment the write becomes safe, and why the API returns 202 Accepted.
  • A consumer reads the log and applies ZINCRBY to the relevant sorted sets and updates the authoritative players.total_score in Postgres.
  • Because the log is the system of record, the sorted set is now fully rebuildable: replay the log (or read the players table) into a fresh ZSET and you're back.

Redis durability knobs

Even though the DB is the truth, you still want Redis itself to survive a restart fast, rather than replaying the whole log every time. Redis offers two persistence modes:

  • RDB snapshots: periodic point-in-time dumps of the dataset to disk. Compact and fast to load, but you lose any writes since the last snapshot.
  • AOF (append-only file): logs every write command. With appendfsync everysec, you risk at most ~1 second of writes on a crash — a good default for a leaderboard, where losing a second of score increments is recoverable from the upstream log anyway.

RDB snapshot

  • Small files, very fast restart load
  • Low runtime overhead
  • Loses writes since the last snapshot

AOF (everysec)

  • At most ~1s of writes lost on crash
  • File grows; needs periodic rewrite/compaction
  • Slower restart (replays the command log)

Failure mode: the Redis node dies

If the primary fails, a replica (kept warm via Redis replication) is promoted by Redis Sentinel or by Redis Cluster's failover, and serving continues within seconds. Worst case — both primary and replica are lost — you rebuild the sorted set from the durable log or the players table. Reads might serve slightly stale ranks during the gap, which our non-functional requirements explicitly permit. The key insight: because the truth lives outside Redis, a total cache loss is a performance event, not a data-loss event.

08 The real bottleneck

The hot-key problem — scaling reads

Here is where the interview gets interesting. The global board is a single key, lb:global, living on a single Redis node. From section 02, reads outnumber writes ~5:1 and spike to well over 100,000/sec when a tournament ends — and every one of them hammers that one key on that one node. That is a hot key: a single piece of data so popular it saturates the machine that owns it, no matter how much total cluster capacity you have. Sharding by key doesn't help, because there's only one key.

Lever 1 — read replicas

Writes must go to the primary (one node owns the authoritative sorted set), but reads can be served by read replicas that stream the primary's changes. Spin up several replicas behind the leaderboard service and fan reads across them. Ten replicas multiply read capacity tenfold. The trade-off is replication lag: a replica may be tens of milliseconds behind, so a player who just scored might briefly see their old rank. For a leaderboard that's acceptable — and it's exactly the eventual-consistency latitude we reserved in section 01.

Lever 2 — cache the top-K

The overwhelming majority of those reads want the same thing: the top 10 or top 100, which barely changes second to second. Compute ZREVRANGE 0 99 once and cache the serialized result for, say, 1 second. Now 99% of read traffic is served from a tiny cached blob and never touches the sorted set at all. This single optimization often does more than adding replicas, because it collapses the hottest read into one computation per second.

Lever 3 — exact near the top, approximate in the long tail

Here's the deepest move. A player ranked #3 cares about the exact number. A player ranked #1,284,902 does not — "top 4%" is just as satisfying, and nobody can tell #1,284,902 from #1,284,915. So:

  • For the top N (say the top 10,000), serve exact ranks straight from ZREVRANK.
  • For the long tail, serve an approximate rank derived from a score histogram — bucket scores into ranges, keep a running count per bucket (updated cheaply), and estimate rank as "(players in higher buckets) + (interpolation within my bucket)." This is O(buckets), independent of how deep you are.

Approximation lets the long tail cost essentially nothing while the part players actually scrutinize stays precise.

Under the hood — why the hot key resists sharding

Normal Redis Cluster sharding distributes different keys to different nodes by hashing the key name. But a single global leaderboard is one key, so it lives entirely on one shard — sharding gives it zero relief. The escape hatches are exactly the three above: replicate the key for reads, cache the dominant read, and shrink the work for the long tail. (Splitting the board into many smaller boards — section 09 — is the other way out, and the cleanest when the product allows it.)

Common follow-up

"How do you scale a global leaderboard that's a hot key?" Lead with: "reads dominate, so I separate the write primary from many read replicas, cache the top-K aggressively because it's the same answer for everyone, and serve exact ranks only near the top while approximating the long tail with a score histogram." Naming all three levers, and why sharding alone fails, is the full-marks answer.

09 Many boards

Segmented and time-windowed leaderboards

Real products rarely show one global board. Free Fire shows your rank among friends, within your region (Singapore vs. Indonesia), and for the current weekly season. Each of these is just another sorted set keyed differently — and segmentation happens to be the cleanest cure for the hot key, because it spreads load across many smaller, independently-shardable keys.

Segmented boards

  • Regional: lb:region:SG, lb:region:ID. A single match event fans out into both the global board and the player's regional board. Regional boards are far smaller, so their reads are cheap and they live on different shards.
  • Friends: you can't precompute a board per friend group (the combinations explode). Instead, fetch each friend's score from the global board with one ZMSCORE call and sort the handful of friends client-side or in the service. A friends list is small (dozens), so this is trivial.

Time-windowed boards and the weekly reset

"Top players this week" needs a board that resets every Monday. The elegant trick is to bake the time window into the key name and let it expire:

# the key itself names the window
ZINCRBY lb:weekly:2026-W22 50 u_8842
EXPIRE  lb:weekly:2026-W22 1209600   # keep ~2 weeks, then auto-delete

# daily boards work the same way
ZINCRBY lb:daily:2026-05-31 50 u_8842

The "reset" is therefore not a destructive operation. When the clock rolls into week 23, writes simply start landing in lb:weekly:2026-W23, a brand-new empty key. The old week's board still exists for a grace period (for end-of-season payouts and history), then Redis's TTL expires it automatically and reclaims the memory. No cron job scans and deletes millions of rows; no risk of a half-cleared board. This is the single most elegant pattern in the whole design.

Common follow-up

"How do you do weekly resets without a giant delete?" The answer interviewers want: "I never reset in place — I encode the week in the key name and set a TTL. Writes roll over to the new key automatically when the week turns, and the old board self-expires." Then mention the edge case: align the key's date label to a fixed timezone (e.g. UTC, or the game's official tournament timezone) so a player crossing midnight doesn't write to the wrong week.

10 Edges & scale-out

Ties, failure modes, and what breaks at 10x

Breaking ties deterministically

Two players with score 7,310 — who's ranked higher? A sorted set orders equal scores lexicographically by member name, which is arbitrary and unfair. The standard fix is to fold the tiebreaker into the score itself. Pack the score and an inverse timestamp into one number: whoever reached the score first ranks higher.

# composite score = points in high bits, "earlier is better" in low bits
composite = points × 1e11 + (TS_MAX − reached_at_seconds)
ZADD lb:global  <composite>  u_8842

Now equal-point players are separated by who got there first, deterministically, with no extra storage. Display logic strips the low bits back off to show the human-readable score. The alternative — a secondary sort on a separate field — means a second round trip; the composite keeps it to one atomic ZADD.

Failure modes recap

  • Redis primary dies: a replica is promoted (Sentinel / Cluster failover); reads may be briefly stale; no score is lost because the durable log holds the truth (section 07).
  • Consumer falls behind: if the applier lags, ranks go stale but never wrong — the Kafka log buffers, and the consumer catches up. Monitor consumer lag as your freshness SLO.
  • A region's board is corrupted or lost: rebuild that one sorted set by replaying its events; the blast radius is one segment, not the whole system — another argument for segmentation.
  • Replication lag spikes: a player sees their old rank for a moment. Acceptable by design; if not, route that one player's own-rank read to the primary (read-your-writes) while everyone else reads replicas.

What breaks at 10x

Push to 400 million members and 500,000 writes/sec, and the comfortable single-node story strains in two places:

  • The global board no longer fits one node's RAM cheaply (~40 GB and climbing). The cleanest answer is to lean harder on segmentation: maybe there is no single global board at all, only regional ones, and "global rank" becomes an approximate figure merged from per-region histograms. Exactness is sacrificed precisely where it doesn't matter.
  • The write primary saturates. Because every increment to lb:global hits one node, 500k writes/sec is its own hot key on the write side. Solution: shard the global board into, say, 16 sub-boards by hash(user_id) % 16, each on its own primary. Top-K then merges the top 100 from each of the 16 shards (a 1,600-entry merge — cheap), and a player's exact global rank becomes a sum of "my rank in my shard" plus an estimate of the others — i.e. approximate again. The recurring theme: exactness near the top, approximation in the tail, and segmentation to spread the load.
The thread that ties it together

Every scaling move in this lesson is the same trade made in a different place: give up a little exactness or freshness where users can't perceive it, to buy a lot of throughput. Cached top-K (slightly stale), replica reads (slightly lagged), approximate long-tail ranks (slightly fuzzy), sharded global board (approximate cross-shard rank). Knowing where the product can absorb that fuzz is the senior-engineer judgment the interview is testing.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: a leaderboard is an in-memory sorted set that turns "update score," "top-K," and "my rank" into O(log n) operations — then the engineering is keeping that one hot key fed, durable, and fast under a read flood. If you can defend the sorted set over SQL, name the three hot-key levers, and explain TTL-based weekly resets, you own this interview.
