Design Object Storage

Object storage hands flat keys to blobs at planetary scale, with extreme durability and near-commodity cost. The design hinges on two ideas: split the metadata from the bytes, and lean on erasure coding so a fleet of cheap disks can survive constant failure without paying for three full copies.

00 The problem

Why object storage?

A traditional database is built to store small, structured rows you query by rich predicates. A file system gives you a hierarchy of directories and bytes you mutate in place. Neither is the right tool when Shopee needs to keep tens of billions of product photos, when GrabPay must retain seven years of immutable PDF statements for the regulator, or when Agoda streams terabytes of hotel imagery to every region. What those workloads share is a different shape: enormous, mostly-immutable blobs, addressed by a simple name, read far more often than written, and expected to never disappear.

Object storage is the system built for exactly that shape. An object is an opaque blob of bytes plus a little metadata, identified by a string key, living inside a flat namespace called a bucket. There are no real directories — 2024/invoices/feb/INV-8841.pdf is just a key with slashes in it, not a tree of folders. You PUT a whole object and GET a whole object; you do not seek into byte 4,000,000 and overwrite it. That deliberate narrowing of the contract is what lets the system scale to exabytes.

  • Flat keys to blobs. No hierarchy to walk, no inodes to lock — a key maps directly to bytes.
  • Effectively unlimited scale. Add disks and nodes; the namespace does not care how big it gets.
  • Eleven nines of durability. The headline promise: lose, statistically, one object in a hundred billion per year.
  • Cheap. Built on commodity disks, with erasure coding driving the storage overhead far below triple replication.

The whole rest of this lesson is the answer to one uncomfortable question that the requirements above raise: how do you promise eleven nines of durability on hardware that fails constantly, while still being the cheap option? Everything — the metadata split, erasure coding, scrubbing, rebuild — exists to close that gap.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Object storage is a deep topic, so the interviewer wants to watch you fence it off before you draw. Separate the functional requirements (what the API must do) from the non-functional requirements (the qualities that make the design hard), and state your assumptions out loud.

Functional requirements

  • Create a bucket and store / retrieve / delete objects by key within it (PUT, GET, DELETE).
  • List the keys in a bucket, optionally filtered by a prefix.
  • Versioning — overwriting a key keeps the old version rather than destroying it.
  • Multipart upload — large objects (gigabytes) uploaded in parallel chunks and assembled server-side.
  • Object metadata — content type, size, custom tags, and a checksum you can read back.

Non-functional requirements

  • Durability of eleven nines (99.999999999%). This is the headline and the source of nearly every design decision.
  • Availability of around four nines (99.99%) — slightly weaker than durability, because a brief read outage is recoverable but a lost object is forever.
  • Virtually unlimited scale — exabytes of data, trillions of objects, no fixed ceiling.
  • Low cost per gigabyte — this is the competitive battleground, so storage overhead must stay low.
  • Read-after-write consistency for newly created objects: a GET immediately after a successful PUT must return the new bytes.

Assumptions worth stating

I will assume objects are immutable once written — you replace a key, you do not edit it in place. The workload is read-heavy (think a 5:1 or 10:1 read-to-write ratio for photos and content). Object sizes are bimodal: a long tail of small objects (thumbnails, JSON, statements at tens of KB) and a heavy tail of large ones (videos, backups at hundreds of MB to GB). And we are designing the storage engine itself, not the CDN that fronts it — though I will note where a CDN belongs.

Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole design: "durability is the hard requirement, availability is secondary, and cost forces us off triple replication toward erasure coding." Say that early and the interviewer knows you understand which constraint is actually binding.

02 Estimate

Back-of-envelope sizing

A quick estimate justifies every later decision — especially the choice to split metadata from data, and the choice of erasure coding over replication. Let me size a plausible regional deployment, say the storage tier behind Shopee's image service in Southeast Asia.

Storage volume

  • Assume 40 billion objects stored, averaging 1 MB each (photos dominate). That is 40 PB of logical data.
  • With 3× replication the raw disk needed is 120 PB. With erasure coding at a 1.5× overhead it is only 60 PB — a 60 PB saving, which at scale is tens of millions of dollars of disk. This single line is why the rest of the lesson cares about erasure coding.
  • A modern storage node packs maybe 20 disks × 20 TB = 400 TB. At 60 PB you need roughly 150 storage nodes in this region, plus headroom — call it 200.

Metadata volume

  • Each object needs a metadata row: bucket, key, version, size, checksum, and the list of fragment locations — call it ~1 KB.
  • 40 billion objects × 1 KB ≈ 40 TB of metadata. That fits comfortably in a sharded database; it does not fit on one machine, which is why the metadata service is itself distributed. Crucially, 40 TB of metadata is four orders of magnitude smaller than 40 PB of data — they have wildly different access patterns and belong in different systems.

Throughput & bandwidth

  • Say 2 billion reads/day and 200 million writes/day (the assumed ~10:1 read:write). 2e9 / 86,400 ≈ 23,000 reads/sec average, and you must provision for a peak of maybe 3× that — ~70k reads/sec.
  • At 1 MB average, 23k reads/sec is ~23 GB/sec of egress sustained. This is the number that makes you put a CDN in front for hot objects, so the storage fleet only sees the long-tail misses.
  • Writes at ~2,300/sec are far gentler — but each write fans out to k+m fragment writes across nodes, so the internal write IOPS is a multiple of the external rate.
Why the estimate matters

Two numbers drive the architecture. First, data is 1,000× larger than metadata — so they get separate, independently scaled systems (the split in step 04). Second, 3× replication doubles your disk bill versus erasure coding — so EC is the default (step 05). You can derive nearly the whole design from these two ratios.

03 The contract

API & data model

Before any boxes, pin down the interface — it disciplines everything downstream. An object store exposes a small REST-ish API. Keep it deliberately narrow: whole-object operations only.

The API contract

# Create a bucket (one-time, per namespace)
PUT  /buckets/{bucket}

# Store an object (whole-object write, idempotent on key+version)
PUT  /{bucket}/{key}
     Headers: Content-Length, Content-Type, x-amz-meta-*
     Body:    <raw bytes>
     → 200 OK  { etag, versionId }

# Retrieve an object (optionally a byte range)
GET  /{bucket}/{key}?versionId=...
     Headers: Range: bytes=0-1048575   # optional
     → 200 OK  <raw bytes> + ETag, Content-Length

# Delete (with versioning on, writes a tombstone marker)
DELETE /{bucket}/{key}

# List keys, paginated, prefix-filtered
GET  /{bucket}?prefix=2024/feb/&max-keys=1000&continuation-token=...

# Multipart upload for large objects
POST   /{bucket}/{key}?uploads              # → uploadId
PUT    /{bucket}/{key}?partNumber=N&uploadId=...
POST   /{bucket}/{key}?uploadId=...         # complete: assemble parts

The data model: two stores, on purpose

The model already foreshadows the architecture. Metadata lives in a structured, queryable store; data lives as opaque fragments on disk. The metadata row is the index that ties a key to where its bytes actually are.

-- METADATA STORE (sharded SQL or wide-column)
objects (
  bucket          text,
  key             text,
  version_id      bigint,        -- monotonic per key
  is_latest       bool,
  is_delete_marker bool,         -- tombstone for versioned delete
  size            bigint,
  content_type    text,
  checksum        text,          -- e.g. SHA-256 of the whole object
  ec_scheme       text,          -- "RS(6,3)"  → k=6 data, m=3 parity
  fragment_locs   json,          -- [ {node, disk, frag_id}, ... ]
  created_at      timestamp,
  PRIMARY KEY ((bucket, key), version_id)
)

-- DATA LAYER (not a table — bytes on storage nodes)
-- each fragment is a file: /disk7/<obj_uuid>/frag_3.dat

Note the primary key is (bucket, key) as the partition key with version_id as the clustering key — so all versions of one key live together and sort newest-first. The fragment_locs column is the heart of the read path: it is the map from "this key" to "these nine fragments on these nine nodes."

The follow-up to expect

"Why store fragment_locs in the metadata DB instead of computing locations from the key with consistent hashing, like a key-value store does?" Good answer: because fragments move — during rebuild and rebalancing a fragment is recreated on a different node, and a pure hash would point at the wrong place. An explicit location map lets you relocate fragments without rewriting the key's identity. (Consistent hashing is still useful for the initial placement decision.)

04 Architecture

The metadata/data split

Here is the central structural decision, and the estimate in step 02 already made the case: data is ~1,000× larger than metadata and has a completely different access pattern, so the two get separate services that scale independently.

The three planes

  • The API / service layer is a fleet of stateless front-end servers behind a load balancer. They authenticate the caller, validate the request, and orchestrate the read/write across the two layers below. Because they hold no state, you scale them by adding boxes.
  • The metadata service maps bucket + key + version → object attributes + fragment locations. It is a sharded, replicated database (the index over your data). It is small in bytes but hot in QPS — every single request hits it at least once.
  • The data layer is the storage-node fleet that holds the actual bytes as erasure-coded fragments on local disks. It is huge in bytes, written once, read many times, and never asked a rich query — it only ever answers "give me fragment X of object Y."

A placement service (sometimes folded into the API layer) decides which nodes a new object's fragments go to, balancing free space and spreading fragments across failure domains. Background controllers handle scrubbing, repair, and rebalancing — covered in step 09.

Why not store the bytes in the metadata database?

Because a database optimized for fast indexed lookups of 1 KB rows is a terrible, expensive place to keep 1 MB blobs. You would blow up the row size, wreck the buffer-pool cache hit rate, and pay database-grade hardware prices for what is really just sequential bytes on a disk. Splitting them lets each side use the right hardware: SSD-backed, replicated databases for the index; dense, cheap spinning disks for the blobs. The split also lets you scale the read-heavy data plane and the QPS-heavy metadata plane on different curves.

The follow-up to expect

"Isn't the metadata service now a single point of failure and a bottleneck — every request hits it?" Yes, and that is why it is itself sharded and replicated (step 10). The reassuring framing: the metadata layer is a classic distributed database problem you already know how to solve (shard by bucket+key, replicate each shard); the genuinely novel part of object storage is the durability of the data layer.

05 The durability decision

Durability: replicas vs erasure coding

This is the deep-dive the whole lesson points at. Disks fail constantly — at a 200-node fleet with thousands of disks, something is broken essentially all the time. To promise eleven nines, you must store enough redundancy that the loss of several disks (or a whole node, or a whole rack) never loses an object. There are two ways to buy that redundancy.

Option A — Replication

Replication keeps N full copies of every object on N different nodes (the classic choice is N = 3, spread across racks). It is conceptually trivial: to read, fetch any one copy; to survive a failure, you still have N−1. It is also fast — a read is a single whole-object fetch with no computation. The cost is brutal: 3× the raw storage, which at 40 PB logical means 120 PB of disk.

Option B — Erasure coding

Erasure coding (EC) trades CPU for disk. You split an object into k data fragments and compute m parity fragments from them using Reed–Solomon math, giving k + m total fragments spread across k + m nodes. The defining property: you can reconstruct the entire object from any k of the k + m fragments — so you can lose any m fragments and still recover. With a common scheme like RS(6,3) — 6 data, 3 parity — the storage overhead is just 9/6 = 1.5× while tolerating 3 simultaneous failures, the same fault tolerance as 3× replication at half the disk cost.

Under the hood — how the parity actually works

Treat the k data fragments as numbers and multiply them by a carefully chosen matrix to produce the m parity fragments. The matrix (a Vandermonde or Cauchy matrix over a Galois field) is built so that any k of the k+m output rows form an invertible sub-matrix. To rebuild a lost fragment you take any k surviving fragments, invert the corresponding sub-matrix, and multiply — recovering exactly the missing piece. The cost is the matrix multiply on every read-with-loss and every rebuild, which is why EC is "CPU for disk."

Replication (3×)

  • Dead simple to reason about and operate
  • Fastest reads — one whole copy, zero math
  • Cheap, fast rebuild: just copy a surviving replica
  • 3× storage overhead — the dealbreaker at scale
  • Great for small, hot, latency-sensitive objects

Erasure coding RS(6,3)

  • Only ~1.5× overhead — roughly half the disk bill
  • Same or better fault tolerance (survives 3 losses)
  • Costs CPU to encode on write and decode on loss
  • Rebuild is expensive: read k fragments to reconstruct 1
  • Higher read latency on a degraded read (must gather k)
  • Best for large, warm/cold objects — the bulk of the bytes

The pragmatic real-world answer is both, tiered: replicate tiny and very hot objects (where the math overhead and the multi-node fan-out hurt latency more than the disk saving helps), and erasure-code everything large and warm/cold (where the disk saving dominates). For the interview, lead with EC as the default — the whole reason object storage is cheap — and mention replication as the tier for small/hot.

The follow-up to expect

"How do you actually reach eleven nines?" You stack independent layers:
(1) EC that survives m simultaneous losses;
(2) spreading the k+m fragments across separate failure domains — different disks, nodes, racks, and ideally availability zones — so one rack losing power can't take more than one fragment;
(3) continuous background scrubbing that detects bit-rot and missing fragments early; and
(4) fast automated rebuild so the window in which you are one failure from data loss is minutes, not days.
Durability is a function of redundancy and how quickly you restore it after a failure.

06 The write path

The write path

Now wire the pieces into an end-to-end PUT. The ordering here is not arbitrary — it is chosen so the system is never in a state where metadata claims an object exists that the data layer can't actually serve. The rule: commit the bytes durably first, commit the metadata last.

  1. Receive & buffer. An API server accepts the PUT, authenticates it, and streams the bytes in, computing a checksum as it goes.
  2. Allocate placement. The placement service picks k+m storage nodes in distinct failure domains with free space.
  3. Encode. The object is split into k data fragments and m Reed–Solomon parity fragments computed.
  4. Distribute & fsync. All k+m fragments are written to their nodes and flushed to disk. The write is acknowledged durable only once a write quorum of fragments is safely on disk.
  5. Commit metadata. Only now does the API server write the objects row with the fragment locations and mark it is_latest. This commit is the atomic moment the object "exists."
  6. Ack. Return 200 OK with the etag and versionId.

Why metadata-last is non-negotiable

Imagine the reverse order — commit metadata, then write fragments. If the API server crashes between the two, the metadata now points at fragments that were never written. A later GET would find the row, try to fetch fragments, and fail — a phantom object that claims to exist but can't be served. By committing metadata last, the worst failure is the opposite and benign: fragments written but no metadata row, so the object simply "never happened" from the client's view (the PUT returns an error or times out, and the client retries). Those orphaned fragments are harmless garbage that a background garbage collector reaps later by finding fragments with no referencing metadata.

The follow-up to expect

"What if a couple of fragment writes fail mid-PUT?" You require a write quorum, not all k+m. For RS(6,3) you might ack once 7 of 9 fragments are durable, then repair the missing 2 asynchronously from the survivors. This keeps write latency bounded by the fastest 7 nodes (hedging against a slow disk) while still guaranteeing enough redundancy to rebuild. The knob to name: how many fragments must be durable before ack — it directly trades write latency against the redundancy you start with.

07 The read path

The read path & consistency

A GET is the mirror image, and most reads are cheap because nothing is missing.

  1. Metadata lookup. The API server reads the objects row for (bucket, key) — the latest version unless a versionId is specified — getting the EC scheme and the fragment location list.
  2. Fetch fragments. It requests fragments from the storage nodes. In the happy path it pulls the k data fragments directly, concatenates them, verifies the checksum, and streams the object back — no decode math needed, since data fragments are just the original bytes split up.
  3. Degraded read. If one of the k data fragments is missing or its node is slow, the server fetches a parity fragment instead and runs the Reed–Solomon decode to reconstruct the missing piece on the fly. Slower, but transparent to the client.

The consistency model

We promised read-after-write consistency for new objects: a GET right after a successful PUT returns the new bytes. The metadata-last write order gives us this almost for free — the object "exists" exactly when its metadata row commits, so any read that sees the committed row also sees a complete, durable set of fragments. The subtlety is the metadata layer's own replication: if the read can hit a stale metadata replica that hasn't yet seen the new row, you'd get a miss. The fix is to route the read of a freshly written key to the metadata shard leader (or read at a quorum), guaranteeing it sees the latest commit.

Overwrites and deletes are typically only eventually consistent — after you overwrite a key, a reader might briefly see the old version while caches and metadata replicas converge. That asymmetry (strong for new, eventual for updates) is a deliberate, well-understood trade-off: it keeps the hot read path fast and cacheable, and immutable-object workloads rarely overwrite the same key in a tight loop.

Where the CDN fits

From the step-02 estimate, the storage fleet would face ~23 GB/sec of egress if every read hit it. In practice you front the read path with a CDN (and an internal cache tier): hot objects — a viral Shopee listing photo, an Agoda hotel hero image — are served from edge caches keyed by the object's URL + etag, and only cache misses and cold objects reach the storage nodes. Because objects are immutable per version, they cache beautifully: a versioned URL never goes stale.

The follow-up to expect

"Why is a degraded read slower, and how do you hide that tail latency?" A normal read fetches k fragments; a degraded read must fetch k fragments plus do a Galois-field decode, and may have to wait on a slow node. To hide it, the API server uses hedged reads — fire requests to a few extra fragments and use whichever k return first — so one slow disk doesn't stall the whole GET. Name that technique and you've answered a question many candidates miss.

08 Object lifecycle

Versioning & multipart upload

Versioning & immutability

Versioning means that overwriting or deleting a key never destroys data — it adds a new entry. Because objects are immutable, this is natural: each PUT to an existing key allocates a fresh version_id, writes a brand-new set of fragments, and flips is_latest from the old row to the new one. The old version's fragments stay on disk, reachable via GET ?versionId=.... A versioned DELETE doesn't erase anything either — it writes a delete marker (a tombstone row with is_delete_marker = true as the new latest), so a plain GET returns 404 while the prior versions remain recoverable.

This is exactly what makes object storage trustworthy for regulated data. If GrabPay accidentally overwrites a customer statement, or a buggy job deletes a thousand objects, versioning means nothing was actually lost — you flip is_latest back. The cost is storage growth, which you tame with lifecycle policies: "expire noncurrent versions after 90 days," "transition objects to a colder, cheaper tier after 30 days." A background job sweeps expired versions and frees their fragments.

Multipart upload for large objects

A single PUT of a 5 GB hotel-tour video over one HTTP connection is fragile — one network blip and you restart the whole 5 GB. Multipart upload fixes this by letting the client split a large object into independent parts (say 100 MB each), upload them in parallel, and retry just the failed parts. The flow:

POST /{bucket}/big-video.mp4?uploads        # server returns uploadId
PUT  ...?partNumber=1&uploadId=X    → ETag e1   # parts upload
PUT  ...?partNumber=2&uploadId=X    → ETag e2   # in parallel,
PUT  ...?partNumber=3&uploadId=X    → ETag e3   # retried per-part
POST ...?uploadId=X  body=[{1,e1},{2,e2},{3,e3}]  # complete

Each part is stored (and erasure-coded) independently as it lands. The final complete call hands the server the ordered list of part ETags; the server validates them and writes a single objects row whose fragment_locs stitches the parts into one logical object — without ever copying the bytes again. Parts of an abandoned upload (no complete within N days) are reaped by the same garbage collector that handles orphaned fragments.

The follow-up to expect

"How do you make a multipart upload's complete step atomic — what if it half-finishes?" The metadata commit of the final stitched object is a single transaction: until it commits, the object doesn't exist (the parts are just staged, addressable only via the uploadId). It mirrors the metadata-last rule from the write path — the object becomes visible at exactly one atomic moment, and a crash before that moment leaves only reapable staged parts.

09 Failure & recovery

Node death & rebuild

The promise of eleven nines is empty unless the system actively repairs itself, because in a large fleet a disk or node is always dying. Three background processes keep durability real, running continuously and quietly.

Scrubbing — catch rot before it bites

Scrubbing is a background controller that walks every fragment on disk, recomputes its checksum, and compares it to the stored value. Disks suffer bit rot — bits silently flip over time — and a corrupted fragment you don't notice is a fragment that won't help you when you need it for a rebuild. The scrubber flags any mismatch and triggers a repair of that single fragment from the survivors, so corruption is caught and fixed proactively rather than discovered at the worst possible moment.

Rebuild — restore the lost redundancy

When a node dies (or a disk fails, or the heartbeat to it is lost for too long), every object that had a fragment on that node is now down to k+m−1 fragments — one closer to data loss. The repair controller finds all affected objects from the metadata (which records fragment locations), and for each one: reads any k surviving fragments, runs the Reed–Solomon decode to reconstruct the missing fragment, writes the rebuilt fragment to a fresh node in a different failure domain, and updates fragment_locs in the metadata. Redundancy is restored, and the object is back to full m-failure tolerance.

Rebalancing — keep the fleet level

When you add new storage nodes (you always are — the fleet grows monthly), they start empty while the old nodes are nearly full. Rebalancing gradually migrates fragments from hot/full nodes onto the new capacity, updating fragment_locs as it goes. This is also what handles permanent node decommissioning. It must run as a low-priority background trickle so it never competes with live read/write traffic for disk I/O.

Under the hood — the rebuild-speed / durability link

Durability is exquisitely sensitive to rebuild speed. After a node dies you're one-failure-closer to losing data until the rebuild finishes; the longer that window, the higher the chance a second (then third) failure lands on the same objects. So real systems parallelize aggressively — the affected objects are spread across thousands of nodes, so thousands of nodes each rebuild a tiny slice simultaneously, turning a multi-day single-stream copy into a minutes-long fleet-wide effort. The EC parameters (k, m) are tuned together with expected rebuild time to hit the eleven-nines target.

The follow-up to expect

"What happens when a storage node dies?" Walk it: heartbeat lost → controller marks the node down → it enumerates affected objects from metadata → for each, reads k survivors, decodes the missing fragment, writes it to a fresh node in a clean failure domain, updates the location map. Then the killer line: "reads keep working the entire time via degraded reads, and durability is fully restored once rebuild completes — which we keep to minutes by parallelizing across the fleet."

10 Scale & limits

Scaling & what breaks at 10x

The data layer scales almost embarrassingly well: bytes are independent, so you grow capacity by racking more storage nodes and letting rebalancing fill them. The pressure shows up elsewhere.

Scaling the metadata service

The metadata service is the part that strains first, because every request touches it. You shard it — partition the objects table by a hash of bucket + key across many shards, each replicated for its own durability and read scaling. This is a familiar distributed-database problem (DynamoDB, Cassandra, sharded PostgreSQL/Spanner-style systems all fit). The interesting failure is listing: LIST bucket?prefix=... wants keys in sorted order, but hash-sharding scatters adjacent keys across shards. The usual answer is a secondary, range-partitioned index sorted by key, or accepting that list is a scatter-gather across shards with a merge.

What breaks at 10x

  • Metadata hot shards. 10× the objects, but one mega-bucket (every Shopee seller dumping into products/) hashes unevenly and overloads a shard. Fix: salt the partition key, or split the bucket's keyspace; watch for and split hot shards automatically.
  • Small-object overhead. EC shines on big objects; a flood of 4 KB thumbnails means each one fans out into 9 tiny fragments — metadata and per-fragment overhead can exceed the data itself. Fix: pack many small objects into larger storage blobs and store an offset+length in metadata, so a 4 KB object is a slice of a packed 256 MB segment, not 9 lonely fragments.
  • Cross-region durability & latency. At 10× you serve multiple regions. Eleven nines within one region isn't enough if a whole region (a flood, a fiber cut) can vanish. Fix: cross-region replication of buckets that need it — asynchronous, eventually consistent copies in a second region.
  • Rebuild storms. 10× the fleet means 10× the absolute rate of disk failures; a correlated event (a bad firmware rollout, a rack power loss) can trigger many simultaneous rebuilds that saturate the network. Fix: rate-limit and prioritize rebuilds (objects at k+m−2 fragments first), and spread fragments across failure domains so correlated losses don't stack on the same objects.
  • Egress bandwidth. 10× reads = ~230 GB/sec if uncached. The CDN tier absorbs the hot set; the storage fleet is sized for the long-tail miss rate, not the raw read rate.
The follow-up to expect

"Where's your single point of failure?" There shouldn't be one: the API layer is stateless behind a load balancer, the metadata service is sharded and each shard replicated, and the data layer survives m losses per object with active rebuild. The honest weak point to name is the placement/controller plane — if it goes down, live reads and writes continue, but rebuild and rebalancing pause, slowly eroding durability. So you run it as a small replicated, leader-elected cluster too. Naming your own weak point and how you contain it is exactly the senior signal interviewers look for.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole design compresses to one sentence: split the small-but-hot metadata from the huge-but-cold data, store the data as erasure-coded fragments spread across failure domains, commit metadata last, and let background scrubbing and rebuild restore redundancy fast enough to hit eleven nines on cheap disks. If you can draw the metadata/data split, explain RS(k,m), and walk the write path and a node-death rebuild, you can carry this interview.
