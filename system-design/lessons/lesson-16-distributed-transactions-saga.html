Distributed Transactions & the Saga Pattern

Once each service owns its own database, a single business action — place an order, charge a card, reserve stock — spans several of them, and no single ACID transaction wraps the whole thing. The saga pattern keeps the world consistent with a chain of local transactions plus explicit compensations. It is the correctness backbone of every payment and order flow.

00 The problem

One business action, many databases

Back in lesson 09 we split the monolith into services and gave each its own database — database-per-service, the rule that no service reads or writes another service's tables directly. That isolation is exactly what lets teams ship independently. It also quietly demolishes the single most useful tool a backend engineer has: the local ACID transaction.

Inside one database, you can wrap several writes in a BEGIN ... COMMIT and get all-or-nothing semantics for free. Either every row lands or none do, and the database guarantees it. But a real business action almost never lives in one database. Consider a GrabFood order in Singapore:

  • Order Service creates the order row (its own Postgres).
  • Inventory Service reserves the merchant's stock (its own database).
  • Payment Service charges the customer's card via GrabPay (its own ledger).
  • Delivery Service assigns a rider.

Four services, four databases, one user intent: "place my order." There is no BEGIN that spans all four. If the card charge fails after stock was reserved, you have just sold inventory you will never get paid for — and the customer's app still shows "order failed." Multiply that by the millions of orders per day a super-app processes, and a fraction of a percent of broken flows becomes thousands of stuck orders, leaked stock, and angry support tickets every hour.

This is the distributed transaction problem: how do you keep state consistent across multiple independent databases when no single transaction manager owns them all? The rest of this lesson is the answer the industry converged on.

Why this is hard, precisely

ACID's atomicity relies on a single point that can hold locks, write a redo/undo log, and decide the outcome. The moment two databases are involved, each has its own log and its own lock manager, and they don't coordinate. You either invent a protocol to make them agree (the next section) or you give up on global atomicity and design for it not existing (the section after that).

01 The tempting answer

Why two-phase commit is usually the wrong tool

The textbook way to make several databases agree is two-phase commit (2PC). A transaction coordinator drives every participant through two phases so they reach the same all-or-nothing decision. Understanding exactly how it works is what lets you explain why it falls over at scale.

How 2PC actually works

  • Phase 1 — prepare. The coordinator sends PREPARE to every participant. Each one does the work, writes it to its log, takes the necessary locks, and replies YES ("I can commit and I promise to hold the line") or NO. Crucially, a participant that voted YES may not release its locks — it is now in an uncertain, prepared state, waiting for the verdict.
  • Phase 2 — commit or abort. If every participant voted YES, the coordinator writes a commit record and sends COMMIT to all; they finalize and release locks. If even one voted NO (or timed out), it sends ABORT and everyone rolls back.

This genuinely gives atomicity across databases. The XA standard, and middleware like a JTA transaction manager, implement it. So why do almost all high-scale product teams avoid it?

The blocking coordinator

The fatal flaw is the window between the two phases. Suppose every participant has voted YES and is holding locks, and then the coordinator crashes before sending the verdict. The participants cannot decide on their own: committing risks violating atomicity if another participant aborted, and aborting risks the same if the coordinator had already decided to commit. So they block — locks held, rows frozen — until the coordinator recovers. This is the well-known blocking problem of 2PC, and "what happens if the coordinator dies between phases?" is a classic interview trap.

Why it does not scale for product flows

Even when nothing crashes, 2PC has three costs that compound under load:

  • Locks span the whole protocol. A row in the inventory database is locked from PREPARE until COMMIT — multiple network round trips. At thousands of orders per second on a hot SKU during a Shopee 11.11 sale, those locks serialize everyone fighting over the same item, and throughput collapses.
  • The slowest participant sets the pace. The transaction can't commit until all participants reply. One slow or far-away service stalls every other participant's locks with it.
  • Availability is multiplicative. The whole transaction succeeds only if every participant and the coordinator are up. Five components at 99.9% each give roughly 99.5% combined — worse than any single piece. Adding services makes you less reliable.

2PC also assumes participants support the prepared state. Many systems you'd want to call — a third-party payment gateway, an email provider, a partner API — simply offer no XA endpoint. You cannot two-phase-commit an HTTP call to Stripe or a card network.

The follow-up to nail

When the interviewer asks "why not just use 2PC / distributed transactions?", the crisp answer is: "It's blocking — a coordinator crash between phases freezes locks across services — and it holds locks for the full multi-round-trip protocol, which destroys throughput and makes availability the product of every participant's. Real payment gateways often can't even participate. So at scale we trade atomicity for an eventually-consistent saga we can reason about."

02 The core idea

The saga: a chain of local transactions

If we can't have one big distributed transaction, we break the work into pieces each service can do atomically on its own. A saga is a sequence of local transactions, one per service, where each step commits independently in its own database and then triggers the next step. There is no global lock and no global commit. The saga as a whole is the unit of business correctness — even though no single database knows about the whole thing.

How a saga runs, step by step

Take the GrabFood order again, modeled as a saga:

  1. T1 — Order Service commits a row: order #88421, status = PENDING. Done and durable in its own DB.
  2. T2 — Inventory Service commits: reserve 1× nasi lemak from merchant stock. Done in its DB.
  3. T3 — Payment Service commits: charge S$8.50 to the customer's GrabPay wallet. Done in its ledger.
  4. T4 — Order Service commits: flip the order to status = CONFIRMED and notify Delivery.

Each Tn is an ordinary ACID transaction in one database — fast, lock-only-locally, releases immediately on commit. Between steps, the system is temporarily inconsistent: after T2 but before T3, stock is reserved but no money has moved. That is the deliberate trade. We accept brief inconsistency in exchange for never holding cross-service locks.

Why this is faster, concretely

Put numbers on it. Under 2PC, the inventory row stays locked for the entire protocol — prepare round-trip, the coordinator's wait for the slowest participant, then the commit round-trip — easily 50–200 ms on a hot SKU, and every other order for that item queues behind the lock. Under a saga, the inventory local transaction commits in single-digit milliseconds and releases its lock immediately; the reservation is then just an ordinary committed row, not a held lock. A thousand concurrent orders for the same flash-sale item serialize for a few milliseconds each instead of contending on a lock held across the network for the whole flow. That is the entire reason sagas exist: they convert a long-held distributed lock into a series of short local ones plus some bookkeeping.

The idea is old — it comes from a 1987 paper by Garcia-Molina and Salem on "long-lived transactions," whose insight was that a long transaction can be modeled as a sequence of sub-transactions each with a compensator, so you never have to hold locks for the lifetime of the whole thing. Microservices simply rediscovered it because database-per-service forces the same shape.

The hard question is obvious: what if T3 fails? The card is declined, the customer's GrabPay balance is short. We've already committed T1 and T2. There is no rollback — those transactions are done and durable, and other readers may already have seen their results. We can only move forward by undoing their effects. That mechanism is the compensating transaction, and it's the heart of the pattern.

The follow-up to nail

"What does a saga give up versus a real transaction?" Be precise: "Isolation. A saga keeps atomicity (through compensation), consistency (eventually), and durability (each local commit), but between steps the intermediate state is visible to other transactions — there's no global lock hiding it. So a saga is ACD, not ACID, and I design the rest of the system to tolerate reading in-flight state." We return to that missing I in the final section.

03 Undoing the done

Compensating transactions

A compensating transaction is a new local transaction that semantically undoes the effect of a step that already committed. Notice the word semantically: you are not rolling back to a prior database state (impossible — the transaction committed and others may have read the result). You are issuing a forward action that cancels the business effect.

For each forward step Tn that has visible side effects, you define a compensation Cn:

  • C1 for "create order" → mark the order CANCELLED (you don't delete the row; you keep the audit trail).
  • C2 for "reserve stock" → release the reservation, returning the nasi lemak to available inventory.
  • C3 for "charge wallet" → refund the S$8.50 to GrabPay. Note a refund is not a deletion of the charge — both the charge and the refund stay on the ledger forever.

When step Tk fails, the saga runs the compensations for all already-committed steps in reverse order: Ck-1, Ck-2, ..., C1. Reverse order matters because later steps may depend on earlier ones — you release the stock reservation before you cancel the order it belonged to.

Compensations must be designed, not assumed

This is where engineers underestimate the work. A compensation is real product logic with real edge cases:

  • Some actions can't be cleanly undone. You can't un-send an email or un-dispatch a rider who's already moving. The fix is to make such steps retriable and place them last in the saga (after everything that might fail), or to compensate with a follow-up action ("send a cancellation email", "recall the rider and pay a cancellation fee").
  • Compensations need their own idempotency. A refund that runs twice must not refund twice. (We'll formalize this in step 07.)
  • Compensations can be observed. Because there are no global locks, a customer might briefly see "stock reserved" or even a pending charge before the refund lands. Your UI and notifications have to tolerate that intermediate truth.
Pivot transactions, under the hood

A useful refinement: classify each step. A compensatable step can be undone (reserve stock). A retriable step is guaranteed to eventually succeed once reached (confirm order, send receipt). The pivot is the point of no return — usually the irreversible commit (the actual card capture). Order the saga so all compensatable steps come before the pivot and all retriable steps come after. Then failure is always either "undo everything before the pivot" or "retry forward past it" — never a messy half-and-half.

The follow-up to nail

"What's your compensation for a failed payment step?" Answer concretely: "Payment is my pivot. Everything before it — order created, stock reserved — is compensatable, so on a card decline I run C2 to release the reservation and C1 to cancel the order, in reverse order, each idempotent. The order ends in a terminal FAILED state the customer can see. If the charge had instead already captured, payment is past the pivot, so I roll forward with retries, not back."

04 Who drives the saga

Choreography vs orchestration

A saga still needs something to decide which step runs next and to trigger compensations on failure. There are two ways to wire that control flow, and choosing between them is a core design decision.

Choreography — services react to events

In choreography, there is no central brain. Each service does its local transaction, then publishes an event; other services subscribe and react. Trace it as a chain of events on the bus:

  • Order Service commits the order, publishes OrderCreated.
  • Inventory Service is subscribed to OrderCreated; it reserves stock, publishes StockReserved.
  • Payment Service is subscribed to StockReserved; it charges, publishes PaymentCharged.
  • Order Service is subscribed to PaymentCharged; it flips the order to CONFIRMED.

Failures flow backward through the same mechanism. If the charge declines, Payment publishes PaymentFailed instead; Inventory is subscribed to that too and reacts by releasing the reservation, then publishes StockReleased, which Order hears and uses to mark the order FAILED. Notice the hidden cost already creeping in: Inventory now has to subscribe to a payment failure event that has nothing to do with inventory's own job, and every service must know which downstream event signals "undo what you did." The events typically travel over Kafka or another durable broker, which is also what makes the whole thing reliable — events are persisted, so a consumer that's down catches up when it recovers.

Orchestration — a central coordinator drives

In orchestration, a dedicated saga orchestrator owns the workflow. It sends a command to each service in turn (ReserveStock, then on success ChargePayment, etc.), waits for the reply, and decides the next move — including issuing compensation commands on failure. The orchestrator is itself a service, usually backed by a state machine persisted in a database so it survives restarts. Tools like Temporal, Netflix Conductor, AWS Step Functions, and Camunda/Zeebe are orchestrators you can buy instead of build.

Choreography

  • + No single point of control; services stay loosely coupled and independently deployable.
  • + Simple for short sagas (2–3 steps) — just publish and subscribe.
  • − Control flow is implicit, scattered across services and event subscriptions. No one place tells you what the saga does.
  • − Hard to trace, test, and debug; cyclic event dependencies creep in as it grows.
  • − Each service must know about compensation events that aren't really its concern.

Orchestration

  • + Workflow logic lives in one explicit place — easy to read, monitor, and reason about.
  • + Compensation logic is centralized; the orchestrator knows exactly what's been done.
  • + Far easier to add steps, visualize state, and test the whole flow.
  • − The orchestrator is a component you must build, scale, and keep highly available.
  • − Risk of a "god service" if business logic leaks into it; keep it to sequencing, not domain rules.

The practical rule: choreography for short, stable sagas; orchestration for anything with branching, more than ~4 steps, or where observability matters — which describes essentially every payment and order flow at a fintech or super-app. SeaMoney or GrabPay flows are firmly orchestration territory, because you need a single auditable answer to "where is order #88421 right now?"

05 Watch it run

An orchestrated saga, happy path then failure

Let's make the orchestration concrete with the GrabFood order. The orchestrator reserves inventory, then charges payment. We'll watch the happy path succeed — and then watch the same flow when the payment step fails, forcing the orchestrator to run the compensation (release the reservation) and mark the order failed. Step through it; pause and predict each next move before you advance.

The shape to remember: the orchestrator only ever talks to one service at a time, waits for the reply, persists the new saga state, and then decides the next command. Because that state is durable, a crash of the orchestrator just means it resumes from the last persisted step on restart — no participant is left holding a lock, unlike 2PC.

How the orchestrator survives a crash

Under the hood the orchestrator is a persisted state machine. After every reply it writes a row like saga=order#88421, state=AWAITING_PAYMENT, lastStep=ReserveStock:OK to its own database before sending the next command. If it dies mid-flow and a new instance takes over (or it restarts), recovery is simply: read the last persisted state, and re-drive from there. Because every command carries an idempotency key (step 07), re-sending ChargeWallet after a crash is safe — Payment either hasn't seen it yet and processes it once, or has seen it and returns the same stored result. Engines like Temporal take this further with event sourcing: they don't store just the current state but the full append-only history of events, then deterministically replay your workflow code against that history to reconstruct exactly where it was — which is why a Temporal workflow can survive a process crash without you writing any recovery code.

Contrast the failure story with 2PC one more time, because the interviewer will: when the 2PC coordinator crashed, participants sat blocked holding locks with no way to decide. When a saga orchestrator crashes, every participant has already committed and released its locks; the only thing lost is the orchestrator's place in the sequence, and that's recoverable from durable state. The saga trades 2PC's strong isolation for a system that never freezes.

06 Publishing reliably

The transactional outbox

A saga only works if step N reliably triggers step N+1. In both styles that hand-off is a message: an event published to Kafka, or a command the orchestrator records. Which surfaces a subtle, vicious bug — the dual-write problem.

The dual-write trap

Suppose Inventory Service does this naively:

// DANGER: two separate writes, no shared transaction
db.commit("UPDATE stock SET reserved = reserved + 1 ...");  // write #1: database
kafka.publish("StockReserved", orderId);                   // write #2: broker

These are two independent systems with no common transaction. Four things can go wrong, and two of them are catastrophic:

  • DB commit succeeds, then the process crashes before publishing → stock is reserved but no StockReserved event ever fires. The saga stalls forever; the order is stuck.
  • You reorder to publish first, then the DB commit fails → you announced a reservation that never happened. Payment charges for stock you don't hold.

You cannot make a database commit and a Kafka publish atomic. So you stop trying to, and route both through the one thing that is atomic: the local database transaction.

How the outbox works

The transactional outbox adds an outbox table to the service's own database. The trick: write the business state change and the event-to-be-published in the same local transaction.

BEGIN;
  UPDATE stock SET reserved = reserved + 1 WHERE sku = 'nasi-lemak';
  INSERT INTO outbox (id, aggregate, type, payload, published)
    VALUES ('evt-7c1', 'order#88421', 'StockReserved', '{...}', false);
COMMIT;   -- both rows land, or neither does — true atomicity, one DB

Now a separate message relay process reads unpublished rows from the outbox and publishes them to Kafka, marking each published = true (or deleting it) once the broker acknowledges. Two ways to run the relay:

  • Polling publisher. A background job repeatedly queries SELECT * FROM outbox WHERE published = false ORDER BY id LIMIT 100, publishes, marks done. Simple; adds a little latency and DB load.
  • Change Data Capture (CDC). A tool like Debezium tails the database's write-ahead log (Postgres WAL, MySQL binlog) and streams new outbox rows straight to Kafka. No polling, very low latency, no extra query load — the production-grade choice. Debezium + Kafka Connect is the canonical stack.

The relay guarantees at-least-once delivery: if it crashes after publishing but before marking the row done, it republishes on restart. That's fine — duplicates are expected, and step 07 makes the consumers idempotent so a duplicate is harmless.

Ordering, the gotcha people miss

One subtlety bites teams in production: events for the same saga must be processed in order. If StockReserved and a later StockReleased for order #88421 arrive out of order, a consumer could release a reservation it then re-applies, or vice versa. The fix is to give the outbox row an aggregate id (here order#88421) and use it as the Kafka partition key. Kafka guarantees ordering only within a partition, so keying by saga id keeps all of one saga's events on one partition, in order, while different sagas still spread across partitions for throughput. CDC tools like Debezium let you configure exactly this routing. Polling relays must also be careful to publish in id (or commit-timestamp) order, not whatever order the rows happen to come back.

A second gotcha: the relay and the broker can themselves fail, so don't delete outbox rows the instant you publish — mark them published and prune on a delay, or you risk a crash between publish and delete losing the row before the broker actually persisted it. The whole design is a careful chain of "commit locally, then forward, then confirm," with idempotency absorbing every duplicate the chain produces.

The follow-up to nail

"How do you publish the event reliably?" The expected answer is the outbox: "I never do a dual write. I commit the state change and an outbox row in the same local transaction, then a relay — Debezium tailing the WAL — publishes outbox rows to Kafka at least once. Duplicates are handled by idempotent consumers." Mentioning Debezium/CDC by name signals you've actually shipped this.

07 Surviving retries

Idempotency & exactly-once effects

Everything in a saga retries. The outbox delivers at-least-once. Networks time out and the orchestrator resends a command. A compensation re-runs after a crash. So every step and every compensation will, sooner or later, be invoked more than once with the same intent. If "charge S$8.50" runs twice, you've double-charged a customer — a fintech incident, not a curiosity.

True exactly-once delivery is impossible across an unreliable network. What you can build is exactly-once effects: the message may arrive many times, but the state changes only once. The tool is the idempotency key.

How an idempotency key works

The caller attaches a stable, unique key to the intent — not a fresh one per attempt, the same key on every retry. The classic choice is the saga/order id plus the step: idem_key = "order#88421:charge". The receiving service keeps a table of keys it has already processed:

BEGIN;
  -- has this exact intent been applied before?
  INSERT INTO processed_keys (key) VALUES ('order#88421:charge');
  -- if the row already exists, the unique constraint fails →
  -- we abort and return the SAME stored result, no second charge
  UPDATE wallet SET balance = balance - 850 WHERE user = 'u-44';
COMMIT;

Because the dedup insert and the effect commit in the same local transaction, a retry either finds the key already present (and is a no-op returning the cached outcome) or applies the effect exactly once. Stripe's API uses precisely this model with its Idempotency-Key header; you should expose the same on your internal payment endpoints.

Where to put the keys

  • Forward steps: key on sagaId + stepName.
  • Compensations: give them their own keys, e.g. order#88421:charge:compensate, so a refund can't run twice even if the orchestrator re-issues it.
  • Consumers of events: dedup on the event id from the outbox, so an at-least-once republish is absorbed.
Under the hood: cleanup & TTL

The processed_keys table grows forever if untended. Add a created_at and expire keys older than your maximum saga lifetime (say 7 days) with a periodic job or a database TTL. Pick the window longer than any legitimate retry could span, or you'll re-process a very late duplicate. This is the same trade-off as a dedup window in any stream processor (Flink, Kafka Streams).

08 The failure of last resort

When a compensation itself fails

Here is the question that separates a memorized answer from real understanding: what if a compensation fails? Payment was charged, the order can't be fulfilled, the orchestrator issues the refund — and the refund call times out, or the wallet service is down. You're now stuck mid-undo, with a charge that should be reversed but isn't.

Sagas guarantee only eventual consistency, and this is exactly the moment that promise is tested. There is no magic rollback. The system's job is to keep trying until it converges, and to make sure a human finds out if it can't. The layered strategy:

  • Retry with backoff. Compensations must be retriable and idempotent (step 07), so the orchestrator simply re-issues the refund command with exponential backoff and jitter. Most transient failures — a brief wallet-service blip — resolve here on attempt two or three.
  • Persist the pending compensation. Because the orchestrator's state is durable, "refund owed for order #88421" survives crashes and restarts. It is a standing obligation the system will not forget, not an in-memory hope.
  • Dead-letter queue. After a bounded number of retries, the failed compensation lands in a DLQ — a quarantine topic for messages that can't be processed. It's out of the hot path but not lost.
  • Alert and escalate. A DLQ message fires a monitoring alert (PagerDuty, an on-call page). The saga is now flagged as needing attention rather than silently broken.
  • Manual intervention. For the residue that automation can't fix — a permanently rejected refund, a regulatory edge case — an operator resolves it through an internal ops console. In fintech this human backstop is mandatory, and reconciliation jobs run nightly to catch any drift between, say, the order ledger and the wallet ledger.

The key mental model: a saga doesn't prevent inconsistency, it guarantees the system relentlessly drives back toward consistency and never quietly drops an obligation. "Eventually consistent" is a promise of convergence, backed by durable state, retries, and — at the very end — a human.

The follow-up to nail

"What if the compensation fails?" Don't claim it can't. Say: "Compensations are idempotent and retried with backoff against durable saga state, so transient failures self-heal. Persistent failures go to a dead-letter queue, fire an alert, and are resolved by ops; nightly reconciliation catches any ledger drift. The saga never drops the obligation — that's what 'eventually consistent' buys us."

09 Putting it together

The full payment saga, end to end

Assemble every piece into one coherent picture — the architecture you'd whiteboard for a "design GrabPay's order-payment flow" question. An API call kicks off the saga; the orchestrator drives commands; each service applies an idempotent local transaction and emits events via its outbox; Kafka carries them; and failures unwind through compensations.

A compact contract makes it concrete. The orchestrator exposes:

POST /sagas/order  →  starts an order saga
{
  "sagaId": "order#88421",        // also the idempotency root
  "steps": [
    { "do": "ReserveStock",   "undo": "ReleaseStock" },
    { "do": "ChargeWallet",   "undo": "RefundWallet", "pivot": true },
    { "do": "ConfirmOrder",   "undo": null }   // retriable, post-pivot
  ]
}

Each command and each event carries the sagaId, a per-step idempotencyKey, and an eventId for consumer dedup. The orchestrator persists its state after every transition, so it resumes cleanly after any crash.

What breaks at 10×

Push this from thousands to tens of thousands of orders per second — an 11.11 or a payday surge — and new pressure points appear:

  • Orchestrator throughput. One orchestrator instance becomes a bottleneck. Partition saga ownership by sagaId hash across a fleet (this is exactly what Temporal does internally), so each saga has a single owner but the fleet scales horizontally.
  • Outbox table hot spots. A high-write outbox can bloat and slow the source DB. Use CDC rather than polling, and prune published rows aggressively.
  • Long-running sagas tie up reservations. If payment is slow, stock sits reserved. Add a timeout on each step: if T3 doesn't complete within, say, 30 seconds, the orchestrator treats it as failed and compensates — releasing stock so it isn't stranded during a flash sale.
  • Observability. At this volume you cannot eyeball failures. Emit the saga state machine to a dashboard and trace every saga with its sagaId as the correlation id across all services.

This pattern is the correctness backbone you'll reuse directly in later lessons: reservation systems (lesson 38, holding seats or stock), payment flows (lesson 39), and the wallet/ledger design (lesson 40). Get the saga right here and those become assembly, not invention.

Deep-dive the interviewer may push for

Isolation, the missing ACID letter. Sagas give you Atomicity (via compensation), Consistency (eventually), and Durability (local commits) — but not Isolation. Between steps, other transactions can read the intermediate state (the "dirty read" of a reservation that may yet be released). Countermeasures: a semantic lock (a PENDING status flag that warns other operations the record is in-flight), commutative updates (so order doesn't matter), or reread-value checks before compensating. Naming this gap unprompted is a strong signal.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: when one business action spans many databases, give up on global ACID, run a chain of local transactions, and clean up failures with idempotent compensations driven by a durable coordinator. If you can sketch the saga, explain why not 2PC, draw the outbox, and answer "what if the compensation fails," you can carry the interview.
