34Lesson · System Design Interview

Design Google Maps

A maps product is really three systems sharing one app: rendering the world from precomputed tiles, routing over a road graph, and keeping ETAs honest with live traffic. We will spend our depth where interviewers actually push — tiles and routing — and treat search and ETA as the supporting cast.

About these notes: original study material on the maps / navigation design problem — a standard, widely-documented system-design topic. The explanations, numbers, examples, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Two diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow. Examples lean toward a Singapore super-app context (Grab) where it sharpens the point.
00 The problem

Three systems in a trench coat

When you open a maps app and type "Marina Bay Sands," three very different machines spring to life. The first draws the world: roads, parks, building footprints, labels — a seamless image you can pan and zoom. The second finds a path from where you are to where you want to be, over a network of millions of road segments. The third predicts time: how long that path will actually take given the traffic on the East Coast Parkway right now. Each one is a hard problem with its own bottleneck, and bolting them into one low-latency app is what makes "design Google Maps" a meaty interview.

The reason this question is loved is that it forces you to reason about scale that cannot be brute-forced. You cannot render the planet on demand for every pan. You cannot run a plain shortest-path search across a continent and answer in 100 ms. And you cannot recompute every route the instant a single car slows down. Every good answer is a story about precomputation — doing the expensive work ahead of time so the live request is cheap.

Keep that three-way split in your head for the whole interview. When the interviewer says "how do you make it faster?" the right reflex is to ask which of the three they mean — the honest answers are completely different.

01 Scope it

Clarify & scope the requirements

Before drawing a single box, narrow the problem out loud. "Maps" can mean a hundred things; the interviewer wants to see you pick the load-bearing subset and state your assumptions.

Functional requirements

  • Render the map. Given a viewport (a center point, a zoom level, and a screen size), show roads, water, labels, and points of interest, and let the user pan and zoom smoothly.
  • Search & geocode. Turn "Changi Airport" into coordinates (geocoding), and turn a tapped coordinate back into an address (reverse geocoding). The text side leans on the autocomplete/search lesson, so we will keep it shallow here.
  • Route. Given an origin and destination (and a travel mode — drive, walk, transit), return one or more paths.
  • ETA. For each route, return an estimated travel time that reflects current traffic, not just road length.

Out of scope (say so explicitly)

  • Street View imagery, satellite layers, indoor maps, and turn-by-turn voice are real but orthogonal; we set them aside.
  • The full search ranking stack — covered elsewhere.

Non-functional requirements

  • Low latency. A tile must arrive in tens of milliseconds; a route should come back in well under a second. People abandon a map that stutters.
  • High availability. A super-app like Grab routes drivers and prices rides off this; an outage stalls the marketplace.
  • Read-heavy. Tiles and routes are read billions of times; the underlying map data changes slowly (road edits land daily/weekly, traffic updates continuously but is small).
  • Freshness where it matters. Tiles can be hours or days stale and nobody notices; an ETA that ignores a jam is a real product failure.
  • Global, but regional. A user in Singapore almost never routes to Lagos in one query. Locality is a gift we will exploit hard.
Interview tip

The single most valuable framing to say early: "Map data is read-mostly and changes slowly, so I will precompute aggressively — tiles offline, routing structures offline — and keep the request path cheap. The one thing I cannot precompute is live traffic, so that gets its own streaming pipeline." That sentence organizes the entire rest of the answer.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope

Quick numbers anchor the design and reveal where the pressure is. Assume 1 billion monthly users and 100 million daily active users. These are round, defensible figures — the interviewer cares about your reasoning, not the decimal.

Tile traffic — the firehose

A single screen of map is not one image; it is a grid of tiles, typically 4 to 12 tiles per viewport. Panning and zooming fetch more. Say each active user triggers ~50 tile fetches per session and has 3 sessions a day:

  • 100M DAU × 3 sessions × 50 tiles ≈ 15 billion tile requests / day.
  • That averages ~175,000 tiles/sec, with peaks easily 3–5× that during morning and evening commutes.

No origin fleet serves a million tile reads per second affordably. This single number forces the headline decision: tiles must be served from a CDN, and the origin only ever sees cache misses.

Tile storage — the pyramid is huge

Tiles exist at every zoom level. Zoom 0 is one tile for the whole world; each level quadruples the count. Through ~zoom 20 the planet is on the order of hundreds of billions of tiles. Most are empty ocean and never stored, but populated land at high zoom is enormous. Vector tiles (geometry, not pixels) compress this dramatically — call it single-digit to low-double-digit petabytes in object storage. Cheap to store, expensive to serve hot, which again points at the CDN.

Routing traffic

Routing is far lighter than tiles — most map opens are "look, don't navigate." Say 100M DAU × 2 route queries/day ≈ 200M routes/day ≈ ~2,300 routes/sec average, peaks ~10k/sec. Small QPS, but each query is compute-heavy (a graph search), so this scales by CPU, not by bandwidth.

Live-traffic ingest

Grab alone has hundreds of thousands of drivers emitting GPS probes every few seconds. At, say, 500k drivers × 1 probe / 4s ≈ 125,000 probes/sec in one region. These are tiny messages but relentless — a streaming aggregation problem, not a request/response one.

What the numbers tell us

Three different scaling regimes fall straight out: tiles are bandwidth-bound (solve with a CDN), routing is CPU-bound (solve with precomputed graph structures + sharding), and traffic is throughput-bound (solve with a stream processor). One question, three answers — exactly the structure to present.

03 Contract

The API & a data-model sketch

Pin down the contracts; they make the rest concrete. Tiles are fetched by a simple addressing scheme; routing and geocoding are JSON over HTTPS.

Tile endpoint

Tiles are addressed by z/x/y — zoom, and the column/row of the tile within that zoom's grid. This is the universal convention (the "slippy map" addressing) and it is why tiles are so cache-friendly: every URL is immutable for a given map version.

# Raster or vector tile, served straight from the CDN
GET /tiles/v8/{z}/{x}/{y}.mvt        # vector (Mapbox Vector Tile)
GET /tiles/v8/{z}/{x}/{y}@2x.png     # raster, retina
# e.g. central Singapore at zoom 15:
GET /tiles/v8/15/25890/16361.mvt
Cache-Control: public, max-age=604800, immutable

Routing endpoint

POST /v1/route
{
  "origin":      { "lat": 1.3521, "lng": 103.8198 },
  "destination": { "lat": 1.2494, "lng": 103.8303 },
  "mode": "drive",
  "departure": "now",
  "alternatives": true
}
// → 200 OK
{
  "routes": [{
    "distance_m": 12840,
    "duration_s": 1080,          // ETA, traffic-adjusted
    "duration_typical_s": 900,   // free-flow baseline
    "geometry": "polyline-encoded-path",
    "steps": [ /* turn instructions */ ]
  }]
}

Geocoding endpoint

GET /v1/geocode?q=Changi+Airport&near=1.35,103.82
GET /v1/reverse?lat=1.2834&lng=103.8607

Data model — what lives where

Different data has different shapes and homes:

  • Tiles are blobs keyed by (version, z, x, y), living in object storage (S3/GCS) and fronted by the CDN. No relational database; they are files.
  • The road graph is the routing heart. Two core tables, then compiled into a binary in-memory structure:
node  ( node_id PK,  lat,  lng,  region_id )
edge  ( edge_id PK,  from_node,  to_node,
        length_m,  road_class,        // motorway, primary, residential…
        speed_limit,  oneway,  region_id,
        base_time_s )                  // free-flow traversal time
edge_speed ( edge_id,  bucket,  speed_kmh )  // live + historical, per time bucket

Here a node is an intersection (or any point where routing decisions can change) and an edge is a directed road segment between two nodes. A two-way street is two edges. The edge_speed table is the bridge to ETA: it holds the current and historical speed for each segment, which is what turns raw distance into time.

Going past the book

A sharp detail to drop: edges are directed, weights are time, not distance, and the graph is stored in a compact array form (adjacency in contiguous arrays, node ids as indices) so it fits in RAM and the CPU cache stays warm. "I'd weight edges by travel time and keep the whole regional graph in memory as packed arrays" signals you have thought about the real bottleneck: cache locality during search.

04 The blueprint

High-level architecture

Now wire the three subsystems together with their front-door infrastructure. The client talks to an API gateway; tile requests are answered almost entirely by the CDN; routing and geocoding hit dedicated services; and a separate streaming pipeline keeps edge weights fresh.

The end-to-end flows, traced once so the parts make sense:

  • Map open. The client computes which z/x/y tiles cover the viewport and requests them by URL. The CDN serves them from edge cache; only a cold tile falls through to the origin in object storage. The app tier is barely involved.
  • Route. The gateway forwards to the routing service, which loads the relevant region graph (already in RAM), runs a shortest-path search using current edge weights, and returns the path plus ETA.
  • Traffic. Driver apps emit GPS probes into a stream; an aggregator map-matches them to edges and updates the live speed for each segment, which the routing service reads on its next query.
05 Deep dive · rendering

Map tiles & the zoom pyramid

Bottleneck: the world is too big and too detailed to draw on demand. Rendering a styled map — anti-aliased roads, label placement, road casings — is expensive CPU work. Doing it per request, per user, for 175,000 requests a second, is hopeless. The fix is the foundational idea of web maps: precompute the map into tiles, once, offline.

How tiling works under the hood

A map tile is a small square chunk of the map, conventionally 256×256 pixels. The whole planet is sliced into a grid of these squares. The trick is doing it at many zoom levels, forming a tile pyramid (a quadtree). At zoom 0 the entire world is one tile. Zoom in one level and that tile is replaced by 4 tiles, each covering a quarter of the area at double the linear detail. Zoom again: 16 tiles. The pattern is 4^z tiles at level z — a quadtree where each tile has four children directly below it.

Addressing falls out naturally: a tile is (z, x, y), where x and y are the column and row in that level's grid. The client, knowing the user's center coordinate and zoom, does pure arithmetic to compute exactly which tiles fill the screen — typically a small rectangle of them — and requests only those. It never asks for the whole world; it asks for the 6 or 9 tiles it can see.

Raster vs vector tiles

There are two flavors of precomputed tile, and the trade-off is worth naming explicitly.

Raster tiles (PNG)

  • Pre-rendered images; the client just paints them — trivial work
  • Styling is baked in: a new map style means re-rendering the planet
  • Heavier bytes; need separate @2x sets for retina screens
  • No smooth rotation or runtime restyling

Vector tiles (MVT)

  • Ship geometry + attributes; the client renders with the GPU
  • Restyle, rotate, and zoom smoothly without new tiles
  • Much smaller, and one tile set serves all styles & resolutions
  • Cost: client needs a capable renderer (most modern devices qualify)

Modern maps overwhelmingly use vector tiles (the Mapbox Vector Tile / .mvt format is the de facto standard). They are smaller and far more flexible — the same tiles power day mode, night mode, and a satellite-hybrid label layer with no re-rendering.

Why precomputation wins, with numbers

Render-on-demand would cost a CPU-second of styling per tile and serve it once. Precompute-and-cache renders each tile once and serves it from RAM at the CDN edge millions of times. For central Singapore at zoom 15, one tile might be requested by tens of thousands of commuters a day; rendering it 30,000 times versus once is a 30,000× compute saving. That is the whole game.

The CDN tier and updates

Because every tile URL is immutable for a given map version, tiles are perfect CDN candidates. Set a long Cache-Control: max-age and let CloudFront / Cloudflare / Fastly cache them at the edge near the user. When a road changes in OpenStreetMap or an internal editor, you re-render only the affected tiles and bump the version in the URL path (/v8/ → /v9/) so clients fetch fresh ones without a global purge — versioning is cheaper and safer than cache invalidation at planet scale.

Failure mode

If the tile origin goes down, the CDN keeps serving everything it has cached — which, for popular areas, is nearly everything. Only requests for cold, rarely-viewed tiles fail, and even those can fall back to a lower zoom level (a blurrier but valid view) while the origin recovers. This graceful degradation is a direct consequence of immutable, cacheable tiles.

Follow-up the interviewer will ask

"How do you serve the map efficiently?" — answer in one breath: precompute vector tiles at every zoom level, address them by z/x/y, make the URLs immutable, and serve them from a CDN so the origin only ever sees cache misses. Then mention versioned re-rendering for updates, and you have covered the whole tile story.

06 Deep dive · model

The road network as a graph

Bottleneck: "find the best way to drive there" is meaningless until we turn the messy physical road network into something a computer can search. The model is a weighted directed graph.

Nodes, edges, and weights

The mapping is direct: every intersection becomes a node; every road segment between two intersections becomes a directed edge. Direction matters — a one-way street is a single edge; a normal street is two edges pointing opposite ways. The crucial modeling choice is the edge weight: not the segment's length in meters, but the time it takes to traverse it. Distance gives you the shortest route; time gives you the fastest route, which is what a person actually wants. A 2 km stretch of the Pan-Island Expressway at 80 km/h beats 1 km of stop-start traffic through Geylang.

That single decision — weight by time — is what makes the same graph the foundation for both routing and ETA. The ETA of a path is just the sum of its edge weights; live traffic is just a continuous rewrite of those weights.

Worked example

In FIG. 4, the fastest A→D path is not the direct-looking one. A→B→D costs 4 + 6 = 10. A→B→C→D costs 4 + 2 + 3 = 9. A→E→B→C→D costs 3 + 2 + 2 + 3 = 10. The winner is A→B→C→D at 9 minutes. A shortest-path algorithm finds this by systematically exploring cheaper-first, never trusting "looks closer."

Dijkstra: the baseline

Dijkstra's algorithm is the textbook answer. It explores outward from the origin in order of cumulative cost, always expanding the cheapest-so-far node next, until it reaches the goal. It is correct (it finds the true shortest path with non-negative weights) but uninformed: it fans out in all directions equally, like ripples in a pond. To route from Jurong to Changi it will happily explore halfway to Malaysia before the ripple reaches the airport. On a continent-scale graph with hundreds of millions of nodes, that is far too slow for an interactive request.

Why this matters

The bottleneck is now sharp: Dijkstra is correct but explores too much. The next two sections are both attacks on that single problem — A* steers the search toward the goal so it explores less, and contraction hierarchies precompute shortcuts so it barely explores at all. Everything that follows is "make shortest-path fast enough to feel instant."

07 Deep dive · routing

Routing with A* — steer toward the goal

Bottleneck: Dijkstra wastes effort exploring in directions away from the destination. A* search fixes this with a single idea: give the search a sense of direction by adding a heuristic — an optimistic estimate of the remaining distance to the goal.

How A* works under the hood

Dijkstra picks the next node to expand by lowest g — the actual cost from the origin so far. A* picks by lowest f = g + h, where h is a guess of the cost remaining to the goal. For road routing, a natural h is the straight-line (great-circle) distance to the destination divided by the top road speed — a time you definitely cannot beat, since nothing is faster than a straight line at full speed.

That biases expansion toward the goal. A node that is geographically behind the origin gets a large h, so its f is high and the search ignores it. A node pointing toward the destination gets a small h and is explored first. The pond ripple becomes a beam aimed at the target — far fewer nodes touched, same optimal answer.

The catch, and the property to name: the heuristic must be admissible — it must never overestimate the true remaining cost. Straight-line time is admissible because the real road is always at least as long and at most as fast. An admissible heuristic guarantees A* still returns the genuinely optimal path; an inadmissible one (too greedy) returns fast but possibly wrong routes.

Dijkstra

  • Explores uniformly in all directions
  • No heuristic needed — works on any graph
  • Touches far more nodes; slow on huge maps
  • Good for "nearest of many" (one-to-all) queries

A*

  • Steers expansion toward a single goal
  • Needs an admissible heuristic (straight-line time)
  • Touches far fewer nodes for point-to-point
  • Still too slow alone for continent-scale interactive routing

Failure mode

A* degrades gracefully: with a weak heuristic it simply explores more (toward Dijkstra's behavior) but stays correct. The real danger is an inadmissible heuristic — say, multiplying straight-line time by 1.5 to go faster — which can return a route that is not actually optimal. Some production systems accept that on purpose for speed, but you must say so out loud; silently returning a worse route is a correctness bug, not an optimization.

Follow-up the interviewer will ask

"Why not just use Dijkstra?" — because it explores in every direction; A* adds a goal-directed heuristic to cut the search. The natural next probe is "is A* fast enough for routing across a continent?" The honest answer is no — even A* touches too many nodes across a country-sized graph in real time. That sets up the next section perfectly.

08 Deep dive · continent scale

Continent scale: precompute the highways

Bottleneck: even A* must touch huge numbers of nodes to route across a continent, and we need sub-100 ms answers. The insight is that most long routes share the same skeleton: to drive from one Singapore neighborhood to another far across the island, you almost certainly get onto a major expressway, ride it, and get off near the destination. Real journeys funnel through a tiny set of important roads. So precompute that structure.

Contraction hierarchies, under the hood

Contraction hierarchies (CH) is the dominant technique. Offline, you rank every node by "importance" (roughly, how central it is to long routes). Then you contract nodes one at a time from least to most important: when you remove a node, you add shortcut edges between its neighbors that preserve the shortest distances that used to pass through it. A shortcut might say "from this on-ramp to that off-ramp is 14 minutes" and stand in for fifty real segments along the expressway.

At query time you run a bidirectional search — one search forward from the origin, one backward from the destination — but you only ever move upward in the importance hierarchy. Both searches climb quickly onto the shortcut network (the "highways"), meet in the middle, and stop. Instead of expanding millions of residential intersections, you expand a few thousand important nodes and shortcuts. Continent-scale queries that took seconds with plain Dijkstra finish in well under a millisecond of graph search.

The trade-off is the classic one: you pay a large offline preprocessing cost (hours to build the hierarchy and shortcuts for a country) and extra storage for the shortcuts, in exchange for blazing-fast queries. Since the road network changes slowly, that is an excellent bargain — exactly the precompute-once, serve-many pattern from the tile story, applied to routing.

Sharding routing by region

The graph is also too big to live in one process, and a Singapore user rarely routes to London. So shard the routing service by geographic region: each shard holds the in-memory graph (with its CH structure) for a country or metro and answers queries within it. The gateway routes a request to the shard that owns the origin's region. Locality means the vast majority of queries are answered entirely within one shard.

Crossing boundaries

The hard part is a route that spans two shards — Singapore to Kuala Lumpur. You handle boundary nodes: the small set of crossing points (border checkpoints, major highways) where regions connect. The query routes from origin to a boundary node in the source shard, hands off across the boundary, and continues in the destination shard. You precompute distances between boundary nodes so the handoff is cheap. This is graph partitioning: cut the graph where traffic between partitions is thinnest, and stitch the cuts back with a small overlay graph of boundary-to-boundary distances.

Follow-up the interviewer will ask

"How do you route across a continent fast?" — say it cleanly: precompute contraction-hierarchy shortcuts offline so a long query climbs onto the highway skeleton and barely searches; shard the graph by region for memory and locality; and stitch shards at boundary nodes with a precomputed overlay. If they push on "what changes when traffic shifts the weights?" admit CH assumes mostly-static weights and needs periodic rebuilds or customizable variants — a great segue into the live-traffic section.

09 Deep dive · freshness

Live traffic & the ETA pipeline

Bottleneck: a route computed on free-flow speeds lies. The PIE at 6 pm is not the PIE at 3 am. The one thing we genuinely cannot precompute is now — so we need a continuous pipeline that turns live driver movement into fresh edge weights, which the router reads on its next query.

How the pipeline works under the hood

The raw signal is GPS probe data: every navigating phone and every Grab driver emits its position every few seconds. The pipeline turns this firehose into per-edge speeds in four stages:

  • Ingest. Probes land on a durable, partitioned log — Kafka, partitioned by region or geohash so each consumer owns a slice of the map. This decouples the spiky, massive write rate from the processing.
  • Map-matching. A raw (lat, lng) is noisy and sits "between" roads. Map-matching snaps a sequence of probes onto the most likely actual edges they traversed — a small hidden-Markov-style inference per trajectory. This is the step that converts "a dot near a road" into "this car drove edge 8842 in 47 seconds."
  • Aggregate. A stream processor — Apache Flink (or Spark Streaming) — groups matched observations per edge over a sliding time window (say the last 2–5 minutes) and computes a robust average speed, discarding outliers (a parked car, a GPS glitch).
  • Publish. The new per-edge speed is written to a fast store the routers read — Redis or an in-memory overlay keyed by edge_id. The router blends this live speed with the historical baseline for that segment and time-of-day to set the current weight.

Worked example — a Grab driver hits a jam

It is 6:10 pm. Thousands of Grab drivers crawling along a segment of the ECP each emit probes showing 12 km/h, far below the 80 km/h baseline. Flink's 3-minute window for that edge averages the observations and writes edge_speed = 12 km/h to Redis. The next rider who requests a route through that area gets a weight five-to-six times higher for that segment; A* now sees it as expensive and may steer onto Marine Parade Road instead. The rider's ETA jumps from 9 to 14 minutes — accurately. Crucially, this happened with no route recomputation push: weights changed, and the very next query reflected reality.

Historical patterns and prediction

Live data alone is jittery and sparse on quiet roads. So edge weights blend live speed with a historical model: the typical speed for this segment at this day-of-week and time-of-day, learned from months of probes. For a long trip, you also predict — the speed on a segment you will reach in 40 minutes should use the forecast for 40 minutes from now, not the speed right now. This time-dependent weighting is why a good ETA accounts for a jam that will have cleared by the time you arrive.

Failure mode

If the traffic pipeline stalls — Kafka lag, a Flink job crash — edge weights go stale. The correct fallback is to detect staleness (each edge speed carries a timestamp) and fall back to the historical baseline for that segment and time. The map keeps routing with typical speeds; ETAs lose live accuracy but stay reasonable. That is far better than failing the route. Recovery is automatic: when the pipeline catches up, fresh weights overwrite the baselines on the next window flush.

Follow-up the interviewer will ask

"How does live traffic change the ETA?" — answer the mechanism, not the magic: driver GPS probes stream into Kafka, get map-matched to edges, are aggregated per edge by Flink into current speeds, and update edge weights in a fast store; the router reads those weights on the next query, so a slowdown both raises the ETA and can trigger a reroute. Then mention historical blending and stale-data fallback, and you have closed the loop.

10 Wrap-up

Failure modes & what breaks at 10x

Tie the system off by stress-testing it. An interviewer's favorite closer is "what happens when X dies?" and "now grow it 10×." Walk each subsystem.

What breaks, and how it recovers

  • Tile origin dies. The CDN keeps serving cached tiles — nearly everything popular. Cold tiles can degrade to a lower zoom. Impact: minimal, by design.
  • A routing shard dies. Queries for that region fail until a replica takes over. Run each region shard with hot standbys; the graph is read-only at query time, so promoting a replica is instant — just repoint the gateway. Cross-region routes touching the dead shard fail over to the replica's boundary handoff.
  • Traffic pipeline stalls. Fall back to historical edge speeds (above). The system stays up; only live accuracy dips.
  • A whole region (data center) fails. Because routing is sharded geographically and tiles are global on the CDN, a regional failure is contained: you reroute that region's traffic to a neighboring DC that also holds a replica of the graph, accepting higher latency until recovery.

What breaks at 10x

  • Tiles (10× reads). Almost free — add CDN capacity and edge POPs. This is the easy axis; the CDN absorbs it. The origin barely notices because miss rate, not request rate, drives its load.
  • Routing (10× queries). CPU-bound, so scale horizontally: more replicas per region shard behind the gateway, and split hot regions into finer shards. Watch the CH rebuild cost — a denser road network means longer offline preprocessing.
  • Traffic (10× drivers). The probe firehose grows linearly. Add Kafka partitions and Flink parallelism; partition by geohash so the work shards cleanly. The risk is map-matching CPU — it is the most expensive per-probe step, so it must scale with ingest.
  • The lurking limit: graph updates. At 10× the map editing rate, rebuilding contraction hierarchies for large regions frequently becomes the real bottleneck. Mitigations: rebuild per-region in parallel, use customizable route planning variants that separate the slow topology preprocessing from fast weight customization, and rebuild on a schedule rather than per edit.
The pattern, one more time

Every scaling answer here is the same shape: precompute the slow-changing part offline (tiles, CH shortcuts, historical speeds), keep the request path cheap (CDN hits, in-memory graph search, a single weight lookup), and isolate the only truly live part (traffic) in a stream that updates weights out-of-band. If you can articulate that, you can defend any 10× the interviewer throws at you.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole design compresses to one sentence: render from precomputed tiles served by a CDN, route over an in-memory road graph using A* sped up by contraction-hierarchy shortcuts and regional sharding, and keep ETAs honest with a streaming traffic pipeline that rewrites edge weights. If you can sketch those three subsystems and explain why each leans on precomputation, you can carry the interview.
