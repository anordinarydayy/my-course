Design a Hotel / Ticket Reservation System

Reservation systems live or die on one rule: never sell the same seat or room twice — even when thousands of people grab for the last one in the same instant. The whole design is an exercise in concurrency control, temporary holds, and a booking saga that survives a failed payment without leaking inventory.

About these notes: original study material on a standard, widely-documented design problem — booking and reservation systems. The explanations, numbers, schemas, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Reference shelf-mark BBG V2·C7. 00 The problem

The one rule: never double-book

Every booking product — Agoda holding a room at a Marina Bay hotel, a Sistic concert on-sale, a Grab ride being matched — reduces to the same hard kernel: a finite pool of inventory (rooms, seats, slots) and many buyers competing for it. Almost everything else can be eventually consistent and a little fuzzy. The inventory cannot. If two travellers each pay for the last deluxe room on the same night, you have an angry guest at a sold-out front desk, a refund, a chargeback, and a trust problem. We call that failure a double-booking, and avoiding it under heavy concurrency is the entire interview.

What makes it genuinely hard is that the contention is not spread out — it concentrates. When a popular item is nearly sold out, every remaining buyer is fighting over the same one or two units. That is exactly the moment your database has to serialize correctly, and exactly the moment traffic is highest. A reservation system is therefore a study in three intertwined mechanisms:

  • Concurrency control on the contended inventory record, so two simultaneous buyers can never both win the last unit.
  • Temporary holds with an expiry, so a buyer can reserve a room while they fill in payment details — but an abandoned checkout releases the room instead of locking it forever.
  • A booking saga: reserve, then charge, then confirm — with a compensating release if any later step fails, and idempotency so a retried request never double-charges or double-books.

Hold that picture. Every section below is one more layer of machinery built to make that single guarantee hold at scale.

01 Scope it

Clarify & scope the requirements

Before drawing boxes, narrow the problem out loud. "Reservation system" covers everything from a 50-room boutique hotel to a global ticketing platform, and the design pivots hard on the answers. I'll scope it as a hotel/room booking service in the spirit of Agoda or Booking.com, then note where ticketing (Sistic, a TikTok live-event drop) differs.

Functional requirements

  • Search availability — given a destination, date range, and guest count, return hotels with rooms free for the whole stay.
  • Reserve & book without double-booking — the non-negotiable core, correct under heavy concurrency.
  • Temporary holds — lock a room for a few minutes while the guest enters payment, then release it automatically if they abandon checkout.
  • Take payment — integrate a payment gateway (think GrabPay, a card processor, or a SeaMoney wallet) and confirm only on success.
  • Cancellation & modification — release inventory back to the pool, subject to a cancellation policy.

Out of scope (state your assumptions)

  • Pricing, promotions, and dynamic rates — assume a price service hands us a number.
  • Reviews, recommendations, the hotel-admin onboarding portal.
  • Fraud scoring beyond what the payment gateway provides.

Non-functional requirements

  • Correctness over availability for writes. A booking is money and a promise; we will favour strong consistency on the inventory write even if it costs latency. Reads (search) can be eventually consistent.
  • High read availability. Search dwarfs booking by volume and must stay fast and up nearly always.
  • Low booking latency — a hold/confirm round trip in the low hundreds of milliseconds, payment aside.
  • Durability. A confirmed booking must never be lost; this implies a persistent, replicated store of record.
  • Idempotency. Mobile networks retry; the same booking attempt must not charge twice.
Interview tip

Lead with the line that frames the whole answer: "This is a strong-consistency-on-write, eventual-consistency-on-read problem — search is read-heavy and tolerant, booking is a small, contended, must-be-correct write." That single sentence tells the interviewer you understand where the hard part lives before you've drawn a single box.

02 Estimate

Back-of-the-envelope estimation

Numbers tell you which components need to be big and which can stay simple. Let's size a regional Agoda-scale service for Southeast Asia.

Inventory & storage

Say 1,000,000 hotels, averaging 50 rooms each, so ~50M physical rooms. We don't store one row per room-night forever; we store availability per (hotel, room-type, date). With ~20 room-types per hotel and a 1-year booking horizon (365 days), that's 1M × 20 × 365 ≈ 7.3 billion availability rows. At ~50 bytes each (ids, a count, status), that's roughly 365 GB of hot inventory data — easily sharded across a handful of database nodes, comfortably in the "big but not exotic" range.

Read traffic (search)

Assume 10 million daily active users, each doing ~5 searches. That's 50M searches/day ≈ 580 search QPS average. Peak is spiky — holiday booking surges or a flash sale push it to ~3,000–5,000 QPS. Search is read-heavy and cacheable, so this lands on a search/cache tier, not the transactional database.

Write traffic (bookings)

Conversion from search to booking is low — say 2%. That's 50M × 0.02 = 1M bookings/day ≈ 12 booking writes/sec on average. Trivially small. This is the trap. The aggregate write rate is tiny, yet the system is hard — because writes are not uniform. They pile onto the same rows. A single sold-out-soon hotel on a long weekend, or a concert with 5,000 seats and 200,000 buyers, concentrates thousands of concurrent writes on a handful of inventory records in seconds.

The estimation insight

The headline number (12 writes/sec) is irrelevant; the contention profile is everything. Reservation systems are not throughput problems, they are per-row contention problems. Design for the hot row, not the average row. Keep this front-of-mind through every later section.

Bandwidth

A search response with ~30 hotels and thumbnails might be ~50 KB. At 5,000 QPS peak that's 250 MB/s ≈ 2 Gbps egress on the read path — handled by CDN and a read cache, not the origin. Booking payloads are tiny (a few KB), so the write path's bandwidth is negligible; its cost is latency and correctness, not bytes.

03 Contracts

API contract & data model

Now make it concrete. A clean way to express the booking flow is three endpoints that mirror the saga — reserve (create a hold), confirm (pay & commit), and the supporting reads. Separating "reserve" from "confirm" is the single most important API decision: it gives the user a window to pay without holding a database lock open for that whole time.

The API surface

# Read path — cheap, cacheable, eventually consistent
GET  /v1/search?city=Singapore&checkin=2026-08-08&checkout=2026-08-10&guests=2
       -> 200 [{hotelId, name, roomTypes:[{id, available, pricePerNight}]}]

# Write path — the saga. Note the Idempotency-Key header.
POST /v1/holds            Idempotency-Key: 7f3a-...      # step 1: reserve
  body { hotelId, roomTypeId, checkin, checkout, qty, userId }
  -> 201 { holdId, status:"HELD", expiresAt:"2026-05-31T10:07:00Z" }
  -> 409 { error:"SOLD_OUT" }                            # no inventory left

POST /v1/bookings         Idempotency-Key: 7f3a-...      # step 2: confirm + pay
  body { holdId, paymentToken }
  -> 201 { bookingId, status:"CONFIRMED" }
  -> 402 { error:"PAYMENT_FAILED" }                      # hold survives, can retry
  -> 410 { error:"HOLD_EXPIRED" }                        # too slow, re-reserve

DELETE /v1/bookings/{id}                                 # cancel -> release inventory
  -> 200 { status:"CANCELLED", refund:"PENDING" }

The Idempotency-Key is the same value on the hold and the booking for a given user attempt; the server stores the first result keyed by it and replays that result on any retry (covered in §7). The expiresAt on a hold is what makes abandoned carts safe.

The data model

Four entities carry the whole design. The one to obsess over is the inventory row, because that is the contended resource.

inventory                       -- the hot row; one per resource+slot
  hotel_id        bigint
  room_type_id    bigint
  date            date              -- a single night
  total_count     int               -- physical rooms of this type
  reserved_count  int               -- held + booked
  PRIMARY KEY (hotel_id, room_type_id, date)
  -- available = total_count - reserved_count

holds                           -- temporary reservation with a TTL
  hold_id         uuid PK
  user_id         bigint
  hotel_id, room_type_id, qty
  checkin, checkout
  status          enum(HELD, CONVERTED, EXPIRED, RELEASED)
  expires_at      timestamp         -- the sweeper / TTL key

bookings                        -- the durable record of truth
  booking_id      uuid PK
  hold_id         uuid
  user_id, hotel_id, room_type_id
  checkin, checkout, total_price
  status          enum(CONFIRMED, CANCELLED)
  idempotency_key varchar UNIQUE    -- dedupes retries

reservation_units              -- OPTIONAL: one row per held/booked unit
  hotel_id, room_type_id, date, unit_no
  hold_or_booking_id
  UNIQUE (hotel_id, room_type_id, date, unit_no)  -- DB-enforced no-dup

There are two ways to represent "is this sold out?", and they lead to two different concurrency strategies you'll see in §5:

  • A counter on the inventory row (reserved_count < total_count) — compact, fast, and you check-and-decrement atomically.
  • A row per unit in reservation_units with a UNIQUE constraint — the database itself rejects a second claim on the same physical unit, no application logic required.
04 The big picture

High-level architecture & request flow

With the contract fixed, here's the shape of the system and how a request travels through it. The design deliberately splits the read path from the write path — they have opposite requirements (cheap-and-fuzzy vs. correct-and-contended), so we let each scale independently.

  • Search service reads from a denormalised, cached availability view (Elasticsearch + Redis). It never touches the transactional store directly on the hot path.
  • Reservation service owns the writes. It performs the concurrency-controlled decrement on the inventory database, creates holds, and orchestrates the booking saga.
  • Inventory DB is the strongly-consistent store of record — PostgreSQL (or a sharded equivalent), sharded by hotel_id so each hotel's contention stays local to one shard.
  • Payment service is an external dependency the saga calls between hold and confirm.
  • Expiry sweeper reclaims abandoned holds.
Why split read from write

If search hit the transactional inventory DB directly, the read flood (5,000 QPS) would crowd out the writes that actually need the database's locks and CPU. By serving search from a cache/search index that lags reality by a few seconds, the precious transactional capacity is reserved for the one thing that must be correct: the decrement. A user occasionally seeing a room that just sold out is fine — they get a clean 409 SOLD_OUT at hold time, which is the authoritative check anyway.

05 The crux

Inventory under concurrency

This is the heart of the lesson. Two buyers want the last room. Both requests arrive at the reservation service within the same millisecond. How does the database guarantee that exactly one succeeds? Naively, each request does: read available, see it's 1, decide "yes there's room", write available = 0. Both read 1, both think they won, both write 0 — and you've sold one room twice. This is a classic read-modify-write race. There are three well-worn ways to serialize it correctly.

Option A — pessimistic locking (SELECT ... FOR UPDATE)

Pessimistic locking assumes a conflict will happen and takes a lock up front. Inside a transaction, the first request runs SELECT reserved_count FROM inventory WHERE ... FOR UPDATE. The FOR UPDATE clause acquires a row-level write lock on that exact inventory row. The second request, hitting the same row, blocks at the database until the first transaction commits or rolls back. Once the first commits its decrement, the second's SELECT unblocks and reads the new count — now sees sold out — and returns 409. The database serializes the two by making the loser wait.

BEGIN;
  SELECT reserved_count FROM inventory
    WHERE hotel_id=42 AND room_type_id=7 AND date='2026-08-08'
    FOR UPDATE;              -- row locked; concurrent txns wait here
  -- app checks: reserved_count < total_count ?
  UPDATE inventory SET reserved_count = reserved_count + 1
    WHERE hotel_id=42 AND room_type_id=7 AND date='2026-08-08';
COMMIT;                      -- lock released, next txn proceeds

Option B — atomic conditional update (the usual winner)

You don't actually need to read first. Push the check into the write with a guarded UPDATE that only fires if inventory remains:

UPDATE inventory
   SET reserved_count = reserved_count + 1
 WHERE hotel_id=42 AND room_type_id=7 AND date='2026-08-08'
   AND reserved_count < total_count;   -- the guard
-- rows affected == 1  -> you got it
-- rows affected == 0  -> sold out, return 409

A single UPDATE statement is atomic under the database's own row locking. Two concurrent updates are serialized by the engine; the first flips reserved_count to equal total_count, and the second's WHERE guard no longer matches, so it affects zero rows and loses cleanly. No explicit transaction or read needed, fewer round trips, and no application-side decision that could race. This is the default answer most interviewers want to hear.

Option C — a UNIQUE constraint on (resource, slot)

For seat-level systems (a specific seat 14C, a specific room 1203), let the database enforce uniqueness structurally. Insert a row into reservation_units with a UNIQUE (hotel_id, room_type_id, date, unit_no) constraint. If two requests try to claim unit 1203 for the same night, the database accepts the first INSERT and rejects the second with a unique-constraint violation — the loser catches the error and returns sold out. The correctness guarantee lives in the schema, which is the hardest place to accidentally break it.

Pessimistic lock (FOR UPDATE)

  • + Conceptually simple; you read the value, then act on it
  • + Natural when you must hold multiple rows consistent in one txn
  • − Holds a lock for the txn's duration — under high contention, requests queue and latency spikes
  • − Risk of deadlock if you lock rows in inconsistent order
  • − A long-running or stuck transaction blocks everyone on that row

Atomic conditional update

  • + One round trip, no explicit transaction, no app-side check to race
  • + Lock held only for the instant of the UPDATE — best under contention
  • + Maps cleanly to a counter; trivially shardable
  • − Only works when the decision is expressible as a WHERE guard
  • − Counter alone can't say which physical unit you got

There is also optimistic concurrency control — read a version column, then UPDATE ... WHERE version = :seen, retrying on a 0-row result. It shines when conflicts are rare (most rows aren't contended). But on a hot sold-out row, conflicts are the norm, so optimistic retries thrash; there, the atomic guard (B) or a pessimistic lock (A) is better. Knowing when optimistic loses is a senior-level point.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you prevent double-booking?" is asked verbatim in almost every reservation interview. The crisp answer: "Make the inventory decrement atomic. I'd use a conditional UPDATE that increments reserved_count only while it's still below total_count — the database serializes concurrent writers, so the guard fails for the loser and it gets a clean sold-out. For seat-level booking I'd back it with a UNIQUE constraint on (seat, slot) so the schema itself can't be violated." Then mention pessimistic locking as the alternative and why you'd avoid it under heavy contention.

06 Holds

Temporary holds & expiry

Booking isn't instantaneous — the guest needs ten, twenty, sometimes ninety seconds to type a card number or approve a GrabPay charge. If you only decremented inventory after a successful payment, two people could both pass the availability check and both reach the payment screen for the last room — and one is guaranteed to be disappointed after paying. So we reserve before payment. But a reservation that never converts must not lock the room forever. The fix is a temporary hold: a reservation that carries an expiry timestamp and is automatically released if the user abandons checkout.

How a hold works under the hood

A hold does the real inventory decrement (the atomic UPDATE from §5) and writes a holds row with status = HELD and expires_at = now() + 7 minutes. From inventory's point of view the room is already taken, so no one else can grab it. Three things can then happen to that hold:

  • Converted — payment succeeds; the hold becomes a confirmed booking and its inventory stays decremented.
  • Released — the user cancels; the hold is voided and the inventory increment is reversed (reserved_count -= 1).
  • Expired — the clock runs out; the system must reclaim the room.

Two ways to expire a hold

The naive idea — a background sweeper job that runs every few seconds, finds rows where status = HELD AND expires_at < now(), and reverses their inventory — works and is simple to reason about. The subtlety: do the reclaim itself atomically (a guarded UPDATE holds SET status='EXPIRED' WHERE hold_id=? AND status='HELD') so it can't race with a confirmation landing at the same instant.

The slicker approach uses Redis with a TTL. When you create the hold, also SET hold:{id} ... EX 420 (a 420-second time-to-live). Redis evicts the key automatically when it expires, and a keyspace-notification listener fires the inventory reversal. This pushes expiry to the millisecond instead of waiting for the next sweep, and offloads the "what's expired?" scan from your transactional database. Most production systems run both: Redis TTL for promptness, plus a slower DB sweeper as a safety net in case a Redis notification is missed.

Under the hood — choosing the TTL

The hold duration is a business knob with real consequences. Too short (60s) and slow payers lose their room mid-checkout — bad conversion. Too long (30min) and a flash sale "sells out" instantly to holders who never pay, freezing inventory that real buyers can't see — bad utilisation. A typical hotel hold is 5–10 minutes; high-demand ticket on-sales often use a tighter 2–4 minute hold plus a visible countdown timer, precisely because frozen inventory is so costly when demand vastly exceeds supply.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do holds expire, and what if the sweeper is down?" Answer: a Redis TTL fires a near-instant release, with a periodic DB sweeper as backstop; each release is a guarded, idempotent UPDATE so it can run twice harmlessly and can't double-count against a confirmation arriving simultaneously. If the sweeper falls behind, the worst case is some rooms appear unavailable a little longer than necessary — degraded utilisation, never a double-booking. Favour leaking availability over leaking correctness.

07 The booking saga

The booking saga: reserve → pay → confirm

A booking spans multiple systems — your inventory database and an external payment gateway — and you cannot wrap them in one database transaction (you can't roll back GrabPay with a SQL ROLLBACK). The pattern for a multi-step operation across services where each step can fail independently is a saga: a sequence of local transactions, each with a defined compensating action that undoes it if a later step fails. Our saga has three forward steps and two compensations.

  • Reserve — atomic inventory decrement + create HELD hold. Compensation: release the hold (increment inventory back).
  • Pay — charge the payment gateway. Compensation: refund.
  • Confirm — mark the booking CONFIRMED, convert the hold. No compensation needed; this is the commit point.

Walking the failure paths

The saga only earns its keep when something breaks. Consider the interviewer's favourite: payment fails (or times out) after the hold. Because we reserved before charging, the room is already held in our name. On a payment failure we have a choice — keep the hold alive so the user can retry payment within the remaining TTL (good UX), or compensate immediately by releasing the hold. Either way, no room is lost and no booking is confirmed without a successful charge. If we instead can't confirm after a successful charge (the confirm write fails), we must refund — that's the pay-step compensation firing.

Idempotency: the retry safety net

Mobile clients on flaky networks retry aggressively. Without protection, a retried POST /v1/bookings could charge twice or create two bookings. The defence is an idempotency key (covered in depth in L08): the client generates a unique key per booking attempt and sends it on every retry. The server records the outcome keyed by it — using the idempotency_key UNIQUE column or a Redis entry — and on a duplicate key simply replays the stored result instead of re-executing. The payment gateway's own idempotency key (passed through on the charge) prevents the double-charge on its side too.

Orchestration vs choreography

Two ways to run the saga. In orchestration, the reservation service is a central coordinator that calls each step and triggers compensations on failure — easy to reason about and to observe, the usual choice for a booking flow. In choreography, services react to events on a bus (Kafka): "PaymentFailed" triggers a "ReleaseHold" consumer. It decouples services but scatters the saga logic across many of them, making "what state is this booking in?" harder to answer. For a bounded 3-step booking, orchestration is cleaner.

Interviewer follow-up

"What happens if payment fails after the hold?" The strong answer names the saga explicitly: "Reserve runs before pay, so on payment failure the inventory is already safely held — I either keep the hold for a retry within its TTL or fire the compensating release. Idempotency keys make the whole POST safe to retry without double-charging or double-booking. The only thing that ever confirms a booking is a committed confirm step after a successful charge." Bonus point: mention the rare charge-succeeded-but-confirm-failed case and that it triggers a refund compensation.

08 Policy

Overbooking as a deliberate business decision

Everything so far fought to never exceed inventory. But airlines and hotels routinely sell more rooms than they have — on purpose. This is overbooking, and it's a business policy, not a bug. The logic: a predictable fraction of bookings no-show or cancel last-minute. If 5% of guests typically don't arrive, a 100-room hotel that sells exactly 100 rooms runs ~95% full on average — leaving paid-for capacity empty. Selling 105 rooms recovers that revenue, accepting the small risk that an unusually full night forces you to "walk" a guest (re-home them at your cost).

Mechanically, overbooking is just a configurable cushion on the guard. Instead of reserved_count < total_count, the check becomes reserved_count < total_count + overbook_buffer, where the buffer is tuned per hotel, room-type, and date from historical no-show rates. The concurrency machinery is identical; only the threshold moves.

Strict (no overbooking)

  • + Every confirmed guest is guaranteed a room
  • + Simplest policy; no walk-cost risk
  • − Leaves revenue on the table from no-shows and cancels
  • − Common for ticketing, where seat 14C cannot be duplicated

Overbook with a buffer

  • + Recovers no-show revenue; higher effective occupancy
  • + Buffer tuned from data, adjustable per date
  • − Rare nights require walking a guest — compensation cost + reputation hit
  • − Needs a defined bump/walk policy and reaccommodation flow
Interviewer follow-up

If you raise overbooking unprompted, you signal product maturity. Be ready for "how do you decide the buffer, and what's the recovery when you oversell?" — answer with a data-driven per-segment no-show model plus a documented walk/bump procedure (upgrade, re-home at a nearby property, compensation). Note that seat-assigned ticketing usually can't overbook the same physical seat, so it's mostly a hotel/general-admission lever.

09 Read path

Read/write split & making search fast

Search is a different animal from booking. It's read-heavy (recall §2: thousands of QPS vs a trickle of writes), tolerant of slightly stale data, and demands rich filtering (city, dates, price, stars, amenities). Forcing it through the transactional inventory database would be a category error — it would compete for the very locks the writes need, and relational range-filtering across 7 billion availability rows is slow. So we serve search from purpose-built read infrastructure.

How the read path is built

  • Search index — an Elasticsearch (or OpenSearch) cluster holds a denormalised hotel document: location, amenities, price band, and a coarse availability summary. It answers "hotels in Singapore, 8–10 Aug, under S$300" in milliseconds.
  • Availability cache — Redis caches per-(hotel, date) availability with a short TTL (seconds), so repeated searches for the same dates don't all hit the index.
  • CDN — static assets (photos, hotel descriptions) served from edge nodes near the user, keeping the ~50 KB search response cheap.
  • Change data capture (CDC) — when the inventory DB commits a decrement, a CDC stream (Debezium → Kafka) propagates the change to the index and cache within seconds, so search stays roughly in sync.

The deliberate consequence: search results are eventually consistent. A room can show as available for a few seconds after it sold out. That's acceptable because search is advisory — the authoritative availability check is the atomic decrement at hold time. A user who clicks a just-sold room gets an honest 409 SOLD_OUT and re-searches. Never let search's looseness leak into the booking decision.

Strongly consistent search

  • + A shown room is truly bookable
  • − Every search hits the transactional DB — kills write capacity
  • − Can't support rich filtering at scale

Eventually consistent search (chosen)

  • + Scales reads independently; rich, fast filtering
  • + Protects scarce transactional capacity for the decrement
  • − Brief window where a sold room still shows — caught at hold time
10 Scale & failure

Hot events, failure modes & what breaks at 10x

The system handles ordinary load comfortably. The interview's final act is stressing it: a concentrated demand spike, and a node dying mid-flow.

The hot-row problem (a concert on-sale)

A Sistic on-sale for a 50,000-seat stadium opens at noon. 500,000 buyers refresh simultaneously. Average write QPS is meaningless here; the contention slams onto a few inventory rows (or, for general admission, a single counter). Even a perfect atomic UPDATE serializes writers on that row — meaning they queue, and tail latency explodes. Three escalating mitigations:

  • Shard / partition the inventory. Split one general-admission pool of 50,000 into, say, 50 sub-counters of 1,000 each. Buyers hash to a sub-counter, spreading contention 50-fold; a request only rolls to another sub-counter if its own is empty. Same total, far less per-row queueing.
  • Queue the requests. Put a virtual waiting room in front: buyers enter a Kafka/Redis queue and are admitted at a controlled rate the inventory tier can absorb. This converts a thundering herd into a smooth stream and gives users a fair, visible position — exactly the pattern big ticketing platforms use for blockbuster on-sales.
  • Decrement in Redis first. Hold the authoritative counter in Redis with an atomic DECR (or a Lua script) for microsecond-level serialization, then asynchronously persist the resulting bookings to the durable store. Redis absorbs the spike; the database catches up. The trade-off is added complexity to keep Redis and the DB reconciled.

Failure modes — what happens when a node dies

  • Reservation service crashes mid-saga. Because each forward step is a local transaction and the saga state is persisted (the holds row, the booking status), a recovery worker — or the next sweeper pass — finishes or compensates the orphan. A hold with no booking simply expires via its TTL and releases inventory. No room is lost.
  • Payment times out (no response). Worst case: the charge may have succeeded. Treat it as unknown — keep the hold, and reconcile asynchronously against the gateway using the idempotency key. Either the charge confirms (we complete the booking) or it didn't (the hold expires). Never confirm on a timeout.
  • Inventory DB primary fails. A replicated setup promotes a replica (failover). Bookings briefly pause rather than risk a split-brain double-decrement — for inventory, correctness beats availability. In-flight holds survive because they're committed rows.
  • Redis cache dies. Search degrades to slightly staler data or falls back to the index; booking is unaffected because the authoritative check is in the DB, not the cache. The cache is an accelerator, never the source of truth for the decrement.

What breaks at 10x

Push every dimension up an order of magnitude and the pressure points reveal themselves. Search QPS scales out cleanly — add index replicas and cache nodes. Storage scales by sharding inventory on hotel_id (each hotel's contention stays on one shard). The thing that does not scale by adding machines is per-row contention: ten times the buyers on the same last room is still one row. That's why the hot-row mitigations above — sub-counter sharding, waiting rooms, Redis decrements — are the real "10x" answer. The bottleneck was never aggregate throughput; it was always the single contended unit, which is exactly where we started in §0.

Interviewer follow-up

Expect: "A celebrity drop melts your single hottest row — what do you do?" Walk the escalation: split the counter into sub-shards, front it with a fair virtual waiting room to rate-limit admission, and consider an in-memory Redis counter for the on-sale window with async persistence. Close with the principle that lands the offer: "Reservation systems don't scale by throughput, they scale by reducing contention on the hot unit — everything else is the easy part."

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: guarantee exactly one buyer wins the contended inventory unit using an atomic decrement, hold it with an expiry so abandoned carts release, and wrap the charge in an idempotent saga that compensates on failure. If you can whiteboard the atomic guard, the hold lifecycle, and the reserve → pay → confirm saga, you own this interview.
