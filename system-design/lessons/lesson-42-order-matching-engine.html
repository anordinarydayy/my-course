Design an Order Matching Engine

A matching engine pairs incoming buy and sell orders against a live order book — with single-digit-microsecond latency, perfectly reproducible results, and strict fairness. It's a stretch drill straight out of crypto and trading-platform fintech, and it rewards three ideas you rarely get to combine elsewhere: an in-memory order book, one sequencer that imposes a total order on every event, and a durable log you can replay to rebuild state from scratch.

About these notes: original study material on matching-engine design — a well-documented topic in market-microstructure and exchange engineering. 00 The problem

What is a matching engine?

Imagine a Singapore crypto exchange — think a Coinhako- or Independent-Reserve-style platform — listing the BTC/SGD pair. Thousands of traders are sending buy and sell orders every second. Someone wants to buy 0.5 BTC at no more than S$92,000; someone else is willing to sell 0.2 BTC at S$91,980. A matching engine is the component that decides, instant by instant, which of these orders trade against each other, at what price, and in what order — then records the resulting trades (also called fills or executions).

It sits at the dead center of the exchange. Everything upstream (web, mobile, API gateways, authentication, wallets) exists to feed it orders; everything downstream (clearing, settlement, ledgers, charts, the trader's screen) exists to consume what it produces. Because it is the single arbiter of who trades with whom, it carries an unusually demanding set of guarantees:

  • Low latency. Serious traders measure round-trips in microseconds. A slow engine loses the venue its professional flow.
  • Determinism. Given the same sequence of input orders, the engine must produce exactly the same trades, every time, on any machine. This is non-negotiable for fairness, auditing, and recovery.
  • Fairness. Orders are matched by price-time priority: the best price wins, and among equal prices, whoever arrived first trades first. No queue-jumping.
  • Durability. A confirmed trade is money changing hands. It can never silently vanish, even if a server dies mid-match.
  • Market-data fan-out. Every trade and every change to the book must be published to potentially tens of thousands of subscribers in near-real-time.

That combination — fast and deterministic and durable — is what makes this a hard, stretch-level design. The whole lesson is the story of reconciling those goals one bottleneck at a time.

01 Scope it

Clarify & scope

The fastest way to lose this interview is to start drawing before you know what you're building. "Trading system" spans risk, clearing, custody, and the engine itself — clarify which slice you own. State the slice as the matching engine for a central limit order book (CLOB) exchange, then nail down the requirements.

Functional requirements

  • Accept orders: place a new order, cancel an existing order, and (often) modify/replace one.
  • Support order types: at minimum LIMIT (trade only at a price or better) and MARKET (trade immediately at whatever price is available). Mention you'd defer stop, iceberg, and FOK/IOC variants.
  • Maintain an order book per trading symbol, with resting (unfilled) orders organized by price.
  • Match incoming orders against the book by price-time priority, producing trades, including partial fills.
  • Publish market data: a trade feed and an order-book-update feed.
  • Persist every accepted event and every resulting trade durably.

Non-functional requirements

  • Determinism / reproducibility. The single most important property. Replaying the same ordered input must reproduce the same book and trades bit-for-bit.
  • Latency. Target a P99 matching latency in the tens of microseconds inside the engine, low-millisecond end-to-end including the network.
  • Throughput. Hundreds of thousands of orders per second per symbol on a hot pair, aggregated across symbols into the millions.
  • Durability & recoverability. No acknowledged order or trade is ever lost; the book is rebuildable after a crash.
  • Availability. Sub-second failover to a standby so a crash doesn't halt trading for long.
  • Fairness & auditability. A regulator (in Singapore, MAS) can demand a full reconstruction of any historical book state.

Assumptions to state out loud

Make your boundaries explicit so the interviewer can redirect you: the engine matches but does not settle (a downstream clearing/ledger system moves the actual coins and fiat); each symbol's book is independent, so we can shard per symbol; and pre-trade balance/risk checks happen at the edge before an order reaches the engine. We'll revisit each of these.

Interview tip

Lead with the line that frames the entire design: "This is a low-latency, deterministic, single-writer system, not a typical scale-out web service — so my instincts about adding more replicas and eventual consistency are mostly inverted here." Saying that early signals you understand why a matching engine looks so different from a CRUD app.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope estimate

A quick sizing pass tells you where the pressure is. Pretend we're running a mid-size regional venue with one very hot pair (BTC/SGD) and a long tail of quieter ones.

Order throughput

  • Hot pair peak: 100,000 orders/sec (placements + cancels; in real markets, cancels often outnumber trades 10:1 because of high-frequency quoting).
  • All symbols combined at peak: ~1,000,000 events/sec across, say, 200 active pairs.
  • Trades are a fraction of order events — perhaps 5–10% result in a fill — so ~50k–100k trades/sec at the very peak.

Memory for the book

Each resting order is small: an ID, price, remaining quantity, side, account, timestamps — call it ~100 bytes packed. A deep, busy book might hold 1 million resting orders, so 1M × 100B = 100 MB per hot symbol. Even 200 symbols at that depth is ~20 GB — comfortably in RAM on a modern server. Conclusion: the entire live order book fits in memory. This single fact drives the whole architecture.

Event log volume

We persist every accepted event. At 1M events/sec and ~100 bytes each, that's 100 MB/sec ≈ 360 GB/hour ≈ 8.6 TB/day of raw event log at sustained peak. Real average rates are far lower, but plan storage and retention (tiered to object storage like S3) for the worst case.

Market-data fan-out bandwidth

This is the sleeper bottleneck. Suppose 100k trades/sec, each serialized to ~150 bytes, fanned out to 20,000 subscribers. Naive unicast would be 100k × 150B × 20k = 300 TB/sec — absurd. That number alone justifies multicast (publish once, the network replicates) for the fan-out path, which we'll get to in Step 09.

Why these numbers matter

Three takeaways fall straight out of the math:
(1) the book lives in RAM, so matching is a memory-speed operation, not a database one;
(2) the event log is high-volume sequential writes, perfect for an append-only log like Kafka or a raw file;
(3) fan-out dwarfs everything in bandwidth, so it gets its own dedicated path. Quote these and the interviewer knows you've found the real constraints.

03 Contract

API & data model

Define the interface before the internals. Clients never speak to the engine directly; they hit a gateway that authenticates, runs pre-trade checks, and forwards a normalized message. Still, sketch the logical API the engine honors.

The order API

Three commands cover the core. Keep them tiny — every byte is on the hot path.

// Place a new order (gateway -> engine)
POST /orders
{
  "client_order_id": "a1f9-7c",   // idempotency key from the client
  "account_id":      "acct_8821",
  "symbol":          "BTC-SGD",
  "side":            "BUY",        // BUY | SELL
  "type":            "LIMIT",      // LIMIT | MARKET
  "price":           "92000.00",   // integer ticks internally; omit for MARKET
  "quantity":        "0.50",       // integer lots internally
  "tif":             "GTC"         // GTC | IOC | FOK
}

// Cancel a resting order
DELETE /orders/{exchange_order_id}

// Acknowledgement carries the engine's authoritative IDs
{ "exchange_order_id": "o_5562001", "seq": 880142, "status": "ACCEPTED" }

Two design choices to call out. First, idempotency: the client_order_id lets the gateway dedupe retries so a flaky network can't accidentally place an order twice. Second — go past the book here — integerize everything. Prices become integer multiples of a tick size and quantities integer multiples of a lot size. Floating-point arithmetic is non-deterministic across compilers and CPUs and would silently break reproducibility; integers are exact and fast.

The event log (the source of truth)

The engine's real persistent state is not a table of orders — it's an append-only, totally ordered stream of events. Everything else is a derived projection of this log.

// One immutable record per accepted event, in sequence order
Event {
  seq:        uint64    // global, gap-free, assigned by the sequencer
  ts_recv:    uint64    // nanoseconds, set once at ingress
  type:       enum      // NEW | CANCEL | TRADE | REJECT
  symbol:     string
  payload:    bytes     // the order or trade body
}

Derived state: the order and trade tables

Downstream consumers materialize the log into queryable stores. These are projections — losing them is survivable because they can be rebuilt by replaying the log.

orders(exchange_order_id PK, account_id, symbol, side, type,
       price, orig_qty, remaining_qty, status, seq_created, seq_updated)

trades(trade_id PK, symbol, price, quantity, seq,
       taker_order_id, maker_order_id, taker_side, ts)

Note the maker/taker split on a trade. The maker is the resting order that was already on the book providing liquidity; the taker is the incoming order that removed it. Exchanges price these differently (maker rebates, taker fees), so the engine records which is which on every fill.

Interview tip

If you say "we'll store prices as floats," expect an immediate pushback. Pre-empt it: "Prices and quantities are fixed-point integers in ticks and lots — floats would break determinism and introduce rounding bugs in money." That one sentence shows real domain awareness.

04 The data structure

The order book

The order book is the heart of the engine: the live record of all resting orders for one symbol. It has two sides. The bids are buy orders (people willing to pay up to some price); the asks (or offers) are sell orders (people willing to sell down to some price). The highest bid is the best bid; the lowest ask is the best ask; the gap between them is the spread.

Price levels and FIFO queues

Orders are not stored as a flat list. They're grouped into price levels — one bucket per distinct price — and within each level, orders sit in a FIFO queue ordered by arrival time. This structure is price-time priority made concrete: to match, you always take the best-priced level first, and within it, the order that has waited longest. A new order joining a level always goes to the back of that queue.

Choosing the data structure

What operations must be fast? On every incoming order we need: find best bid / best ask (very frequent), insert at a price level, remove from the front of a level (a fill), and cancel an arbitrary order (frequent). A naive sorted array makes inserts O(n); a single balanced tree makes best-price lookups log(n) but cancels awkward. The production pattern combines structures:

  • A price-indexed map from price level to that level's FIFO queue. Because tick sizes bound the number of realistic prices, many engines use a flat array indexed by tick (a "price ladder") for O(1) access near the touch, falling back to a tree for far-out prices.
  • Each price level holds an intrusive doubly linked list of orders, so appending to the back and removing from the front are O(1).
  • A hash map from exchange_order_id to the order's node, so a cancel is an O(1) lookup-then-unlink — no scanning.
  • The best bid and best ask are cached as pointers and updated as levels empty and fill, so reading "the touch" is O(1).

The net result: place, cancel, and best-price reads are all effectively O(1) in the common case. That's what lets a single core chew through 100k+ orders/sec.

Under the hood: memory layout

The fastest engines obsess over cache locality. Orders and price levels are pooled in pre-allocated arrays (no per-order malloc on the hot path, which would cause unpredictable latency spikes and GC pauses). The "linked list" is built from array indices, not pointers, so hot levels stay in L1/L2 cache. In garbage-collected languages (Java engines are common), teams go to extremes to produce zero garbage per order so the GC never pauses mid-match.

05 The core loop

The matching algorithm

Now the algorithm itself. When an order arrives, the engine asks a single question: does this order cross the book? A buy order crosses if its limit price is at least the best ask; a sell crosses if its limit price is at most the best bid. If it crosses, it matches; if not, it rests.

Step by step

For a crossing incoming order (the taker), the loop is:

  • Look at the best opposite price level (best ask for a buy, best bid for a sell).
  • Take the order at the front of that level's FIFO queue (the oldest maker) and trade as much as possible: fill = min(taker_remaining, maker_remaining).
  • The trade prints at the maker's resting price — the price that was already on the book — not the taker's. This is the price-improvement rule and it's a classic gotcha.
  • Decrement both sides. If the maker is fully filled, remove it and advance to the next order in the queue (or the next price level). If the maker has quantity left, the taker is exhausted.
  • Repeat until the taker is fully filled or the book no longer crosses its limit price.
  • Any unfilled remainder of a LIMIT order rests on the book at its own price, becoming a maker for future orders.

Partial fills, limit vs market

A partial fill happens whenever the taker is larger than the order it hits — it trades against several makers, possibly walking up multiple price levels, generating several trades from one incoming order. A LIMIT order will never trade worse than its price; leftover quantity rests. A MARKET order has no price limit — it sweeps the book until filled or the book is empty (at which point a sensible engine rejects or cancels the remainder rather than resting it, since a market order has no price to rest at).

Worked example

The best ask is 92,000 with 0.2 BTC resting (a maker that arrived at 10:00:01). A taker arrives: BUY 0.5 BTC @ limit 92,010. It crosses (92,010 ≥ 92,000). Trade 1: 0.2 BTC @ 92,000 (the maker's price), filling that maker completely. The taker still wants 0.3 BTC and its limit (92,010) still crosses the next ask level at 92,010 holding 1.0 BTC. Trade 2: 0.3 BTC @ 92,010, filling the taker. Result: one incoming order, two trades, two price levels touched, taker done. Had the taker been a LIMIT @ 92,005, it would have filled 0.2 @ 92,000 then rested the remaining 0.3 at 92,005 (the 92,010 level is above its limit).

Interviewer follow-up

"At what price does a trade execute — the buyer's or the seller's?" Answer: the resting (maker) order's price. The incoming taker gets price improvement if its limit was more aggressive. Follow that with the fairness rule: within a price level, the maker that arrived first is filled first — strict price-time priority — which is exactly why each level is a FIFO queue.

06 Determinism

Deterministic sequencing

Here is the bottleneck that separates a matching engine from any ordinary service. Orders arrive concurrently from many gateways over many network connections. The instant two orders compete for the same liquidity, which one wins? If the answer depends on thread scheduling, network jitter, or which CPU happened to be free, the system is neither fair nor reproducible — and you can never prove to a regulator what "should" have happened.

The single sequencer

The fix is a sequencer: a single logical component that all orders funnel through, which stamps each one with a strictly increasing, gap-free sequence number (seq). That number defines the one and only total order of events. Everything after the sequencer — the matching itself — is a pure, deterministic function of that ordered stream. Same input order, same output, always.

This is event sourcing applied to trading: the sequenced event log is the source of truth, and the order book is just a fold (a left-to-right reduction) over that log. Two consequences follow immediately, and both are gold in the interview:

  • Fairness becomes well-defined. "First" means "lower sequence number," full stop. No ambiguity, no race.
  • Recovery becomes trivial in concept. To rebuild the book, replay the log from seq 0. You'll get the identical book, because matching is deterministic. (Step 08 makes this concrete.)

How real systems do it

Two well-known patterns. The LMAX Disruptor popularized a lock-free ring buffer where a single producer claims slots and assigns sequence numbers, and downstream consumers (journaller, matcher, publisher) read from the same buffer — millions of events per second on one thread. In Kafka-based designs, a single partition per symbol provides the total order for free: Kafka offsets are the sequence numbers, and the matcher consumes that partition in order. NASDAQ's classic ITCH/OUCH protocols and the open-source Chronicle Queue sit in the same family.

Interviewer follow-up: "Why deterministic sequencing?"

Three reasons, and you should fire all three: fairness (a single, defensible definition of who was first), recoverability (replay the log to rebuild exact state after a crash), and auditability/regulation (you can prove to MAS what the book looked like at any historical sequence number). The follow-on trap: "doesn't a single sequencer cap your throughput?" — yes, and that's why we shard by symbol so each shard has its own sequencer (Step 07).

07 Latency & parallelism

Single-threaded per symbol, sharded across symbols

We have determinism. Now reconcile it with speed. The instinct of a web engineer is "throw threads at it" — but a multithreaded matcher needs locks around the shared book, and locks bring two killers: contention (threads waiting on each other, destroying latency predictability) and nondeterminism (lock acquisition order depends on the scheduler, which breaks reproducibility). For a matching engine, both are unacceptable.

One thread, no locks

So the matcher for a given symbol runs single-threaded. One core owns one book and processes its sequenced events one at a time. There is no lock because there is nothing to contend over — a single thread can't race itself. Counter-intuitively, this is also the fastest design: the whole hot book stays warm in that core's cache, there are no atomic-instruction stalls, no cache-line ping-pong between cores, and latency is beautifully predictable. A single modern core can match well over a million simple orders per second.

Shard by symbol to scale out

One thread can't serve every symbol on a busy exchange, so we shard by symbol. Each trading pair (or a small group of pairs) is assigned to one engine instance running its own single-threaded matcher and its own sequencer. BTC-SGD lives on engine 1, ETH-SGD on engine 2, and so on. Crucially, order books for different symbols never interact — you can't trade BTC against ETH directly — so this partitioning is clean and embarrassingly parallel. Add capacity by adding shards and rebalancing symbols.

Single-threaded per symbol

  • No locks → no contention, no scheduler nondeterminism
  • Hot book stays in CPU cache → lowest, most predictable latency
  • Trivially deterministic and easy to reason about
  • Ceiling: one symbol can't exceed one core's throughput

Multithreaded shared book

  • Could in theory use many cores on one hot symbol
  • Locks add latency spikes and unpredictable tail behavior
  • Lock order depends on scheduler → breaks determinism
  • Far harder to test, audit, and recover correctly

An aside: kernel-bypass networking

If the matcher is microseconds and the network is milliseconds, the network dominates. The lowest-latency venues bypass the OS kernel's network stack entirely with technologies like DPDK, Solarflare/OpenOnload, or RDMA, reading packets straight from the NIC into user space. That's beyond scope for a typical interview, but mentioning it signals you know where the remaining latency actually hides once the algorithm is fast.

Interviewer follow-up: "Why single-threaded?"

"Because locks would add nondeterminism and tail-latency, and a matching engine cannot tolerate either. A single thread on a cache-resident in-memory book is both the most deterministic and the lowest-latency option; I scale across symbols by sharding, not across cores within a symbol." If they push on the hot-symbol ceiling, talk about splitting by region/segment or pre-aggregating — but never sacrifice the single-writer property of one book.

08 Don't lose money

Durability & replay

The book lives in RAM. RAM is volatile. If the engine process crashes, the entire book — every resting order — is gone from memory. For a system whose output is money, "we lost the book" is catastrophic. So how do we make an in-memory system durable? The answer is the same insight as sequencing, used a second time: persist the ordered event log, not the book.

Journal first, then match

Every event, the moment the sequencer stamps it, is appended to a durable log (the journal) before the matcher acts on it and before the client is acknowledged. This is write-ahead logging: the durable record exists first. Because the log is append-only and sequential, writing it is extremely fast — sequential disk and modern NVMe sustain enormous write throughput, which is exactly the access pattern from our estimate in Step 02.

Recovery = replay

To recover a crashed engine, you start a fresh process with an empty book and replay the journal from the beginning (or from the last snapshot). Because matching is a deterministic function of the ordered log, replaying the same events reconstructs the exact same book and the exact same trades — bit-for-bit. The recovered engine is indistinguishable from the one that died.

Snapshots to bound recovery time

Replaying billions of events from genesis would take far too long. So the engine periodically writes a snapshot: the full serialized book state tagged with the sequence number it reflects (e.g. "book as of seq 9,000,000"). Recovery then loads the latest snapshot and replays only the events after it. Snapshot every few minutes and recovery replays only a few minutes of log.

Failure mode: sequencer / engine death and failover

What happens when the active engine node dies mid-match? You run a hot standby consuming the very same journal in lockstep — it replays every event the primary does and holds an identical book in memory. A leader election (via a coordinator like ZooKeeper, etcd, or Raft) detects the primary's failure and promotes the standby, which is already caught up and can take over in well under a second. Because both fed off the one ordered log, the standby's book is provably identical — there is no "split brain" about what trades happened, since the log is the single source of truth. The journal itself is replicated (e.g. Kafka with acks=all and replication factor 3, or synchronous disk replication) so the log survives a disk failure too.

Interviewer follow-up: "How do you recover the book after a crash?"

Crisp answer: "The book is a deterministic fold over the ordered event log. I journal every event before matching, snapshot the book periodically, and on recovery I load the latest snapshot and replay the tail of the log. A hot standby does this continuously, so failover is a sub-second promotion of an already-warm node." If asked about exactly-once: idempotent client_order_id plus gap-free seq means a replayed or duplicated event is detected and ignored.

09 Edges & outputs

Pre-trade risk checks & market-data fan-out

Two things bracket the matcher: what guards orders before they reach it, and how its results reach the world after.

Pre-trade risk checks

Pre-trade risk checks validate an order before it can touch the book. Does the account have enough SGD (for a buy) or enough BTC (for a sell)? Is the price within sane bounds (a "fat-finger" check — reject a buy 1000% above the market)? Is the account allowed to trade this symbol? These run at the gateway / risk service, on the edge, deliberately outside the single-threaded matcher — because they require account-balance lookups that would pollute the matcher's tight, deterministic loop with I/O. The matcher trusts that anything reaching it has already passed risk. This keeps the hot path pure and fast, and it's the reason we listed "risk happens at the edge" as an assumption back in Step 01.

Persisting fills

When the matcher produces a trade, that trade is part of the same ordered event stream and is therefore already journaled (Step 08). Downstream, a consumer reads the TRADE events and writes them into the queryable trades store and forwards them to clearing & settlement, which actually moves the coins and fiat between accounts. The matcher never blocks on settlement — it emits the trade event and moves on, keeping latency low. Settlement is asynchronous and idempotent off the durable log.

Market-data fan-out

Market data is the public output: a stream of trade prints (someone traded X at price P) and order-book deltas (level 92,000 now has 0.2 BTC; level 91,980 is gone). Charts, trading bots, mobile apps, and other venues all subscribe. Recall the estimate: unicasting to tens of thousands of subscribers is bandwidth-impossible. The classic solution is multicast — the engine publishes each update once, and the network fabric replicates it to all subscribers, so the publisher's cost is independent of subscriber count. Public internet clients, which can't receive raw multicast, are served via fan-out gateways using WebSockets, fed from the same multicast feed. Subscribers reconstruct the book locally by applying deltas in seq order, and use periodic full snapshots to recover from any gap.

Under the hood: sequenced deltas

Every market-data message carries the same seq as the event that produced it. A subscriber that sees seq jump from 441 to 443 knows it dropped 442 and requests a re-sync or applies the next snapshot. Tagging market data with the engine's sequence numbers is what lets thousands of independent consumers stay perfectly consistent with the authoritative book without ever talking to the engine directly.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you keep market data consistent with the book, and why not just query the engine?" Answer: the engine is single-threaded and on the critical path — letting subscribers query it would destroy its latency. Instead it pushes sequenced deltas; consumers maintain their own copy and self-heal from snapshots on gaps. This decouples read load from the matcher entirely.

10 Wrap-up

Scaling & what breaks at 10x

Step back and assemble the whole system, then stress it. End to end: clients hit gateways (auth + pre-trade risk + idempotency); accepted orders go to the sequencer for the target symbol, which stamps seq and journals the event durably; the single-threaded matcher for that symbol folds the event into its in-memory order book, producing trades; trades and book deltas flow out via market-data fan-out and to clearing/settlement; a hot standby shadows the journal for failover.

Where 10x hurts first

  • A single hot symbol outgrows one core. This is the hardest limit, because you can't shard a symbol's book without breaking the single-writer guarantee. Mitigations: a faster core and tighter code, splitting an instrument into independent micro-markets where the product allows, or accepting the core's ceiling as the venue's true capacity for that pair. Be honest that this is a real wall.
  • Journal write throughput. At 10x event volume the durable log becomes a bottleneck. Use NVMe, batch fsyncs (group commit), and partition the journal per symbol so each shard writes independently.
  • Market-data fan-out. 10x subscribers is exactly why multicast exists — publisher cost stays flat. The pressure moves to the WebSocket fan-out gateways for internet clients, which scale out horizontally and stateless-ly.
  • Recovery time. A 10x-bigger log means longer replays — counter it with more frequent snapshots and parallel snapshot loading, not by replaying from genesis.
  • Cross-symbol fairness during failover. When a shard fails over, its symbol pauses briefly. Keep the standby hot and the snapshot recent so the pause is sub-second.

Failure modes, summarized

  • Matcher process crash: standby promoted, book rebuilt from snapshot + journal tail. No trades lost (journal-before-match).
  • Journal disk failure: replicated log (RF=3, acks=all) survives a node loss.
  • Sequencer failure: the sequencer is the single writer; its journal is the recovery anchor, and election promotes a replica that resumes numbering from the last persisted seq with no gaps or reuse.
  • Subscriber falls behind: detected via the seq gap; the subscriber re-syncs from a snapshot — the engine is never affected.
★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole design compresses to one sentence: funnel every order through one sequencer to get a total order, fold that ordered log into an in-memory book with a single lock-free thread per symbol, journal the log so you can replay it to recover, and fan trades out cheaply with multicast. Determinism is the thread that ties fairness, recovery, and auditing together — keep returning to it.
