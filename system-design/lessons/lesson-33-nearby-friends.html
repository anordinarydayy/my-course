Design Nearby Friends

Nearby Friends takes proximity search and adds a brutal twist: every user is a moving dot, locations change every few seconds, and the answer must be pushed to interested friends in real time. Under the hood it stops being a query problem and becomes a high-frequency ingestion plus pub/sub fan-out problem layered on top of a geo-index.

00 The problem

Why this is hard

Imagine you are at a hawker centre in Singapore and you open the "Nearby Friends" tab in a super-app like a Grab- or Shopee-style social layer. You expect to see a short list of friends within, say, 5 km — and you expect that list to shift in real time. When your friend leaves their office in Raffles Place and rides toward Marina Bay, their distance to you should tick down on your screen within seconds, with no refresh button. When they cross out of range, they should silently disappear.

That single sentence hides two genuinely hard sub-problems stacked on top of each other:

  • High-frequency writes. A location service that backs a feature like this might track millions of users, each emitting a fresh GPS fix every few seconds. That is a relentless firehose of updates, not the occasional read of a static dataset.
  • Real-time fan-out. Each of those updates is not a private write — it must be delivered to a (potentially long) list of friends who care, and only to the ones who are both nearby and online right now.

This is the contrast the interviewer is fishing for. In the previous lesson (proximity search / "find restaurants near me") the system answers a one-shot query: the client asks "what is near coordinate X?" and the server runs a lookup against a geo-index and returns. The data is comparatively static, and the read pattern is pull-based. Nearby Friends inverts almost everything: the data is volatile, the dominant operation is a write, and delivery is push-based over a persistent connection. Get this framing out loud in the first two minutes and you have already shown the interviewer you understand the assignment.

Why it matters

Almost every hard decision downstream — pub/sub instead of polling, ephemeral storage with a TTL instead of durable rows, throttling the update rate, capping the fan-out — traces straight back to these two pressures: a write firehose and a real-time delivery requirement. Keep returning to them.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Resist the urge to draw. The first move is to bound the problem with questions, then state the assumptions you will design against. Here is the set I would walk through.

Functional requirements

  • Continuous nearby list. A user sees the subset of their friends currently within a radius — call it the search radius, default 5 km — along with each friend's distance and a rough last-seen time.
  • Real-time updates. As friends move, the distances refresh and friends enter or leave the list within a few seconds, with no manual reload.
  • Bidirectional and friends-only. Location is shared only between mutual friends who have opted in. This is not a "find any stranger near me" feature; that consent boundary is also the thing that bounds our fan-out.
  • Online presence. A friend who is offline (app closed, no connection) does not appear as a live dot, though we may show a stale "last seen near…" hint.

Non-functional requirements

  • Low end-to-end latency. From a friend's phone emitting a new location to your screen updating, target a few seconds at most. This is a soft real-time system.
  • Eventual consistency is fine. Nobody is harmed if your view of a friend's position lags by a second or is briefly stale. We will trade strict freshness for scalability all day long.
  • Massive write throughput. The location-update path is the hot path; it must absorb a firehose without falling over.
  • High availability. A dropped connection or a dead node should degrade gracefully (a slightly stale list), never crash the feature.
  • Privacy & control. Users must be able to turn sharing off instantly; locations must not be retained longer than needed.

Assumptions I will design against

I will assume a large super-app footprint: 100 million daily active users of the broader app, of which roughly 10 million have Nearby Friends switched on and open at peak. I will assume an average user has around 400 friends, but only a small fraction are nearby and online at any moment. The client emits a location every ~10 seconds while the feature is active. These are the numbers I will carry into the estimation step — and I will say out loud that they are assumptions, so the interviewer can correct them.

Interview tip

The single most important clarifying question here is: "are we pushing updates in real time, or is the client polling?" If you assume polling, you have designed the wrong system. Confirm push early — it justifies the entire pub/sub and websocket half of the architecture.

02 Estimation

Back-of-envelope estimation

The numbers exist to prove one point: the write path dominates, and the fan-out is the thing that can explode. Let me size both.

Location-update write QPS

With 10 million concurrent active users each sending a fix every 10 seconds:

# incoming location updates
10,000,000 users / 10 s  =  1,000,000 updates/sec  (1M write QPS, steady)
peak factor ~2×          ≈  2,000,000 updates/sec

One million writes per second, sustained, is already a serious ingestion problem. This is why the design leans on an in-memory store and why we will talk about sampling and batching the updates rather than treating every fix as sacred.

Fan-out (the dangerous number)

Each location update may need to be delivered to friends who are nearby and online. The fan-out multiplier is what matters. If, naively, each update fanned out to every online friend (say 40 of 400 are online), we would get:

1,000,000 updates/s × 40 online friends = 40,000,000 pushes/sec

That is the cliff. Forty million websocket messages per second is a different and much larger system. The whole back half of this lesson — push only to friends who are both nearby and online, subscribe by region, throttle and batch — exists to drag that 40M down by one or two orders of magnitude. In practice only a handful of a user's friends are within 5 km at any time, so the realistic delivered fan-out is closer to 1–3 per update, giving low single-digit millions of pushes per second. We will spend a whole section making sure the realistic case stays realistic.

Storage

The live location of a user is tiny: a key, two floats for lat/long, and a timestamp — roughly 40–60 bytes. For 10M active users that is well under 1 GB of hot state. The point is not the size; it is that this state is ephemeral — it is constantly overwritten and is worthless once stale, so it belongs in memory with a short expiry, not on durable disk.

Under the hood — why these numbers shape everything

Notice the asymmetry: storage is trivially small, but write throughput and fan-out are enormous. That tells you immediately that the bottleneck is movement of data (ingestion + delivery), not volume of data at rest. A junior answer over-indexes on database choice; a strong answer spends its time on the ingestion tier, the pub/sub mesh, and fan-out control.

03 Contract

API & data model

Go past the bullet points and sketch the actual contract. There are two surfaces: the upstream the client uses to report its own location, and the downstream push the client receives about friends.

Upstream: reporting my location

Because updates are so frequent, the client holds a long-lived WebSocket to a connection server and streams location frames over it, rather than paying TCP/TLS handshake cost on an HTTP request every 10 seconds. A frame looks like:

// client → server, over the persistent WebSocket
{
  "type": "loc_update",
  "user_id": "u_88213",
  "lat": 1.28437,
  "lng": 103.85148,
  "ts": 1717142400          // unix seconds, client clock
}

Downstream: updates about my friends

The server pushes a compact frame whenever a nearby friend moves, enters range, or leaves range:

// server → client, over the same WebSocket
{
  "type": "friend_update",
  "friend_id": "u_41902",
  "lat": 1.28051,
  "lng": 103.85972,
  "distance_m": 940,
  "event": "moved"      // "moved" | "entered" | "left"
}

There is also a small REST surface for the slow-changing concerns — fetching the initial nearby snapshot on app open, toggling sharing, and managing the friend graph:

GET  /v1/nearby?radius_m=5000        // initial snapshot on open
PUT  /v1/sharing  {"enabled": false}  // kill switch, takes effect instantly
GET  /v1/friends                     // the consent / friend graph

The data this implies

Three distinct stores, each chosen for its access pattern — this separation is itself an interview point:

  • Ephemeral location store (hot, in-memory). A key-value entry per user, overwritten on every update, expiring on a TTL. Conceptually user_id → {lat, lng, ts}. This is also where the geo-index lives.
  • Presence / subscription state. Which users are online, which connection server holds their socket, and which channels they subscribe to. Volatile, tied to connection lifetime.
  • Durable friend graph (cold). The mutual-consent edges, in a regular database. This changes rarely relative to locations, so it can be a sharded relational store or a key-value/graph store.
-- durable friend graph (rarely changes), e.g. PostgreSQL / Cassandra
friendship(user_id, friend_id, status, created_at)
  PRIMARY KEY (user_id, friend_id)   -- adjacency, sharded by user_id

-- hot location, e.g. Redis with per-key expiry
SET   loc:{user_id}  "{lat},{lng},{ts}"   EX 30        # 30s TTL
GEOADD geo:{cell}    {lng} {lat}  {user_id}            # geo-index per region cell
Interviewer follow-up

Expect: "why split the friend graph from the location store?" Answer: their lifetimes and access patterns are opposite. The graph is read often, written rarely, and must survive restarts (durable). Locations are written constantly, read by fan-out, and worthless after seconds (ephemeral, in-memory). Forcing both into one database makes you pay durability costs on a firehose that does not need it.

04 The big picture

High-level architecture

Here is the whole system in one frame, named tier by tier. The shape to remember is a diamond: updates funnel in through ingestion, spread out through a pub/sub mesh, and land on the connection servers that hold each friend's socket.

  • Connection servers hold the persistent WebSocket for each online user (tie this to Lesson 25 on long-lived connections). They receive a user's outgoing location frames and deliver incoming friend updates. They are stateful with respect to which user is on which socket, but hold no business logic.
  • Location ingestion tier is a stateless service that receives each location frame, writes it to the hot store, updates the geo-index, and decides who needs to hear about it.
  • Hot location store + geo-index (Redis-style) keeps the current position of every active user with a short TTL, and answers "who is near coordinate X?".
  • Pub/sub mesh is the routing fabric. A user's new location is published to channels; connection servers subscribe to the channels for the friends their users care about, and forward what arrives.
  • Friend-graph service answers "who are this user's mutual friends?" from the durable store, usually behind a cache.

The end-to-end flow in one breath: a client streams its location to its connection server → the connection server forwards it to the ingestion tier → ingestion writes the hot store and geo-index → ingestion publishes the new location to the right channels → connection servers subscribed to those channels receive it → they push a friend_update down the appropriate sockets. The next section animates exactly that.

05 The hot path

The push flow, step by step

This is the heart of the system: what happens, mechanically, when one friend moves. Walk it slowly, because every later optimisation is a tweak to one of these steps. Let "Friend A" be your friend riding through Marina Bay, and "You" are the one watching.

  1. A moves and emits a fix. A's phone produces a new GPS reading and sends a loc_update frame over its WebSocket to A's connection server.
  2. Ingestion records it. The frame reaches the ingestion tier, which overwrites loc:{A} in the hot store (resetting its TTL) and updates the geo-index so the index now reflects A's new cell.
  3. Find who cares. Ingestion (or a routing component) determines A's nearby, online friends — the intersection of A's friend graph, the geo-index around A, and the presence table.
  4. Publish. A's new location is published to the channels those friends are listening on.
  5. Push. The connection server holding your socket is subscribed to A's channel; it receives the message and pushes a friend_update down to your app, which animates A's dot.
Why pub/sub and not direct delivery?

Ingestion does not know which connection server holds your socket, and that mapping changes constantly as users reconnect to different nodes. A pub/sub indirection decouples the publisher (ingestion, who only knows "channel for A") from the subscriber (whichever connection server currently holds an interested friend). Add or remove connection servers freely; the channels stay stable.

06 Holding the sockets

Connection servers & subscriptions

The connection server is the component that terminates each user's long-lived WebSocket. (If you have done Lesson 25 on persistent connections, this is the same machinery.) Each online user is "pinned" to exactly one connection server for the life of their connection. That server is responsible for two things: forwarding the user's outgoing location frames upstream, and delivering incoming friend updates downstream.

How a subscription gets set up

When you open the app and connect, your connection server must arrange to hear about your nearby friends. The mechanism, step by step:

  • On connect, the server authenticates you and marks you online in the presence table, recording presence:{you} → conn_server_id.
  • It fetches your friend graph (from the friend-graph service, cached) and your initial geo-cell from your last known location.
  • It subscribes to the pub/sub channels that carry the friends you care about. The cleanest scheme is to subscribe by region/channel: a channel per geo-cell, e.g. geo:cell_4a91. Your connection server subscribes to the cells around you; any friend who publishes into one of those cells is automatically routed to you.

Subscribing by region rather than by individual friend is a deliberate choice. If every connection server subscribed to one channel per friend, a server holding 50,000 users each with 400 friends would manage up to 20 million subscriptions. Subscribing per geo-cell collapses that: a connection server subscribes to the handful of cells its users are physically in, and filters the incoming stream down to "is this publisher actually a friend, and within radius?" before pushing.

Under the hood — moving across cells

As you move, your own location updates shift you into new geo-cells. Your connection server must then re-subscribe: drop the channels for cells you have left, add channels for cells you have entered. This is a continuous, cheap bookkeeping operation, and it is why cells should be sized so a normal walking/riding user crosses them every minute or two, not every few seconds.

Interviewer follow-up

Expect: "how does the connection server know which sockets to push to when a message arrives on a channel?" Each server keeps an in-memory map from geo-cell → set of local user sockets interested in that cell. When a message lands on a subscribed channel, it looks up that set, applies the friend-and-radius filter per user, and writes to the matching sockets. No cross-server lookup is needed on the hot path.

07 The real bottleneck

Bounding the fan-out

This is the section the interview is really about, and it is where the 40-million-pushes-per-second cliff from the estimation lives. Three levers, applied together, keep fan-out bounded. Think of them as three filters in series — each one strips the firehose down further.

Filter 1 — nearby AND online only

Never push a friend's movement to someone who is not currently subscribed to the area and online. The intersection nearby ∩ online ∩ friends is tiny: of 400 friends, maybe 40 are online, and of those maybe 1–3 are within 5 km of you right now. By publishing into geo-cell channels and letting connection servers subscribe only to nearby cells, an update naturally reaches only the friends who are co-located. A friend in Jakarta receives nothing when you move in Singapore, because their connection server never subscribed to your cell.

Filter 2 — throttle the update frequency

The client does not need to emit at the maximum GPS rate, and you do not need to forward every fix. Throttling caps how often a user's location is propagated — e.g. at most one published update per 10 seconds per user, even if the phone produces fixes more often. A stationary user (sitting in a café) can fall back to a much slower heartbeat or stop publishing entirely until they move past a minimum distance threshold. This is the single biggest knob: halving the publish rate halves the entire fan-out downstream.

Filter 3 — batch the pushes

Batching coalesces multiple friend updates destined for the same user into one message on a short timer (say every 1–2 seconds), instead of one websocket frame per friend per move. If five of your nearby friends all move within the same second, your phone receives one frame with five deltas, not five frames. This slashes per-message overhead and the number of socket writes — the thing that actually saturates connection servers.

Aggressive throttling & batching

  • Fan-out stays bounded; connection servers survive
  • Far fewer socket writes and far less bandwidth
  • Cost: dots move in slightly choppy, delayed steps

Push every fix immediately

  • Buttery-smooth, near-instant dot movement
  • Cost: fan-out can explode in dense areas
  • Cost: connection servers and bandwidth saturate first
Interviewer follow-up — "how do you keep the fan-out bounded?"

The crisp answer is three words: filter, throttle, batch. Filter so you only ever push to friends who are nearby and online; throttle so a single user can't publish more than N times a window; batch so a receiver gets one coalesced frame instead of one per mover. Then add: in pathological hot regions (a stadium), cap the visible list and degrade update frequency further rather than letting a single cell take down a connection server.

08 Storage

Ephemeral location & the TTL

The location store is deliberately forgetful. A user's position is interesting for a few seconds and then meaningless — there is no reason to durably persist where someone was at 14:03:21. So we store live location in a fast in-memory key-value store (Redis is the canonical choice) and attach a time-to-live (TTL) to every entry.

How the TTL actually works

Each time a user reports a location, ingestion writes loc:{user_id} and resets the key's expiry — for example EX 30 for a 30-second TTL. As long as the user keeps moving and reporting, the key is continually refreshed and stays alive. The moment they stop reporting — app closed, phone in a tunnel, battery dead — no more refreshes arrive, and after 30 seconds the store automatically evicts the key. From that point the geo-index no longer returns them, and friends watching them see them go stale and then drop off. We never had to write any explicit "user went offline" logic for the location half; expiry does it for free.

The same idea governs the geo-index entry: a user who has not refreshed should not keep showing up as "near" forever. Tie the index entry's lifetime to the same TTL, or sweep stale members out as part of the read.

Choosing the TTL

The TTL is a freshness-vs-flicker trade-off. Too short (say 5 s) and a user on a flaky connection flickers in and out of the list every time a packet is dropped. Too long (say 5 min) and a friend who closed their app keeps appearing as a ghost long after they left. A value of 2–4× the publish interval (so ~30 s for a 10 s publish cadence) tolerates a missed update or two without flicker, while still expiring a genuinely-gone user quickly.

Interviewer follow-up — "how fresh are locations and how do stale ones expire?"

Locations are fresh to within the publish interval plus throttle/batch delay — a few seconds. Stale ones expire passively via the per-key TTL: no heartbeat, no explicit tombstone, no background reaper required for correctness. State the number (e.g. 30 s) and the reasoning (a small multiple of the publish cadence) and you have answered it cleanly.

09 Scaling out

Scaling & hot regions

One million writes per second does not fit on one box, and neither does the pub/sub mesh. Scaling here is mostly about partitioning by geography and coping with the fact that geography is wildly uneven.

Sharding the location store and pub/sub by region

Partition the hot store and the channel space by geo-cell. All locations and channels for a given cell live on the shard that owns that cell. This keeps a publish-and-subscribe interaction local: a friend moving in a cell publishes to that cell's shard, and the connection servers interested in that cell subscribe to the same shard. Cross-shard traffic only happens at cell boundaries, which is rare per-user.

Connection servers scale horizontally and statelessly with respect to business logic — add more behind a load balancer as concurrent users grow. Ingestion is stateless and scales the same way. The hot store scales via clustering (e.g. Redis Cluster) keyed on geo-cell.

The hot-region problem

Geography is not uniform. A National Day fireworks crowd at Marina Bay, a football match at the National Stadium, or a Friday-night Orchard Road throng packs an enormous number of users — and their friends — into one or two geo-cells. That cell's shard and the channels for it become a hot spot: huge subscribe lists, huge fan-out, the works.

  • Variable cell sizing. Use a hierarchical grid (geohash prefixes, S2 cells, or H3 hexes) and subdivide dense cells into smaller ones so each sub-cell holds a manageable population, while sparse rural cells stay large.
  • Cap the visible set. In a stadium with 500 nearby friends, no user needs all 500 dots updating live. Cap the rendered list (e.g. the 50 closest) and degrade update frequency for the rest.
  • Replicate the hot channel. Spread the subscriber load for a hot cell across replicas of that channel so no single pub/sub node carries the whole crowd.
Why adaptive cells beat one fixed size

If cells are large enough for a stadium, rural users see almost nobody and the index is useless. If they are small enough for the countryside, a city block becomes thousands of microscopic cells with churn at every step. A hierarchical, adaptive grid lets the same system serve a quiet kampong and a packed CBD by choosing the resolution per area — the standard trick behind geohash/S2/H3 indexing.

10 When it breaks

Failure modes & what breaks at 10×

An interviewer will push on resilience. Walk the obvious failures and how the design recovers — most of them are absorbed by choices we already made.

A connection server dies

Every user pinned to it loses their socket. The clients detect the dropped connection and reconnect, landing (via the load balancer) on a different connection server. On reconnect the new server re-runs the subscribe flow: mark online, fetch friends, subscribe to nearby cells. Meanwhile the dead users' location keys expire by TTL, so from friends' perspectives they briefly go stale and then refresh once reconnected. No central state was lost because connection-to-user mapping was always rebuildable from the reconnect, not from a durable record.

A pub/sub node dies

Messages routed through it are lost in flight — which is acceptable, because location updates are disposable. The very next update (a few seconds later) supersedes the lost one. Subscribers re-subscribe to a healthy node for the affected cells. This is why a fire-and-forget pub/sub (Redis pub/sub style) is a reasonable fit here: we explicitly do not want the cost of durable, replayable delivery for data that is stale within seconds.

The hot store has a hiccup

If the location store is briefly unavailable, ingestion can shed load (drop updates) and the feature degrades to slightly staler dots — it should never block the rest of the super-app. Replicate the store per shard so a single node loss promotes a replica rather than losing a region.

A client on a flaky network

Dropped update frames just mean a missed refresh; the TTL is sized to tolerate a couple of misses without flicker. If the gap exceeds the TTL, the user correctly drops off friends' lists and reappears when connectivity returns. No special handling needed — the TTL already encodes the policy.

What breaks at 10×

Push the assumptions to 100M concurrent and the strain shows up first in two places:

  • Fan-out, again. Even with filter/throttle/batch, 10× the users in dense cities means hot cells get hotter. The fix is more aggressive adaptive subdivision and harder caps on visible-list size and update cadence in dense cells.
  • Connection-server socket count. Holding tens of millions of concurrent WebSockets means many more connection-server instances and careful tuning of per-process file-descriptor and memory limits. The pinning and reconnect logic stays the same; you just run far more of them and shard the presence table to match.

What does not break is storage — live location stays tiny even at 10×, reaffirming that this system is bound by data movement, never data volume.

Interviewer follow-up — "how is this different from a proximity search?"

The cleanest contrast: proximity search is a pull over comparatively static data (one query, one geo-index lookup, one response). Nearby Friends is a push over volatile data — a continuous ingestion firehose, a pub/sub fan-out, persistent connections, ephemeral TTL'd state, and presence. The geo-index is shared between the two, but everything wrapped around it — writes, delivery, connections, expiry — is what makes this the harder design.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: ingest a location firehose into an ephemeral, TTL'd geo-index, then fan updates out through a pub/sub mesh to the connection servers holding friends who are nearby and online — and keep that fan-out bounded with filter, throttle, and batch. If you can draw the diamond (in through ingestion, out through pub/sub) and explain why each piece exists, you can carry this interview.
