37Lesson · System Design Interview

Design Metrics Monitoring & Alerting

A monitoring system swallows numbers from thousands of hosts, stores them as time series, paints dashboards, and pages on-call when a rule trips — and it must stay up even while the systems it watches are falling over. The interview turns on three choices: pull versus push collection, a purpose-built time-series store, and an alert pipeline that warns you without burying you.

About these notes: original study material on metrics monitoring and alerting — a standard, widely-documented infrastructure topic. The explanations, numbers, examples, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Two diagrams are interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow.
00 The problem

Why monitor at all?

Imagine GrabPay processes a payment every few milliseconds across hundreds of services, and at 2 a.m. the checkout success rate quietly slips from 99.6% to 91%. No exception is thrown, no service crashes — a downstream bank connector is just timing out a little more often. Without a monitoring system, nobody knows until customer-support tickets pile up the next morning. With one, an alert fires within a minute and a human is paged before most users ever notice.

A metrics monitoring and alerting system is the always-on observer that turns the raw operational state of your fleet into numbers you can graph, query, and trigger on. It answers three different questions for three different audiences:

  • Is it broken right now? — for on-call engineers who need a page the moment an SLO is at risk.
  • Why did it break? — for whoever is debugging, who needs to slice latency by region, version, and endpoint.
  • Is it trending toward broken? — for capacity planners watching disk and CPU creep up over weeks.

Crucially, this system is infrastructure for infrastructure. It must be more reliable than anything it watches, because the one moment you most need it — a partial outage — is exactly when it is under the most load and most likely to share a failure with the thing that broke. That single constraint shapes nearly every design decision that follows.

Scope check

This lesson is about metrics — numeric, regularly-sampled measurements like request rate, error count, and p99 latency. That is deliberately narrower than full observability, which also covers logs (discrete text events) and traces (the path of one request across services). Metrics are cheap and aggregatable; logs and traces are richer but far heavier. Say this out loud early so the interviewer knows you are scoping on purpose.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements

Before sketching boxes, narrow the problem. Monitoring means very different things at different scales, and the interviewer is watching whether you ask. I would settle the following.

Functional requirements

  • Collect metrics from many hosts and services — counters, gauges, and histograms tagged with dimensions (region, service, instance).
  • Store them efficiently as time series with configurable retention.
  • Query them: aggregate, filter by tag, and compute rates and percentiles over time ranges.
  • Visualize them on dashboards that refresh in near real time.
  • Alert on rules — when a query crosses a threshold for some duration, notify on-call through email, Slack, PagerDuty, or SMS.

Non-functional requirements

  • High write throughput. Writes vastly outnumber reads. Millions of new data points per second is normal; most are never read by a human, only scanned by alert rules.
  • Low query latency for dashboards — a graph should render in well under a second.
  • Reliability and independence. The stack must outlive what it monitors and share as little failure domain as possible with it.
  • Scalability to grow with the fleet, and bounded cost as data ages.
  • Tolerance for small inaccuracy. A dropped sample or a slightly approximate percentile is fine; we are not doing accounting.

Explicit non-goals

I would push log aggregation and distributed tracing out of scope, and assume metrics are operational, not billing-grade — that last assumption is what frees us to drop data and downsample aggressively later. State your assumptions plainly: roughly 10,000 hosts, each exposing on the order of 100 to 1,000 metrics, a scrape every 10 seconds, retention of 1 year at decreasing resolution, and a few hundred engineers building dashboards and rules.

Interview tip

The single sentence that frames the whole design: "writes dominate reads by orders of magnitude, data is append-only and time-ordered, and the system must be more available than the systems it watches." Say it now and every later decision — the storage engine, the pull model, the HA story — will look like an obvious consequence.

02 Estimation

Back-of-envelope sizing

Numbers turn vague requirements into design pressure. The key quantity in monitoring is the count of active time series — every unique combination of a metric name and its label values is one series.

Write throughput

Take 10,000 hosts, each emitting 500 metrics, scraped every 10 seconds:

  • Active series ≈ 10,000 × 500 = 5,000,000 series.
  • Data points per second ≈ 5,000,000 / 10s = 500,000 points/sec.
  • At 10x growth — more hosts, finer scrape interval, richer labels — you are staring at 5 million points per second. That number alone rules out a generic relational database.

Storage

A naive data point is a (timestamp, value) pair plus the series identity — say 16 bytes raw. Without compression: 500,000 pts/s × 16 B × 86,400 s/day ≈ 690 GB/day. That is enormous and would dominate cost. The saving grace of time-series data is that it compresses ferociously — consecutive timestamps are evenly spaced and consecutive values barely change. Real engines push each point down to roughly 1 to 2 bytes on average:

  • Compressed ingest ≈ 500,000 × 1.5 B × 86,400 ≈ 65 GB/day of raw-resolution data.
  • That is why retention is tiered: you keep the heavy raw layer for days, not years (covered in step 07).

Read & query load

Reads come from two places: humans loading dashboards (bursty, maybe hundreds of queries per second across the company) and the rule evaluator re-running every alert query on a fixed interval (steady, predictable). With 2,000 alert rules each evaluated every 15 seconds, that is a constant ~130 evaluations/sec — small compared to writes, which confirms the design instinct: optimize the write and scan path first.

Why this matters

The estimation is not arithmetic for its own sake — it is the justification for every later choice. 500k writes/sec and a 100:1 write-to-read ratio is precisely why we reach for a column-compressed, append-optimized time-series database instead of a B-tree-indexed RDBMS, and why we downsample old data instead of keeping everything forever.

03 Contract

API & data model

Two interfaces matter: how a data point looks going in, and how a query reads it back out. Get the data model right and the rest of the system falls into place.

The shape of a metric

A single observation is a data point: a metric name, a set of labels (key–value tag pairs that give the metric its dimensions), a timestamp, and a floating-point value. The metric name plus the exact set of label values defines one time series.

# one data point — name{labels} value timestamp
http_request_latency_ms{service="checkout", region="ap-southeast-1",
                        instance="i-0af3", le="500"}  4123  1717142400

Change any label value — a different region, a different instance — and you have a brand-new series. That is the engine of both the system's power (slice anything by any dimension) and its biggest danger (cardinality explosion, step 10).

Write path: a thin ingest endpoint

In a pull model, each target simply exposes its current numbers over HTTP and the system scrapes them:

# GET /metrics on a target — text exposition, scraped every 10s
http_requests_total{service="checkout",code="200"} 480231
http_requests_total{service="checkout",code="500"} 142
process_resident_memory_bytes{service="checkout"} 734003200

In a push model, short-lived jobs send points to a gateway instead:

POST /api/v1/push
# body: batched (series, timestamp, value) tuples, often protobuf + snappy
{ "series": "batch_job_duration_ms{job=\"daily-reco\"}",
  "samples": [ [1717142400, 88210] ] }

Read path: a query API

Queries select series by name and label matchers, then aggregate over a time range. A purpose-built query language expresses rates and percentiles directly:

# 5xx error ratio for checkout over the last 5 minutes, per region
sum by(region) (rate(http_requests_total{service="checkout",code=~"5.."}[5m]))
  /
sum by(region) (rate(http_requests_total{service="checkout"}[5m]))

The HTTP surface is small and read-heavy on the query side:

  • GET /api/v1/query?query=…&time=… — an instant value, used by alert rules and single-stat panels.
  • GET /api/v1/query_range?query=…&start=…&end=…&step=… — a series of points for a graph.
  • GET /api/v1/labels and /label/{name}/values — autocomplete for dashboard builders.
Interviewer follow-up

Expect "why labels instead of separate metrics per region?" Because dimensions you can aggregate over are far more flexible: one http_requests_total with a region label lets you sum across all regions or split by one of them at query time, whereas baking the region into the metric name (http_requests_total_sg) forces you to know every dimension up front and makes cross-region rollups painful.

04 The big picture

High-level architecture

Now assemble the pipeline end to end. Five stages move a number from a host to a human: collection, storage, query, visualization, and alerting. Each is a separate concern so it can scale and fail independently.

  • A collector gathers data points — by scraping targets (pull) or receiving them at a gateway (push).
  • The time-series database (TSDB) persists the points, compressed and indexed by series.
  • A query service answers range and instant queries from the TSDB.
  • A dashboard / visualization layer (think Grafana) reads the query service.
  • A rule evaluator periodically runs alert queries; when one trips, it hands an alert to the alert manager, which groups, deduplicates, and routes it to on-call.

Two design instincts are baked in here. First, the write side (collector → TSDB) and the read side (query → dashboards, rule eval → alert manager) are decoupled, so a flood of dashboard queries can never stall ingestion. Second, the alert manager is a distinct component from the rule evaluator: evaluation decides whether something is wrong; the manager decides who hears about it and how loudly. Keeping them apart is what lets you suppress an alert storm without touching evaluation logic.

05 Deep dive · collection

Pull vs push collection

The first real fork in the design: how do data points get from a target into the collector? There are two models, and the choice has consequences that ripple through service discovery, security, and failure handling.

Pull: the scraper fetches

In a pull model, each target exposes a /metrics endpoint, and a central scraper fetches it on a schedule — every 10 seconds, say. This is the Prometheus model. Under the hood the scraper holds a list of targets (populated by service discovery — Kubernetes, Consul, or a cloud API), and a scheduler loops over that list firing HTTP GETs, parsing the text response, attaching the scrape timestamp, and appending to the TSDB.

The quiet superpower of pull is that the scraper controls its own load: it decides how often and how many targets to hit, so it can never be overwhelmed by a misbehaving target spraying data. It also gives you a free liveness signal — if a scrape fails, the target is probably down, and you can synthesize an up == 0 metric and alert on that directly.

Push: the agent sends

In a push model, an agent on each host (StatsD, the OpenTelemetry collector, Telegraf) sends data points to a gateway. This wins precisely where pull struggles: short-lived jobs that finish before any scrape could reach them (a nightly Shopee recommendation batch that runs for 40 seconds), and targets behind a firewall or NAT that the central scraper cannot reach but which can dial outward.

The hybrid in practice

Real systems usually do both: pull for long-lived services where discovery is easy, and a push gateway as a bridge for ephemeral jobs — the job pushes to the gateway, and the scraper pulls the gateway like any other target. That keeps the scraper's clean pull semantics while still capturing work that vanishes too fast to scrape.

Pull

  • Scraper controls load — can't be flooded by a target
  • Free up/down liveness signal per target
  • Service discovery centralizes the target list
  • Struggles with short-lived jobs and firewalled targets
  • Needs network reachability to every target

Push

  • Captures ephemeral jobs and serverless functions
  • Works through NAT/firewalls — agent dials out
  • No central reachability requirement
  • A buggy client can flood the gateway (needs rate limits)
  • No automatic liveness — silence is ambiguous
Interviewer follow-up

"Pull or push, and why?" Default to pull for the operational clarity — pacing and built-in liveness — then add a push gateway for the cases pull genuinely can't cover. The wrong answer is dogma in either direction; the right answer names the trade-off and picks pull as the backbone because the scraper paces itself and gives you up/down for free.

06 Deep dive · storage

The time-series store

This is the heart of the system. A time-series database (TSDB) is a store specialized for data that is append-only, timestamped, and queried in ranges. Our estimation said 500k points/sec and a 100:1 write-to-read ratio — so the store must absorb a firehose of writes and answer "give me this series over this window" fast.

Why a generic RDBMS struggles

You could put metrics in PostgreSQL — and at small scale people do. But it breaks down for three concrete reasons:

  • B-tree write amplification. A relational index is a B-tree tuned for random reads and updates. Time-series writes are a relentless stream of new rows; every insert rebalances the index, and at hundreds of thousands of inserts per second the index maintenance becomes the bottleneck.
  • Row storage wastes the structure. A row store keeps each (timestamp, value, all-the-labels) tuple together, so it can't exploit the fact that timestamps march evenly and values barely change. It stores 16 honest bytes where a TSDB stores one or two.
  • No native rollups or retention. Downsampling and expiry are bolt-ons you have to script, not first-class features.

How a TSDB works under the hood

The standard architecture is a log-structured merge (LSM) tree feeding compressed, time-bucketed blocks:

  • In-memory head block. Recent points land in a per-series in-memory buffer plus a write-ahead log (WAL) on disk for crash recovery. Writes are sequential appends — no random I/O, no B-tree rebalancing.
  • Periodic flush to immutable blocks. Every couple of hours the head is sealed and written to disk as a read-only block covering a fixed time range, with its own index from series → label values.
  • Columnar, delta-compressed chunks. Within a block, each series' points are stored together. Timestamps are delta-of-delta encoded (the gap between scrape intervals is nearly constant, so the second derivative is usually zero — a single bit), and values are XOR-compressed against the previous value (a gauge that barely moves XORs to mostly zero bits). This is what drives 16 bytes down to ~1.5.
  • Background compaction. Small blocks are merged into larger ones, which is also the natural moment to apply downsampling and drop expired data.

Real systems & knobs

The named options: Prometheus (single-node TSDB, the de-facto standard), Thanos and Cortex / Mimir (turn Prometheus into a horizontally-scalable, long-term store backed by object storage like S3), InfluxDB, VictoriaMetrics, TimescaleDB (a time-series layer on Postgres), and for the largest fleets columnar stores like ClickHouse. Knobs you tune: scrape/sample interval, block duration, WAL flush policy, compaction schedule, and the retention period per resolution tier.

TSDB (LSM + columnar)

  • Sequential appends — no index rebalancing
  • Delta + XOR compression → ~1.5 B/point
  • Native retention and downsampling
  • Fast time-range scans of a single series

Generic RDBMS

  • B-tree write amplification at high insert rates
  • Row storage wastes the regular structure
  • Retention/rollups are manual scripts
  • Strong consistency & joins you don't need here
Interviewer follow-up

"How do you store time series cheaply?" Lead with the two compression tricks — delta-of-delta on timestamps, XOR on values — because they exploit the defining property of metrics: regular sampling and slowly-changing values. Then mention tiered retention and object-storage offload. If you can say "16 bytes raw collapses to about 1.5 because the second derivative of the timestamp is almost always zero," you have shown you understand the mechanism, not just the buzzword.

07 Deep dive · cost

Downsampling & retention

Compression alone is not enough. At 65 GB/day of compressed raw data, a year is over 23 TB per cluster — and almost none of those fine-grained points are ever read once they are a few days old. Nobody graphs 10-second resolution from eight months ago; they want a daily trend. So we trade resolution for cost as data ages.

What downsampling is

Downsampling (also called rollup) replaces many fine-grained points with fewer coarse ones by aggregating over time buckets. Take all the 10-second points in a 5-minute window and store their min, max, sum, count, and avg as a single 5-minute point. A graph spanning a month renders from the 5-minute or 1-hour tier and looks identical to the human eye, while reading a fraction of the data.

How tiered retention works under the hood

A background job — usually folded into compaction — reads sealed raw blocks and writes downsampled blocks into a separate, coarser tier, then lets the raw tier expire by dropping whole blocks once they age past their retention. A query is routed to the finest tier that still covers its time range and step. Asking for the last hour hits raw; asking for the last quarter hits the hourly tier automatically.

The cost win is dramatic. Going from 10-second to 5-minute buckets is a 30x reduction in point count; to 1-hour is 360x. A year that would cost 23 TB at raw resolution costs a small fraction once 99% of it lives in the hourly tier — often offloaded to cheap object storage like S3, with only the recent raw tier on fast local disk.

Under the hood: choosing the rollup function

Not every metric downsamples the same way. A counter (monotonically increasing, like total requests) is rolled up by keeping the last value and computing rates; a gauge (a momentary level, like memory) keeps min/max/avg; a histogram rolls up by summing per-bucket counts so percentiles still work. Store enough aggregates (count + sum lets you recompute averages later) so the coarse tier can still answer the queries you care about — losing min/max means you can never again see the spike that mattered.

08 Read path

Query & dashboards

Storage is useless without a fast read path. The query service is a stateless layer that parses a query, selects the matching series from the TSDB, and applies aggregation functions over the requested time range.

The aggregation vocabulary

Three families of function cover almost everything you graph:

  • Rate over time — turn a counter into a per-second rate (rate(http_requests_total[5m])). Counters only ever go up, so the raw value is meaningless; the rate of change is what you watch.
  • Aggregation across series — sum, avg, max grouped by a label (sum by(region)(...)), to collapse thousands of per-instance series into one line per region.
  • Quantiles — histogram_quantile(0.99, ...) to read p99 latency out of histogram buckets. This is approximate by design: the percentile is interpolated within a bucket, which is exactly the tolerable inaccuracy we scoped for in step 01.

How a dashboard panel renders

A dashboard panel is just a saved query_range call with a refresh interval. When a Grab on-call engineer opens the "Checkout health" dashboard, the browser fires one range query per panel; the query service selects series, runs the aggregation, and returns a downsampled set of points sized to the panel's pixel width (no point returning 10,000 points for a 600-pixel graph). Caching recent query results and pre-aggregating common dashboards keeps the p99 render under a second even when dozens of engineers reload during an incident.

The dominant tool here is Grafana, which sits in front of any of the TSDBs above and turns saved queries into refreshing panels. The key scaling insight: dashboard load is bursty and human-driven — it spikes during incidents, exactly when the system is already stressed — so the query layer must be horizontally scalable and isolated from the write path, and aggressive result caching is non-negotiable.

Interviewer follow-up

"What happens to your dashboards during a big incident?" Everyone reloads at once, query load spikes, and if the query layer isn't isolated from ingestion you get a feedback loop where monitoring degrades just as you need it most. The answer: separate read and write paths, autoscale the stateless query tier, cache results, and pre-compute the handful of dashboards on-call actually opens first.

09 Deep dive · alerting

The alerting pipeline

Collecting and graphing metrics is half the job; the other half is waking the right person at the right time and not waking them otherwise. The alerting pipeline has two distinct stages, and conflating them is the classic design mistake.

Stage 1 — the rule evaluator

The rule evaluator runs each alert rule on a fixed interval (every 15 seconds, say). A rule is a query plus a threshold plus a duration: "if the 5xx ratio for checkout exceeds 2% for 3 minutes, fire." The for duration matters — it prevents a single noisy scrape from paging anyone. The evaluator queries the TSDB, compares the result to the threshold, and tracks each rule's state machine: inactive → pending (threshold breached but not yet for the full duration) → firing. Only on entering firing does it emit an alert.

# an alert rule
alert: CheckoutHighErrorRate
expr:  sum(rate(http_requests_total{service="checkout",code=~"5.."}[5m]))
      / sum(rate(http_requests_total{service="checkout"}[5m])) > 0.02
for:   3m
labels:   { severity: page, team: payments }
annotations: { summary: "checkout 5xx ratio above 2%" }

Stage 2 — the alert manager

Firing alerts do not go straight to a phone. They flow into the alert manager, whose entire job is to make sure on-call gets signal, not noise. It does four things:

  • Grouping — bundle related alerts into one notification. If a database node dies and 200 services suddenly report errors, on-call gets one grouped page ("payments cluster degraded"), not 200.
  • Deduplication — multiple redundant evaluator replicas may fire the same alert; the manager collapses identical alerts (keyed by their label set) into one.
  • Silencing & inhibition — during a planned deploy you silence expected alerts; inhibition suppresses downstream alerts when their root cause is already firing (if "datacenter unreachable" is active, mute the 50 "service X down" alerts it obviously caused).
  • Routing & escalation — send severity: page to PagerDuty, severity: ticket to a queue, and escalate to the secondary on-call if the primary doesn't acknowledge within N minutes.

Interviewer follow-up

"How do you avoid alert storms?" Name the four alert-manager jobs — grouping, deduplication, silencing/inhibition, routing/escalation — and the for duration on the rule. The deepest version: a single root cause (a dead DB node) should produce one grouped, inhibited page, not a flood, because a flooded on-call ignores everything and the real signal drowns. That is a reliability property, not a UX nicety.

10 Scaling

Cardinality & scaling out

A single-node TSDB tops out somewhere around a few million active series. Past that, two pressures force you to scale, and one of them — cardinality — is the most common way a monitoring system silently kills itself.

The cardinality explosion

Cardinality is the number of distinct time series, and it is the product of every label's distinct values. The danger is multiplicative. Suppose someone adds a user_id label to a latency metric "to debug a customer issue." With 50 million users, a metric that was one series per endpoint becomes 50 million series per endpoint. Memory blows up (the head block holds an index entry per active series), ingestion stalls, and queries crawl. This is the number-one operational incident for monitoring teams.

How to bound it: keep labels to low-cardinality dimensions only (region, service, status code, instance — values in the dozens or hundreds), never put unbounded identifiers (user_id, request_id, raw URLs, email) in labels; high-cardinality identity belongs in logs or traces, not metrics. Enforce it with per-target series limits that reject or drop a target exceeding a cap, so one misconfigured service can't take down ingestion for everyone.

Sharding the TSDB

To go beyond one node, partition by series. A hash on the series identity (metric name + labels) assigns each series to a shard, so all points for a given series always land on the same node and a query for that series hits exactly one shard. This is the same consistent-hashing idea used everywhere — it spreads write load evenly and lets you add nodes by rebalancing a fraction of series.

  • Functional sharding — split by team or service first (payments cluster, ride-hailing cluster), which also isolates blast radius.
  • Hash sharding within a cluster — spread series across nodes for write balance.
  • Fan-out query layer — a query that spans shards (sum across all regions) scatters to each shard and gathers the partial results. Systems like Thanos/Cortex/Mimir do exactly this on top of sharded Prometheus.
Why hash on the whole series

If you sharded only on metric name, every http_requests_total point in the fleet would pile onto one node — a hot shard. Hashing the full identity (name + all label values) spreads even a single popular metric across all shards, while still guaranteeing that any one series stays whole on one node so range scans don't fan out unnecessarily.

11 Resilience

Failure modes & what breaks at 10x

The defining constraint returns: the monitoring system must be more reliable than what it monitors. So name the failure modes explicitly and design for independence.

What happens when a node dies?

  • A scraper dies. You lose visibility into its targets. Fix: run scrapers in redundant pairs that scrape the same targets, and deduplicate at the query/alert layer. A dead scraper means a gap, never a silent blind spot.
  • A TSDB shard dies. Recent in-memory points are recovered from the WAL on restart; older points sit in immutable blocks already on disk (and ideally replicated to object storage). Replicate each shard, or accept a short gap for non-critical data — remember, metrics tolerate small loss.
  • The alert manager dies. This is the scary one — a dead alert manager means no pages, a silent failure exactly when you need it. Run it as a small redundant cluster that gossips state so any replica can dedup and route, and add a dead-man's-switch alert: a rule that is always firing, routed to an external service that pages you if it ever stops arriving — proving the whole pipeline is alive.

Independence from the monitored system

Run monitoring in a separate failure domain: different hosts, ideally a different availability zone or account, with its own DNS and network path. If GrabPay's payment cluster and its monitoring share the same database or the same AZ, a zone outage takes out both — and you are blind during the very incident you most need to see. The principle: never let the watcher and the watched fail together.

What breaks at 10x

  • Single-node TSDB ceiling. 5M series becomes 50M — past any single node. You must shard (step 10) and offload cold data to object storage; this is the jump from vanilla Prometheus to Thanos/Cortex/Mimir.
  • Ingest pipeline saturation. 500k writes/sec becomes 5M. Put a buffer in front of the TSDB — a durable queue like Kafka — so a write spike or a slow flush absorbs into the buffer instead of dropping points or stalling scrapers.
  • Rule evaluation load. Thousands more rules each re-querying the TSDB can swamp the read path. Sharding the rule evaluator by rule group, and reusing recording rules (pre-computed common sub-queries stored as new series), keeps evaluation cheap.
  • Cardinality, again. At 10x the fleet, the temptation to add "just one more label" is 10x stronger. Enforce series limits as a hard guardrail, not a guideline.
Deep-dive the interviewer may push for

Two favorites. Recording rules: if many dashboards and alerts compute the same expensive sub-query, pre-evaluate it on a schedule and store the result as a new series — trading a little storage for a lot of read CPU. The dead-man's switch: "how do you know your monitoring itself is healthy?" The answer — an always-firing heartbeat alert whose absence pages you via an independent channel — shows you've internalized that the watcher needs a watcher.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole system compresses to one arc: collect numbers from a fleet (pull, with a push gateway for the edge cases), pack them into a purpose-built time-series store that exploits regular sampling, downsample as they age to bound cost, and run an alert pipeline whose manager turns a storm of related alerts into one actionable page — all in a failure domain independent of what it watches. If you can whiteboard the five-stage pipeline and defend pull-vs-push, the compression tricks, and the rule-eval-vs-alert-manager split, you can carry the interview.
