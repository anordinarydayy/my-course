07Lesson · System Design Interview

CDN & Content Delivery

When every image, script, and video segment is served from one origin, distant users pay the speed-of-light tax and your origin drowns in bandwidth. A content delivery network pushes copies of that content to edge servers around the world, so most requests are answered close to the user — and never reach you at all.

About these notes: original study material on content delivery networks — a standard, widely-documented engineering topic. The explanations, examples, numbers, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. One diagram is interactive — press Play (or step with Next) to watch the flow.
00 The problem

The single-origin problem

Imagine Shopee runs its entire app from one data centre in Singapore. A shopper in Jakarta opens a product page. Their browser asks for the product photos, the JavaScript bundle, the CSS, the web fonts, and a short promo video. Every one of those bytes has to travel from Singapore to Jakarta and back. That sounds close — it is only about 900 km — but the round trip still costs real time, and for a shopper in São Paulo, roughly 16,000 km away, every byte crosses an ocean and several network operators before it arrives.

Two distinct forces make a single origin painful, and you should name both in an interview because they are different problems with the same cure.

Latency: you cannot out-engineer the speed of light

Data in a fibre cable moves at roughly two-thirds the speed of light. Singapore to São Paulo is about a 160 ms round-trip time (RTT) at best, and real networks add queuing, routing detours, and congestion on top. A single HTTPS connection is not one round trip — it is several: a DNS lookup, the TCP handshake, the TLS handshake, then finally the request itself. Stack three or four RTTs of 160 ms each and the page is visibly stalling before a single image byte has moved. No faster server in Singapore fixes this; the bottleneck is the distance, not the CPU.

Origin load and egress cost

The second force is volume. A product page might pull 2 MB of static assets. If a flash sale draws 500,000 shoppers in ten minutes, that is a terabyte of bandwidth your origin has to push, plus the connections, TLS handshakes, and disk reads behind it — all for files that never change between users. Worse, cloud providers charge for egress (data leaving their network to the internet), and origin egress is among the most expensive bandwidth you can buy. You are paying premium rates to send the exact same logo to half a million people.

The realisation that unlocks the whole lesson: the assets causing the most traffic — images, scripts, fonts, video — are the same for everyone and change rarely. If the same bytes go to millions of people, you should not fetch them from Singapore millions of times. You should keep a copy near each person. That copy-near-the-user idea is a CDN.

01 The fix

What a CDN actually is

A content delivery network is a globally distributed fleet of caching servers — called edge servers or points of presence (PoPs) — placed in data centres and internet exchanges close to where users live. Each edge server holds copies of your static content. When a user requests a file, they are routed to a nearby edge instead of your origin, and most of the time that edge already has the file and answers in a few milliseconds.

The provider that runs the edge fleet — Cloudflare, Amazon CloudFront, Akamai, Fastly, Google Cloud CDN — operates hundreds of PoPs across dozens of countries. You keep one (or a few) origin servers holding the master copy of everything; the CDN's job is to fan that content out to the edges and keep the copies fresh enough.

Why this is more than just "another cache"

You met caching in an earlier lesson as a layer in front of a database. A CDN is the same idea — store a copy so you do not recompute or refetch — moved out to the network's edge and specialised for HTTP. It buys you three things at once:

  • Lower latency. The byte travels tens of kilometres, not thousands. The Jakarta shopper now talks to an edge in Jakarta or Singapore, turning a 160 ms ocean crossing into a sub-10 ms hop.
  • Origin offload. Requests answered at the edge never touch your origin, so your servers, database, and origin egress bill all see a fraction of the real traffic.
  • Absorbed spikes. A flash sale or a viral TikTok clip lands on hundreds of edges in parallel rather than one origin, so the load is spread and your origin stays calm.
Under the hood

Edges do more than store files. Modern CDNs terminate TLS at the edge (so the expensive handshake happens close to the user), keep warm keep-alive connections back to the origin so a miss does not pay a fresh handshake, and run on backbone networks with better peering than the public internet. Even a cache miss served through a CDN is often faster than hitting your origin directly, because the edge-to-origin path is optimised.

02 Routing

How a request finds an edge

A natural question: if there are hundreds of edges, how does the Jakarta browser end up talking to the Jakarta edge and not the one in Frankfurt? Two mechanisms do the routing, and an interviewer may ask you to distinguish them.

DNS-based routing (geo / latency steering)

You point your asset hostname — say cdn.shopee.example — at the CDN by adding a CNAME record in DNS. When a browser resolves that hostname, the CDN's authoritative DNS server inspects the resolver's location and returns the IP address of a nearby edge. Jakarta gets a Southeast-Asian edge IP; São Paulo gets a South-American one. This is the older, widely supported approach. Its weakness is that DNS only knows where the user's resolver is, which is usually close but occasionally wrong (corporate VPNs, public DNS in another region).

Anycast routing

Anycast announces the same IP address from every edge location simultaneously. The internet's own routing protocol (BGP) then delivers each user's packets to whichever edge is closest in network terms. The browser thinks it is talking to one IP; the network silently steers it to the nearest PoP. Cloudflare and Fastly lean heavily on anycast; it also helps absorb DDoS traffic because attack packets get spread across many PoPs instead of pointed at one box.

DNS steering

  • Works everywhere; no special network needed
  • Can route on latency, geography, or load
  • Routes by resolver location, which can be off
  • TTL on the DNS record delays re-routing during a failure

Anycast

  • Routes by true network topology, very fast failover
  • Naturally disperses DDoS load across PoPs
  • Less granular control over which PoP a user lands on
  • Needs the provider to control IP announcements (BGP)

You usually do not pick this yourself — the CDN provider does, and many combine both. The point to land in an interview is that routing the user to the right edge is a solved, automatic part of the product, so you can spend your design time on caching behaviour instead.

03 The core mechanic

Hit, miss & pull-through

Here is the heart of how a CDN keeps content fresh without you having to upload anything to every edge. The default mode is origin-pull (also called lazy or pull-through caching). When a request arrives at an edge, the edge checks whether it holds a fresh copy of that exact object:

  • A cache hit means the edge already has the object and serves it immediately from local memory or disk. The origin never hears about this request.
  • A cache miss means the edge does not have it (the first request for that object at that edge, or the cached copy has expired). The edge fetches the object from your origin, stores a copy locally, and returns it to the user. Every subsequent nearby request for the same object is now a hit.

So the first user to ask for /img/durian-sale.jpg at the Jakarta edge pays the full origin round trip; the next ten thousand Jakarta users are served locally in milliseconds. The cache fills itself, on demand, shaped by real traffic — popular objects naturally end up cached at the edges that need them, and obscure ones do not waste space.

Edge hit ratio: the number that matters

The single metric that tells you whether a CDN is doing its job is the edge hit ratio — the fraction of requests answered from the edge without touching the origin. If 95 of every 100 requests are hits, your hit ratio is 95%, and your origin sees only 5% of traffic. Pushing that ratio from 90% to 98% does not sound like much, but it cuts origin load by five-fold (from 10% to 2% of requests), which is the difference between a comfortable origin and one falling over during a sale.

Hit ratio drops when objects expire too quickly, when URLs vary needlessly (a tracking query string on every link makes each URL look unique and cache separately), or when the edge fleet is so large that traffic for one object is spread thin across many PoPs. The fixes — longer TTLs, normalising URLs, and a shield / parent tier — appear in the next sections.

Under the hood — the shield tier

A subtle scaling problem: if you have 300 edges and each misses independently on a cold object, your origin gets hit 300 times for one file. CDNs solve this with a mid-tier "shield" cache (a regional parent). Edges that miss ask the shield first; the shield misses once, fetches from origin, and serves all the edges behind it. So the origin sees one fetch, not 300 — this is sometimes called cache hierarchy or origin shielding, and it is worth naming when an interviewer asks "what protects the origin during a cold cache?"

04 Filling the cache

Pull vs push

Origin-pull is the default, but it is not the only way content gets onto the edges. The two strategies trade simplicity against control.

Origin-pull (lazy)

As above: you do nothing special. You publish files on your origin, point the CDN at it, and the edges fetch on first request and cache for the object's TTL. This is self-managing and ideal for large catalogues — Shopee has tens of millions of product images, and you would never want to manually push all of them to every edge. The catch is the cold-start miss: the very first user at each edge for each object pays the origin round trip, so a brand-new viral asset hammers the origin until the edges warm up (this is exactly what the shield tier mitigates).

Push

With push (sometimes called pre-warming or static-origin upload), you proactively upload content to the CDN's storage before anyone requests it. You decide what lives where and for how long. This shines for large, infrequently changed files and predictable launches: a software installer, a game patch, or a marketing video for a campaign that goes live at a fixed time. You push it the night before, every edge is warm at launch, and the first user gets a hit. The downside is operational overhead — you own the publishing pipeline and the job of removing stale files.

Origin-pull (lazy)

  • Zero management — edges self-populate from real demand
  • Perfect for huge, churning catalogues
  • Only popular objects occupy edge storage
  • Cold-start: first request per edge is a slow miss
  • A sudden spike on a cold object can stress the origin

Push

  • No cold-start — content is warm before launch
  • Full control of what is cached and when
  • Great for big static files and timed releases
  • You own the upload pipeline and cleanup
  • Wasteful for assets few users will ever fetch
Interview follow-up

If asked "push or pull for this workload?", answer by the access pattern. A long-tail catalogue with unpredictable demand (product images, user uploads) screams pull — you cannot pre-warm 50 million images. A small set of large files with a known launch moment (a GrabPay app update, a campaign trailer) leans push, so the launch spike lands on warm edges. The honest senior answer is "mostly pull, with push pre-warming for the handful of assets behind a big launch."

05 Freshness control

TTL & cache headers

An edge needs to know how long it may keep an object before checking again. That lifespan is the object's time-to-live (TTL), and you control it with HTTP response headers your origin sends. Get this right and you tune the dial between freshness (always current) and origin load (rarely refetch). This is the single most consequential thing you configure on a CDN.

Cache-Control

The Cache-Control response header is the modern way to set caching policy. Its directives mean specific things, and an interviewer will respect you for knowing them precisely:

  • max-age=N — how many seconds any cache (including the browser) may treat the object as fresh.
  • s-maxage=N — overrides max-age for shared caches like the CDN, letting you keep an object at the edge for a week while the browser only caches it for an hour.
  • public — the response may be stored by shared caches (CDN, proxies). Required for a CDN to cache it.
  • private — only the end user's browser may cache it; shared caches must not. Use for per-user responses.
  • no-cache — a confusing name: the object may be stored, but the cache must revalidate with the origin before reusing it.
  • no-store — never store this anywhere. Use for genuinely sensitive responses (a bank statement, an auth token).
Cache-Control: public, max-age=300, s-maxage=2592000
ETag: "v3-9f3a1c"

That header says: browsers may treat the file as fresh for 5 minutes, but the CDN edge may hold it for 30 days. A long edge TTL is exactly what drives the hit ratio up.

ETag and revalidation

When an object expires, the edge does not always re-download the whole file. With an ETag (an opaque version fingerprint the origin attaches to a response), the edge sends a conditional request using If-None-Match: "v3-9f3a1c". If the file has not changed, the origin replies 304 Not Modified with an empty body — a tiny message that costs almost no bandwidth and refreshes the TTL. Only if the ETag differs does the origin send the full new body. (The older Last-Modified / If-Modified-Since pair does the same job with a timestamp.) Revalidation is how you keep things fresh cheaply instead of re-transferring megabytes.

stale-while-revalidate

The directive stale-while-revalidate=N is a quietly powerful trick: it tells the edge that when an object expires, it may serve the slightly stale copy immediately to the user while it refreshes from origin in the background. The user never waits on the revalidation — they get an instant (maybe seconds-old) response, and the next user gets the fresh one. This hides origin latency entirely and is great for content that tolerates a few seconds of staleness, like a homepage banner or a category listing.

The freshness vs origin-load trade-off

A short TTL means edges revalidate often, so content is always current but the origin sees more traffic and the hit ratio falls. A long TTL means the origin is barely touched and the hit ratio soars, but a change can take the full TTL to propagate to every edge. The standard resolution: give versioned, immutable assets (covered next) a very long TTL — a year — and give mutable but cacheable content (an API-driven listing) a short TTL plus stale-while-revalidate. The trick is that you rarely need short TTLs once you make your asset URLs change whenever the content changes.

Interview follow-up

Expect "what TTL would you set?" The strong answer separates two cases. Fingerprinted static assets (app.9f3a1.js) get max-age=31536000, immutable — a year, because the URL changes when the content does, so a stale copy is impossible. Dynamic-but-cacheable content gets a short s-maxage (say 30–60s) with stale-while-revalidate so users never block on a refresh. Saying "one year for fingerprinted, seconds for the rest" shows you understand the freshness dial.

06 Updating content

Invalidation & cache-busting

You have set a 30-day edge TTL on your JavaScript bundle to get a great hit ratio. Then you ship a critical bug fix. The new code is on your origin, but hundreds of edges around the world are still happily serving the 30-day-old broken version to users. This is the classic CDN problem: cache invalidation. You have two ways out, and one of them is much better than the other.

Explicit purge — the slow, expensive way

Every CDN offers a purge (or invalidate) API: you call it with a URL or a path pattern and the CDN evicts that object from its edges. It works, but it has real downsides. Purges are relatively slow — propagating an eviction to every PoP can take seconds to minutes. Providers often rate-limit or charge for purges, especially wildcard purges that wipe a whole prefix. And there is a window during which some edges have purged and others have not, so users may see a mix of old and new — bad if the old HTML references assets the new code expects. Purge is the right tool for an emergency (legal takedown, leaked file), not your everyday deploy mechanism.

Cache-busting with versioned URLs — the right default

The elegant fix is to never update an object in place. Instead, make a new version of the asset a new URL. During your build, compute a hash of each file's contents and embed it in the filename — a content fingerprint:

before deploy:  /static/app.9f3a1c.js   (Cache-Control: max-age=31536000, immutable)
after deploy:   /static/app.7b22e8.js   <- new content = new hash = new URL

Because the URL changed, the browser and every edge see a brand-new object they have never cached, so they fetch the fresh version on first request — no purge needed. The old app.9f3a1c.js simply stops being referenced and ages out of caches on its own when its TTL lapses. You get the best of both worlds: a one-year TTL (maximum hit ratio) and instant updates on deploy.

Build tools do this for you automatically: webpack, Vite, esbuild, and Rollup all emit [name].[contenthash].js filenames and rewrite the HTML references to match. You then serve the HTML itself with a short TTL (it is small and must point at the latest hashes), and the heavy assets with a one-year immutable TTL.

Interview follow-up

The exact question is often "how do you invalidate a stale asset?" The senior answer is: "Prefer not to invalidate at all — fingerprint asset URLs so a new deploy is a new URL and old copies age out naturally; keep the small HTML entry point on a short TTL. Reserve explicit purge for emergencies like a legal takedown, because purges are slow, sometimes rate-limited, and leave a propagation window of inconsistency."

07 Cacheability

What belongs on a CDN

A CDN only helps for content that is the same for many users and safe to store. The dividing line is whether a response is cacheable — and getting this wrong is dangerous, because caching a personalised response means serving one user's data to another.

Static and shared content — cache it

These are identical for everyone and change rarely, so they are ideal edge content:

  • Images — product photos, thumbnails, icons, banners.
  • CSS and JavaScript bundles — fingerprinted, so cached for a year.
  • Web fonts — small, reused on every page, never change.
  • Video and audio segments — the chunked files of an HLS or DASH stream (see below).
  • Downloadable files — PDFs, installers, app binaries.

Dynamic and personalised content — generally not

A logged-in user's cart, a personalised feed, the GrabPay wallet balance, search results tied to one account — these differ per user and per moment. Caching them at a shared edge would leak data or show stale state, so they are marked private or no-store and go straight to the origin. The rule of thumb: if the response depends on who is asking or changes every second, it does not belong in a shared cache.

Streaming video: where the CDN earns its keep

Modern video uses adaptive bitrate streaming via HLS or DASH. The player downloads a small manifest (a playlist of available quality levels) and then fetches the video as a long sequence of short segments — typically 2–6 second chunks, each a separate, immutable file with its own URL. Those segment files are perfect CDN content: identical for every viewer, never changing, and enormous in aggregate. A single popular video might be tens of thousands of segment files across several bitrates; serving them from the edge is the only way to deliver video at scale without your origin egress bill exploding. The manifest gets a short TTL (it can change for live streams); the segments get a long one.

The middle ground: edge compute

Between "fully static" and "fully dynamic" sits content that is nearly the same for everyone but needs a small per-request tweak — injecting a locale, doing an A/B split, checking an auth token. CDNs now run small functions at the edge (Cloudflare Workers, AWS Lambda@Edge, Fastly Compute) so you can personalise or make a routing decision at the edge without a round trip to the origin. It is worth a one-line mention: "the parts that vary slightly can run as edge functions, so even semi-dynamic responses stay close to the user."

Why the Vary header matters

Some responses differ by request header — for instance, you serve WebP to browsers that support it and JPEG to those that do not, keyed on the Accept header, or a compressed body keyed on Accept-Encoding. The Vary response header tells the cache "store a separate copy per value of this header." Used carefully it is essential; used carelessly (e.g. Vary: User-Agent, which has thousands of distinct values) it shatters your cache into near-unique entries and destroys your hit ratio.

08 Security

Private media & TLS

"Cacheable static content" sounds like it must be public. But plenty of valuable static media is private — a paid course video, a user's uploaded receipt on a SeaMoney KYC flow, a premium ebook. You still want the CDN's speed and offload, but you must not let just anyone fetch the file by guessing the URL. The mechanism is signed URLs (or signed cookies).

How signed URLs work

Your origin generates a URL containing a cryptographic signature and an expiry timestamp, signed with a secret key the CDN also knows. The edge validates the signature and expiry before serving the file; if either is wrong or expired, it returns 403 Forbidden without touching the origin. So you hand an authenticated, logged-in user a URL like video.mp4?Expires=1717200000&Signature=... that works for, say, the next ten minutes and only for them. The file is cached at the edge for everyone (great hit ratio), but only holders of a valid, unexpired signature can pull it. Anyone who copies the link finds it dead after the window closes.

TLS at the edge

Every CDN terminates TLS at the edge: the HTTPS handshake — the most latency-sensitive part of a connection — happens at a PoP a few milliseconds from the user rather than across an ocean to your origin. The CDN holds (or auto-provisions, e.g. via Let's Encrypt) the certificate for your asset hostname. From the edge back to your origin, traffic rides a separate, often longer-lived secure connection. The user gets a fast handshake and a padlock; you get the connection setup cost moved off your origin entirely.

Interview follow-up

A common probe: "how do you serve private or authenticated media through a CDN?" The crisp answer: "Use signed URLs or signed cookies — the origin issues a short-lived, cryptographically signed link tied to the user; the edge validates the signature and expiry and rejects anything else with a 403. The object is still cached at the edge, so you keep the performance, but access is gated. Pair it with HTTPS so the link cannot be sniffed in transit." Mentioning expiry windows shows you have thought about leaked-link abuse.

09 Resilience & economics

Failure, fallback & cost

Two final things an interviewer expects you to address for any infrastructure component: what happens when it breaks, and what it costs.

When an edge dies

A CDN is built to lose nodes gracefully. If a single edge server fails, the routing layer (DNS health checks or anycast withdrawal of that PoP's route) simply stops sending traffic there and steers users to the next-nearest healthy PoP. Those users see slightly higher latency until the node recovers, but no errors. Because edges hold only cached copies of content the origin still has, losing an edge loses no data — the new edge just re-pulls on its first miss. This statelessness is precisely why CDNs scale: any edge can serve any request, and adding or removing edges is routine.

When the whole CDN is unreachable

Rarer, but it happens — a provider-wide outage or a regional network partition. The safety net is origin fallback: you keep the origin reachable and healthy so that, worst case, traffic can flow directly to it (slower and more expensive, but functioning). Robust setups go further with multi-CDN: register two providers and use DNS or a traffic-steering layer to fail over from one to the other. The principle is the same as everywhere in distributed systems — do not let a component you do not fully control become a single point of failure for the whole product.

The cost argument

CDNs are not only about speed — they are frequently a cost saving. CDN bandwidth is typically much cheaper per gigabyte than cloud origin egress, and you buy far less of it because the edge absorbs the bulk of requests. Walk the numbers: suppose a campaign serves 100 TB of video in a month. From cloud origin egress at, say, US$0.08–0.12/GB, that is roughly US$8,000–12,000 — and your origin servers have to push every byte. Move it behind a CDN at a blended US$0.02–0.04/GB with a 95% hit ratio, and not only is the per-GB rate lower, but your origin only ever serves the 5% of misses. The bandwidth bill drops by most of its value and your origin fleet shrinks. The CDN often pays for itself purely on egress.

What breaks at 10x

Scale a Shopee-style flash sale 10x and the failure mode is not the edges — they scale horizontally — but the cold-miss stampede on a freshly deployed asset. Ten million users hit edges that all miss simultaneously, and without origin shielding those misses pile onto one origin. Mitigations: pre-warm (push) the launch assets, enable origin shielding so misses collapse to a single origin fetch, set immutable year-long TTLs on fingerprinted assets so nothing needlessly revalidates, and keep dynamic API traffic on a completely separate path so a static-content spike cannot starve it.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: keep copies of your shared, rarely-changing content on edge servers near users, so most requests are answered fast and locally while your origin sees only the misses. If you can reason about hit ratio, TTLs, pull-vs-push, and how you update a stale asset, you can carry this part of the interview.
