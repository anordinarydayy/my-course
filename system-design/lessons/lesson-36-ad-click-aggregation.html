Design Ad Click Event Aggregation

Counting ad clicks sounds trivial — increment a number — until you remember those counts are what advertisers get billed for, so they must be exactly-once accurate, while the dashboard on the screen wants them fresh by the minute. That tug-of-war between accuracy and freshness is the entire design, and it's exactly where the lambda-versus-kappa argument lives.

About these notes: original study material on stream aggregation and exactly-once processing — standard, widely-documented data-engineering ground. The explanations, numbers, schemas, and diagrams here are my own; nothing is copied from any paid course or book. Singapore context (Shopee / TikTok / Sea ads) is used to make the examples concrete. 00 The problem

Why counting clicks is hard

Imagine you run the ads platform behind Shopee's search results or TikTok's in-feed video ads in Singapore. A merchant pays for clicks. Every time a shopper taps an ad, the system records a click event — and at the end of the day (or sooner) the merchant is charged for the total. The product team also stares at a live dashboard: clicks per minute, click-through rate, spend pacing, which creatives are winning.

The naive version is one line of code: counter[ad_id] += 1. It falls apart the moment you take it seriously, because three forces pull against each other:

  • Volume. Tens of thousands of clicks per second at peak, billions per day. No single database row can absorb that write rate, and no single machine can hold the counters.
  • Billing-grade accuracy. The count is money. If you count a click twice, you over-charge a merchant; if you drop one, you under-bill yourself. A double-count rate of 1% on a million-dollar campaign is a five-figure dispute. So aggregation must be exactly-once, not "roughly right."
  • Freshness. Advertisers adjust bids in real time. A dashboard that's an hour stale is useless for pacing a flash-sale campaign. They want per-minute numbers within a minute or two.

Accuracy says "take your time, double-check, reconcile." Freshness says "show me now." A click that arrives late — a shopper on the MRT whose phone buffered the event for ten minutes underground — sits squarely in the crossfire: do you wait for it (accurate) or publish without it (fresh)? Holding both is the whole job, and it pushes you straight into stream processing.

01 Scope it

Clarify the requirements & state assumptions

Before drawing boxes, narrow the problem out loud. The interviewer wants to see you separate what matters from what doesn't.

Functional requirements

  • Aggregate clicks per ad per time window. The core output is "ad X got N clicks in minute M." We'll treat impressions the same way; clicks are the higher-stakes path because they drive billing.
  • Serve two consumers. A near-real-time dashboard (top-N ads, clicks over the last few minutes) and a billing pipeline that reads daily totals to invoice merchants.
  • Support filtering. Queries by ad_id, by country (SG vs ID vs MY), by time range. Pre-aggregation has to anticipate these dimensions.
  • Allow late-data correction. A click that shows up minutes after it happened must still land in the correct window's total, even after that window was first published.

Non-functional requirements

  • Correctness: billing must be exactly-once. Every real click counts exactly one time, no matter how many times it is retried or replayed.
  • Latency / freshness: dashboard end-to-end lag under ~1–2 minutes.
  • Scalability: handle today's tens-of-thousands-per-second and a 10x spike during a regional mega-sale (think 9.9 / 11.11 / 12.12).
  • Availability & durability: no click is silently lost; the system survives a node death and can recover from a bad deploy by replaying history.

Assumptions worth saying aloud

  • Each raw click already carries a unique event_id stamped at the edge (we'll lean on this hard for dedup).
  • Click fraud detection (bots, click farms) is a separate upstream system; we ingest already-validated clicks. Worth naming so the interviewer knows you know it exists.
  • We need minute-level granularity for dashboards and day-level for billing.
Interview tip

The single sentence that frames everything: "this is a write-heavy aggregation problem where the same data feeds a fresh-but-approximate dashboard and an exact-but-slower billing total — so my whole design is about reconciling those two." Say that and the interviewer knows you've seen the trap.

02 Estimation

Back-of-the-envelope sizing

Numbers anchor every later decision — they tell you whether one machine or a thousand. Pick round figures and reason from them.

Throughput (QPS)

  • Assume 1 billion clicks/day. Average rate: 1e9 / 86,400 s ≈ ~11,600 clicks/sec.
  • Traffic is peaky. Use a 5x peak factor → ~58,000 clicks/sec at peak. During a 12.12 mega-sale, layer a 10x event spike on top and you're staring at hundreds of thousands per second.
  • Impressions run ~10–50x clicks, so if you aggregate those too, the ingestion layer must comfortably handle hundreds of thousands to millions of events/sec.

Storage

  • A raw click record — event_id, ad_id, user_id, timestamp, country, device — is roughly 0.1–0.5 KB. Take 0.2 KB.
  • Raw events: 1e9 × 0.2 KB = ~200 GB/day, or ~73 TB/year if kept raw. You keep these in the durable log + cheap object storage, not in the hot query store.
  • Aggregated data is tiny by comparison. Say 1 million active ads × 1,440 minutes/day × ~50 bytes per (ad, minute) row ≈ ~72 GB/day of minute-level rollups — and most ads are inactive most minutes, so the real figure is far smaller. The win of aggregation: queries hit gigabytes, not terabytes.

Read load

  • Dashboard reads are modest — thousands of analysts/advertisers polling, maybe a few thousand QPS, all served from pre-aggregated rollups. The system is overwhelmingly write-heavy.
What the numbers tell us

Writes dwarf reads by orders of magnitude, and raw volume (TB/day) dwarfs aggregated volume (GB/day). That justifies the architecture before we draw it: ingest into a horizontally-scalable log, aggregate in-stream, and store only the small rollups in a fast query store. Keep the firehose of raw events in cheap durable storage for replay.

03 Contract

API & data model

Two surfaces: how events get in, and how aggregates come out. Sketching real shapes here scores points and forces clarity about the dedup key.

Ingestion: the click event

The edge (web/app SDK or an ad server) emits an event. The non-negotiable field is event_id — a globally unique id minted at the source so we can deduplicate downstream.

POST /v1/events
{
  "event_id":  "0b9e...e41",   // UUID minted at the edge — the dedup key
  "ad_id":     "ad_88421",
  "campaign_id":"cmp_502",
  "user_id":   "u_173",        // hashed; for fraud + uniqueness, not billing
  "event_type":"click",        // click | impression
  "event_ts":  1733980812345,  // when it HAPPENED (event time, ms)
  "country":   "SG",
  "device":    "ios"
}
=> 202 Accepted              // we durably enqueue, then return fast

Two timestamps matter and they are not the same: event time (event_ts, when the click happened on the device) versus processing time (when the server received it). The gap between them is exactly the late-data problem from Step 00.

Query: the aggregate read

GET /v1/aggregate?ad_id=ad_88421&from=2026-05-30T10:00&to=2026-05-30T11:00&granularity=minute
=> {
  "ad_id":"ad_88421",
  "granularity":"minute",
  "buckets":[
    {"window_start":"2026-05-30T10:00", "clicks":1422, "version":3},
    {"window_start":"2026-05-30T10:01", "clicks":1380, "version":1}, ...
  ]
}

GET /v1/top?country=SG&window=2026-05-30T10:00&n=10   // top-N ads by clicks

Note version on each bucket — it bumps when a late event corrects an already-published count, so clients can tell a number is final vs still settling.

The aggregated table

The heart of the storage layer is one wide row keyed by (ad, minute), pre-aggregated along the dimensions we query:

TABLE ad_click_minute
  ad_id        STRING        -- partition key (route + shard by this)
  window_start TIMESTAMP     -- clustering key, minute-aligned
  country      STRING        -- secondary dimension
  click_count  COUNTER/BIGINT
  version      INT           -- incremented on late correction
  updated_at   TIMESTAMP
  PRIMARY KEY ((ad_id), window_start, country)

And a small dedup ledger the stream processor consults (or, better, holds inside its own checkpointed state — more in Step 06):

TABLE processed_events     -- only if dedup is externalized
  event_id   STRING  PRIMARY KEY
  seen_at    TIMESTAMP
  ttl        -- expire after the max allowed lateness window
Follow-up to expect

"Why event_id at the edge and not generated server-side?" Because retries. The SDK may resend the same click after a flaky network; if the id is minted server-side per arrival, the two arrivals look like two distinct clicks and you double-bill. Minting it once, at the source, makes the click identifiable across every retry and replay.

04 The skeleton

High-level pipeline & data flow

Now the architecture. The shape that handles high-volume, accurate, fresh aggregation is a streaming pipeline: durable log → stream processor → aggregated store. Walk it left to right.

The durable log (Kafka)

The ingestion service does almost nothing but validate and append the event to a durable, append-only log — in practice Apache Kafka (or AWS Kinesis / GCP Pub-Sub). This is the single most important box. It decouples a bursty producer from a steadier consumer (absorbing the 10x sale spike as backlog instead of dropped data), and because the log retains events for days, it is also our source of truth for replay. Lose your aggregates to a bug? Rewind the consumer offset and recompute. Topic ad_clicks is partitioned by ad_id so all clicks for one ad land on one partition and one consumer — which keeps a window's counting on a single worker and gives ordering per ad.

The stream processor (Flink)

A stream processor — Apache Flink is the strongest default here, with Spark Structured Streaming as the alternative — consumes the log. For each event it: extracts event_ts, assigns the event to a time window, deduplicates by event_id against its keyed state, and increments that window's running count. Flink earns its place because it gives us native event-time windows, watermarks, and checkpointed exactly-once state — the three things this problem is made of (Steps 05 and 06).

The aggregated store + dashboard

When a window closes (or on a periodic flush), the processor writes the rollup to an OLAP / time-series store — ClickHouse, Apache Druid, Apache Pinot, or Cassandra for counters. The dashboard and billing job read from there. Raw events also fan out to cheap object storage (S3) for long-term replay and audit.

Why a log and not a database

If the ingest service wrote straight to a database, the database's write capacity would cap your peak QPS and a slow downstream would back-pressure the edge into dropping clicks. The log inverts this: ingest is a fast append, consumers pull at their own pace, and a backlog is just disk — not data loss. The log is the shock absorber and the time machine in one.

05 Time, properly

Windowed aggregation & watermarks

"Clicks per minute" means grouping events into buckets of time. That grouping is windowing, and getting it right under late data is most of the deep-dive.

Tumbling vs sliding windows

A tumbling window chops time into fixed, non-overlapping slices — minute 10:00–10:01, then 10:01–10:02. Each event belongs to exactly one window. This is the natural fit for "clicks per minute" and for billing (each click is billed once, in one window). A sliding window overlaps — "clicks in the last 5 minutes, recomputed every minute" — so each event lands in several windows. Sliding is for smoothed trend lines on the dashboard; tumbling is for the canonical, bill-once counts.

Tumbling window

  • Non-overlapping; each event counted once → matches billing
  • Cheap state: one accumulator per (ad, window)
  • Clear "this window is now closed" semantics

Sliding window

  • Overlapping; an event feeds multiple windows
  • Great for smooth moving-average dashboards
  • More state and compute; wrong for bill-once totals

The late / out-of-order problem

Events do not arrive in event-time order. The MRT-tunnel phone buffers a click and sends it 10 minutes later; a retry resurfaces an old event; partitions deliver at slightly different speeds. If you close the 10:00 window the instant your wall clock hits 10:01 and a real 10:00 click arrives at 10:03, you've under-counted minute 10:00. So the question becomes: how long do you wait before declaring a window final?

Watermarks

A watermark is the stream processor's assertion: "I believe I have now seen all events with event_ts at or before time W." It is event time's progress meter. Flink computes it from the timestamps it observes, minus a configured allowed lateness bound — e.g. "watermark = max event_ts seen − 2 minutes." A window fires (emits its result) only when the watermark passes the window's end. So the 10:00–10:01 window emits when the watermark reaches 10:01, which — with a 2-minute lateness bound — happens around processing-time 10:03. That 2-minute wait is the price you pay to catch stragglers; it is the freshness-vs-accuracy dial, made concrete.

Events later than the watermark are late events. You have two honest choices: drop them (and accept a tiny under-count on the dashboard), or keep the window's state around a while longer and emit a correction/update — which is exactly what billing needs (Step 08). Flink supports both via allowedLateness and side-outputs for the truly-too-late.

The classic follow-up

"How do you handle a click that arrives 10 minutes late?" Answer in two moves:
(1) the watermark's allowed-lateness sets a wait — say 2–3 minutes — that catches normal jitter and is the freshness cost.
(2) For events past that bound, you don't drop money: keep window state long enough to emit a correction to the store (bumping version), or route truly-ancient stragglers to a side stream that the daily batch reconciliation sweeps up before billing closes. Name both the stream fix and the batch backstop.

06 The money path

Exactly-once counting for billing

This is the section that wins the interview, because it's where most candidates wave their hands. The trap: a durable log gives you at-least-once delivery by default, and at-least-once double-counts money.

Why at-least-once double-counts

Picture the processor consuming event e42, incrementing the count, and then crashing before it commits its read offset back to Kafka. On restart, it doesn't know it already processed e42, so it reads it again and increments a second time. The merchant is now billed for one click as if it were two. The same happens on producer retries (the SDK resends a click whose ack was lost) and on every replay. At-least-once is the default precisely because guaranteeing the message is delivered is easy; guaranteeing it is effected exactly once is the hard part.

Mechanism 1 — idempotent dedup by event_id

Because every click carries a unique event_id, the processor can make counting idempotent: before incrementing, check whether this event_id has already been counted in this window's state. If yes, skip it; if no, record it and increment. Replaying the same event a hundred times then changes nothing after the first. The dedup set lives in the processor's keyed state (keyed by ad_id, so it's local and fast), scoped to the window and bounded by a TTL equal to the max lateness — you don't need to remember every id forever, only long enough that no duplicate can still arrive.

Mechanism 2 — checkpointing & atomic commit

Dedup state is only trustworthy if it survives a crash consistently with the read offset. That's what checkpointing does. Flink periodically snapshots all operator state — the window accumulators and the dedup set and the Kafka offsets — into durable storage (S3/RocksDB) as one atomic checkpoint. On failure it restores the latest checkpoint: state and offset rewind together. Combined with a transactional / idempotent sink (Flink's two-phase-commit sink, or writes the store can dedup by a key), the end-to-end effect is exactly-once: a replay re-reads events the snapshot hadn't yet committed, and the idempotent sink + dedup state ensure each is applied once.

Idempotent sink — the last mile

Even with perfect processing, the write to the store can be retried. So the sink must be idempotent too. Two common patterns: write the rollup with a deterministic key like (ad_id, window_start) as an upsert/overwrite of an absolute value (not a blind += delta), so replaying the same write yields the same final number; or attach a version/checkpoint-id so the store ignores a stale or duplicate apply.

At-least-once (default)

  • Simple; no dedup state needed
  • Never loses an event
  • But replays/retries double-count → over-bills
  • Only acceptable for fuzzy dashboards, not billing

Exactly-once (billing)

  • event_id dedup + checkpointed state + idempotent sink
  • Replays are safe → correct invoices
  • Costs state, dedup lookups, and 2-phase-commit latency
  • The required mode for the money path
The headline follow-up

"How do you count exactly once for billing?" The crisp answer: "unique event_id minted at the edge → dedup against checkpointed keyed state in the processor → write through an idempotent sink keyed by (ad_id, window). The log gives at-least-once delivery; the dedup + atomic checkpoint + idempotent sink upgrade it to exactly-once effect." Stress the word effect: you can't stop a message being delivered twice, but you can ensure it changes the count only once.

07 The big architecture call

Lambda vs Kappa

Now the architecture question this whole topic is famous for. You have a fast-but-fuzzy dashboard need and a slow-but-exact billing need from the same events. Two schools of thought reconcile them.

Lambda architecture

Lambda runs two pipelines over the same source. A speed layer (the stream processor) produces fresh, approximate counts within seconds for the dashboard. A batch layer reprocesses the full raw event log periodically (say hourly/daily, via Spark over S3) to produce a slow, authoritative, exactly-correct total — naturally catching every late event because batch sees the whole day at once. A serving layer merges them: show the speed number now, overwrite it with the batch number when it lands. Billing reads the batch result.

Kappa architecture

Kappa says: maintaining two codebases that must agree is a bug factory. Instead, run one stream pipeline. When you need to correct history — a bug, a logic change, a late-data sweep — you replay the durable log from an old offset through the (possibly fixed) same code, and overwrite the results. There is no separate batch layer; "batch" is just "a replay of the stream." This is viable precisely because we already insisted on a durable, replayable log (Kafka) and idempotent sinks (Step 06) — replaying can't double-count.

Lambda

  • Batch layer is dead-simple to reason about → high confidence in the billing total
  • Speed + batch decouple freshness from accuracy cleanly
  • Cost: two codebases, two infra stacks, logic must match or numbers disagree — the dreaded reconciliation drift

Kappa

  • One codebase, one mental model → far less drift
  • Corrections = replay the log through the same logic
  • Cost: the stream layer alone must be exactly-once and correct under late data; long replays can be slow and need enough log retention
Which to pick

Be opinionated but reasoned. Kappa is the modern default when your stream engine genuinely supports exactly-once and event-time windowing (Flink does), because one codebase means one source of truth and no reconciliation drift. Lambda still earns its keep when billing teams demand a dead-simple, independently-auditable batch total they trust over any streaming number — common in finance and at firms with regulatory scrutiny on revenue. A strong answer: "Kappa for the pipeline, with a thin daily batch reconciliation/audit job over S3 as a safety net — so I get one codebase plus an independent check on the money."

The direct ask

"Lambda or kappa?" Don't hedge into mush. Lead with kappa-plus-audit, justify it with "we already have a durable replayable log and idempotent sinks, so a replay is our batch layer without a second codebase," then concede the one case where pure lambda wins (an auditor who must verify revenue from raw data with code no streaming engineer can touch).

08 Where counts live

Storage, pre-aggregation & reconciliation

The processor emits small rollups; where they land shapes query latency and how corrections work.

The right store: OLAP / time-series

Dashboard queries are "sum clicks for ad X over a time range, maybe grouped by country, give me top-N." That's an analytical, range-scan, aggregation workload — the home turf of column-oriented OLAP stores: ClickHouse, Apache Druid, Apache Pinot, or BigQuery for batch. A row store like PostgreSQL chokes on these scans at scale; a counter-friendly store like Cassandra works if you model carefully. The key property: data already aggregated to (ad, minute) so a query touches thousands of rows, not billions of raw events.

Pre-aggregation & rollups

Pre-aggregation means the heavy GROUP BY ad_id, minute happens once, in-stream, at write time — not on every dashboard read. We also roll up minute data into hour and day tables on a schedule, so a "this campaign, last 30 days" query reads ~30 day-rows instead of 43,200 minute-rows. Trade-off: rollups cost storage and a little write amplification, but turn an expensive scan into a cheap lookup. This is the read-vs-write trade in its purest form, and it's the right call here because reads must be fast and writes are already the bottleneck we engineered for.

Late-arrival correction & reconciliation

Because the store uses upsert by (ad_id, window) with a version, a late event that re-fires a window simply overwrites the row with the new, higher count and bumps the version — the dashboard re-reads it and the number ticks up. For billing, the reconciliation job is the backstop: before the daily invoice closes, a batch sweep over the raw S3 log recomputes each ad's true daily total and corrects any drift the stream left behind (super-late events, a transient bug). Billing reads the reconciled total; the dashboard happily showed the slightly-fuzzy live number hours earlier. That split is the accuracy-vs-freshness resolution in one sentence.

-- idempotent correction write (overwrite absolute value, not a delta)
UPSERT INTO ad_click_minute (ad_id, window_start, country, click_count, version)
VALUES ('ad_88421', '2026-05-30T10:00', 'SG', 1423, 4)   -- was 1422 v3
ON CONFLICT (ad_id, window_start, country)
  DO UPDATE SET click_count = 1423, version = 4 WHERE version < 4;
Follow-up

"What if the streaming number and the reconciled batch number disagree?" The reconciled batch total over raw data is authoritative for billing; the stream value was always advertised as provisional (that's what version and the dashboard's "settling" indicator signal). The interesting follow-up underneath: persistently large drift means a real bug — a partition lagging, dedup TTL too short to catch late dupes, or a sink dropping writes — so you alert on drift magnitude, not just correct it silently.

09 Breaking it on purpose

Scale, failure modes & what breaks at 10x

Stress every box and name what gives first.

Partitioning & the hot-ad problem

We partition the Kafka topic and shard the store by ad_id so one ad's clicks are ordered on one worker. That works until a single ad goes viral — a TikTok creative or a Shopee 12.12 hero banner gets a disproportionate share of all clicks. Now one partition and one processor task is a hotspot while the rest idle, and that one window's state and throughput become the ceiling. Fixes: a two-stage aggregation — salt the key (ad_id + bucket_0..N) to spread the hot ad across N partitions for a first partial count, then sum the N partials in a second stage. Or detect hot keys dynamically and split them. Trade-off: more partials to merge, but no single task melts.

Failure: replay from the durable log

What happens when a processor node dies mid-window? Flink restores the latest checkpoint — state and Kafka offset rewind together — and reprocesses from there; the idempotent dedup + sink mean the reprocessed events don't double-count. What if the aggregates are corrupted by a bad deploy? Because the raw events live in Kafka (days of retention) and S3 (forever), you rewind the consumer offset and replay the affected window through fixed code; idempotent upserts overwrite the bad rollups with correct ones. This is why the log being the source of truth, plus idempotency, isn't optional — it's what makes recovery a config change instead of a data-loss incident.

What breaks at 10x (the mega-sale)

  • Ingest QPS: the log absorbs the spike as backlog — that's its job — but the processor lags. Fix: autoscale processor parallelism (more Flink task slots, more Kafka partitions provisioned ahead of the sale). The dashboard goes a few minutes staler; billing is unaffected because reconciliation catches up.
  • Dedup state size: 10x events means 10x event_ids held in keyed state within the lateness window. Mitigate with RocksDB-backed state (spills to disk) and a tight, correct TTL; don't keep ids longer than a duplicate could possibly arrive.
  • Hot partition: sale traffic concentrates on a few hero ads → the hot-key problem above, now acute. The salted two-stage aggregation is what saves you.
  • Store write pressure: more (ad, minute) upserts. ClickHouse/Druid ingest scales horizontally; pre-rollup and shard-by-ad keep any single shard bounded.
  • Watermark stalls: if one slow partition holds back the watermark, every window stops firing and the whole dashboard freezes. Mitigate with idle-partition detection and bounded watermark skew so one lagging shard can't stall the world.
The recurring shape

Every failure mode resolves to the same two tools: the durable log (absorb spikes, replay to recover) and idempotency (so absorbing and replaying never corrupt the money). If you can name those two as your answer to "what breaks and how do you recover," you've understood the system.

Last follow-up

"A whole region's data center goes down — now what?" Kafka is replicated across brokers/AZs so the log survives; the processor restarts in another zone from its last checkpoint; in-flight clicks queued at the edge SDK retry into the surviving region (same event_id → dedup makes the retry safe). The only visible effect is a brief dashboard lag while the consumer catches up on backlog — and billing, reading the reconciled total, never notices.

★ Review

Summary & self-test

The whole topic compresses to one sentence: ingest into a durable, replayable log, aggregate it in-stream into time windows with watermarks, make the count exactly-once via event-id dedup plus checkpointing plus an idempotent sink, and resolve freshness-vs-accuracy by serving provisional stream numbers to the dashboard while billing reads a reconciled total. If you can walk that chain and defend lambda-vs-kappa, you can carry the interview.
